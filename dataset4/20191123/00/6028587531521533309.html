Box Office: ‘A Beautiful Day In The Neighborhood’ Earns $4.5 Million Friday As ‘21 Bridges’ Nabs $3.3 Million

23 Nov 2019 by Scott Mendelson

In Friday box office news that isn’t about Frozen II, Sony’s A Beautiful Day in the Neighborhood earned $4.545 million on its first day, including $900,000 in Thursday previews. That positions Marielle Heller’s terrific drama, starring Tom Hanks as Mister Rogers, for a $14 million opening weekend. That’s on the low end of expectations, but the hope is that the $25 million drama will leg out over the next month. It’s a terrific movie and absolutely should be in the awards season conversation. And if it clicks, relatively speaking, then it could end up with around $50-$55 million domestic. The only caveat is that it’s closer in spirit to The Secret Life of Walter Mitty and Saving Mr. Banks, seemingly kid-targeted films that are very much for adults, as opposed to Wonder or The Blind Side.

STX Entertainment’s 21 Bridges opened yesterday with $3.33 million on Friday, including $700,000 in Thursday previews. That positions the $33 million, R-rated Chadwick Boseman action flick for a $10 million opening weekend. That’s a not great start for the decent, well-made cop thriller (it earned a B+ from Cinemascre), but this as a co-production between STX, MWM Studios and Huayi Brothers.

Yes, I will again note that these kind of non-franchise star vehicles were supposed to be part of the proverbial “end game” for the push for diversity/inclusivity, where Chadwick Boseman could have the kind of star vehicles that Ryan Reynolds took for granted. And yet, we remain more excited about Naomi Harris playing Moneypenny or a baddie in Venom 2 than starring in the original, topical, and very good actioner Black & Blue.

In platform debuts, Focus Features’ Dark Waters opened with $33,000 on Friday in four theaters. That sets Todd Haynes’ well-reviewed (and, yes, I watched it last night and it’s quite good) Mark Ruffalo/Anne Hathaway/Tim Robbins legal drama up for a $110,000 weekend. That’s an okay $27,460 per-theater average, and frankly I’d be pleasantly surprised if this grim and mournful true-life tale of corporate corruption broke out in this “escapism only” era.

Adults have Ford v Ferrari and (starting next week) Knives Out for high quality grown up movies that are “fun” as well as “good.” It will be interesting to see how the “good but grim” likes of Honey Boy ($951,000 after 17 days), Waves ($375,000 after ten days) and Queen and Slim (opening next week and, yes, it’s excellent) perform alongside the conventionally escapist fare.
