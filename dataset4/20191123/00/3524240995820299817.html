As a child, I was pushed into pit, pelted with stones

23 Nov 2019, 00:07 by Alexander Okere

Precious Agaezichi was born with phocomelia (thalidomide syndrome), a rare birth defect that can affect the upper and/or lower limbs which could be caused as a side effect of the drug – thalidomide (marketed as a mild sleeping pill) – taken during early pregnancy. The 20-year-old student and aspiring model from the Udi Local Government Area of Enugu State, tells ALEXANDER OKERE how she motivates herself to carry on

You don’t have a fully-developed left limb. How did it happen?

I was born with it. My mum took a drug called Thalidomide when she was pregnant. The drug was taken to reduce morning sickness. She didn’t know it had a side effect. So, most of the women who took that drug had kids that were born with malformed limbs.

As a child, when did you realise your left hand was malformed?

I noticed it at the age of five. I thought I wouldn’t have friends. But I quickly adapted to the knowledge that I couldn’t do well with my left hand what my right hand could do.

How did that affect your interaction with other children?

I felt different from other kids. As a kid, I had to beg people to talk to me. I was actually a loner. I was rejected by my classmates (in primary school) and other children I met in my neighbourhood. Those experiences made me feel very bad as a child. But now, they don’t.

As a teenager, were there also challenges with getting people to accept you as you were?

I found it difficult talking to people other than those close to me. I couldn’t socialise and was always indoors.

Did your condition affect your relationship with boys?

I don’t think it did. But it’s personal. I have few friends; I can count them.

What forms of discrimination have you faced as a result of your condition?

As a child, I was mocked. My peers in primary school stared a lot at my hand. At nursery school, I had to part with my lunch or hair ribbon before my classmates would agree to talk to me. When I taught we had become friends, at a time, I was pushed into a pit, with stones thrown at me and sand poured on my head.

In secondary school, I was bullied and maltreated. My dad had to change the nursery and primary schools I attended when the maltreatment was too much and I complained to him. I attended Chiazor Nursery and Primary School; Maranatha Nursery and Primary School; Kingdom Heritage Nursery and Primary School; and Marvellous Grace International School, all in Enugu. I also attended Queen’s Secondary School, Enugu; Landmark University, Kwara State, before I transferred to Godfrey Okoye University, Enugu.

At Queen’s School, I was also maltreated. I remember when I resumed as new pupil, a girl kept touching the affected hand and I hate it when people do that. I told her to stop but she refused.

Having faced those challenges as a child, how do you manage similar ones as an adult?

I learnt to build my self-esteem. That was the first thing I did. I have realised that when people mock me and it gets to me, it is because I don’t have a high self-esteem. I really don’t care about what people think about me now. That’s how I overcome discrimination. Obviously, I look beyond rejection and discrimination to achieve what I set out to achieve.

What was your aspiration as a teenager?

As a teenager, I wanted to be a banker but my mum said I liked money too much so I couldn’t be a banker. She wanted me to be a lawyer. However, I later realised that I didn’t like mathematics, so I had to drop the idea of going into banking. She later advised me to study International Relations. I was studying Political Science at Landmark (University) before I left for Godfrey Okoye University, where I am studying History, International Studies and Diplomacy. But I would like to be an air hostess. I would like to have a home for persons living with disablities. I would also like to have my own clothing line.

How do you cope with activities you ordinarily have to do with two hands?

We all have challenges but all that matters is how we face them. My mum taught me and I taught myself how to cope with them. So, I do 99 per cent of what I do myself.

What are some of the basic things you do by yourself?

I wash my clothes. I wash plates and do anything a normal person does.

How have your siblings been supportive?

They have been very supportive. They are very real with me. I used to think my brother hated me but I later realised that what he did was to help me learn how to cope.

When did your interest in modelling start?

As a child, I always dreamt about seeing myself on the cover of magazines. But it got to a point in primary school when someone told me that if my arms were complete, I would be very beautiful and could become a model. That really hurt me but I had to move on. But when I got to secondary school, people started complimenting me on my skin and looks. So, I started considering it (modelling). At university, I got calls for photo shoots. So basically, I went into modelling because of my skin and the structure of my body. I have modelled for clothing lines and makeup studios.

Are you involved in any form of advocacy of other people with a similar condition?

I do that when they come on my (social media) page. Some people there ask me how I cope mentally.

What are your thoughts about the welfare of persons living with disabilities in Nigeria?

In Nigeria, people look down on persons living with disabilities. I think such individuals (PLWDs) should not be referred to as disabled because a person with all their body parts complete could be disabled mentally. Some people who are physically challenged are more reasonable than some who claim to be complete.

When someone wants to start a conversation with you, what do you want that person to know?

First, I hate meeting people and if I do, I may not know how to keep up with you. So, if someone wants to talk to me, I hate attracting sympathy. I hate it when people say ‘sorry’ to me. I want them to see how strong I am. I want them to see a bold person in me. I want them to feel encouraged when they see me – a bold, strong and young girl.

What motivates you?

I think about me and people – I motivate myself.

Were there times you thought about the condition and felt sad?

Yes. Sometimes, I think about myself and ask, “Why me?” Sometimes, I ask myself whether I would ever get married or whether my kids would ever be proud of me.  But those are stupid thoughts. I once contemplated suicide because I broke down in frustration. I just wanted to know what it felt like having two hands, even if it was for one second. I really felt depressed that I looked different from every other person. But I thank God that I thought about what would happen if I killed myself. So, I moved on.

What lessons has being born with phocomelia taught you?

It has taught me to be strong, bold and confident and never to give up.
