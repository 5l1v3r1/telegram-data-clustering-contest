Aston Martini set to be shaken not stirred if it moves into 007 cocktail range

23 Nov 2019, 21:08 by Steve Bird

For decades, the luxurious Aston Martin sports car has been as much a part of James Bond’s slick image as the spy’s favourite vodka martini, shaken not stirred.

But now the British car manufacturer has obtained a trademark for its name and logo to be used for alcoholic drinks.

The company has registered its winged Aston Martin badge for “alcoholic beverages”, including whisky, distilled spirits, liqueurs, wine and, perhaps inevitably, cocktails. 

However, while the official registration for the trademark embraces high class beverages likely to be drunk in upmarket casinos, it pointedly states that the logo should not be used for beer.

It is the second time the British sports car manufacturer has traded in on its connections to Ian Fleming’s famous 007 character. 

A few years ago it put its name to a silver martini set, complete with two glasses (lined with 24k gold), two olive picks and a cocktail shaker, all with intricate cross hatching patterns reminiscent of the carbon fibre used on the most contemporary cars. Only 100 were produced, apparently justifying its price tag of nearly £8,000.

Another company produced a sleek champagne case to fit snugly in the Aston Martin Milano’s trunk leaving little room for any luggage. 

However, the decision to protect the manufacturer’s name to be potentially used for alcoholic drinks has been met with caution by road safety campaigners.

Joshua Harris, director of campaigns at Brake, the road safety charity, said: “Drink driving is a serious issue on our roads, with the latest figures indicating that it is still the cause of around 250 deaths a year. 

“Driving brands must think and act responsibly and ensure that nothing they do encourages unsafe behaviour behind the wheel. 

“The current drink-driving limit gives a false impression that it is safe to drink and drive – this is a dangerous message and one that couldn’t be further from the truth.”  

An Aston Martin spokesman refused to say whether the company actually planned to produce or licence a drink or whether it was simply ensuring others could not cash in on its brand. 

“We are very protective of the Aston Martin brand, and we are continually filing trademarks for the Aston Martin brand in various sectors to do this,” he said. 

The Bond character first appeared with a DB5 Aston Martin in the 1964 film Goldfinger.
