Labour will let football fans vote on who manager is - and block unpopular choices

EXCLUSIVE: Under Labour proposals, at least two club director spots will be saved for accredited supporters' trusts and the board members will be chosen by ballot

23 Nov 2019, 21:18 by Chris McLaughlin

Labour has vowed to give footie fans a say on who scores the manager’s job at their beloved clubs.

The move is part of a raft of measures intended to ensure ­supporters’ views are represented in all key decisions on how our major sides are run.

And it could help clubs avoid unpopular decisions like the recent appointment of former Chelsea and Manchester United manager Jose Mourinho at Tottenham Hotspur – which ­powerless fans would have loved to be able to red-card.

Under Labour proposals, at least two club director spots will be reserved for accredited ­supporters’ trusts and the board members will be chosen by ballots of signed-up fans.

They will get full voting rights on equal status with other commercially appointed members.

Shadow Sports Minister Rosena Allin-Khan said: “Labour will put fans at the heart of football by giving them a far greater say over the way clubs are run.

“We will give them a voice over who the manager is.”

Under the bid to overhaul ­footie club governance and ownership, supporters’ trusts would also be allowed to buy block shares when clubs change hands – bringing the ­possibility of securing more board places.

Other aspects of the Labour package include a Fan’s Fare scheme under which supporters would get cash for travel paid back if matches are changed for the ­benefit of broadcasters.

And new regulations would cover safe standing areas with decisions made jointly by fans, club management and safety authorities.

Proposals also include a guarantee that the Premier League will be legally ­required to invest five per cent of income from TV rights in the grassroots game to help new generations of players and coaches, and to improve facilities and pitches.

And reviled zero-hour contracts will be banned, preventing the exploitation of ground workers whose loyalty is used to undermine wages.

Meanwhile, women’s football and the Paralympics will be added to the list of key sporting events required to be broadcast on free-to-air TV.

The plans come after the ­fan-based model of footie club ownership has already proved a success on the Continent.

In Germany, a majority of club shares must be owned by followers.

Ticket prices are low and the stadiums are full – with safe standing.

Barcelona is owned by 180,000 fans who pay £140 a year and have the right to elect the club president every four years.
