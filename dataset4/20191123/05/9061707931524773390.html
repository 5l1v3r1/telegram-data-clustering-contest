Quirky things that Burton people do not realise are odd to outsiders

Why does everybody keep calling me a duck?

23 Nov 2019, 05:30 by Ben Waldron

Burton is a fantastic, unique and, often, obscure place to live.

Since moving to the town, there have been many phrases, words and places that have utterly boggled my mind.

When I question my colleagues on their meanings - or my mispronunciations - I am met with pitiful looks that say "one day he'll learn".

Ranging from the age-old question of what you truly call a bun - is it a roll or is it a bap? - to why people are telling me they have "got a cob on", there are a whole manner of things that still confuse me even to this day.

Here are just a few instances where I have been left wondering whether I have suddenly been transported to a faraway land.

Quirky things about Burton

  1. What do you call a bun?

    Since moving to the town I must have heard four or five names for the breaded thing you use to house various meats and sandwiches.

    Whether it be a bun, bap, roll, cob or even a bread cake - there seems to be far too many options.

  2. Winshill, Stapenhill, Newhall ...

    I recall getting a very odd look from a bus driver when I got on one and asked for a return ticket to Newhall - pronouncing it "New Hall" in the process.

    That feeling of shame and horror at the expression on that bus driver's face will stay will me until the end of my days. It will certainly not be a mistake I will repeat in the future.

    Winshill and Stapenhill also fall into this same category, with both pronunciations being corrected to me on my first couple of weeks on the job.

    Note to self - do not pronounce the "h" under any circumstances.

  3. Splash on your sandwich?

    My mind was sent racing when ordering a bacon sandwich one day. I was asked whether I would like a "splash" with it.

    "Are you about to throw my sandwich in the Trent?", I thought. Is there a special sauce that nobody has warned we about? What could this 'splash' be?"

    Luckily, I soon learned that it was actually just a little dab of tinned tomatoes on the bap (or should that be cob? Or roll?).

  4. I am not a duck

    Many different words and phrases are used in the area that leave a puzzled, blank look on my face whenever they are uttered.

    None more so have taken as much getting used to as being called a "duck" at least 43 times a day.

    That includes being greeted on the bus to a warm "ey up me duck" or being told "cheers me duck" when paying in a shop.

    Last time I checked I did not have a feathered bottom and a bill, but I must admit I do love it - it's warm and welcoming.

  5. Council pop

    Staying on the topic of food and drink, when offering someone a drink once, I was met with the strange request for "council pop".

    Despite racking my brain for what brand of fizzy drink or beverage to pour my guest, I was soon put out of my misery.

    It turns out "council pop" is simply just water, with the renaming very similar to the "corporation pop" that I know it to be called.

  6. Pair of odd statues

    The Shovel in Burton is a bit of a strange one.

    You walk round the corner of Union Street, onto High Street, and come face to face with a strangely massive shovel with a print of a beer bottle cut out of it.

    After some research I found out that the 30ft stainless steel malt shovel sculpture had stood on the junction of the street since 2001.

    It was funded by a Â£45,000 National Lottery grant, with designers at the time saying it represented Burton's brewing past, paying homage to the malt shovels used by brewery workers.

    In the same vein, a large concrete jar of Marmite can be seen on the Washlands, near Burton Library, clearly celebrating the salty spread's link to the town.

  7. What do you say when you are grumpy

    From where I am from in Worcestershire, if you are unhappy, you are simply moody, grumpy or just a bit bothered.

    However, there seems to be all manner of phrases in these parts for letting others know you are not too pleased with them.

    "Gob a cob on", "got a monk on" and "got a bag on" have all been thrown around - many in my direction, unfortunately.
