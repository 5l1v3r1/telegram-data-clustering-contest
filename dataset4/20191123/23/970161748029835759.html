Freddie Mercury's friend says Queen frontman wanted to 'die on own his terms'

23 Nov 2019, 23:56

Freddie Mercury passed away 28-years ago to the day after a long battle with AIDS.

And to mark the anniversary of his death, the singer’s longtime friend Peter Freestone has spoken out about the Queen frontman’s final days.

Calling himself Freddie’s ‘chief cook and bottle washer, waiter, butler, secretary, cleaner and agony aunt,’ the star opened up to The Express about how the late icon wanted to die on his ‘own terms’.

He told the publication: ‘The last time I saw him he was relaxed and prepared…

‘We all knew it could not be too long, but Freddie’s doctor had said that he could be with us for quite a few days longer. I tend to feel that Freddie had decided he had had enough and it was time to go under his own terms.’

Peter added: ‘He didn’t get depressed. He wasn’t afraid. It was a fact he was going to die. In those days there was absolutely nothing could change that. So he got on with his music.

‘He did so much in those last four years.’

Peter isn’t the only one of Freddie’s friends to talk about the Radio Ga Ga hitmaker’s death, as Elton John recently spoke about the last time he saw the singer when chatting to David Walliams.

Speaking in front of a live audience at Eventim Apollo, in London, the 72-year-old said up until his death Freddie was only thinking of everyone else.

‘I didn’t go and see him often as Aids was terrifying and he was physically terrifying to look at,’ Elton said, according to Daily Star. ‘While he was dying he was still buying things at auction.

‘He wasn’t thinking about dying at all. He showed no fear to me about dying, no fear or sadness. When you went there you had an audience with Freddie.’

The Honky Cat hitmaker recalled receiving a gift from Freddie of a watercolour painting that continues to sit next to his bed.

‘On Christmas morning Tony King came round and gave me this pillowcase,’ Elton explained.

‘As you read in the book my drag name is Sharon and Freddie was Melina.

‘In this beautiful pillowcase was this watercolour painting. In the note that went with it said “Dear Sharon, I saw this at auction and I thought you would love it. I love you Malena”.’
