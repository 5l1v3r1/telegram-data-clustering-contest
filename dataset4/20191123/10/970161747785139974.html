Tories in major blunder as they announce manifesto launch with wrong date

23 Nov 2019, 10:58

Boris Johnson was left red-faced today after announcing the launch of the Conservative Party’s 2019 manifesto – but getting the date wrong.

The Tories took to Twitter this morning to inform followers that their long-awaited manifesto would finally be landing on November 24.

However, in the picture that accompanied their post, they wrote that the manifesto would be coming today, November 23.

Clearly not realising the mistake, Boris Johnson retweeted the post, reminding his followers to ‘Vote Conservative’.

However, both posts were hastily deleted minutes later.

The Tories likely hoped that no one would have noticed their blunder.

However, it was too late to hide it and people in the comment section soon clocked on.

Twitter user @Remain4U wrote: ‘Is it today or tomorrow?! You just deleted a tweet saying it was launching today?! #Weakandwobbly #GetBrexitGone’.

Another person with the Twitter handle @SectionY2018 found the situation amusing, writing: ‘Ha ha ha ha ha ha ha. Quick delete and get the date right’.

@milkywaykks also took a swipe at the Conservatives putting ‘How can we trust a government who can’t even get the date right?!’ while @DrDublin1 added: ‘We all saw you lie again about the date.’

Metro.co.uk has contacted the Conservative Party for comment.

The Tories are the last of the major parties to unveil their manifesto, with the Lib Dems doing so on Wednesday and Labour on Thursday.

The arrival date had been hotly debated and there had been speculation that the party would unveil individual policies at regular intervals throughout their campaign to maximise media coverage.

Although their full manifesto is still yet to come, the Tories have said they plan to ‘get Brexit done’ which will allow them to invest in public services.

Mr Johnson has stated that the Conservatives will recruit 6,000 extra GPs and deliver 50 million more appointments a year by 2024-25 as part of a £33.9 billion boost for the health budget.

The prime minister also wants to recruit 20,000 new police officers and increase stop-and-search powers in a bid to tackle rising knife crime.

The manifesto is likely to include promises to cut taxes for ‘working people’ by raising the threshold for national insurance contributions to £12,000 and to deliver ‘at least’ one million homes in the next five years.
