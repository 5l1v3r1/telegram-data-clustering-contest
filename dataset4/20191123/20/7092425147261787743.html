Exclusive: Róisín Upton on qualifying for the Olympics with a broken wrist

23 Nov 2019, 20:04 by James Cox

Speaking to Róisín Upton, it’s clear that the joy of qualification for the 2020 Olympics in Tokyo hasn’t faded.

Upton stepped up to score the crucial penalty in sudden death in the deciding play-off against Canada at Donnybrook’s Energia Park, with Ayeisha McFerran’s follow-up sealing the victory.

Upton told BuzzSport: “It’s just started to sink in a small bit now. The atmosphere was absolutely phenomenal, the place was booming with over 6,000 fans.

“It was unlike anything we’ve experienced in Ireland before.”

Upton’s heroics were even more remarkable as she subsequently discovered she’d fractured a bone in her wrist during the game!

“It was a physical game and I took a few knocks. I remember I’d just played a long pass and I felt something wasn’t right in my wrist,” she said.

“There was this unbelievable pain and even opening my bag after it was hurting. I got a scan the next day and found out I’d fractured a bone in my wrist.”

A lot of players would feel the pressure of such an important penalty shoot-out, but Upton prefers to be in the thick of it.

“I’d always prefer to be taking one actually. I don’t like looking in [on a shoot-out] with too much time to think.”

She was full of praise for goalkeeper McFerran, who made two crucial saves.

“The girls were phenomenal and I think some people had us written off, especially when we were 3-1 down.

“Canada had three or four chances to kill us off but thankfully Ayeisha came up with some wonderful saves and we took our chance.”

The Irish Women’s Hockey Team won the hearts of the nation with their stunning run to the final of the 2018 World Cup.

And while they all made lifelong memories that summer, Olympic qualification has also been the main goal, according to Upton.

“That was phenomenal but I think this was a little bit better [qualifying for the Olympics]. To do it on home soil in front of so many friends and family was even sweeter,” she said.

“We’ll always have the World Cup but the Olympics is the big one. So many people travelled across to London but we had a huge home crowd here this time.”

Graham Shaw left to take the job as head coach of New Zealand but the transition was seamless under new head coach Sean Dancer.

Upton revealed it was a case of keeping most things the same with a few tactical tweaks.

“There was a period of 5 or 6 weeks that we had an interim coach so he was probably assessing our game then,” she said.

“He said there were a lot of things he wanted to keep the same, like our defensive qualities, and a few things to work on too like our scoring.”

Upton spent three years at the University of Connecticut, captaining the Hockey team to the NCCA title in 2014.

“It was really a once-in-a-lifetime experience. I had only planned to go over for a year but ended up staying for three.”

Now back at home, Upton is happy in Ireland but she hasn’t ruled out playing in one of the top European leagues one day.

“It’s a chance to immerse yourself in a new culture and do some travelling, from talking to the others [about their experiences playing in Europe], it’s definitely something I’d like to try.”

The hardest thing for Upton and her international teammates is training like professionals, putting in the hours on top of work and study.

Upton recently completed a Masters in Primary Education at Limerick’s Mary Immaculate College, which wasn’t easy on top of training and travel to Dublin.

“It was really tough, you’re doing 25-30 a week including placement and then there’s the gym and training on top of that,” she explained.

A place in the Olympics has always been the goal though and that’s what makes it all worthwhile for Upton.

So what’s the limit for this team? There is none and they genuinely believe they can provide a big upset like they did at the World Cup.

“The World Cup definitely put us on the map. There’s only 12 teams, compared to the 16 in the World Cup, so the competition is elite.

“Qualification has always been the goal but Sean was clear in his message to us that it’s just a stepping stone. The new goal will be trying to win a medal in Tokyo."
