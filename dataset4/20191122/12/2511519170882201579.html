Pakistan feminism panel attracts backlash after no women included

The all-male line up was to "share views" on feminism 

22 Nov 2019, 12:15 by Moya Lothian-McLean

The Arts Council of Pakistan has been forced to alter a panel event on feminism after it was revealed the line-up included no women. 

Outcry on social media forced the organisation to add two women guests to the panel, which is planned to take place on 22 November in Karachi. 

The event has also been renamed from “Feminism: The Other Perspective” to “Understanding Feminism”. 

"This is such a horror," wrote one Twitter user. "Before women feminists find platforms and political voice, men helping themselves to more. Opposite of feminism."

"These men wanna give the ‘other perspective’ on feminism,"added writer Aiman Rizvi.

"Maybe the intention here was to use their privilege for the cause - but this isn’t how allyship works. This is, however, what taking up space looks like. It is also what men exploiting feminism to build capital looks like."

However, the inclusion of feminist Mehtab Akbar Rashdi and journalist Quatrina Hosain still means that women make up less than half of the panel.

Originally, five men were lined up to speak at the event, apparently to “share views on feminism,” explained organisers. 

"Our purpose was to get men having decision-making powers in major media houses and those with a following to talk about their understanding of feminism,” discussion host Uzma al-Karim told BBC Urdu.

"We wanted to register their perception because they were in a position to influence public opinion. And that's why we called it 'the other perspective'."

However, human rights activist Jibran Nasir, a speaker on the panel, said he’d been misinformed regarding the purpose of the event. 

"I was informed the panel is about men talking to other men about rethinking masculinity and why [we] as men we need feminism”, Nasir tweeted, along with a longer clarification. 

“It wasn't to explain feminism or talk about women’s issues as men.” 

Pakistan is one of the lowest ranked countries in the world for gender equality. 

In 2018 it ranked 148 out of 149 countries in the World Economic Forum’s gender parity report. 

However, strides towards equality are slowly being made, with more women running for political office than ever before in the past two years, and female education rates increasing.
