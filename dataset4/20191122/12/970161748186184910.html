Man on £80,000 salary refuses to believe he's in the top 5% of earners

22 Nov 2019, 12:57

A Question Time audience member has refused to believe he is in the top 5% of earners despite raking in £80,000 a year.

Rob Barber went on the BBC flagship show and complained about Labour’s election policy of planning a 45p rate on earnings over that figure.

Jeremy Corbyn’s party are adamant that their proposed tax hike would only affect the top 5% of earners.

The average UK salary is £29,000 and figures from the Office for National Statistics show that anyone earning more than £75,300 is in the top 5%.

His rant has now gone viral with him refusing to believe Labour’s statistics, calling them ‘liars’.

He then goes on to suggest that his salary is not even in ‘the top 50% of earners.’

Mr Barber, who is believed to be an IT consultant, said while jabbing his finger: ‘I’d like to call out Labour as liars.

‘I am one of those people he will tax more and I am nowhere near in the top five so I calling you a liar.

‘That 5% is a lie, I am nowhere near that and you are going to tax me as an employee.

‘You are not going after the billionaires, you are going after the employees because it’s easy money and I have no choice because it’s PAYE.’

Mr Barber was directing his anger towards Labour’s Richard Burgon who attempted to correct the mistake during the show in Bolton, Lancs.

He said: ‘You are mistaken – we are not going to raise income tax apart from those in the top 5%.

‘And that’s why we are not going to increase your income tax.’

Question Time chair Fiona Bruce attempted to clarify how much the man earnt and why he thought he did not fall within the top level of earners.

He said he did earn above the £80,000 threshold just like ‘every doctor, every accountant and every solicitor.’

Official figures suggest all those professions fall below £80,000 – a doctor on £76,000, an accountant on £62,000 and a solicitor around £43,000.

Mr Burgon pointed out then when he was a solicitor he was paid around £40,000.

Under Labour’s plan, Mr Barber would have to pay roughly an extra £50 for every £1,000 he earns over £80,000.
