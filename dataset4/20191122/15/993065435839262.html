Sentence imposed on man caught storing guns belonging to dissident republicans `unduly lenient'

22 Nov 2019, 15:30

AN 18-month sentence imposed on a man caught storing guns and ammunition belonging to dissident republicans was unduly lenient, the Court of Appeal has ruled.

But with Edward Corr having already served a term behind bars for weapons he claims he was pressured into keeping, judges decided not to order his return to jail.

Lord Justice Stephens said it would unfair to expose him to the risk of a significantly heavier prison sentence on a new basis never advanced at trial.

The verdict came in a challenge mounted by the Director of Public Prosecutions (DPP).

Corr (40) was arrested after police searched a shed at his Foxes Glen home in Dunmurry in October 2016.

Bags found contained two blank calibre pistols, magazines, ammunition and a Yugoslavian Skorpion M48 submachine gun.

Corr claimed he had been threatened at gunpoint to store the weapons six weeks earlier by a man who said he was on official Oglaigh na hEireann business.

During police interviews he said he had not slept for nearly six weeks and feared being shot.

In May 2018 he admitted possessing firearms and ammunition with intent to endanger life and possession of a prohibited weapon.

A judge at Belfast Crown Court imposed an 18-month sentence for the offences - half to be served in prison.

The DPP appealed the term.

It was contended that the trial judge was wrong in finding exceptional circumstances to justify departing from the five-year minimum for the offences.

Ruling on the appeal, Lord Justice Stephens agreed with the original assessment of exceptional circumstances, based on the pressure placed on Corr as a vulnerable individual.

However, he identified a failure to place sufficient emphasis on the specific criminal intent linked to a haul including a "lethal, deadly" submachine gun.

"These weapons and ammunition were possessed by the respondent with the intent to enable members of a dissident terrorist organisation to endanger life," he pointed out.

A sentence of three years and six months would have been appropriate, the court concluded.

But appeal judges also held that the prosecution was raising an entirely new case.
