How much more a Labour Government would tax you and what your pay might fall by

Labour's manifesto has set out big plans to change the way the UK works - taking more from the rich and using it to support essential services - but who are they classing as rich? We explain all

22 Nov 2019, 14:31 by James Andrews, http://www.facebook.com/jameswritesmoney/

The Labour Party has just unveiled one of the most radical plans to change Britain by a major party for decades.

The goal is to improve creaking services and support the worst off and make big businesses and the rich pay for it.

But who are they classing as rich?

On the surface it's pretty clear - no change to income tax or national insurance for 95% of the country - with just the best-earning 5% paying more.

In their own words: “We’ll ask those who earn more than £80,000 a year to pay a little more income tax, while freezing National Insurance and income tax rates for everyone else.”

Labour plans to tax people 45% tax on earnings over £80,000 (up from 40%) while earnings above £125,000 a year will be hit with a 50p tax band.

You can see exactly how this will your income tax will - or won't - change with this calculator .

But things aren't quite that simple - with some seeing pay rises and others seeing their take-home pay falling as a result of policies in other parts of the manifesto.

The first direct pay impact is pretty good news for people working in the public sector.

For years the Conservative Party has frozen wages for public sector workers - making them effectively poorer as bills rise and their earnings don't.

Labour has vowed to reverse this - saying public sector workers will see their wages rise 5% , or £1,643 a year on average.

People on the minimum wage will also see earnings rise - especially if they're under 25.

That's because Labour has pledged to introduce a "Real Living Wage" of at least £10 an hour for all workers aged 16 and over.

New mums and dads will also get a boost, with maternity and paternity pay changes adding as much as £2,226 to new parent's incomes (£2,000 for mums, £226 for dads).

But the marriage allowance - offering couples where one partner doesn't work a £250-a-year tax break - will go.

People on low-incomes and self-employed workers outside the public sector are also due a 3% pay rise thanks to the manifesto - but it's one that would see the amount of cash they receive each month drop.

That's thanks to the plan to extend pension entitlements to those earning less than £10,000 a year per job or working for themselves.

Currently 14million people are excluded from the scheme that sees bosses forced to boost workers' pay by at least 3% if that money's saved into a pension for them.

The downside is that to get the boost you need to save 5% of your pay yourself as well - seeing the amount you take home each month drop.

But overall, it's something that will see people better off.

Aegon pensions director Steven Cameron said: “Labour’s commitment to look to widen access for the low paid and self-employed to auto enrolment schemes is welcome and likely to be mirrored by all parties.”

There are other changes that could see the self-employed lose out though - as a fallout from changes designed to tax the rich more.

Seeing bosses and owners pull money our of companies in the form of dividends, rather than as wages, Labour said it would tax these.

The danger is this will spill over to affect more than just billionaires trying to beat tax by sending company profits to wives living in Monaco.

Rachael Griffin, tax expert at Quilter, said: "There are around five million self-employed workers in the UK and a number of these will pay themselves through dividends taken from the companies they run to provide their services, and as such this will have a significant impact on the way they run their businesses."
