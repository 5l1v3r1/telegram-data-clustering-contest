Scott Fardy refuses to put a number on when he'll call time on world-traversing career

Fardy was 35 last July but remains a key figure for Leinster in their bid to win the Champions Cup for a fifth time

22 Nov 2019, 06:00 by Michael Scully

Scott Fardy refuses to put a number on when he'll call time on his world-traversing career.

Fardy was 35 last July but remains a key figure for Leinster in their bid to win the Champions Cup for a fifth time, a campaign that continues in Lyon on Saturday.

But he was happy to only agree a one-year contract encompassing this season.

“I'm pretty old," the versatile forward explained with a smile.

“It was mutual, my decision as well. I just take it as it comes. 

"I'm really enjoying it here and having a lot of fun, and I'm learning a lot every day. 

"I don't know whose decision it was to make it one in the end, I think I just said, 'let's just do one and take it as it comes'. 

"When you come to my age you don't want to let anyone down. I just want to keep playing and see how I'm progressing in the early parts of the season and into the New Year. 

"I didn't want to be in a situation where you're struggling physically and you still have another year to go. 

"It wasn't about the finance of the thing, it was more about where my head would be at some stage in the future."

Fardy still feels fresh for a guy in his mid-Thirties who has played Test rugby with Australia.

He points to the fact that he spent three years in club rugby in Japan before his career really took off back home with the Brumbies.

"I feel well," he said.

"There's no number...I think if you put a number in your head, that's where you'll go to.  "I'm not desiring to play forever, I know there's an end date on when you play. 

"Every guy is different. I had a period at the start of my career when I was in Japan when I wasn't getting bashed around. You look at a guy making Test debuts at 21 or 22, I didn't do that. 

"I didn't debut in Test rugby until I was 29. In terms of life in my legs, I'm feeling pretty good compared to what other guys might be going through.

"But you look at Thorny (Brad Thorn) who played from when he was 19 (until 38). Every guy is different. I feel pretty good at the moment."

Despite plying his trade on three continents, Fardy dismisses the suggestion that he's a wandering spirit.

"I'm actually a bit of a crab - a Cancer - so I actually like to stay at home a lot, that's what my wife says," he remarked.

"I like home life, my family is very comfortable here in Dublin, we enjoy the set-up here at the moment. 

"Life has changed a lot coming here. We really enjoy it. I have no real interest in going around the world too much."

His wife, Penelope, is a singer and he's not sure if the family will remain in Ireland in the long term.

"We're not sure, it's always a tough thing," he said.

"Australia is always there for us, in terms of going back and living there. 

"We enjoy here, we're really comfortable here. My son has only been to Australia for a couple of weeks of his life so he doesn't know anything else. 

"I feel part of the community, playing here, they're all good guys here."  

Leo Cullen considering his options

Leinster boss Leo Cullen will look at the possibility of bringing in a big foreign name or two in the months to come.

Scott Fardy and Joe Tomane will be the only foreign players on the Blues books this time next year, as Jamison Gibson-Park is now Irish qualified - and James Lowe will be in the autumn of 2020.

"It's still important to get some of that outside influence," said Cullen. 

"Predominantly we want to bringing mostly our own guys. That's the model but every now and again we look at players and think he can add something to this group. 

"It's always worth having the conversation at least".
