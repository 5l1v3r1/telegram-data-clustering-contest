Plymouth Marjon University declares a climate emergency

"None of us, regardless of age, should be shrugging our shoulders when future generations ask us what we did when the climate emergency was declared"

22 Nov 2019, 11:37 by Molly Dowrick

Students, staff and guests of Plymouth Marjon University have come together to discuss ways they can respond to the climate crisis, after Plymouth City Council declared a climate emergency earlier this year.

The university held a Climate Emergency Forum earlier this month, which saw students, staff and guests unite to 'talk about how the University will respond to the climate emergency'.

The forum was set up after Plymouth City Council declared a climate emergency and 'acts off the back of an international aim to keep global temperature rises below 1.5Â°C'.

The Climate Emergency Forum hosted several speakers, including Cal Major, founder of Paddle Against Plastic; Paul Elliott, Low Carbon Delivery Officer with Plymouth City Council; SCOFF, an aquaphonics farm in Modbury; Architype, who spoke about Campus Development Plans; and Plymotion, a Plymouth City Council initiative to encourage and enable people to choose green transport.

Speaking at the forum, ocean advocate and world record stand-up paddle boarder ('SUP') Cal Major, who is founder of Paddle Against Plastic said: "There isn't a one-size-fits-all answer for these crises that we face."

Cal, who recently received an honorary degree from Marjon in recognition of her environmental efforts, founded Paddle Against Plastic in 2016 to bring a positive message to the 'plastic pollution crisis,' and to inspire and empower positive change through stand-up paddle boarding adventures.

Cal said: "We all need to look at the whole picture and see what the best approach is, and see what we can do individually and collectively to make a big difference.

"We all need to change our attitudes around how we live."

The forum was planned and hosted by graduate sustainability officer Matthew Tosdevin, who also planted a magnolia tree to commemorate Marjon's declaration of a climate emergency.

Matthew said: "The climate emergency forum was a great success. Our group workshops have given the Marjon a community a say in how we tackle the climate emergency, focusing on topics such as green transport, waste, and campus energy.

"We finished the event by planting a tree as a symbol of hope; by the time it reaches maturity, we should be able to reflect back on our success as a community in this moment in making a commitment for a sustainable future.

"We're now using the ideas from the event to influence our policies and actions moving forward to reach a zero-carbon target by 2030."

Plymouth Marjon University's Vice Chancellor, Professor Rob Warner, spoke about how the 'university can work collectively to become more sustainable'.

Professor Warner said: "None of us, regardless of age, should be shrugging our shoulders when future generations ask us what we did when the climate emergency was declared.

"My hope is that we can all work together at Marjon, to be the eco-community of the future, today."
