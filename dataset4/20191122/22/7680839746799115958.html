Ford responds to Tesla's new Cybertruck

22 Nov 2019, 22:11 by Graham Rapier
  • Tesla CEO Elon Musk unveiled the Cybertruck, the company’s first passenger truck, on Thursday night.
  • During the presentation, Musk, in his usual fashion, took shots at traditional automakers.
  • “We’ve always focused on serving our truck customers regardless of what others say or do,” a Ford representative told Business Insider.

Tesla CEO Elon Musk didn’t hold back on his usual criticism of traditional automakers as he unveiled Tesla’s long-awaited Cybertruck on Thursday. But Ford doesn’t seem too scared.

“Trucks have been the same for a very long time – like, 100 years,” Musk told the crowd at Tesla’s design center in Hawthorne, California. “We wanted to try something different.”

After showing that the Tesla truck could withstand more damage from a sledgehammer than a regular truck’s door could, Musk said, “You want a truck that’s really tough, not fake tough.”

That might be the case, but for Americans, the truck they’ve wanted for decades has been the Ford F-150.

In response to Musk’s shade at it and other automakers, Ford sent the following statement:

“With America’s best-selling truck for 42 years, we’ve always focused on serving our truck customers regardless of what others say or do. We look forward to our all-new F-150 hybrid coming next year and all-electric F-150 in a few years.”

Musk has long expressed disdain for traditional automakers. And for the most part, he’s beat them at their own game, at least when it comes to electric vehicles. The Model 3 was the best-selling electric vehicle in the US in the first half of 2019, according to data from InsideEVs.

Analysts estimated that Tesla could likely produce up to 175,000 Cybertrucks in its first full year of production in 2020. That’s a far cry from the roughly 1 million Ford F-150s sold every year.

“In a nutshell, we believe this next generation Cybertruck model could help Tesla expand its market opportunity outside its core customer base over time,” Daniel Ives, an analyst at Wedbush, said in a note to clients, adding that, however, “gaining market share with stalwarts such as Ford and GM entrenched in this landscape will be a difficult task for Fremont.”
