Tribal health needs special focus, tailored interventions: WHO official

The average maternal mortality rate in India is 130 deaths per 100,000 births, while tribal communities average 230 deaths per 100,000 births. Other health indicators such as infant mortality, child malnutrition rates, incidence of malaria and TB are higher among tribals than the general population.

22 Nov 2019, 05:44 by Express News Service

Welcoming the efforts of the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation, which has partnered with Piramal Foundation to set up a tribal health collaborative, Dr Soumya Swaminathan, chief scientist at the World Health Organisation (WHO) said improving indicators of health among tribal communities will need special focus and tailored interventions.

The collaborative will work with the centre towards achieving Universal Health Coverage that will focus on improving the health and nutrition in high-burden and tribal districts in the country, an official statement issued here said.

The average maternal mortality rate in India is 130 deaths per 100,000 births, while tribal communities average 230 deaths per 100,000 births. Similarly, other health indicators such as infant mortality, child malnutrition rates, and incidence of malaria and tuberculosis are much higher among tribal communities than the general population.

The setting up of such a collaborative is towards achieving the Sustainable Development Goal (SDG) 3 by 2030 on ensuring healthy lives, said Ajay Piramal, founder of Piramal Foundation, in a statement.

The focus is on addressing the needs of marginalised sections of society, particularly tribal communities, covering a population of more than 150 million across several districts including Aspirational Districts in Central, Eastern, and North-Eastern India, he said. India’s tribal population has poor health indicators when compared to the general population.

The Piramal Foundation has been working with underserved tribal communities in areas including 25 aspirational districts. Joining hands with the Gates Foundation and working with the Centre will help facilitate the implementation of key public health interventions in partnership with government functionaries across all levels, the statement added.

Gynaecologist, commando trainer among those awarded for their work

Written by Audita Bhattacharya

Gynaecologist Indira Hinduja was presented with the Woman Researcher National Award for her pioneering work including a role in the birth of India’s first test tube baby in 1986, as part of the annual Kirtan Sanjeevani Pushpalata Ranade awards, on Thursday.

Hinduja, who pioneered the Gamete intrafallopian transfer technique, has contributed through her work and research in IVF technology. She has been awarded numerous honours, including the Padmashree in 2011.

The Kirtan Sanjeevani Pushpalata Ranade Woman Bravery National Award was given to the country’s first and only woman commando trainer from Mumbai, Dr Seema Rao. Rao, who secured a doctorate degree from Harvard Medical School, has in the past 20 years provided commando training free of cost to at least 20,000 jawaans from the Indian Army and police force. For her contribution, she has also been awarded the World Peace Award and US Presidential Award, as well as the Indian government’s highest civilian award. She has written 10 books so far and Forbes Magazine has taken cognizance of her work under its ‘Women Power’ segment.

The chief guest for the function was former vice-chancellor of Mumbai University and eminent pediatric surgeon Snehalata Deshmukh and the chairperson for the ceremony was former governor of Uttar Pradesh Ram Naik. Every award winner was given Rs 2 lakh and a citation.
