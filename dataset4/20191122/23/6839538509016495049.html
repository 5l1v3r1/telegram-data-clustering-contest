Raucous debut a vivid snapshot of eastern suburbs

22 Nov 2019, 23:30

A micro-budget can go a long way if you have Bondi Beach as your backdrop and an 18-year-old’s reckless bravado as your driving force.

STARRING: Hal Cumptson, Frederick Du Rietz, Mitzi Ruhlmann

RATING: MA15+

RUNNING TIME: 97 minutes

VERDICT: Homegrown coming-of-age comedy

THREE STARS

A micro-budget can go a long way if you have Bondi Beach as your backdrop and an 18-year-old’s reckless bravado as your driving force.

Writer-actor-producer Hal Cumpston wrote the screenplay for this raucous coming-of-age comedy on his return home from Schoolies Week. It took him just 10 days.

That raw energy — together with the immediacy of his recent experience — is translated onto the big screen thanks to the efforts of a fresh, talented young cast and the support of seasoned elders such as composer Jonathan Zwartz.

Cumpston’s father, Jeremy, one of the co-founders of two independent Sydney theatre institutions — Tamarama Rock Surfers and The Old Fitzroy Theatre — directs.

Feeding the anarchic spirit of this naturalistic, Australian answer to Superbad, which charts 24 hours in the life of a bunch of Year 12 students, is a cracker soundtrack featuring more than half a dozen contemporary Australian bands, including Violent Soho, Dune Rats, Hockey Dad and Drunk Mums.

Lending some texture to the hard-partying narrative is an insider’s view of the eastern suburbs, which despite its privileged reputation, is also home to a dwindling population of battlers.

While the film’s protagonists — a motley bunch of slackers and jocks — get to party in harbourside mansions, most of them live in fairly modest circumstances.

The tensions between these two worlds plays out in a long-running feud between the public students and their private school counterparts.

Perhaps because he knows the material so well, Cumptson Jr is a very relaxed screen presence as Hal, a smart, mouthy, Year 12 student who harbours a secret dream of attending acting school (he auditions with a moving monologue from the play Shadow Boxing, written for Australian actor David Field).

Frederick Du Rietz is strong, too, as Hal’s loyal offsider, Matt. Mitzi Ruhlmann also stands out as Lucy, the good sport relegated to the friend zone by Hal’s emotional cowardice.

It might be a little rough around the edges, but Cumpston’s attention-grabbing debut offers a vivid snapshot of a very particular time and place. Not so much Dazed and Confused as grilled and sunburnt.
