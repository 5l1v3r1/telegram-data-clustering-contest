A life-saving education

22 Nov 2019, 10:15 by Nicholas Johansen

The opioid crisis in B.C. affects people from all walks of life in the community, and next week, a UBC Okanagan student will be training people how to stop an overdose.

Ariel Smith, a medical student at UBCO, is hosting a free naloxone training workshop at the Kelowna university on Monday, to help educate people about how naloxone works.

The medication, when injected, can reverse the effects of an opioid overdose.

“Through conversations with family and friends, I recognized a huge knowledge gap still existed in our community,” said Smith. “Especially, considering the majority of opioid overdose deaths in B.C. happen to people living inside a private residence.”

Smith recently implemented naloxone training as part of the Faculty of Medicine's flexible and enhanced learning course.

Prior to entering the medical program, Smith volunteered with HOPE Outreach, a local organization that supports exploited women in downtown Kelowna and Vernon. Through her work with HOPE, she trained many of the Okanagan's most vulnerable people how to use naloxone.

She now offers free training sessions to businesses and volunteer organizations, in partnership with the outreach.

“There is still a large stigma associated with opioids and naloxone training,” Smith said. “In our workshops, we create a safe learning environment for people to ask questions, learn to recognize the signs of an overdose and practise with real equipment.”

The UBC Okanagan event will be held at the Reichwald Health Sciences Centre in Room RHS260 at 6 p.m.
