Great Britain overcome Andy Murray absence to reach Davis Cup semi-finals as Dan Evans finally enjoys win

22 Nov 2019, 20:27 by Simon Briggs

Great Britain face the ultimate test in Saturday's Davis Cup semi-final: Rafael Nadal and his Spanish amigos, lining up in front of a passionate home crowd at the Caja Magica. But whatever happens, this has already been a triumphant campaign.

What a performance Leon Smith’s produced on Friday at the Caja Magica. To leave Andy Murray out on fitness grounds, and still beat Germany by a comprehensive 2-0 margin, underlines the fact that tennis in Britain is no longer a Murray-centric monoculture.

It was Dan Evans who sent Britain through, turning around what had previously been a demoralising week for him personally. He was up against a far bigger hitter in Jan-Lennard Struff – a man who stands seven inches taller and possesses a blunderbuss of a serve.

But Evans came up with some wonderful pinpoint tennis in the decisive tie-break, claiming a 7-6, 3-6, 7-6 victory that he will surely rank among the finest moments of his career. He whirled his arms at the end in delirious celebration, looking as chuffed as a small boy on his first visit to the fairground as he charged towards Smith for a celebratory hug.

"It's great,” said Evans afterwards. “I lost some tough matches just lately [against Robin Haase on Wednesday and Alexander Bublik on Thursday, after leading by a set each time]. I felt like I had let the team down the last two days. So to come back and get it like that, it's amazing.”

The rewards, in a monetary sense, will be substantial: £1.1m to be shared between the team, and £585,000 for the Lawn Tennis Association. But the honour of reaching a Davis Cup semi-final is a far bigger prize.

Great Britain have thus outperformed their world ranking of No. 5, and all without their most famous player. Murray’s absence is not thought to be related to any serious injury, just a slow return to match-readiness after a month away from competitive tennis.

It seems unlikely that this setback will affect Murray’s build-up to next season in Australia. But he has clearly miscalculated how long it would take him to spring back into optimal shape after the birth of his son Teddy – and a diet which, by his own account, featured too many chocolate biscuits and pieces of cake. “Where Kyle and Evo are at is ahead of where Andy's just now,” said Smith after the tie. “I wouldn’t say he’s in his best condition.”

In the absence of Murray, Edmund played the No 2 spot for Great Britain, and made the perfect start as he overpowered Philipp Kohlschreiber by a 6-3, 7-5 scoreline. This was Edmund’s second dominant performance in as many days, showing that he has moved on mentally from the eight straight losses that he suffered between August and October.
