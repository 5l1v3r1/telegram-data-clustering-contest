Serious childhood illness more common than cancer, diabetes and AIDS combined

Health experts and teachers have called the situation a crisis

24 Nov 2019, 11:59 by Neil Shaw

One-in-seven children develop a mental illness - more than the number with cancer, diabetes and AIDS combined, according to new research.

They range from anxiety and ADHD (attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder) to schizophrenia and depression.

And neuro-developmental conditions could be getting missed in girls in particular because of "delayed detection." They tend to be diagnosed much later.

The findings published in JAMA Psychiatry is based on 1.3 million Danish youngsters who were tracked from birth to 18 years of age.

Corresponding author Professor Soren Dalsgaard, a child psychologist at Aarhus University in Denmark, said: "Worldwide, this nationwide study is the first, to our knowledge, of the incidence of the full spectrum of diagnosed mental disorders in childhood and adolescence."

Earlier this year a study of NHS trusts found child referrals to mental health clinics in the UK rose by nearly 50 per cent over three years.

The probe revealed 31,531 under 11-year-olds were sent by their school for treatment in 2018 - an increase of 10,406 from the 21,125 in 2015.

Mental health experts and teachers called the situation a 'crisis' - with school staff even seeing youngsters 'headbutt walls on a daily basis'.

Online bullying has been blamed for the rise in 'extreme mental health distress', which has caused some students to even attempt suicide on school grounds.

Prof Dalsgaard's team found a total of 99,926 participants (15%) were diagnosed with a mental disorder before 18 years of age.

They included 41,350 girls (14.6%) and 58,576 boys (15.5%). Anxiety was the most common diagnosis in girls (7.85%) and ADHD in boys.

Girls had a higher risk than boys of schizophrenia, obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) and mood disorders.

But boys were much more prone to mental illness at younger ages with incidence of ADHD peaking at eight compared to 17 for girls.

The trend extended to intellectual disability and other developmental disorders - each tending to start at the age of five compared to 14 and 16, respectively, for girls.

The overall risk of being diagnosed with a mental disorder before 6 years of age was 2.13% - and was almost twice as high in boys (2.78%) than girls (1.45%).

Eating disorders were much higher in girls (1.8%) than boys (0.28%).

Prof Dalsgaard said: "These findings suggest precise estimates of rates and risks of all mental disorders during childhood and adolescence are essential for future planning of services and care and for research."

Previous estimates in the UK has suggested they effect about one in ten youngsters. The findings published in JAMA Psychiatry suggest the figure is much greater.

Prof Dalsgaard said: "Since 1990, studies on the global burden of disease have continuously ranked mental disorders as some of the most impairing conditions worldwide."

The Global Burden of Disease studies recently reported that in children and adolescents, mental disorders account for the most years lived with disability.

The World Health Organisation has emphasised the strong need for age and sex-specific data on child mental health.

Prof Dalsgaard said: "Knowledge about the epidemiology of mental disorders in children and adolescents is essential for research and planning of health services.

"Surveys can provide prevalence rates, whereas population-based registers are instrumental to obtain precise estimates of incidence rates and risks."

In the first study of its kind his team analysed all individuals born in Denmark between January 1, 1995, and December 31, 2016 - including those who died during the study, possibly as a result of their mental illness.

Prof Dalsgaard said: "This nationwide population-based cohort study provides a first comprehensive assessment of the incidence and risks of mental disorders in childhood and adolescence.

"By 18 years of age, 15.01% of children and adolescents in this study were diagnosed with a mental disorder.

"The incidence of several neuro-developmental disorders peaked in late adolescence in girls, suggesting possible delayed detection."

He added: "We believe the novelty of this report is the comprehensive estimates of risks of diagnosed mental disorders throughout childhood and adolescence in a total nationwide population, and this adds new knowledge to the field of mental health.

"The existing literature mainly reports prevalence rates in a population alive at a certain point, only obtaining data on surviving individuals, thereby potentially missing data on those with the most severe psychopathologic diagnoses who did not survive until start of the study."
