The Dividend Dogs Rule

Stocks earned the "dog" moniker by exhibiting three traits: (1) paying reliable, repeating dividends, (2) their prices fell to where (3) yield (dividend/price) grew higher than their peers. Thus, the highest yielding stocks in any collection became known as "dogs." More precisely, these are, in fact, best called, "underdogs".

Actionable Conclusions (11-20): 10 Top Kiplinger Reliable Retirement Stocks By Yield

Top ten Kiplinger Reliable Retirement stocks selected 11/21/19 by yield represented seven of eleven Morningstar sectors. First place was claimed by one of two real estate sector representatives, Public Storage [1]. The other real estate representative placed eighth, American Tower Corp. (AMT) [8].

In second place was the lone technology representative, International Business Machines Corp. [2]. Two consumer cyclical pups claimed third and fourth places, Home Depot Inc. (HD) [3], and McDonald's Corp. [4]. Then the lone Financial Services firm placed fifth, Goldman Sachs Group Inc. (GS)[5].

A lone representative form the energy sector placed sixth, Magellan Midstream Partners LP (MMP)[6]. Then the single industrials representative took seventh place, Union Pacific Corp. (NYSE:UNP) [7].

Finally, two utilities claimed the last two slots in the top ten, Duke Energy Corp. [9] and Dominion Energy Inc. (D) [10], to complete the Kiplinger reliable retirement top ten by yield for November.

The five lowest-priced top-yield Kiplinger most reliable retiree dividend dogs as of November 21 were: Magellan Midstream Partners LP; Dominion Energy; Duke Energy Corp.; International Business Machines; Union Pacific Corp., with prices ranging from $61.17 to $174.17.

Five higher-priced Kiplinger top yield most reliable retirement dividend dogs as of November 21 were: McDonald's Corp.; Public Storage; American Tower Corp.; Goldman Sachs Group Inc.; Home Depot Inc., whose prices ranged from $192.35 to $218.54.

The distinction between five low-priced dividend dogs and the general field of ten reflected Michael B. O'Higgins' "basic method" for beating the Dow. The scale of projected gains based on analyst targets added a unique element of "market sentiment" gauging upside potential. It provided a here-and-now equivalent of waiting a year to find out what might happen in the market. Caution is advised, since analysts are historically only 20% to 80% accurate on the direction of change and just 0% to 20% accurate on the degree of change.

The net gain/loss estimates above did not factor in any foreign or domestic tax problems resulting from distributions. Consult your tax advisor regarding the source and consequences of "dividends" from any investment.
