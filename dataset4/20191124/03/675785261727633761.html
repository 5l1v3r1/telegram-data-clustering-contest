Stalking victims feel 'betrayed' as more than half of offenders avoid prison

EXCLUSIVE - CONTAINS GRAPHIC IMAGE: The Sunday People can reveal that despite a record number of convictions for stalking last year, 58 percent got community or suspended sentences

24 Nov 2019, 03:02 by Dan Warburton

Stalking victims accused the justice system of a cruel betrayal as more than half of offenders dodge jail.

The Sunday People can reveal that despite a record number of convictions for stalking last year, 58 percent got community or suspended sentences.

It comes just a year after tougher sentences for stalkers were rolled out.

Victim and anti-stalking campaigner Zoe Dronfield suffered a horrific attack by her former partner – and later found he had 13 previous stalking victims.

She said: “Stalking ruined my life and almost led to my murder.”

The 42-year-old, from Coventry, was held captive for eight hours by Jason Smith in 2013.

During her ordeal, she suffered a cracked cheekbone, a brain bleed and a stab wound to her neck.

The brute – who was calling her a hundred times a day – got 14 years in 2015, ten in jail and four on licence.

Slamming the latest figures, Zoe said: “Offenders should have harsher and longer sentences.

"Stalking is like ­murder in slow motion. It’s a betrayal of victims that so many are let off without custodial sentences.”

London’s Independent Victims’ Commissioner Claire Waxman – herself harassed for 12 years – also blasted the figures.

She said: “It’s disappointing to see a shockingly lenient approach. It’s crucial more is done to ensure sentencing reflects the severity of this crime.”

And the Suzy Lamplugh Trust’s Suky Bhaker said: “Community sentences should never be considered as an alternative to prison if the risk cannot be managed appropriately – and what victims tell us is risk management is not always adequate.”

In September last year, the maximum sentence for stalking was raised, doubling prison time from five to ten years.

In total, 1,022 people were convicted of stalking in England and Wales in 2018.

But MoJ data shows 396 (36 percent) got community sentences while 250 (23 percent) got suspended terms. And 58 people got just a fine, while a further 80 were just cautioned.

In August, police were told not to give fixed penalty notices in stalking and harassment cases following the murder of Shana Grice, 19.

She complained about stalker ex Michael Lane five times in six months – but was fined for wasting police time.

Lane slit her throat in 2016 in Portslade, East Sussex. He was jailed in 2017 for a minimum 25 years.

The Tory government has long promised to clamp down on domestic abusers with a raft of measures in their Domestic Violence Bill, which has been delayed since its introduction in 2017.

David Spencer, of the Centre for Crime Prevention, said: “There has been tough talking from the MoJ but it is time to follow up with action.”

The Ministry of Justice said it was down to each individual judiciary to determine sentences based on the full facts of each case.
