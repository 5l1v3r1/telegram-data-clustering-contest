Money Saving Expert Martin Lewis says you should not ignore private parking tickets

He told ITV This Morning viewers that he was misheard

24 Nov 2019, 13:23 by Rebecca Koncienzcy

Martin Lewis has moved to put an end to rumours that he said you don't have to pay private parking tickets.

The Money Saving Expert told presenters Holly Willoughby and Phillip Schofield on ITV's This Morning it was a Facebook post that misquoted him.

The 47-year-old appeared on the daytime TV show to answer viewers' money questions when one caller asked about private parking tickets - which are different to council tickets.

Vicky said: "I've had a parking ticket sent to me in the post from a private company, my dad said that he'd heard that as it's private I don't have to pay it. Please can you give me some advice?

Martin said: "That's our fault. That's incorrect information, it's our fault, because we did it on this show a few years ago, someone misheard, did a Facebook post about it that was then shared 300,000 times - and I've been trying to correct this one ever since. So I am quite glad of that correction.

"What I say is, private parking tickets are invoices. So if they're unfair invoices you don't have to pay them.

"But I would always inform them that you believe they're unfair, if they have an appeals system go back through the appeals system.

"If they don't, write back and say it's unfair, and why you think it is.

"I would not ignore them and I have never taken that stance.

"Some militant anti-parking campaigners do take that stance, I don't because judges have said to me 'well, if you ignore them, we don't look well on it if they take you to court in the end'.

"There are guides online about what to do with private parking tickets, but I am not a fan of ignoring them, contrary to what that hugely shared Facebook post said, never my words."

He said you should go to the private firm and tell them it is unfair and tell them all the reasons why, such as small signage and ask them to reply.

Or some private parking companies have an appeals process.

Martin added: "Ignoring the letter, it works for some people, but it is very risky."
