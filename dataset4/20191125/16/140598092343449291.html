StubHub is just the ticket for Viagogo after $4bn swoop

25 Nov 2019, 16:18 by James Cook

Viagogo, the ticket reseller condemned by MPs for its misleading sales practices, is buying rival site StubHub from its owner eBay for $4bn (£3bn).

The auction site paid $310m for the ticket business in 2007 and it has become the largest ticket reseller in the US, with revenues of about $1.1bn last year.

Eric Baker, chief executive of Viagogo, co-founded StubHub while still in business school but left before it was sold.

“It has long been my wish to unite the two companies,” he said on Monday and claimed that the deal will give customers “more choice and better pricing”.

The combined companies will sell hundreds of thousands of tickets a day in more than 70 countries.

The deal is likely to be welcomed by activist investors such as Paul Singer’s Elliott Management, which has called for eBay to sell divisions including the ticket site.

“StubHub is non-core to the eBay Marketplace but would command significant value as a market-leading, scale business,” wrote Jesse Cohn, a partner at Elliott, in a letter to eBay’s board in January.

The hedge fund said that it believed StubHub would be worth about $4bn.

Representatives from Elliott and Starboard Value joined eBay’s board earlier this year and backed a strategic review which examined ways to improve the company’s finances.

Ticket resale websites have faced frequent scrutiny by UK regulators and politicians.

In September the Competitions and Markets Authority suspended legal action against Viagogo after finding that the site had made changes to address concerns that it was not following the law.

The watchdog had warned the business that it was not consistently giving information about tickets, including exact seat numbers and a warning that resold tickets may not automatically grant customers entry.

Cris Miller, managing director, said Viagogo has met with a number of regulators to hear their concerns. 

“What we've been doing over the past year has made over 1,000 changes to the website,” he said. “That includes the messaging on our website to provide more transparency [to] pricing to bring all the fees and everything closer to the first place the customers see the price.”

Earlier this year MPs warned the public not to buy or sell tickets through Viagogo, which is based in Geneva, until it "fully complies with consumer law".

The Digital, Culture, Media and Sport Committee criticised the business for what it called “misleading sales practices.”
