Center Parcs Ireland: Quiet Longford town now booming thanks to new holiday resort

It gave us Maura Higgins – the Queen of Love Island – and now it’s vying for the crown of fastest-growing town in the Midlands

25 Nov 2019, 21:58 by Michelle Fleming

Eileen McLoughlin loves to sit out on her front porch at lunchtimes on Mondays and Fridays and watch the Mullingar Road burst into life.

And for those few hours, to her eyes, the buzz is more Manhattan than Ballymahon.

Twenty years ago kids could play football on this Co Longford town’s Main Street.

These days you’re lucky to find a parking space. Welcome to Boomtown.

Eileen, 77, said: “I sit on my porch on a Friday and the crowds and traffic is unreal, and you know where they’re going with all the bikes on the back.”

Once a sleepy corner of Longford – the forgotten county that often came last in the tourist league tables – Ballymahon is having one heck of a moment.

It gave us Maura Higgins – the Queen of Love Island – and now it’s vying for the crown of fastest-growing town in the Midlands.

On a drizzly Wednesday afternoon, workers in high-vis jackets are busy digging roads so they can lay pipes to pump natural gas from the junction at Athlone Road up to homes and businesses along Main Street.

It will save locals a fortune. There are roadworks also underway on the R312 Mullingar Road.

The new €8.5million ESB power station nearby will bring back the workers to put down the power-lines, when the roads and paths will also get a spanking new upgrade.

Locals have been waiting patiently for the White Bridge over the River Inny in nearby Newcastle Woods to be reinstated since 1963.

And yesterday those of a certain vintage crossed the gorgeous white latticework iron footbridge for the first time in 56 years.

Another €1million has been spent by Longford County Council renovating the Royal Canal Greenway.

Soon cyclists and walkers will bego all the way from Dublin’s
Docklands to Ballymahon.

It will eventually extend into Roscommon, and hook into Westport’s Greenway, heading out towards Achill. It will be the longest off-road cycle route in Europe – with Ballymahon bang in the middle of it.

Just five years ago, all of this would have seemed like a pretty wacky dream.

But that was before the €233million Center Parcs juggernaut came to town.

The 395-acre Center Parcs Longford Forest has been transformed this week into a glittering Winter Wonderland.

But the twinkly lights are well hidden from mere mortals behind 24-hour security posts, fences and thick conifer canopies around the periphery of the resort, 4km outside the town.

Locals who hadn’t been able to find a job since the days Manford Clothing’s suits and overcoats – Longford’s answer to Magee’s of Donegal, which closed in 1989 with the loss of 200 jobs – have swapped hellish commutes for a job that gets
them home in time for tea.

Most of the 1,000 jobs at Center Parcs went to locals, with others bussed in on a fleet of local coaches from outlying towns such as Longford and Mullingar.

On Mondays and Fridays – changeover day at the resort – 400 housekeepers are bussed in. And Main Street is choc-a-block.

Peter Dennehy, a third generation mechanic at Finnegan’s Garage, said: “Center Parcs has been the catalyst for a lot of things that would never, ever have happened – and soon we’ll be the Westport of the Midlands.”

Everywhere you look, Main Street is hopping.

There’s a new Fallon & Byrne-style cafe and gift store, Diffley’s DIY is doing a roaring trade in wellies and outdoor gear, while Hailo and Occasions hairdressers have bookings for Communion parties at Center Parcs next year.

SuperValu is expanding with a new car park being built out the back.

In a warehouse a little out the road, local Sean Colohan and his French wife Carla Nattchayan are living their dream making beer in their year-old Wide Street Brewing Company.

It’s a hit with craft buffs in Dublin’s hipster Stoneybatter.

Savvy investors were keen to back the couple, given their location a few miles from Center Parcs, and they’ve plans to open a visitor centre.

Cooneys Hotel has never been busier – part in thanks to the 10.30pm last orders at the resort’s bars.

And it’s not just the estimated 2,500 holiday-makers who are coming and going every Monday and Friday who are doing the splurging.

There’s also the €27million in wages flooding into the local economy.

Eileen is getting her perm done in Occasions.

She said: “My daughter-in-law, her daughter, my grandson and his mother all work there and they love it.” Father-of-five Paddy Diffley, of Diffleys DIY, is rethinking his stock to suit the holiday crowd.

He said: “I didn’t think there would be such a big move on wellies and the wetsuits for the kids so we’ll be getting in a lot more of that.

“It’s all a bit late for me though. I’m here since 1973 and it’s almost time to be getting out.”

But – in Paddy’s favour – it’s never been a better time to sell.

Property prices in the town – in a county lumbered with ghost estates not so long ago – are surging.

In 18 months, one estate agent sold the same apartment for €20,000, €40,000 and €60,000.

John Byrne has happy memories as a small boy helping his late father Charlie at his block plant Readymix on the Edgeworthstown Road.

But in 1983, the recession forced Charlie to close.

Then, in September 2015 – five months after Center Parcs announced it was coming to Ballymahon – John bought back his dad’s old site.

He’d had enough of the 2.5-hour daily commute to Vodafone in
Sandyford. The arrival of Center Parcs’ made up his mind and his dad’s old site had just been put on the market.

Since then his logistics and distribution company Droplink has grown its workforce with 40 more staff.

Sadly Charlie, who died in 2001, never got to see his son buy back his old site.

John said: “My dad struggled so hard building his business from
the ground up and it was tough for him to lose it. He’d be so delighted – over the moon – to see me back here.”

And according to Peter Dennehy, this is just the start of it.

He said: “When we were growing up there were kids in every house in the Main Street.

“Now there are just three kids and two are mine.

“But that’s all about to flip back again. Soon they’ll all be back.”
