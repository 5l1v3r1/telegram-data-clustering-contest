3 Psychological Drawbacks of the Popularity of "OK Boomer"

The meme promotes a negative stereotype, is divisive and discriminatory.

25 Nov 2019 by Utpal Dholakia Ph.D.

Utpal Dholakia Ph.D.

The Science Behind Behavior

“They always say: ok, boomer. but never: are u ok, boomer? – Asuka Boi (@Asuka_Boi) on Twitter.

Whassup? Cash me ousside howbow dah? On fleek. OK Boomer.

We live in a time when, fueled by social media shares, new memes are constantly bubbling up to the surface of our collective cultural consciousness. Memes are juxtapositions or extensions of the familiar. The ones that go viral often reveal an insight about someone or something that was hidden and unnamed, just beneath the surface. They allow the user to vent, to challenge, to flirt, to admire, or to express another complex emotion. Viral memes capture a society’s ethos, appearing from just the right source in the right medium at the right time. Users embrace the meme as a membership ticket to the in-crowd, to be counted among the knowing, and to push away from other not-so-desirable, not-so-knowing groups.

OK Boomer checks all these boxes. The meme was virtually unknown just a few weeks ago. Today, it is all over the internet, with articles about the meme appearing in practically every media outlet. Dictionary.com already has a definition up for it:

“OK boomer is a viral internet slang phrase used, often in a humorous or ironic manner, to call out or dismiss out-of-touch or close-minded opinions associated with the baby boomer generation and older people more generally.”

Such descriptions fail to shine a light on the harm generated in the wake of its growing use. When a young person or a trying-to-be-hip reporter writes or says OK Boomer ironically, or tweets the phrase in response to an article, post, or tweet from an older person, an insidious knowledge structure is created or reinforced in users’ and observers’ minds through intentional learning.

With repeated use, it has the potential to affect their attitudes. Interactions with an older person may be viewed through the lens of irony and anger. Three significant psychological downsides of OK Boomer’s popularity are:

  1. It spreads a negative stereotype that stigmatizes older people.
  2. The phrase is inherently divisive, highlighting differences between the generations.
  3. Its use and endorsement encourage age-based marginalization and discrimination.

By itself, the term "Boomer" or “Baby Boomer” is descriptive. It refers to people belonging to a particular generation, those born between 1946 and 1964. It has limited evaluative content. But add OK in front of Boomer, and suddenly, the phrase is highly evaluative and not in a good way.

Virtually all evaluative content associated with the meme is negative. It characterizes older people as outdated, out-of-touch, self-serving, old, cognitively slow, narrow-minded, clueless, part of the problem, and unconcerned about the environment. In fact, I could not find a single positive association or concept in relation to OK Boomer. What’s more, the meme brands tens of millions of people, basically anyone who looks old (age indeterminate), in this negative light.

Some users have argued that OK Boomer is meant to apply only to the “most privileged members of the boomer generation.” But as social psychologists know all too well, negative stereotypes often begin from narrow, limited roots and spread far beyond the original scope to stigmatize large groups of people, including entire races, ethnic groups, or, in this case, age groups, who have little relation to the initial target.

Because of its one-dimensional, negative nature, OK Boomer serves as a tool of division, to segregate older people, put them on the spot, and ridicule them. When a younger politician retorts “OK Boomer” to an older colleague, she is going beyond criticism of his immediate behavior and disparaging him in a personal way for who he is. Although it is socially acceptable at the moment, the take-down is not that different from directing a racist or sexist remark at someone.

Social psychologists also know that it is a short jump from the creation and reinforcement of a negative stereotype to behavior that matches it. One of the most robust findings in social psychology is that people’s attitudes towards an object or person drive their behavior. When OK Boomer is at the top of their minds, younger people will act in ways that are consistent with the negative stereotype.

It’s only a matter of time before an impatient teenager brushes off a frail person in a supermarket checkout line or a young manager humiliates an older subordinate, retorting “OK Boomer” just because they are old.
