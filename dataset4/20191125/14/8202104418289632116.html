Dumont: Portrait of the artist as an artist

25 Nov 2019, 14:00 by Dawn Dumont, Saskatoon StarPhoenix

She wanted me to make her into Minnie Mouse, complete with a pink polka dotted bow. I was armed with only Q-tips and a lot of paint ...

I volunteered at the kid’s area at the FSIN pow wow a couple weeks ago. I started off small by organizing the paper, glitter and dull scissors — but then I noticed a long line forming for the face-painters.

There were multiple lines of little girls in ages ranging from five to eight and while they were patient, they had that desperate look on their faces that I get when I see a 50 per cent off sale at Old Navy. I thought, how hard could it be?

Still I hesitated before sitting down at the face-painter table because I am not good at drawing. In fact, during my lifetime I have twice seen my drawings held up to the mockery of my coworkers, “THAT’S SUPPOSED TO BE A HORSE? WHY DOES IT HAVE A DOG’S HEAD?”

To be fair, horses are like level 10 when it comes to art.

But all the self-help books tell me that you are supposed to jump into challenges, not run from them. That you are supposed to feel uncomfortable and then more uncomfortable … and then, I dunno, something magical happens (or you get diarrhea)?

The first little girl sat down and asked for a dreamcatcher on her cheek. I Googled “dreamcatcher” and shook my head. I pointed at one of the other volunteers, a woman with her eyeliner and blush perfectly executed: “Now that’s your girl over there. Next?”

The next little girl was about seven and her name was Marie. She explained that she had already had her face painted, washed it off and wanted something new. I felt like I had been sent to teach her the lesson that the grass is always greener or don’t go chasing waterfalls.

She wanted me to make her into Minnie Mouse, complete with a pink polka dotted bow. I was armed with only Q-tips and a lot of paint, but I decided to go for it. I was having trouble with the black paint for her mouse ears and Marie pointed out that you were supposed to dip the Q-tip in water. That’s a pro-tip. (Maybe Marie was sent to teach me!)

We got the ears done and then I started on the bow, which was a herculean task because it had to go all the way across her forehead. I started sweating like Don Cherry at a multicultural festival, but I had committed to that bow and I finished it. After that it was just dotting in the polka dots. I hesitated to show Marie the picture, but when she saw it, she smiled.

There was no time to celebrate as the next girl was already in the seat. This one had shrewd eyes and she asked for a small bee on her cheek, having already figured out that my skill was in dots and not large pictures. I nailed the bee and thought maybe I should switch careers. Perhaps I’ve had it all wrong — novels aren’t the place for me, children’s parties are the way to go.

That’s when the five-year-old sat down. Her mother hovered behind her and though she didn’t look mad, I already felt scared. Her daughter asked for a lady bug on her cheek. It was small and not complex, so I took a deep breath and dived in.

Things started to go sideways right from the start. First of all, ladybugs have these curly whisker things that are really hard to make with a big, fatheaded Q-tip. Second, five-year-olds do not sit still the same way that seven-year-olds do. She kept turning her head this way and that. My outline of the lady bug was uneven … and it was only the beginning …

I realized that I had reached the extent of my ability to decorate tiny faces and I tapped out. I called in one of the more senior face painters (meaning they had been there a couple hours longer than me). “So, this picture is a two face-painter-job?” the mom asked with more than a little bit of sarcasm.

“It sure is,” I answered as I ran away, glad to be out of the firing line and a bit pleased to be mistaken for a real face-painter.
