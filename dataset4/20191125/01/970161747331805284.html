Golem review - the limits of virtual reality

25 Nov 2019, 01:00

A team of ex-Bungie developers and Halo composer Marty O’Donnell present a PSVR exclusive four years in the making.

Developer Highwire Games, with its small team of industry veterans, announced Golem in 2015, a full year before PlayStation VR was released. With staff who worked on Halo and Destiny, and a musical score by the legendary Marty O’Donnell – who was responsible for Halo’s soundtrack, expectations were sky high. Following a series of delays Golem is finally here, but has the extended gestation been worth it?

Golem tells the story of a young girl, Twine, whose family subsists on plundering relics from an ancient city. The game starts with an incident that kills her sister and leaves Twine bedridden, but also in possession of a dream stone, an artefact that lets her take full bodily control of a four-metre tall stone golem. For the rest of the game you play as Twine, who in turns controls the golem, in a sort of Being John Malkovich-style person inside a person inside a golem.

Taking control of the golem is immediately striking, the sense of becoming a hulking monster carrying a massive iron sword, stomping your way through beautiful ancient ruins, is one that you won’t soon forget. But it’s a short honeymoon period because Golem has many quirks, not all of which are good.

The first oddity is that you play the game with a single PlayStation Move controller, giving you command of the golem’s weapon arm as you wield a sword, halberd, or mace. There is another arm, but it hangs by the golem’s side throughout the game and is never used for anything. The other peculiarity of the one Move control method is that you use your upper body to steer.

Holding down the trigger, you gently lean in the direction you want to go and off the golem lumbers. When you’re leaning forwards, looking left or right will realign you to that direction, and after a bit of experimentation you find that rather than leaning your whole body, a modest inclination of your head is all it takes, making for a workable if mildly uncomfortable control interface.
