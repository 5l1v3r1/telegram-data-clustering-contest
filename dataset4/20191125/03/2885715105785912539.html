How an unknown female senator came to replace Bolivian strongman Evo Morales

In just two days, Jeanine Anez rose from political obscurity to supplant Morales, the country's first Indigenous leader and a leftist who led with a single-minded pursuit of his vision for 14 years.

25 Nov 2019, 03:06 by New York Times

Written by Anatoly Kurmanaev and Cesar Del Castillo (Elisabeth Malkin and Elda Cantu contributed reporting)

A day after the downfall of Bolivia’s longest-serving president, Evo Morales, the country was in chaos: Soldiers and protesters were out in the streets, looters terrorized the suburbs and no one was in charge.

Catholic church leaders called an urgent meeting of the country’s main political forces on that Monday, Nov. 11, to decide who should take the reins. The line of succession leading out of the power vacuum traced to Jeanine Añez, a senator from a remote tropical region who had sat out October’s elections and was about to retire.

“Who was Jeanine? No one knew,” said Jorge Quiroga, a former president of Bolivia and a conservative statesman who helped organize its political transition this month. “But we knew that she was the only constitutional thread that we had.”

In two days, Añez rose from political obscurity to supplant Morales, the country’s first indigenous leader and a leftist who led with a single-minded pursuit of his vision for 14 years.

The story behind Añez’s rise, told by five people in the meetings that decided the country’s future, is a story of stunted political parties and a bitterly divided society, which continue to convulse this South American nation.
