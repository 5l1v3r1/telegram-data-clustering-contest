ASIC launches investigation into Westpac

25 Nov 2019, 03:58 by Clancy Yeates

The corporate watchdog has launched an investigation into Westpac over potential legal breaches linked to the bank's money laundering compliance scandal, ahead of a series of critical meetings between chairman Lindsay Maxsted and investors.

The Australian Securities and Investments Commission (ASIC) on Monday took the unusual step of publicly confirming it had started looking into possible breaches of laws it administers — most likely a reference to the Corporations Act.

The fresh investigation is the latest blow to the bank, after it was last week thrown into crisis by a regulatory lawsuit that says it breached anti-money laundering laws 23 million times, including failing to adequately vet thousands of payments potentially linked to child exploitation.

"ASIC can confirm that it commenced an investigation on Thursday, 21 November 2019 concerning possible breaches of legislation it administers arising from AUSTRAC’s actions in relation to Westpac," a spokesman said on Monday.

ASIC did not provide further details of its investigation into Westpac. But the Australian Financial Review last week reported that the watchdog could look at a Corporations Act duty to act "efficiently, honestly and fairly," and a duty to take the care and diligence expected of a reasonable person.

A bank spokesman said: "Westpac will co-operate with any regulatory inquiries.”

ASIC also launched an investigation into the Commonwealth Bank after it was hit by an anti-money laundering lawsuit more than two years ago. The ASIC spokesman on Monday said this CBA probe was ongoing.

As Westpac shares continued to slide, falling 1.3 per cent to $24.43, credit ratings Moody's on Monday also warned the alleged money laundering breaches would be "credit negative" for the bank.

Moody's highlighted the high cost of fines for breaches of anti-money laundering laws, though it added that if Westpac got a fine as large as Commonwealth Bank's record $700 million penalty for anti-money laundering breaches, this would be "manageable" for the bank.

Confirmation of the ASIC investigation, a day after the Australian Prudential Regulation Authority (APRA) said it too was investigating the bank, comes as chairman Lindsay Maxsted will meet with key advisers and investors this week.

The meetings could be crucial in determining the futures of Mr Maxsted and the bank's chief executive Brian Hartzer, as politicians and investors demand accountability over the scandal.

The meetings will include governance adviser Institutional Shareholder Services, superannuation giant AustralianSuper, and various other big investors in the bank and proxy advisers.

Amid predictions from some investors and business commentators that Mr Hartzer will likely lose his job over the scandal, Mr Maxsted on Sunday strongly backed the CEO, saying the board had seen no evidence of failure by Mr Hartzer.
