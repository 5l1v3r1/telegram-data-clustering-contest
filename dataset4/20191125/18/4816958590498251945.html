No, koalas are not 'functionally extinct'

The onslaught of bushfires in Australia has pushed the koala to the forefront of people's minds, but it's not as bad as you think

25 Nov 2019, 18:05 by National Post Staff

Over the weekend, social media users grew alarmed over reports that koalas are now “functionally extinct” following devastating wildfires. Turns out, the iconic Australian creature is not gone anywhere.

Unprecedented fires ripped through Queensland and New South Wales this month, killing six people and burning hundreds of thousands of hectares of land. Firefighters were battling a 6,000 kilometre-long front at one point, the Guardian reported.

The onslaught pushed the koala to the forefront of people’s minds, with manyheadlines, peppered with images of charred and nearly lifeless koalas, indicating that the animal is pretty much on its outs because its habitat has been lost.

But while it is true that many koalas perished in the flames, not enough of them died for them to be considered wiped out, koala expert and biologist Christine Hosking told SBS.

“Functionally extinct” means the population of a species has declined so much that they don’t have a significant impact on their ecosystem. As well, they cannot reproduce fast enough to grow the population, Hosking wrote in The Conversation in May.
