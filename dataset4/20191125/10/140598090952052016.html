Sam Mendes's single-take WW1 film 1917 declared an Oscar frontrunner after early screenings

25 Nov 2019, 10:29 by Telegraph Reporters

Sam Mendes' World War 1 epic 1917 has just injected the Oscars race - led by Quentin Tarantino's Once Upon a Time in Hollywood and Martin Scorsese's The Irishman - with serious competition. 

The two-hour film, written by Mendes and Krysty Wilson-Cairns, stars George MacKay and Dean-Charles Chapman as two soldiers given eight hours to run into German territory and deliver a message to another British front line to stop an imminent British attack that will lead 1600 soldiers into a trap.

The film plays out as one continuous shot, under the guidance of 14-time Oscar nominee Roger Deakins as director of photography, so that the point of view never leaves that of the soldiers. 

Critics who saw an advance screening of the film last night in New York - where Mendes told the audience many of the film's scenes were inspired by stories related to him by his war veteran grandfather - have been blown away. 

Critic Clayton Davis described the film as the Oscars "frontrunner": "1917 is the best war film since SAVING PRIVATE RYAN. The cinematography of the year. The cinematography of the decade. Thomas Newman's orchestral masterpiece. Sam Mendes gift to cinema...and his family. Every ounce is powerful."
