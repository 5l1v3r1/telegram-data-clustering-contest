Crum: A pilgrim's journey ends, but not his work

25 Nov 2019, 10:00 by William Crum

Earlier coverage:

Bob Waldrop had a way of making you laugh even as he twisted the knife.

“The executive summary of my opinion is: ‘This is social cruelty in action,’” was his assessment of the Oklahoma City Council’s 2015 attempt to crack down on panhandling.

Pilgrim, friend to the poor, liturgical music director, political candidate, Robert Max Augustine Waldrop (@bobwaldrop) died this year at age 66, succumbing to cancer on Aug. 30.

Bob made his living as music director at Epiphany of the Lord Catholic Church and did his living in a 1929 bungalow in the Gatewood neighborhood, where he devoted himself to a sustainable lifestyle at his “Gatewood Urban Homestead,” a nod to his Tillman County roots.

He founded the Oscar Romero Catholic Worker House, with its mission to feed the needy.

And he used his losing challenge to Councilwoman Meg Salyer in 2015 to highlight unmet needs in Ward 6, saying residents felt “left behind and ignored.”

Even as his cancer advanced last summer, he busied himself with commentary on MAPS 4, Municipal Court amnesty, and health coverage for the poor.

Bob likely was heartened by MAPS 4’s pivot toward sheltering the homeless, treating the mentally ill, and connecting the impoverished; disheartened by insistence in some quarters that social service agencies justify their claim on the public purse with charts and graphs showing how they intended to measure “outcomes;” and heartened by the pushback from current Ward 6 Councilwoman JoBeth Hamon.

Bob had written last spring that there continued "to be no good news from the docs."

“Friends are sending me to Lourdes,” he said, “and the original plan was an October trip but the oncologist said to not wait that long.

“I continue to be at peace and even content," Waldrop continued, "which is sort of a mind-blowing experience since I used to be a world-class worrier about my health.

“It’s not over until it’s over and even then, it’s not really over, it’s more of a transition to a new reality.

“Details of that, of course, are somewhat sketchy but if there are jobs in the afterlife, I intend to apply for one to visit certain politicians in their dreams.”

He closed that note with reports on work (playing all 11 Holy Week services at Epiphany), insurance (thankful to have it), writing (how to condense a life into an affordable newspaper obituary notice), and medical marijuana (helps with the nausea and appetite).

He signed himself “rmw” and hit send; his note arrived at 3:46 p.m. on Good Friday.

Six weeks later he was on his way to France.

Along the way, he managed to start a lively Twitter debate with photos of sidewalk vendors and street musicians (legal in Paris, "basically" outlawed in Oklahoma City).

Bob is poised to add a legislative victory to his legacy when the Oklahoma City Council votes next week on proposals sponsored by Hamon to drop licensing and permit requirements for most street entertainers.

"I believe this might possibly be the first Twitter-instigated ordinance change in city history," Mayor David Holt said earlier this month.

On June 2, Bob took the train to Lourdes (U.S transportation is “primitive” by comparison, he observed) and tweeted a photo captioned, in Twitter shorthand, “The Grotto at Lourdes where Our Lady appeared to Saint Bernadette.”

Then the pilgrim headed home.
