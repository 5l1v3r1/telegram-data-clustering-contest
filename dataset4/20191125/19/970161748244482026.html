General Election 2019: What are the parties promising on benefits?

25 Nov 2019, 19:17

The impact of the Conservative government’s flagship Universal Credit benefit system has proven to be incredibly controversial.

It’s divided opinion and sparked fiery debate both in Westminster and the streets of Britain.

So what are the main parties planning to do with the benefits system if elected into power on December 12?

Conservatives

The Tories say they will continue to roll out Universal Credit across the country despite widespread criticism, arguing it has transformed the ‘impossibly complex’ old system.

But they are ending a freeze on benefits, expected to result in an extra 1.7 per cent in money people receive from April next year.

In a bid to stop people going through the system for an unreasonable amount of time, the Tories have vowed to reduce the number of reassessments for disabled claimants.

They have pledged to ‘do more to make sure that Universal Credit works for the most vulnerable’ but have not gone into much detail as to how.

Labour

Jeremy Corbyn has vowed to completely scrap Universal Credit, which critics say has led to a rise in homelessness and food bank use.

If elected, Labour says it will start developing ‘an alternative system that treats people with dignity and respect’ to ‘end poverty by guaranteeing a minimum standard of living’.

The party promises to end the five-week wait for first time payments by introducing an interim payment based on half an estimated monthly entitlement.

Labour also wants to scrap the benefit cap and the two child limit, which they say will ‘stop 300,000 children from being in poverty’.

Liberal Democrats

The Lib Dems have promised to reform Universal Credit to be ‘more supportive for the self-employed’.

Leader Jo Swinson has pledged £6 billion per year to ‘make the benefits system work for people who need it’.

They want to replace the current system with an incentive-based one and to remove the benefits cap and two child limit.

The Lib Dems also want to reduce the five-week wait to just five days.

Green Party 

Co-leaders Siân Berry and Jonathan Bartley have promised a Universal Basic Income (UBI) by 2025.

It would see adults receiving a minimum of £89 per week with additional payments going to those facing barriers into work, such as disabled people and single parents.

The party says this will ‘create more jobs than ever before’ and raise the income of someone working full time on minimum wage by 32 per cent.

Housing benefit and carers will continue being paid to whoever received it before UBI was introduced.

Brexit Party

Nigel Farage says the current Universal Credit system has ‘not achieved what was intended’ and  ‘needs to be revisited’.

In its ‘contract with the people, the party vows to ‘support those who have paid into the system with an accelerated payment process’ – with a maximum wait of five weeks.

The party promises a 12 month review of Universal Credit and to bring in reforms in less than two years.

It also wants to ‘extend the use of dormant funds to support civil society’.
