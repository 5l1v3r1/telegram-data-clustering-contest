Conor Murray has no problem with World Cup post-mortem as he returns to Munster

Now fully back in the Munster fold, Ireland's no 9 is long enough in the tooth to know what was coming after the latest disappointment on the world stage

20 Nov 2019, 06:00 by Michael Scully

Conor Murray has no problem with the public inquest that has been conducted since Ireland's World Cup quarter-final collapse.

Now fully back in the Munster fold, Ireland's no 9 is long enough in the tooth to know what was coming after the latest disappointment on the world stage.

"I understand it," said Murray. "I've been around long enough now to understand how people react.

"It's because this team has been really, really successful over the past five years. We hadn't seen that success in an Irish team, or been involved in it.

"So I hadn't seen the spin-off when things don't go that well - the spin-off from the heights.

"When things are going so well, I genuinely don't see much media - hand on heart, I don't -  but you get a feeling of it, that people are building you up to this massive thing.

"I didn't see the other side of it or expect it because we hadn't been at that level of the past four or five years.

"People are probably upset. They do want us to go well. So when it doesn't happen, people are looking for reasons. There are all sorts of opinions.

"We were really confident about what we were trying to do. Our game plan was brilliant. It was just a shame we didn't execute because we created chances, but we didn't finish.

"New Zealand were brilliant that day. Out of the top six teams, anyone can beat each other.

"You saw it with England, dominating New Zealand and then being beaten by South Africa. Obviously, our defeat was quite heavy. But you process it, try to get over it.

"You'll carry it for a while and there are certain motivational things...but the best is to be positive, try to get back out there, play the game you love and enjoy it."

Murray will even be giving Joe Schmidt's self-penned memoir 'Ordinary Joe' - which hits the shelves tomorrow - a wide berth, at least until he gets word that he's not on the receiving end of criticism.

"It might be really damaging to read it for yourself...I might leave it off until I finish playing," he laughed.

"I'm sure it will be brilliant, a great insight into his mind, if it's going to be a coaching book or whatever way he wants it to come across.

"It will be fascinating. I think he wrote it himself, so I'll get someone to read it first and if there's anything bad about me, I'll wait until I finish playing!"

Murray wants to bounce back from Japan by having a successful season with Munster, who face Racing on Saturday in their second Champions Cup pool game.

"First of all, get over the World Cup, enjoying playing and being part of a really determined and motivated group," he remarked.

"The aim is to win trophies with this club. It's just being back in here trying to get better, enjoy rugby, improve your craft."

Attack coach Stephen Larkham claims it's not a frustration that Joey Carbery returned from the World Cup with an ankle problem that originated in August.

"It’s not at the moment because Tyler (Bleyendaal) and JJ (Hanrahan) are playing really good footy," said Larkham.

“It’s a World Cup, comes around once every four years. It’s something that’s very special.

"Joey wanted to push himself, the coaches wanted him out there because he’s such a talented player. I can understand the situation.

“We want to make sure that we rehab this properly, give him a good chance to get back to peak fitness".
