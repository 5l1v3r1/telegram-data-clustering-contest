Tributes: Bob Hammack created the local hockey character 'High Stick Harry'

20 Nov 2019, 06:05 by Scott Munn

Farewell to people with Oklahoma ties who enjoyed the game day experience:

Bob Hammack, 71, of Oklahoma City. Played football at Northwest Classen High School and briefly for Panhandle State. Hammack, who graduated from Oklahoma State with a degree in journalism, became an award-winning advertising executive for New West Group. He had many sports-related accounts in Oklahoma City, including the old Cavalry basketball team and the Blazers hockey club. Hammack created the popular, mythical character "High Stick Harry" in the 1990s. Harry, a beer-loving puckhead with a thick Chicago-esque accent, was featured on many radio commercials making light of the Blazers' main rivals, the Tulsa Oilers and Wichita Thunder.

Hammack was crazy about all sports, whether he was playing or just watching. He was a member of a 3-on-3 basketball team that won three gold medals in the U.S. Senior Olympics. He was the coach and quarterback of the Oklahoma City Blackhawks' adult flag football team for 25 years. Hammack helped the Hawks win several state championships and qualify for many national tournaments. He also organized an annual Turkey Bowl, a Thanksgiving Day football game played — rain or shine — at Taft Stadium between Northwest Classen alums.

Austin Boss, 23, of Alvin, Texas. He played baseball and golf for Moore High School. The Thunder fan was a student at Alvin Community College at the time of death.

Tony Gwinn, 55, of Del City. He played basketball and baseball at Newcastle High School. Gwinn went on to play baseball at Seminole Junior College, Oklahoma City U., and Oklahoma. The catcher spent three seasons in the Yankees' minor league system. At the time of death, he was a physical education and health teacher at Hefner Middle School in OKC.

Rev. Ronnie Hurst, 72, of Checotah. He was a star running back and track athlete at Tecumseh High School in the 1960s. He turned down a scholarship offer to play football at Cameron so he could attend barber school. Hurst owned a barber shop in Shawnee and was pastor at the Pentecostal Church of God in Tecumseh.

Darryll Matz, 70, of McKinney, Texas. The Classen High School graduate coached wrestling while living in Texas.

Pat Morgan McBride, 86, of El Reno. She was a forward for the girls basketball team at Erick High School. Also a pep club member. An avid OU and OKC Thunder fan.

Ken Love, 93, of Oklahoma City. An area manager for AT&T who loved to play golf. He had two holes-in-one during his life. A World War II veteran.

Paul Kloberdanz Sr., 84, of Nichols Hills. He played football and baseball at the Colorado School of Mines. The oilman coached his kids' basketball and soccer teams, but he had a passion for helping them accomplish goals in tennis.

Sue Long Brown, 82, of Norman. She played the French horn in the Pride of Oklahoma marching band on football Saturdays in 1954 and 1955.

Fred Baker, 95, of Edmond. The retired postal worker and Korean War veteran collected, traded and sold sports cards. Baker played golf into his 90s.

Billy Ray Gregory, 84 of Shawnee. He starred on the offensive line for Tecumseh's unbeaten football team in 1953. Moved to fullback in 1954, helping the Savages finish 7-2. Gregory captained Tecumseh's basketball team, which finished 31-2 for the 1954-55 season. Also played baseball and ran track.

Kelsey Browne Hall, 85, of San Marino, California. The Oklahoma City native and mother of three boys was an official scorer for Little League baseball.

L.D. Brandon, 88, of Midwest City. He played baseball for Byng High School's 1948 state championship team. As an adult, he trained bird dogs and participated in field trials.

Gary Huckabay, 81, of Mustang. He enjoyed quail hunting since he was a boy. Huckabay and his prized English Pointer won the Grand National Quail Hunt in 1978. He was a banker.

Bob Neale, 81, of Purcell. The accountant was an accomplished tennis player. Neale was ranked No. 4 in the Missouri Valley Men's 35-and-over singles division. He was also a tennis instructor.
