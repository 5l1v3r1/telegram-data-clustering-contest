Rio Tinto fears fresh blow to Oyu Tolgoi expansion in Mongolia

20 Nov 2019, 12:55 by Nick Toscano

Rio Tinto's $8 billion expansion of its underground copper and gold mine in Mongolia is headed for another setback as the mining giant fears a court challenge to the validity of the government's 2015 approval agreement looks likely to succeed.

An administrative court has heard claims by a non-government organisation that the Mongolian government failed to follow due process when its former prime minister struck the agreement with Rio Tinto chief executive Jean-Sebastien Jacques.

Rio Tinto on Tuesday said "early reports" suggested the court has upheld the claims by the group, Darkhan Mongol Nogoon Negdel.

The court's official ruling will be released in coming weeks, Rio said. In a statement to the sharemarket, the company said it was defending the claims.

"Rio Tinto strongly refutes any suggestion that the underground development and financing plan or any of the foundation Oyu Tolgoi agreements are illegal," the statement said.

The Anglo-Australian miner's plans to enlarge the Oyu Tolgoi mine, its flagship growth project, has been beset by a series of delays and cost blowouts since its construction began in 2019. In June, the company issued a warning to investors noting the project could cost as much as $1.9 billion more than forecast.

Oyu Tolgoi is 34 per cent owned by the Mongolian government and 66 per cent owned by Turquoise Hill Resources, of which Rio Tinto holds a 51 per cent stake. Since 2010, Rio has been the manager of the Oyu Tolgoi project.

The mine in Mongolia's Gobi desert is the country's biggest source of foreign investment and provides thousands of jobs.

Rio Tinto, the world's second largest mining company, said it would update the market as further information becomes available.
