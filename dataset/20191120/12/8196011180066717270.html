Hospital and HSE apologise to family of brain-dead pregnant woman kept on life support over Eighth amendment concerns

20 Nov 2019, 12:32

The family of a brain-dead pregnant mother of two who was kept on life support for almost four weeks due to concerns about the Eighth amendment has received an apology from a hospital and the HSE at the High Court.

The apology is part of a settlement which also provides for various payments, including a €150,000 payment for Natasha Perie’s daughter who was “horrified” when, then aged six, she saw her mother in a deteriorating and near unrecognisable condition on life support in late 2014.

Her daughter, now aged 11, was described in a recent psychological assessment as a “sad child” who, when asked what would she ask for if she had three wishes, said: "I want my mam back."

Nervous shock claims by six other family members were also settled today and further damages arising from Ms Perie’s death, the larger aspect of the claim, will be assessed for her two children and other family members in January.

In approving the €150,000 settlement for Ms Perie’s daughter, Mr Justice Kevin Cross said the child had endured "a terrible time". He hoped, as she comes to adulthood, she would be comforted by good memories of her mother.

He said Ms Perie was kept alive due to what the High Court later decided was an erroneous view that there was some obligation to do so as a result of the Eighth amendment.

The HSE earlier this year accepted liability concerning the main claim by members of the family - that Ms Perie’s death was caused by failure to diagnose a brain cyst which ruptured, leaving her brain-dead days after her admission to the Midland Regional Hospital Mullingar on November 27, 2014, having complained of severe headaches on a number of occasions. A mediation to assess damages for that will be held in January.

In an apology read to the court today, Anita Brennan, general manager of the Regional Hospital Mullingar, said, on behalf of the HSE and the hospital, she wanted to offer “sincere and deepest sympathy on the tragic death of your much loved daughter Natasha”, and mother of two, while in the hospital’s care in December 2014.

I would like to convey our sincere apologies to you and your family for the failings in the care provided to Natasha and for the consequential upset, distress and trauma you and your family have endured.

Ms Perie’s widowed father Peter, brother Daniel, aunts Mary and Susan, and several other family members were in court.

They were clearly distressed as Denis McCullough SC set out the effect on Ms Perie’s daughter of seeing her on life support and being present when the ventilator was switched off on December 27, 2014.

Counsel said that, during this time, her daughter was brought to see her and was horrified at the condition her mother was in. She was taken there on the advice of a social worker.

Counsel said Natasha’s son, then only four, was less affected and a nervous shock claim was not brought on his behalf.

He said Natasha’s daughter was very close to her mother and, as a result of her death, had to leave the home she shared with her mother, half brother and grandfather and uncle and go and live with her father and half brother. She was "unhappy for a long time and grieving very deeply".

She rated her own mood when assessed by a child psychiatrist as five out of 10 and missed seeing her grandfather and other family members as often as she had previously.

Counsel outlined Ms Perie was at 15 weeks gestation when she was declared clinically brain dead on December 3, 2014, at a Dublin hospital as a result of a ruptured cerebral cyst suffered at the Mullingar hospital on November 29, 2014, two days after admission. She remained on that after being formally declared brain dead on December 3, 2014, until the support was turned off on December 27.

The delay arose from doctors’ concerns taking her off life support could breach the constitutional right to life of the unborn in the Eighth amendment, repealed last year.

After a hearing in Christmas week 2014, a three-judge High Court ruled on December 26 the life support could be ended as the only prospect for the unborn was “distress and death”. Maintaining support would deprive the woman of dignity in death and subject her family to “unimaginable distress in a futile exercise”, it also said.

Ms Perie, described as having a rotting brain with an open infected head wound, was buried afterwards in a closed coffin.

In a statement on behalf of the family after today’s hearing, Peter Perie said he wanted “to express how traumatic and heart-breaking my daughter’s death has been for all of the family”, but especially her children.

“Five years ago, my beautiful daughter Natasha was admitted to Mullingar general hospital suffering from severe headaches and was misdiagnosed as suffering from morning sickness. She was a 26-year-old pregnant young girl. Sadly, Natasha never came home. She died as a result of a large cerebral cyst that went undiagnosed while in hospital.”

What followed was nothing short of a nightmare.

He said this was the family’s third encounter with the legal system as five years ago, they fought to have somatic care withdrawn “to allow my daughter and her unborn baby a dignified funeral and burial”.

Instead, we faced Christmas Eve and St Stephen’s Day in court,” he said. Now, for Natasha’s two children, Christmas “is forever associated with the loss of their beloved Mum.”

“Whilst most of the media acted in a respectful way, we endured a torrent of abuse on social media,” he added.
