How one of our smallest seabirds can tell us a lot about climate change

New study into the behaviour of Arctic terns, which migrate from places like the Farne Islands to Antarctica, can reveal evidence of climate change

20 Nov 2019, 08:00 by Tony Henderson

A new study shows how climate change may be impacting on one of the world’s smallest seabirds, which has major breeding colonies in the North East.

Data collected from electronic tags retrieved from 47 journeys made by the Arctic terns nesting on the Farne Islands, has revealed for the first time how climate change might be affecting their behaviour.

With around 2,000 individual birds, the beach at Long Nanny near Beadnell in Northumberland is the biggest UK mainland breeding colony for Arctic terns. There are also around 2,000 breeding pairs on the Farne Islands.

This year, 1,155 pairs bred on Coquet Island, off Amble.

Arctic terns, which are our longest-migrating seabird, spend their non-breeding season in the Antarctic, and the remoteness of this part of the world means that until now there has been a very limited understanding of their behaviour and distribution while they are there.

Analysing the data from 47 migrations over two study years, 2015 and 2017, the research team found that Arctic terns live on the Antarctic ice for one third of their annual lifecycle.

Analysis of their feathers also showed their main food source is krill or similar crustaceans.

There were however marked differences in the birds’ behaviour and distribution between those tagged in 2015 compared with those tagged in 2017.

This coincided with a substantial change in ice conditions, with high ice cover in 2015 followed by unusually warm conditions which led to the break-up of the ice in late 2016, and lower ice cover than normal throughout the following year.

Dr Chris Redfern of Newcastle University, who led the study, said: “Sea ice is an important habitat for juvenile krill as it provides protection from predators and from the intense light of the Antarctic summer.

“We now know that krill are the main food source for the terns so it seems likely the warmer weather during 2016/2017 led to reduced krill abundance, and so the birds were forced to forage in different areas.

“In fact, in that second year, the birds converged on the Shackleton Ice Shelf rather than being spread out along the East Antarctica coastline.

“Polar regions are particularly sensitive to climate change and even small shifts can have major implications throughout the entire food web.

“This is why it is critical to understand how seabirds such as Arctic terns are affected by environmental change, both short and long term.”

Co-author Professor Richard Bevan of Newcastle University said: “In the course of this study, we have been continuously amazed by the incredible journeys that these seabirds make each , and now we are beginning to get a glimpse into what they are doing in their wintering areas in Antarctica.

“Arctic terns are one of the few non-breeding birds that are present in Antarctic waters during the summer. This means the birds are not constrained to a nest site, but can move to where the best feeding sites are located.

“By following the journeys made by these birds, we can monitor changes in the location of these hot spots and get some insight into environmental changes that are taking place in these very remote locations; areas to which few scientists ever venture or monitor.”

The data was collected as part of a study led by Newcastle University in collaboration with the National Trust, BBC Springwatch and the Natural History Society of Northumbria.

The first data retrieved from the study highlighted the incredible journey travelled by these seabirds, with one flying an estimated 96,000Km (almost 60,000 miles) from its breeding grounds on the Farne Islands to its winter quarters in Antarctica.

Mapping in unprecedented detail the route and stop-off points from the Farnes to Antarctica for the winter and back again to breed, the team tracked:

* An 8,000 km, 24-day, non-stop flight over the Indian Ocean, feeding on the move;

* An overland detour from the Farne Islands to the Irish Sea and over Ireland to the Atlantic;

* A short stay on the New Zealand coast before completing the final leg of their journey;

* And a stop-off at Llangorse Lake, in the Brecon Beacons National Park, during their return journey in the spring.

“The Arctic tern’s dependence on the ice throughout their non-breeding period in Antarctica highlights the vulnerability of the species to climate change,” said Dr Redfern.

“It is well known that Arctic Terns have relatively shorter legs than other tern species and in light of what we now know, this may be an adaptation to life associated with low temperatures.

“The freezing point of sea water is -1.8 C and the daily average minimum temperatures recorded by the birds’ data loggers was between 0 to -5 C.

“The trackers have given us a unique glimpse into the lives of these incredible birds, surviving against all the odds, and highlights how precarious their future is in the light of anthropogenic climate change.”
