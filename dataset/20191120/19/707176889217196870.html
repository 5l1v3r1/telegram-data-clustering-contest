Prince Andrew pulls out of public duties

Humiliating move reflects damage caused by his links to Jeffrey Epstein

20 Nov 2019, 19:20 by Alex Barker

Prince Andrew agreed to withdraw from public duties “for the foreseeable future” on Wednesday in a humiliating move reflecting the deep damage done to his reputation by the Jeffrey Epstein scandal.

In a statement that sought to limit harm to the charitable causes he supports, the Duke of York also offered to help US authorities with any investigations relating Epstein, the late financier and sex offender.

The decision to withdraw from public duties is a rare and historic step for a member of the royal family that underlines how seriously Buckingham Palace is viewing the fallout from the affair.

It comes after a brutal week of press coverage that followed the Duke’s weekend interview with the BBC, where he failed put to rest questions about his friendship with Epstein and allegations that he had sex with a teenager. The Duke has denied the claims.

The Duke’s announcement on Wednesday came as supporters of his charitable causes — including companies such as KPMG, BT and Standard Chartered — began to distance themselves from him to protect their own reputations.

“It has become clear to me over the past few days that the circumstances relating to my former association with Jeffrey Epstein has become a major disruption to my family’s work and the valuable work going on in the many organisations and charities that I am proud to support,” he said.

“Therefore, I have asked Her Majesty if I may step back from public duties for the foreseeable future, and she has given her permission.”

The Duke’s interview with BBC Newsnight was widely criticised for its tone and for the manner in which the royal addressed allegations that he had sex with a teenager coerced by Epstein.

In an attempt to address some of the objections raised, the Duke said in his statement on Wednesday evening that he continued “to unequivocally regret my ill-judged association with Jeffrey Epstein” — a stronger form of words than he used in the BBC interview.

“His suicide has left many unanswered questions, particularly for his victims, and I deeply sympathise with everyone who has been affected and wants some form of closure,” the Duke said.

“I can only hope that, in time, they will be able to rebuild their lives. Of course, I am willing to help any appropriate law enforcement agency.”
