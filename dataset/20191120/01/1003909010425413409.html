Braid: Fired election investigator says wrongdoers must be punished

20 Nov 2019, 01:42 by Don Braid, Calgary Herald

Lorne Gibson, the fired election commissioner, stuck it straight to the UCP government in his first public statement since he was fired Monday.

While saying he can’t be specific about details, Gibson defended his hot pursuit and big fines for people who violated funding laws during the UCP leadership campaign.

“Participants who seek to violate or take unfair advantage of those rules must be held accountable for actions that cross legal and ethical lines,” Gibson said in a written statement.

“After thorough and fair investigations, they must be subject to penalties appropriate to the nature of the offense.

“Fairness requires that offenders pay a price for their wrongdoing, including negative publicity.

“Effective enforcement serves as a deterrent to others contemplating illegal activities and prevents the emergence of a culture of corruption within the political and electoral process.”

That’s exactly what Gibson did for a remarkable year.

He levied $210,000 in fines to people who evaded and ignored fundraising laws, mostly in the leadership race for the party that won the election.

Now, Gibson and his entire staff will be terminated, and the function rolled into Elections Alberta.

The UCP argues that this is just one of many cost-saving measures.

We might swallow that — if only they weren’t firing the guy who’s investigating them.

The Opposition NDP responded with a pair of rare gestures that went beyond the usual grandstanding. For once, they were entirely justified.

Notley asked the lieutenant-governor to “deny assent of Bill 22” on grounds that it’s an abuse of power.

That’s as unlikely as getting the Queen to comment on Prince Andrew. But it made a serious point.

In the legislature, Notley said: “This premier’s decision to fire the election commissioner, someone who’s actively investigating the UCP fraud, is an attack on democracy, a coverup of the truth and could even constitute an obstruction of justice.”

Premier Jason Kenney didn’t have a response. He’s conveniently in Houston for the week.

Later, Notley got herself kicked out for the day by Speaker Nathan Cooper after she refused to apologize for saying government house leader Jason Nixon misled the house.

That’s a banned phrase since it suggests a politician might, gosh, lie.

Notley said Bill 22 “is a far bigger threat to this house than whether or not I said people over there misled the house.”

At that point, Cooper expelled her.

It wasn’t clear what Gibson had in store next. But if anybody has an idea, it must be people in the UCP. It was their leadership contest, after all.

Only this month, Gibson issued more big fines.

There’s also an RCMP investigation going on, remember?

In May, after the Mounties visited Justice Minister Doug Schweitzer, the prosecution service (not the minister) asked for an outside prosecutor from Ontario.

There was a lot of pressure building up in this boiler.

The UCP claims the investigation can continue and all fines will stand. The chief electoral officer “may” (not will) hire Gibson to do the same job under the new order.

If that happens, and Gibson continues investigating as before, I will be the first to admit the skepticism was misplaced.

But Gibson’s office is right now in turmoil as people ponder their futures.

Even if the service is reborn under Elections Alberta, with many of the same cast, work will be delayed for months.

If Gibson isn’t rehired by Elections Alberta, he gets six months’ severance.

And he isn’t allowed to sue.

He professes surprise and disappointment at being canned. But the most alarming part of his statement comes at the end.

His office has now received more than 800 complaints, he says.

Investigators try hard to keep up but “not all of these complaints and allegations have been dealt with.”

Really now.
