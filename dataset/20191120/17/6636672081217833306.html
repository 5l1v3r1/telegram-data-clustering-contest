Deep optical modulation surprise in thin water layer

20 Nov 2019, 17:37 by Steve Bush

Japanese researchers created an optical modulator using a thin layer of water, and got surprisingly good results.

The Tokyo University of Science team was trying to find a way to exploit the large Pockels effect discovered in water by some of the same scientists in 2007.

This effect is the linear change in refractive index with applied electric field that is exploited in commercial lithium niobate crystal modulators.

However, in water the effect only occurs in quite contrived circumstances and, until now, not in ways that could provide much modulation depth.

It only occurs at the interface between water and certain other substances, but not in bulk water.

The Pockels coefficient of this interface was high enough to excite researchers, but the thin nature of interfaces meant any resulting modulation was tiny – there was not enough thickness for even a large Pockels coefficient to do much with light passing through it.

“It is difficult to measure the electro-optic signal using water as the medium because it occurs in only a thin layer. Therefore, we wanted to find a way to extract a large signal from the medium, which would not require high-sensitivity measurements and would be easier to use,” said Professor Eiji Tokunaga, a member of both the 2007 team and group that just announced significant modulation.

In slightly more detail: the effect is seen in the capacitor-like ‘electric double layer’ (EDL, like in supercapacitors) that forms in water that is in contact with a transparent electrical conductor, when that conductor is biased against an electrode on the ‘far’ side of the water. The EDL is a few nanometres thick.

To get more modulation, an experiment was set-up to shine the light sideways through a piece of glass topped with an conductive transparent oxide, submerged in water (see diagram).

The idea was that total internal reflection would bounce the light up to the interface many times, adding to the modulation at each bounce.

However, “we were surprised to obtain a huge signal from a single reflection” according to the paper ‘Giant Pockels effect in an electrode-water interface for a “liquid” light modulator‘, which describes the work in the journal OSA Continuum.

It transpired that the thin transparent-oxide electrode, whose thickness was similar to the wavelength of visible light, served as a Fabry–Perot resonator, “and our first trial happened to nearly satisfy the conditions for coherent perfect absorption at a specific wavelength”, according to the paper. “As a result, a sharp deep absorption dip appeared in the total-reflection spectrum, and a large differential reflectance was obtained due to a shift in the resonance wavelength caused by the change in the refractive index in the interfacial layer.”

The whole modulator – the bottom rectangle is the glass with its conductive oxide top coating, the top rectangle represents a metal electrode.

In short, there was also a change in refractive index in the conductive oxide layer that worked in conjunction with the change in refractive index in the EDL.

“Since the refractive index in the transparent electrode is larger than for both water and glass – 1.33 and 1.52, respectively, the amount of light reflected at both ends increases, thereby causing an enhanced Pockels effect,” according to the University.

Said Professor Tokunaga : “Through our technique, we observed light modulation with a maximum intensity change of 50% proportional to the applied AC voltage.”
