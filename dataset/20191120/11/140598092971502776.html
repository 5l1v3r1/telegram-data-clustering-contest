Classic porcini risotto recipe

20 Nov 2019, 11:27 by Angela Hartnett

For me, porcini is one of the best risottos of all time. Any dried porcini works well; they’re not cheap, but you don’t need a whole load of them.

Any leftovers from this can be allowed to set, then you can roll portions of the risotto into small balls, coat them in breadcrumbs and fry to make arancini.

Prep time: 15 minutes | Cooking time: 30 minutes

SERVES

Four to six

INGREDIENTS

  • 50g dried porcini mushrooms
  • 1 tbsp olive oil
  • 1 small white onion, finely chopped
  • 1 garlic clove, finely chopped
  • 150g cold butter, diced
  • 300g risotto rice
  • 100ml white wine
  • 100g freshly grated Parmesan
  • 20g chopped parsley

METHOD

  1. Place the mushrooms in a bowl and pour over 450ml hot water to cover them. Leave to soak for 10 minutes to rehydrate them.
  2. To drain, lift out the mushrooms with a slotted spoon (the mushrooms will have grit in them so this gets left behind) and chop them roughly. Strain the mushroom water through a fine sieve or coffee filter into a saucepan and keep hot.
  3. Heat the oil in a pan and cook the onion and garlic until soft and transparent.
  4.  Add one teaspoon of the butter then add the rice and toast it, stirring, for three minutes.
  5.  Pour in the wine and heat until the liquid disappears, deglazing the pan, then add the chopped porcini.
  6. Make sure the mushroom liquor is hot before you start to add it to the rice. If it tastes too strong you can let it cool down with a splash of water. Add the liquid a spoonful at a time, waiting for it to disappear before adding the next lot. The risotto should take around 17 to 20 minutes to cook.
  7. The rice will lose its whiteness but should retain a slight bite when eaten.
  8. To finish the risotto, remove it from the heat and stir in the rest of the butter along with the parmesan and parsley, mixing with a wooden spoon so that it forms a creamy risotto. Serve immediately.
