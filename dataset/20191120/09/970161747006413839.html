Police sergeant 'mocked' Jewish colleague by dropping pepperoni pizza on meal

20 Nov 2019, 09:08

A police sergeant who dangled a slice of pepperoni pizza over a Jewish colleague’s meal has narrowly clung on to his job.

Sergeant Gary Jacobs, 43, said ‘whoops, I will eat the incriminating evidence’ after a piece of the meat dropped onto the junior officer’s special kosher pizza.

A disciplinary hearing at Sussex Police headquarters heard the woman, referred to only as PC A, felt ‘mocked for her faith’ following the incident.

Sgt Jacobs, based in Crawley, argued it had been a misguided joke-gone-wrong and accepted that his actions amounted to misconduct, but denied gross misconduct.

However, the panel decided his behaviour constituted the latter and warned him his career as a police officer was on the verge of ruin.

The panel chair old him he had ‘never found any case that has fallen so close’ to dismissal.

The incident occurred on March 8 when Sgt Jacobs, PC A and other colleagues were relaxing before a shift eating pizza, the hearing was told.

Amy Clarke, bringing the allegation on behalf of the police force, told the tribunal: ‘PC A had deliberately ordered a pizza that was compliant with her dietary requirements.

‘Sgt Jacobs picked up a piece of pepperoni pizza and dangled it over or near PC A’s food.

‘A piece of the pepperoni fell onto PC A’s pizza.’

That was despite him being aware of PC A’s faith and another officer having just pointed out that her kosher pizza should be kept separate, Ms Clarke said.

The panel heard Sgt Jacobs ‘waved’ his slice over PC A’s chicken pizza saying ‘technically not touching’.

When a piece of meat fell onto her meal he remarked: ‘Whoops, I will eat the incriminating evidence.’

The Jewish officer, who had only recently joined the force, was left feeling ‘embarrassed, isolated and as if she was being mocked for her faith’, Ms Clarke said.

Colin Banham, representing Sgt Jacobs, said: ‘No-one says this was a malicious act.’

Mr Banham added it was ‘an incident that he bitterly regrets and one that he is clearly remorseful about’.

‘There was no intention to contaminate food,’ he continued.

Mr Banham argued that the incident was not pre-meditated and happened in a ‘jocular atmosphere’ when officers were relaxing before a shift began.

He told the panel that Sgt Jacobs had been under various pressures in his home and work life at the time.

Trevor Bowles, who has been managing Sgt Jacobs since he changed teams following the pizza incident, said he had been impressed with his work, adding: ‘He has substantial potential if his skills are channelled in the right way.’

Panel chair Michael Salter said Sgt Jacobs’ actions were ‘serious and they caused discredit to the police service’.

Speaking to Sgt Jacobs, he said: ‘Officer, I cannot tell you how close you came to losing your job today.

‘I have done this job for a number of years now.

‘I have not found any case that has fallen so close to the boundary as yours has.’

Sgt Jacobs will receive a final written warning as a result of the hearing.
