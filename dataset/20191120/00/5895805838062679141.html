4 Tips to Surviving the Holidays As a Newlywed

How the chaos of the holiday season can wreak havoc on the newly wedded.

20 Nov 2019 by Goali Saedi Bocci Ph.D.

Goali Saedi Bocci Ph.D.

Millennial Media

It’s taken four years of holiday misery for me to be able to finally talk about it. So here it is: holidays as a newlywed can be tough. And that’s a gross understatement. Perhaps it’s the fault of those overly perfect Hallmark Christmas movies (now on their tenth anniversary of setting false hopes and expectations), or the endless Instagram #newlyweds posts.

Needless to say, when my husband and I were married four years ago, my vision of our first holiday season was drastically different than reality. Finally, after what felt like an eternity of waiting to meet my life partner, I could partake in all of those Christmas traditions I ever only saw on the silver screen. Picking out our first Christmas tree, buying Mr. and Mrs. stockings, sipping cocoa while we decorated our tree, and endless other traditions I yearned to integrate into our brand new family.

As it turns out, our parents had vastly different ideas. They had traditions, and we were simply meant to follow theirs. After a delicious breakfast at a favorite restaurant, we planned to set off to the Christmas tree farm. We were just getting ready to leave as my father-in-law called, yelling at my husband that we had to drop everything and immediately rush over to their house as my sister-in-law had decided suddenly to come down from Seattle to visit. My husband, the perpetual peacekeeper, tried to appease an angry yelling father while attending to his new bride. We dutifully wound up driving with a giant tree strapped to the roof of our car to their house. Meanwhile, at some point during a holiday visit, my mother and husband wound up in some sort of political debate (really?!) that put everyone in a deeply uncomfortable state.

As we kept trying to please each side of our family with holiday dinners, Christmas Eve tea services, and more and more events, we were feeling utterly depleted and fried. So much so that in a last ditch effort to finally make a tradition that no one could ruin, I nearly cut off a finger cutting up bakery bread (well past midnight) for a new tradition of overnight French toast to be enjoyed Christmas morning (fail).

Suffice it to say, over the last few years, my absolute favorite holiday came to quickly become my most dreaded. Disturbing what enormous influences one’s family of origin can have on something formerly so sacred. However, something else also started to happen over these years. We went to a couple of candlelight Christmas church services, I learned to a make a spot-on replica of the famous gingerbread with cream cheese frosting that rivaled any found at a national chain coffee shop. I bought us some puzzles on a whim (ahem, discount at local wholesale big box store), and we had fun completing those while watching Yule Pups. We got excited about getting the annual Notre Dame Christmas ornament (and tried to track down antiques on eBay) and watched the videos the alumni association put out showing chapels across the campus. I even perfected vegan hot cocoa that was neither too clumpy, sweet, or bitter (hint: 1 cup milk, 1 T sugar, 1 T cocoa powder, 1 T chocolate chips on medium low heat). And slowly but surely, we started making our own traditions.

Of course, we set some major boundaries with family—and there was that one email from my mother-in-law about how we were going to turn into the cold, childless couple from the National Lampoon’s Christmas Vacation. But all that aside, for the first time in four years, I’m finally a tiny bit excited (although cautiously) about the holidays. I don’t have to dread leaving with a migraine from a visit to the in-laws due to the incessant yelling that happens the entire time as everyone fights to be heard over one another. No, we have an adorable puppy now that yells at us instead, but at least he is unbelievably cuddly.

Throughout it all though, there have been some major lessons learned:

  1. Check Your Expectations: Probably the first part of my real downfall. I expected a magical Hallmark Christmas right off the bat. I expected our families would understand that as newlyweds we would want our space. Instead, it was a smother fest followed by an ego affront if we didn’t behave like dutiful children. I wish I had known it would take time to really pull off the stuff of the movies.
  2. Set Boundaries: We were naive to say the least. We didn’t think they were necessary. It seemed obvious to us at the time that family would respect our desire to have our own Christmas day and eve traditions, but that should have likely been communicated ahead of time. Sharing when we would see family and where well in advance would have helped. That said, my husband feared conflict all season long, so we kept punting the difficult conversations until the eleventh hour which only further exacerbated our anxiety.
  3. Communicate With Your Partner: There is nothing like poorly behaving parents to try to drive couples apart. Each trying to control their child and by extension their child’s partner which becomes emotionally exhausting. Staying a team is critical. Parents know our strengths and weaknesses, including exactly what buttons to push and how to manipulate us to get exactly what they want. Whether it is playing the guilt card or the “you owe us” schtick, staying united as a couple and communicating your feelings and hurts is critical.
  4. Start Small Traditions: Part of the error of my ways was expecting we’d hit up every holiday train ride, ornament display and so forth. Keep it simple! Not every meal and event has to be a huge production. Traditions take time and as a newlywed, you have an n of 1! So be patient, and savor the small moments together.

Perhaps post-wedded holiday misery is a uniquely millennial phenomenon. After all, the enmeshment between millennials and their parents is strong. But holidays need not involve resentment or hurt feelings. Newlyweds may need extra nurturing in the early stages of a budding marriage. This is important to remember on the part of parents, but also the newlyweds themselves. So for those of you in the newlywed phase of bliss, take heart if this holiday season doesn't go exactly as planned. Make some hot cocoa, and consider fast forwarding through that Hallmark film...
