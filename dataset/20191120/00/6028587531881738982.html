NYT: Google Hires Anti-Union Consulting Firm

20 Nov 2019 by David Beasley

Google has hired an anti-union consulting firm to help it manage conflicts between activist workers and management, the New York Times reports.On its website, consulting firm IRI Consultants touts helping a large health company persuade employees to avoid a union election. “Despite dedicating millions of dollars to their organizing campaigns, the unions did not gain enough support to hold an NLRB-conducted election and were not able to organize any of the employees,” the website states.

Since Google employees around the world walked out last fall to protest how the company handled sexual harassment cases, workers have continued to protest company actions and policies, including its now-disbanded ethics council and at its shareholders meeting. While Google employees have taken cues from unions in their organizing efforts and a group of Google contractors in Pittsburgh voted to unionize in September, there are currently no serious efforts for direct employees to create a union, The Times reports. However, organizers of the Google walkout suggest that some of the company's tactics in response to employee organizing were affected by its relationship with IRI. 

Google recently limited discussions on internal message boards to avoid politics or news stories, created a calendar extension to flag meetings of more than 100 people, and has restructured its weekly all-hands meetings. Many Google employees believe the calendar extension was designed to crack down on union organizing, which the company denied.

Google also denies that IRI advised on its changes to its community guidelines or any other policies. 

"We engage dozens of outside firms to provide us with their advice on a wide range of topics," Google spokesperson Jenn Kaiser said. "To suggest this particular firm had anything whatsoever to do with the recent calendar extension - or any internal policies whatsoever - is absolutely false.”

Employees discovered Google’s relationship with IRI last fall, two employees who wished to remain anonymous out of fear of relation told the Times.

IRI did not immediately respond to a request for comment.
