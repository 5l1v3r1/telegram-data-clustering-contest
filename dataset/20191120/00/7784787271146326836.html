‘Great singer, good bastard’: Meet the new Wallabies coach

20 Nov 2019, 00:06 by Sam Worthington

A scroll through the photo archives would suggest Dave Rennie is a very serious man.

Shot after shot catches the New Zealand coach with his game face on, a stern look on his face as he watches players warming up or running drills like a hawk.

He is a former schoolteacher, after all.

But away from the cameras a different picture emerges.

Good natured and deeply caring with a wicked sense of humour and will whip out a guitar in the dressing room after a good win.

“He’s a great singer, you should get him to sing you a song,” laughed former All Blacks lock Ross Filipo, who has known Rennie since he was 14.

“Very honest, he loves a laugh, he’ll sit there and have a joke.

“He’s very charismatic, he’s just a good bastard, to be honest.”

And he is also the new Wallabies coach.

The 55-year-old is currently in charge of Glasgow Warriors but with two Super Rugby titles under his belt it is time to step up to the Test arena.

So what can Australian fans expect?

Filipo — who Rennie tried to recruit to his Upper Hutt club as a teenager — also played under him for Wellington and the Chiefs, winning back-to-back titles in 2012-13.

“He played for Wellington, a handy midfielder back in his day with really silky skills,” Filipo told foxsports.com.au, before the Wallabies move was confirmed.

“That has transferred into his coaching in terms of what he tries to grow in his players — whether it be technical skills, vision, decision making — he’s a class act in that area.

“The teams he coaches have really amazing skillsets and keep the ball alive.

“Those types of skills are always on display in teams he coaches — a very positive type of rugby.

“And that’s probably a fit for Australian rugby.

“When I look back at the times I got to play Australia and in Super Rugby for a number of years, my memories are very much ruck and run with really high skillsets.

“Run, catch, pass, kicking, offloading — all those types of things were a real feature of Australian rugby for a number of years.

“So that side of things seems like a match.”

But of course it will be no cakewalk.

He is inheriting a Wallabies side ranked sixth in the world on the back of their quarterfinal humbling at the hands of England in Japan.

One could argue the only way is up and the expectation is that Rennie will usher in a youth movement and four year plan for 2023 World Cup glory in France.

Australia’s runners-up finish in this year’s under-20 world championship is no guarantee of a golden generation but it was certainly a promising sign.

Rennie becomes Australia’s second New Zealand coach after Robbie Deans, who had a 58.1% winning rate in 74 Tests in charge from 2008-13.

But Deans was never fully embraced by large sections of the Australian rugby community, who understandably would prefer one of their own guiding such a proud sporting nation.

Cheika said as much after falling on his sword while former Wallaby Greg Martin led the howls of disapproval on Fox Sports News.

“I don’t want another Kiwi coaching the national team,” Martin said.

“I’m hearing a terrible rumour Dave Rennie has already been virtually signed.

“I want an Australian to coach the Australian team, someone to bring on the Junior Wallabies who beat the Kiwis and obliterate this Cheika era of being outcoached and out-strategised.”

Filipo — now working as a coach for the Waikato Rugby Union in New Zealand — has strong insight, having been coached by both Deans and Rennie.

“He’s a great mentor and if he gets that Wallabies job he’ll be making sure that he has an impression down at grassroots level — schoolboys, club and under-20s rugby,” Filipo said.

“I’m just thinking what do Australians want right now because there’s a lot of chat on this side of the ditch around Australians calling for an Australian coach.

“Could he end up with the same fate that Robbie did?

“Robbie also coached me and is one of my favourite coaches but things didn’t necessarily match up there because he wasn’t Australian.

“I completely understand the Australian standpoint.

“I’m not saying Robbie didn’t get a fair crack because Australians are obviously hugely competitive and want to grow it from their own culture.

“So there’s always going to be that elephant sitting in the corner of the room.

“Because we do think about the game slightly differently.

“Michael Cheika’s said he’d like to see an Australian coach come in and that’s awesome — I don’t disagree with that point at all.

“It would be weird for us to have a foreigner coaching the All Blacks.

“I can completely understand that view so it’s just a question of how is this going to work and is it going to work?

“I don’t know, it could because he’s great at pulling a team together and delivering a great team culture, getting everyone going in the same direction.

“But so was Robbie, those were huge strengths of Robbie’s as well.

“So it’ll be interesting to watch.

“I’d love to see him go there and succeed.

“It’d be cool to have a really strong Australian side again.

“They’ve got the athletes to do it.”

Apart from his technical and tactical nous, Filipo said one of Rennie’s great strengths was building meaningful relationships with his players and knowing how to press each individual’s buttons.

“He makes that personal connection, he’s very much a relationships man,” Filipo said.

“He drives very high standards and that’s a good thing if you want to be winning consistently.

“He’s a very genuine man — I’ve got a lot of respect for him.

“He’s not a ranter and raver at all.
