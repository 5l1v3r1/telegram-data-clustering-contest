Man forced to sleep in mum's shed because social services fear violent past will hurt siblings

Kieron Smith has a troubled past - with a stint in jail - but his mum fears unless he can find somewhere to stay he may die

19 Nov 2019, 08:00 by Ian Johnson

A mum fears her homeless son will die this winter unless he finds somewhere to live.

Kieron Smith currently sleeps in either tent on a muddy field or his mum's garden shed.

He has a troubled past, including a spell prison and drug abuse, while he suffers with autism, ADHD and mental health issues.

Having been moved around the North East, he claims it has taken 19 years to even get a social worker and feels he isn't being given the help he needs to try and make a fresh start.

But all his heartbroken mum wants is for someone to help her son - before it is too late.

"The tent is on a big field about five minutes from our home," said mum-of-three Pamela Laanani, from Alnwick.

"Recently I hadn't seen him for a few days so I wondered up to the tent to check he was ok.

"I unzipped it and I expected to find his corpse inside.

"To my relief there wasn't, but there were empty packets of tablets scattered around as he had taken an overdose.

"My heart is breaking and I don't know what to do - I keep thinking will I bury my son."

She claims the 26-year-old is barred from sleeping at her Sycamore Avenue home by social services.

With two young children, Pamela claims officials fear Kieron - who has convictions for violence - could pose a risk to their safety.

For Kieron though, he claims he's stuck between a rock and a hard place after being released from jail.

Once able to lift over 200lbs, his weight has plummeted to nine stone.

Unable to fill in paperwork properly and with mental health issues which affect his decision making, he claims he's reached out to get the support he needs - only to be told no.

Now he fears his story is just like thousands of other men who are "tossed from pillar to post" by services, without the meaningful help they desperately need.

He said: "If you looked at me story wise that has been in the Chronicle and I'd look like a w*****.

"But meet me face to face, people say that I don't seem like that sort of person and that's because I'm not.

"I got dragged into the wrong crowd, and had issues with education. But when I ask for help, I get sent from pillar to post because of my past.

"And I've been to services who only go to refer me on to other services."

He claims he has approached charities for help curbing his cannabis use only to be told no.

He added: "It is moronic to say to someone get off the drugs first, then we can help you.

"I understand now why people go back to jail, because when you ask for the help and try and go to the right places - but the services just throw it back in their faces.

"It seems to be the only way you get to the services is to lie, when you want to be open, honest and truthful."

For his mum, his issues mean she has little choice but to leave him in the cold.

"It means I have to pick between my two youngest, who I have a duty to care for, but also my other son when my biggest worry is that he will either kill himself or freeze to death," said an emotional Pamela, 45.

"It is a horrendous choice.

"My son shouldn't have to be sleeping rough, yet he's out there in the freezing cold and rain.

"But if I get caught with him in the house, I could get in trouble but he is still my son - so what can I do?"

Since moving to Alnwick from Benwell two years ago, she claims she's pleaded with Northumberland County Council to find him a home - even filling in forms on his behalf - only to be told no.

Now with temperatures set to plunge, she has pleaded with the council for a change of heart.

A Northumberland County Council spokesperson said: "We are aware of this case and are looking at a range of options, including support from partners, to assist this man.

"Having assessed his current personal position and need, we do not believe that there is a statutory duty to provide emergency accommodation, but this will be kept under review."

For Keiron, whose convictions include aggravated burglary and having an imitation firearm, he doesn't want his history to stop him freezing this winter - or to stop him moving on with his life.

"Unfortunately with the past that I've had, it has caused some complications at home.

"I've been a little b***** in the past and as soon as there's an incident at home, it is easy for social services to say I'm the cause.

"But I'm homeless - I either live in a tent most of the time, or the odd night in my mam's shed or a friend's sofa.

"It just isn't a great situation for me."
