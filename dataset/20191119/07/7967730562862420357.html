'Could do better': End-of-year reports don't make the grade

19 Nov 2019, 07:29 by Michael Fowler

Families are braced for end-of-year reports but Australia's leading testing authority says teachers should be grading students continuously throughout the year.

The Australian Council for Educational Research says teachers should be saved from preparing avalanches of reports at the end of each semester.

Traditional report cards place undue emphasis on student achievement and neglect personal development, according to a report by the council based on three years of data.

Continuous assessment, the council argues, provides students and their families with real-time updates on progress.

Schools can adapt traditional reporting online, using portals that students, parents and teachers can access.

St Helena Secondary College in Melbourne's north-east introduced "progress tasks" – small, specific and frequent assessments – last year leading to an end of semester "assessment task".

Associate principal Daniel Stephens said teachers also began updating parents on their child's progress by uploading bi-weekly "traffic light ratings" of red, amber or green to an online portal.

"We can then ascertain: what are the appropriate intreventions a child needs? We're acting on the data. It’s one thing to assess, then it's how do you intervene," Mr Stephens said.

He said St Helena's traffic light ratings and more consistent feedback was "formalising what teachers are doing anyway" and prevented an end of semester "bottleneck".

"We just find exhaustive feedback at the end of semester isn’t as effective as during the cycle of learning that you’re in ... the world is so fast-paced it seems a bit archaic to say, 'here’s the end of semseter, here’s your report'. The world has changed and we’re trying to evolve too."

Nearly 40 per cent of 159 teachers surveyed by the council said they did not think end-of-semester reports were useful to students. More than 80 per cent said they believed continuous reporting would be useful for parents and students.

Dr Hilary Hollingsworth, one of three researchers involved in the study, said more consistent reporting could monitor when high-achieving students are "coasting" and help lower-achieving students better understand their progress.

"We’re not saying necessarily throw the semester report out, or you must do online continuous reporting ... we think current reporting practices are simply inherited from days gone by," she said.

"So now is the time to stop and have a really strong conversation to work out what is the purpose of all the things we do - online reporting, written reporting, portfolios and parent-teacher interviews."
