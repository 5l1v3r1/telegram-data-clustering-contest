Major supermarkets to ban black plastic by end of 2019

Tesco, Sainsbury’s, Waitrose and Asda are following in Morrison's footsteps by banning black plastic.

19 Nov 2019, 10:42 by Sarah McGee

Four leading supermarkets are trying to do their bit for the environment and will stop using black plastic in stores by the end of 2019, according to a report by The Grocer.

Morrisons was the first supermarket to scrap black plastic packaging , as it is difficult to recycle and usually ends up in landfill. Black plastic also contains a carbon pigment that makes it hard for the recycling machines to detect, meaning it's often improperly sorted.

Black plastic is usually found in food packaging, such as sausage and ready meal trays.

Iceland and Aldi are also working on their eco-friendly business plan and intend to ditch plastic packaging in their stores by the end of 2020.

Tesco also said it's working with branded suppliers about the plastic problem to see if they're willing to use more eco-friendly packaging.

Asda has already placed the trays on their ready meals to make them 100% recyclable, swapping the plastic trays on their Extra Special Aberdeen Angus steaks for recyclable cardboard trays, which will save more that 50 tonnes of plastic each year.

Co-op is also confident about removing the black packaging by the end of the year, hoping to eradicate it from all 300 lines.

By the end of December, Sainsbury's also aim to completely eradicate the packaging from stores and more than 6000 tonnes of plastic material will be swapped for recyclable alternatives.

Waitrose said they're on track to eradicate black plastic packaging by the new year as they had removed nearly 90% of the 2291 tonnes of black packaging the chain uses by the end of October.

Stuart Lendrum, head of recycling at Iceland said: "we have been making good progress with eliminating black plastic across the board," hoping to remove black plastic packaging in stores by the end of January 2020.

Marks & Spencer and Aldi are further behind on their sustainability targets, but both said they're on track to remove black plastic by the end of 2020.
