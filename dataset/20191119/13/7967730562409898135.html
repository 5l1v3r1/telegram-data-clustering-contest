Richter family property portfolio fetches more than $25m

19 Nov 2019, 13:00 by Simon Johanson and Nicole Lindsay

A portfolio of apartment blocks held by family interests associated with high-profile criminal lawyer Robert Richter, QC, has sold for more than $25 million.

The seven apartment blocks around Melbourne and a commercial property in Northcote – owned by Mr Richter’s mother, other family members and associates – were auctioned over three days last week to individual investors and syndicates.

The stand-alone apartment buildings in Ascot Vale, Essendon, Fawkner, Glenroy, Box Hill and Chadstone and a prominent warehouse on the crest of Rucker’s Hill in Northcote leased to Danish secondhand furniture firm Grandfather’s Axe, fetched a combined $25,311,000.

Nearly 50 people gathered in Box Hill for the auctions of 8 James Street and 499 Station Street – although at least 10 of the crowd were members of the family selling the properties.

When the bidding for 8 James Street reached $6.66 million, a nervous giggle rippled through the crowd, proving even Europeans have numerological superstitions.

However, the buyer held no superstitions about the number 666 and auctioneer Paul Tzamalis' hammer came down at that point.

The auction for the smaller Station Street property followed and took 112 bids, most of them for $1000 increments, to sell for $1.701 million.

The large warehouse occupied by Grandfather’s Axe changed hands for $3,215,000 on 4 per cent yield.

The property is zoned industrial 3, an anomaly as neighbouring properties in the area are covered by a commercial 1 zone which allows a greater variety of uses.

Vinci Carbone selling agent Stephen Speck said the buyer purchased it as a “long-term play” and would look to get the property rezoned in the future.

The apartment buildings attracted a diverse range of bidders from real estate agents, to builders and syndicates, and averaged a yield of 3.6 per cent on gross rent, Mr Speck said.

“Everybody wanted income-generating assets,” he said.

The properties were amassed by Mr Richter’s father and uncle, along with another business partner, who constructed the apartments over a decade between 1976 and 1986 and had since been held by various family members and associates under different individual or company structures.
