When was the last General Election and why was it called?

19 Nov 2019, 13:46

As the general election looms, political parties’ campaigns are ramping up in the hopes of winning the votes of people across the country.

Prime Minister Boris Johnson Labour leader Jeremy Corbyn are set to go head to head in a series of leadership debates in the run up to the 12 December poll.

It will mark the first winter vote the country has seen since 1923, when Conservative Prime Minister Stanley Baldwin sought to strengthen his party leadership.

But it’s also the third election we’ve had in four years – so just when was the last one?

Here’s what you need to know…

When was the last General Election?

The last general election was held on Thursday 8 June 2017.

Then-Prime Minister Theresa May – who had a working majority of just 17 – went head to head against Labour leader Jeremy Corbyn to retain control of the country.

First Minister Nicola Sturgeon, Lib Dems’ Tim Farron and Green Party’s Caroline Lucas and Jonathan Bartley were among other party leaders to enter the leadership race.

Why was it called?

The election was called just two months prior to Mrs May repeatedly promising not to call an early election.

As per the Fixed-Term Parliament’s Act, the next general election was not scheduled until 2020.

However, in the midst of Brexit referendum the previous year, Mrs May claimed divisions within Parliament were hindering negotiations with the European Union as she called for unity in Westminster.

In a statement outside Downing Street, she said: ‘The country is coming together, but Westminster is not.

‘In recent weeks Labour has threatened to vote against the deal we reach with the European Union.

‘The Liberal Democrats have said they want to grind the business of government to a standsill.

‘The Scottish National Party say they will vote against the legislation that formally repeals Britain’s membership of the European Union.’

She continued: ‘So we need a General Election and we need one now, because we have at this moment a one-off chance to get this done while the European Union agrees its negotiating position and before the detailed talks begin.

‘I have only recently and reluctantly come to this conclusion.’

Who won the 2017 General Election?

The 2017 General Election was won by the Conservative Party however Ms May lost her majority, having required the support of two-thirds of Parliament to continue her leadership.

In an effort to prop up her minority government, she controversially agreed to a Confidence and Supply Agreement with the Democratic Unionist Party of Northern Ireland.

The election saw the Conservative lose 13 seats with 42.4% of the vote while Labour gained 30 seats with 40.1%.

The SNP, which had won 56 of the 59 Scottish seats at the previous general election, lost 21.

The Lib Dems gained three seats while the Green Party retained its only seat.

UKIP – the third largest party in the 2015 election and arguably instrumental to the result of the referendum – lost its only seat.
