Top judge says legal challenge against Boris Johnson’s Brexit deal to be resurrected in Scotland’s highest civil court

19 Nov 2019, 12:40 by James Mulholland

A TOP judge has given permission for a legal challenge against Boris Johnson’s Brexit deal to be resurrected in Scotland’s highest civil court.

Lord Boyd of Duncansby gave the go-ahead to a case which has been brought by leading remain campaigner Jo Maugham QC to be heard in the Court of Session in Edinburgh.

The English barrister wants a Scottish judge to pass an order which would suspend the withdrawal protocol which was negotiated earlier this year by the Prime Minister.

Mr Maugham believes that aspects of the withdrawal agreement are unlawful.

He believes that plans to have Northern Ireland follow EU customs laws whilst remaining in the UK’s customs territory isn’t lawful.

The lawyer believes this is the case because of a piece of legislation which was passed in 2018 called the Taxation (Cross Border Act).

Leading Brexiteer Jacob Rees Mogg and his supporters in the European Research Group passed an amendment to the legislation when it was going through Parliament.

The amendment - which forms section 55 of the legislation - dictates that Northern Ireland cannot be part of any political arrangement in which it follows rules and regulations which differ from the ones used in other parts of the UK.

During a hearing at the Court of Session last month, Lord Pentland rejected the claims advanced by Mr Maugham’s legal team.

He said: “There is nothing to show that this will not play out as intended and work in a satisfactory manner.”

However, Mr Maugham’s legal team have brought the case back to court.

Mr Maugham is a director of the Good Law Project.

The organisation has commissioned reports from trade experts which Mr Maugham says supports the views which were advanced at the October hearing.

Now Lord Boyd has given permission for Mr Maugham’s lawyers to adjust their arguments in the case.

They have until December 20 for them to prepare their new case.

Lord Boyd added: “I will allow for a one day hearing to take place in the case no later than January 10 next year.”
