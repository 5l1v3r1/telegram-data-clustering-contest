Black history shouldn't be erased in order to call out Labour's antisemitism

22 Nov 2019, 16:34 by Nadine Batchelor-Hunt

Labour’s failure to deal with antisemitism in the party properly and efficiently over the last few years has rightfully gathered anger from the British Jewish community.

The pain it has caused, and some outstanding issues on the matter, continue to make this problem an explosive one to discuss.

However, as a black Jew who occupies a unique space in this debate, I began to notice a disturbing trend of people entering the ‘antisemitism in Labour’ debate by drawing false parallels with discrimination to black people – with Rachel Riley’s t-shirt now becoming a watershed moment.

The t-shirt, in which she photoshopped Jeremy Corbyn’s ‘Defend the right to demonstrate against apartheid’ message to ‘Jeremy Corbyn is a racist endeavour,’ is a manifestation of how severe the practice of co-opting black suffering has become.

Erasing a message about protesting South African apartheid and replacing it with one that suggests similarities between Corbyn’s Labour and apartheid shows little to no understanding of anti-black racism – and verges on becoming racist in itself.

This isn’t the first time Riley has done something like this; she compared a brass-band playing ‘Hava Nagila’ at the Durham Miners’ Gala to ‘As tasteful as showing Black Panther at a Klan rally’.

Whatever your opinions on Corbyn or Labour, I think it’s pretty clear to anyone with a basic understanding of racism that suggesting either are like South African apartheid or the Ku Klux Klan (which used to lynch and burn black people alive) is beyond disrespectful and trivialises and devalues black lives.

This is not just something Riley is guilty of but it is part of a broader trend; prominent figures in fighting antisemitism in Labour have also fallen into the same behaviour.

Chief rabbi Lord Jonathan Sacks has compared Corbyn’s comments to Enoch Powell’s ‘rivers of blood’ speech – something echoed during a Telegraph video yesterday on antisemitism in Labour.
