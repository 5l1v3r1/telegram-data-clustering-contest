Police force send out 'welfare van' to crime scenes so officers can take a break on long jobs

22 Nov 2019, 16:08 by Phoebe Southworth

Police are sending out a special 'welfare van' to crime scenes so officers can have a cup of tea and keep warm and dry during long jobs.

Hampshire Police Federation bought the transit van three months ago and it has been used dozens of times in the last eight weeks.

On board is a toilet, hot drink facilities, a table and a microwave to rustle up some snacks. It was recently deployed to Portsmouth and Southampton.

The move is part of a push to boost morale in the force, after a recent UK-wide survey by the Police Federation revealed that 67 per cent of respondents would not recommend joining the police and 87 per cent felt morale within the job was low.

Large sprinter vans, coined 'wellbeing wagons', are also being used by Hampshire Constabulary to help keep officers in peak condition.

They are parked up at police stations across the county and offer services ranging from blood pressure and cholesterol checks to sleep and diet advice.

The force are also offering their staff psychological screenings to ensure they aren't suffering in silence and to root out those who need additional support. 

It is the first time this service has been offered to all frontline staff and officers in the UK, rather than only those deemed at high risk of suffering trauma - such as firearms officers and child sex abuse investigators.

Employees are asked to complete an online questionnaire every two years and any issues flagged will then be fleshed out in a face-to-face interview.

In the six months since the scheme was introduced, some 600 officers have been assessed.

Deputy Chief Constable Sara Glen said her employees must not be regarded as "cannon fodder" and it must be recognised that they work in a "high trauma environment".

She added: "They need to be protected. That's why we've needed to get far more proactive, so if we are showing signs of burnout we can get services in much quicker."

The measure was introduced after a former officer revealed that his Post Traumatic Stress Disorder led to him having to leave the police.

Keith Waller dealt with horrific road accidents, violence and suicides throughout his 19-year-career.  He was admitted to hospital twice after suffering episodes of PTSD, which led to him retiring in 2013, aged 54.

The 60-year-old, from Bournemouth, said: "We need to talk about it. If you don't talk about it you end up in a situation like I was. I was no longer able to police. The screening can only be a good thing."

The chairman of Hampshire Police Federation, Alex Charge, said: "It's really important we change that culture and we change that stigma. And people recognise, to use a cliche, that it's ok to not be ok.

"The reality is all of our officers are in high risk roles. Every one of them will see horrible things.

"It's so important that other police forces look at what Hampshire are doing so we can make sure we look after the people who look after our communities."
