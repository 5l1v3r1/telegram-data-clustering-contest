Plymouth dad diagnosed with MND at 24 to marry love of his life this weekend

Jon Adams was diagnosed with Motor Neurone disease at the age of just 24

22 Nov 2019, 11:24 by Jess Morcom

A young Plymouth dad who was diagnosed with Motor Neurone disease at the age of just 24 is set to marry this weekend - after the generosity of local businesses helped to make the day happen.

In January, Plymouth Live told of how Jon Adams was diagnosed with the terminal illness after experiencing symptoms at just 22.

Since then, he has become a dad to baby Arthur with his girlfriend Holly, and the pair are just days away from tying the knot.

"From the bottom of my heart all I can say is thank you to everyone that has helped out with the wedding, no matter how big or small," Jon said.

"People's kindness and generosity shows that not only do they support you but they truly care and it's so heartwarming to see."

Jon's mum Alison also wishes to thank everyone that has contributed to the day after a particularly tough year for the family.

"Since people heard about Jon's story, the kindness they been shown is overwhelming," she said.

"We just want to thank all of the wonderful companies that have been so generous to ensure Jon and Holly's day will be the best, without them it would not be possible.

Earlier this year, Jon's sister Abbie set up a JustGiving page to ensure her brother was able to complete a bucket list while he's still fit enough to do so, and the family have plans to donate the other half of the funds to the MND Association Plymouth.

Jon is one of about 5,000 people living in the UK with Motor Neurone Disease, which affects the neurons in the brain and spinal cord.

Unfortunately there is no cure for the devastating illness and sufferers can go from fit and healthy to tetraplegic within a matter of months.

What is Motor Neurone Disease?

Motor neurone disease (MND) is a uncommon condition that affects the brain and nerves. It causes weakness that gets worse over time.

It's caused by a problem with cells in the brain and nerves called motor neurones. These cells gradually stop working over time. It's not known why this happens.

Moving around, swallowing and breathing get increasingly difficult, and treatments like a feeding tube or breathing air through a face mask may be needed.

The condition is eventually fatal, but how long it takes to reach this stage varies a lot. A few people live for many years or even decades with motor neurone disease.

"Since my diagnosis, I have no had to give up work permanently due to the condition," Jon said.

"The mobility in my arms has become quite bad to the point where there is not much strength in them and daily tasks become very difficult for me.

"My walking has also got worse but I am determined to stay on my feet for as long as possible, only using a wheelchair when needed.

"My speech has slightly changed as the muscles inside of inside of my mouth are not working as well so I have a bit of a slur, but trying to look at the positives, my respiratory is all in perfect condition as said by the doctors.

"As for my mental state, I have good days and bad days but I remain as positive as I can be as I am now a father and my little boy keeps me going as well as my beautiful wife to be." 

Plymouth Live wishes Jon and Holly all the best on their wedding day.
