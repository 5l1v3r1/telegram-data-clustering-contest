Controversial housing plans for Track 2000 site in Grangetown, Cardiff pulled by Rightacres

Local residents were vocal about their concerns with the plans from the property developer

22 Nov 2019, 11:22 by Ruth Mosalski

The site of a controversial planning scheme has been sold by developer Rightacres.

Rightacres had bought the former Track 2000 site in Grangetown and planned a development of flats.

The developer, which is behind the Central Square scheme in Cardiff, had wanted to build flats up to six storeys high.

The disused building was knocked down and the land has been empty since.

It had planned a building, which was to be known as the Bottle Works, which would have contained 74 flats.

It would have contained two, one-bed studios, 39 one-bed flats, 32 two-bed flats and one three-bed flat.

There would have been 57 parking spaces and 72 bike spots.

However, Rightacres has confirmed it has sold the land to an unnamed housing association.

Paul McCarthy, Rightacres Chief Executive said: "We acquired the site with the intention to build a much needed residential scheme.

"Rightacres always take into account the sensitivities of the local community and having received an offer from a housing association we felt that affordable housing for local people was the most appropriate solution for this site."

The Rightacres scheme was unpopular with local residents.

Local residents feared a loss of privacy and worsening problems with traffic if it went ahead.

There were concerns they would pay only £30,000 out of the £1.3m that the council had asked for, under what is known as the Section 106 agreement.

Cardiff council had originally asked for £1,361,363 from the developer - £1,157,912 would have gone towards affordable housing while the rest would have gone towards public open space, community facilities and jobs in Grangetown, as well as traffic regulations.

But an independent review said the development "would be rendered unviable" if any contributions were sought by Rightacres.

However, as planning permission has been granted by Cardiff council, it will remain valid for the standard five years.

It will be up to the new owner whether to continue with the plans which were given permission by Cardiff council's planning committee.

The three Labour ward councillors, Ashley Lister, Lynda Thorne and Abdul Sattar issued a statement in response.

"As ward councillors we have opposed the development of the Track 2000 site on a number of factors from traffic and waste issues, to loss of light.

"We’re now aware that the site has been sold to a housing association to develop and provide much needed social housing in the area.

"Every week we hear from residents living in poor quality conditions who are desperate to move into a good standard, affordable home.

"Whilst we welcome the move towards social housing, we will continue to raise our longstanding concerns with the new land owner."
