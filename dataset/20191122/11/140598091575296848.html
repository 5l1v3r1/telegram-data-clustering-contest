Seven parishioners bear responsibility for keeping 1,000-year-old village church alive

22 Nov 2019, 11:25 by Gabriella Swerling

Seven parishioners are bearing the responsibility for keeping an almost 1,000-year-old village church open by raising donations out of their own pockets.

The latest Church of England data shows that church attendances have fallen steadily across Britain over the last decade, to just 1.18million regular churchgoers last year.

Such a decline puts churches representing small communities, such as All Saints' Church, in Graveney, Kent - which has a population of 490 - at risk of closure. 

The 900-year-old Grade-I listed village church is facing having its doors shut closed for good because it has just seven regular parishioners. 

The Vicar Jean Burrows says this is a concerning trend reflected in rural parishes across the country.

Parishioners say that unless the number of regular churchgoers increases, or a new funding source opens up, the centuries-old church will have to close.

It hosts weddings, funerals, baptisms, christenings and all sorts of non-Christian events - such as helping rough sleepers and raising money for charity.

However, just to keep the building open and pay the vicar, parishioners are having to fork out £40 a week.

Janet Turner, 75, Parochial Church Council secretary, said that if this carries on, the church could  be forced to close in less than two years.

"This is a building that has been part of the life of the village for 900 years,” she said. “What happens if you let it go - people may regret it."

She explained that regular attendees at All Saints' Church had dropped to around seven in recent years.

"Families tend not come to church on Sundays any more, they do other things," she said.

"But sadly many very beautiful, historic buildings, which have served communities for hundreds of years are in danger of closing.

"Our church is not only there for worship, but we also raise funds for charity and give people a place to come and talk if they need to.

"We are trying to innovate and make it place for everyone - worshipers and non-worshipers. We want to stay alive, and it would be nice to have more people come and join us."

The church was built in the 12th century, but a prayer building had existed on the site since the early 800s. The box pews are also Grade I listed.

Ms Turner says the light which filters through the stained glass windows from the marshes gives the inside of the church a beautiful hue.

Vicar Jean, who covers three churches, said: "They are a small congregation, but they are very committed to keeping the church open.

"We have lots of fundraising events for the building and fundraising events for the congregation."

She added that the risk of village churches closing was an issue across the whole country.

"There are hundreds of churches like ours," the vicar explained. It is concerning because the church is part of the village.

"People come for baptisms, weddings and funerals - it's always there.But the time will come when it may not be there, if the locals don't support it."

A spokesperson for the Diocese of Canterbury said, “All Saints’ is a beautiful church with a small, but committed, congregation and there are no current plans to close it. 

"However, it is clear that many churches across the country are facing the challenge of sustainability, especially those with older buildings or smaller congregations. 

"We continue to work with our churches to rise to this challenge, seeking creative ways to reimagine worship, to make the best use of our buildings and to serve our communities.”
