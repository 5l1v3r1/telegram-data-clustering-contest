NASA finds astronaut blood moving backwards on ISS – and bizarre phenomenon could be deadly

22 Nov 2019, 14:00

NASA scientists have been shocked by the 'extremely abnormal' medical test results returned by astronauts on the International Space Station.

NASA has observed a bizarre effect of human spaceflight: blood going backwards in astronauts' veins.

The strange phenomenon also resulted in blood clotting for one astronaut, sparking a panic among NASA doctors, The Sun reports.

An unnamed astronaut on the International Space Station was carrying out an ultrasound on their body – guided by experts on the ground.

Similar tests before the astronaut went to space came back normal.

But the scan taken in space revealed a clump of clotted blood, shocking NASA doctors.

"We were not expecting this. This has never been reported before," said NASA scientist Karina Marshall-Goebel, speaking to The Atlantic.

The astronaut didn't have any clot-related symptoms, but was given blood-thinning drugs for the rest of their time in orbit.

This clotting was declared a new risk for humans in spaceflight – but the mysterious phenomenon didn't stop there.

Nasa researchers observed the jugular veins of 11 astronauts on the ISS using ultrasounds.

And in five of those 11 astronauts, blood flow in the jugular vein had stalled.

"Sometimes it was sloshing back and forth a bit, but there was no net-forward movement," Ms Marshall-Goebel explained.

The jugular vein is one of the most important parts of the body.

It runs between the heart and the head, draining deoxygenated blood from the brain.

This "draining" process is essential to reduce pressure in the brain.

Stalled blood flow in veins is really rare, and is typically only seen in legs – often after long plane flights.

Stagnant blood is a major concern because it can lead to clotting, which can cause more serious problems like damage to the lungs. Serious clotting can be fatal.

Even more bizarrely, the blood in the jugular vein began moving in the opposite direction (from the heart towards the head) for two astronauts.

NASA scientists described this as "extremely abnormal", and said that the blood may have switched directions due to a blockage.

A similar phenomenon has been observed on Earth for patients with tumours that force blood to find a different route to the heart.

"It's almost like a detour," explained Ms Marshall-Goebel.

"When you're in your car and you sometimes have to go down the wrong street to get to where you need to go."

This strange condition vanished when the astronauts returned to Earth.

"None of the crew members actually had any negative clinical outcomes. I think it was probably scary for everybody.

"But I think the fact that we found this now is really, really good news, because if you know this is a risk factor of spaceflight, it's something that you can monitor and prevent," concluded Ms Marshall-Goebel.

A NASA spokesperson said the ISS "is equipped with appropriate treatments in the medical kit available to crew members".
