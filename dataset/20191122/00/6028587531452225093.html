No, ‘Cats’ Didn’t Cost $300 Million

22 Nov 2019 by Scott Mendelson

There was a post in The Guardian the other day, timed to the release of the second Cats trailer, which suggested that the film cost £230 million which equates to $297 million U.S. dollars. If accurate, that would be one of the most expensive movies of all time, but it’s (all due respect) not accurate. I’ve spoken to people I know and trust, and, as I noted back in early October, the Tom Hooper-directed adaptation of Andrew Lloyd Weber's stage play cost around $100 million. Look, just watching the trailers: Does Cats look like a movie that cost $300 million? Moreover, does Universal resemble a studio that would spend $300 million not on Jurassic World 3 or Fast & Furious 9, but on Cats?There was a jump-covered interview with director B.A. Bayona three years ago which implied that Jurassic World: Fallen Kingdom cost $260 million. While it was debunked in an hour or so, it didn’t sound that insane. Sure, that would be a big step up from Jurassic World’s $150 million budget, but the last dino adventure had earned a stunning $652 million domestic and $1.671 billion worldwide. For example, after The Dark Knight earned $1.004 billion worldwide on a $180 million budget, Warner Bros. and friends gave Chris Nolan $250 million for The Dark Knight Rises. I’m not sure any movie at that price would be a bargain, but there was zero chance of Chris Nolan’s trilogy capper not recouping its investment. It earned $448 million domestic and $1.081 billion.

Cats is a live-action musical from Universal, a studio whose three $200 million-plus films have been Furious 7 ($250 million, partially due to insurance-covered expenses related to the death of Paul Walker), Fate of the Furious ($240 million after Furious 7 earned $1.515 billion) and Hobbs & Shaw ($200 million). They have turned Illumination into an animation powerhouse without a single toon costing more than $80 million. They have drowned in profits from cheap (or cheap-ish) movies from the likes of Jason Blum (Get Out) and Will Packer (Ride Along), while even their big swings tend to be closer to Skyscraper ($125 million) and The Mummy ($125 million) than Warcraft ($165 million) and Doctor Dolittle (coming next January at the low, low price of around $175 million).

Even those Jurassic World movies have been surprisingly “responsible. Jurassic World cost $150 million in 2015 while Fallen Kingdom cost $170 million (including pay bumps for Chris Pratt and Bryce Dallas Howard) in 2018. I have no idea what Jurassic World 3 will cost when it opens in June of 2021, but if Universal and friends want to throw $250 million at it, well, that (and Fast & Furious 10) would probably be the one to justify such an expense. Universal’s Les Miserables, also directed by Tom Hooper, earned $441 million worldwide on a $61 million budget in 2012. You really think Universal is going to then give him so much money for a Cats movie that Les Miz numbers would be a disaster?

At $300 million, grosses equal to Mary Poppins Returns ($349 million worldwide on a $130 million budget), The Greatest Showman ($435 million/$84 million), La La Land ($441 million/$50 million) and Mamma Mia! Here We Go Again! ($395 million/$50 million) would qualify as a disaster. The only live-action musicals, sans inflation, to reach even $600 million were Universal’s Mamma Mia! ($609 million on a $40 million budget), Aladdin ($1.05 billion/$184 million), Beauty and the Beast ($1.263 billion/$175 million) and The Lion King ($1.65 billion/$165 million). Not only would Cats at that budget be Universal’s most expensive movie by 24%, it would be the most expensive musical ever by a lot and would need to be the biggest non-Disney live-action musical of all time just to break even.

Nothing about Cats costing almost double what The Lion King did, or more than five times what Les Miserables did, makes any sense. It doesn’t make sense for the studio and their “much expense spared” production history, it doesn’t make sense for the box office history of the live-action musical and it doesn’t make sense that Cats would be the biggest-budgeted non-sequel (sorry Avatar) in Hollywood history. Yet the Cats figure was paraded around mostly so we could point and laugh at how much silly Hollywood spent on their silly cat movie. I don’t know how well Cats is going to perform when it opens next month. But it’ll be aiming for Mamma Mia! Here We Go Again numbers, not Beauty and the Beast grosses.
