ENDO HELL

BBC Scotland journalist Fiona Stalker says endometriosis pain was like ‘having searing hot knife dragged across stomach’

22 Nov 2019, 15:14 by Jennifer Hale

A SCOTTISH BBC journalist has said her period felt like a ‘searing hot knife was being dragged across her stomach’.

Fiona Stalker, 48, suffered from agonising pain for 15 years before being diagnosed in her 30s with endometriosis - a chronic gynaecological condition that affects one in 10 UK women.

Endometriosis is caused when cells like the ones in the lining of the womb are found elsewhere in the body.

But instead of leaving the body as a period the blood has no way to escape leading to inflammation, pain and formation of scar tissue - which can eventually cause infertility.

Fiona, who co-presents BBC Scotland review show Seven Days, told The Scottish Sun: "I just assumed it was normal to be in excruciating pain until I found out a friend of mine was finally diagnosed with endometriosis.

“The only way to describe it is as if having a searing hot knife being dragged across the stomach.

“For so many women they just think that's how it is.”

The busy mum-of-two, who lives in Aberdeen, says initially she was ‘angry with her body’ and used to deliberately plan her holidays around her period to minimise the disruption to her working life.

She added: “I remember being quite angry with my body when I was diagnosed - like ‘why are you giving me this pain every month?’

“Looking back I occasionally used to try and take a day’s holiday when I knew it would be really awful.

“I would stay at home with hot bottles and painkillers and sometimes just lie in a hot bath for “as long as I could.

“I remember having to go out in the middle of the night to find a 24 hour supermarket to find painkillers because if not I was not going to find a wink of sleep.”

Fiona spoke out about her experience on BBC Scotland’s The Nine after more than 13,500 women took part in a BBC survey last month.

The investigation found that millions of women with the hidden condition are plagued with suicidal thoughts and rely on highly addictive painkillers.

It sparked a conversation around the hidden condition which saw MPs promise to launch an enquiry.

She said: “I’ve always been quite open about it at work with my colleagues so I decided I was going to talk about it whether they liked it or not!

“When I saw the stuff on the BBC it was quite emotional as it was the first time I’d seen people really publicly talking about endo.

“I think what really brought home to me in the BBC coverage was how physically and mentally debilitating it can be because you live with extreme pain.

“For it not to be about someone famous for it to be about normal women struggling on a daily basis was incredible to see.”

What are the symptoms of endometriosis?

There are many different symptoms of endometriosis and they can vary in intensity. Some women with endometriosis will have no symptoms at all.

The most common symptoms are:

  • Painful periods
  • Painful sex
  • Infertility
  • Painful bowel movements
  • Fatigue

Other symptoms include:

  • Lethargy
  • Nausea
  • Extreme tiredness
  • Depression
  • Frequent infections such as thrush
  • Feeling faint/fainting during periods
