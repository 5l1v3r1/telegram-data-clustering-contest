Jeremy Corbyn reveals he'll remain 'neutral' in second Brexit referendum

22 Nov 2019, 19:53

Jeremy Corbyn says he will adopt a ‘neutral stance’ in a second Brexit referendum.

The Labour leader was the first to be grilled by voters during a special episode of BBC’s Question Time.

Mr Corbyn was quizzed over fears for businesses, anti-Semitism, misogyny, freedom of speech and his support of ousted Bolivia president Evo.

When asked how he would campaign if he wins next month’s election, he said he would start by negotiating a ‘credible’ Leave deal before he was interrupted by groans from sections of the audience.

However, making his clearest comments to date on the issue, he continued: ‘My role and the role of our government will be to ensure that that referendum is held in a fair atmosphere and we will abide by the result of it.

‘And I will adopt, as prime minister, if I am at the time, a neutral stance so that I can credibly carry out the results of that to bring communities and country together rather than continuing an endless debate about the EU and Brexit.’

Mr Corbyn began his slot on the defensive, asked whether businesses ‘should be frightened of an incoming Labour government’.

But he argued only the ‘biggest businesses will be asked to pay a little more’ in corporation tax, but added the level will be lower than that in 2010.

He also fended off an angry audience member who declared ‘freedom would be completely eroded’ if he got the keys to Number 10.

‘I have spent my life getting into hot water for defending people because I believed their human rights should not be violated and that’s the kind of government that I intend to lead,’ Mr Corbyn said.

Another confronted him about sexism and anti-Semitism faced by his MPs – in particular Ruth Smeeth, who is Jewish – saying he does not ‘buy this nice old grandpa’ image.

‘Nobody should suffer any abuse in public life or privately,’ he replied.

‘Many women Labour MPs, Ruth Smeeth included and others, have suffered the most unbelievable levels of abuse.’

He then reminded the audience ‘it was a Labour MP, Jo Cox, who was murdered because she stood up in public life’.

‘I simply say to you that bad behaviour, misogynism, racism in any form is absolutely not acceptable in any form whatsoever in my party or in society,’ Mr Corbyn added.
