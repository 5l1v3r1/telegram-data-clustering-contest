Dog years are a myth and puppies are 'middle aged' by the time they are two, new study suggests

22 Nov 2019, 20:32 by Telegraph Reporters

Dog years are a myth and puppies are actually middle-aged by the time they are two, a new study suggests. 

One year for a dog is often said to be equivalent to around seven human years, but new research has suggested they are closer to a 50-year-old human by the age of three. 

A study at the University of California examined how dogs matured by focusing on a natural phenomenon called DNA methylation in more than 100 labradors, aged between 14 weeks and 16 years.

The DNA contains marks which change over time as a cell matures, allowing scientists to track the biological age of a species. 

The findings were then compared to around 300 humans - revealing that young dogs with boundless energy may be considerably older than they appear. 

By the age of two, the DNA of the labrador was roughly equivalent to a human entering their early forties, as opposed to a 14-year-old, which the traditional formula would suggest. 

Ageing slows in dogs over time, however, meaning that, by the age of 10, a labrador is similar to a person aged 68.

The previous formula for calculating the age of the dog was apparently developed on the assumption that most canines lived until around 10, while humans lived until around 70.

The US researchers developed a new method to work out the age of a dog - but unfortunately without the charming simplicity of its predecessor.

Multiplying the natural logarithm of a dog's age in calendar years by 16 and adding 31 will show its equivalent age in human years, the study said.

“The expected lifespan of labrador retrievers, 12 years, correctly translated to the worldwide lifetime expectancy of humans, 70 years,” the researchers said.

There were also similarities in the ageing process between humans and dogs, the study said, with eight-week-old puppies resembling nine-month-old babies. 

The research, posted on the biorxiv.org website, is yet to be peer-reviewed.
