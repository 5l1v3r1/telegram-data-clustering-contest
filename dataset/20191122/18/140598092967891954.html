Glasgow health  board placed into 'special measures' after child deaths

22 Nov 2019, 18:17 by Auslan Cramb

Scotland’s biggest health board has been placed into “special measures” following the deaths of two children in cancer wards at a scandal-hit hospital where the water was contaminated.

An oversight board will be put in place by the Scottish Government to effectively run NHS Greater Glasgow and Clyde, and the £840 Queen Elizabeth University Hospital (QEUH), which only opened in 2015.

The move was announced on Friday by the under-fire Health Minister, Jeane Freeman, who earlier this week apologised to the parents of the two youngsters.

They were being treated at the Royal Hospital for Children (RHC), which is part of the so-called “super-hospital” campus.

Ms Freeman also revealed this week that she had received emails from four families at the weekend over the problems, while a professor she has appointed to help resolve the issue "has had much more than that.”

In a letter to Holyrood's health and sport committee, she said the board would be moved to stage four of a five-stage scale that is used to show the level of oversight for stricken health boards.

NHS Greater Glasgow and Clyde now joins NHS Tayside, Highland and Borders at stage four.

In her letter, Ms Freeman said: "The intention of the escalation would ensure appropriate governance is in place to increase public confidence and strengthen current approaches that are in place to mitigate avoidable harms.”

Miles Briggs, the Tory health spokesman, said more than a third of mainland health boards were now at the second highest stage of escalation, adding: “What more proof could we need that this SNP government is utterly unfit to run the health service?”

The move was announced as the mother of a three-year-old boy who died said her family had been treated unfairly and ignored.

Mason Djemat, who was being treated for a rare genetic disease, died in August 2017 at the RHC, weeks before 10-year-old Milly Main, who was recovering from leukaemia treatment.

Mason's mother, Victoria Freeman, said she was still seeking answers over the death of the son she "absolutely adored".

She told BBC Scotland: "There was no-one listening to me. No-one wanted to help. No-one at all.”

Ms Freeman said she first wrote to the health minister last September and was disappointed by the response.

She added: "I don't think that Mason was acknowledged, particularly by her, and I feel that she did not take Mason's death seriously."

She also said she got no response from the board until she went to the hospital and demanded to speak to somebody.

Kimberly Darroch has already said she is 100 per cent certain that the death of her daughter Milly was linked to the water issue.

A whistle-blower from the hospital came forward earlier this month, telling the Labour MSP Anas Sarwar there had been 26 cases of infection, with Milly being among those hit. 

Mr Sarwar said the minister had taken the right course of action, adding: “The Glasgow health board is not fit for purpose and this is a necessary step following the unforgivable failings of senior management.

“The focus now must be to tell parents, patients and the public the truth about infections at the hospital.”

A spokesman for the health board said Mason’s case was fully investigated and the outcome shared with the family.

The oversight board will be chaired by Prof Fiona McQueen, the chief nursing officer
