'UTTERLY SHOCKING'

Scots health chiefs warned of major infection control failings at crisis-hit hospital two weeks before two patients died

25 Nov 2019, 00:06 by Graham Mann

HEALTH chiefs were warned of major infection control failings at a crisis-hit hospital weeks before two patients died, it has emerged.

Tory MSP Miles Briggs branded the Health and Safety Executive papers “utterly shocking” after they raised concerns for staff and the public.

It came amid claims bosses could’ve faced criminal charges over the scandal at Glasgow’s Queen Elizabeth Uni Hospital.

Shadow health secretary Mr Briggs said: “We have a duty of care to patients and our NHS staff. This shows a complete lack of that. It’s utterly shocking.”

The November 2018 report listed numerous problems linked to training staff.

An accompanying letter demanded urgent action to protect workers, patients and the public.

But within weeks two had died from bugs linked to pigeon droppings.

And on Sunday, it was claimed 10 more infections linked to contaminated water were identified as far back as 2016.

Top bacteriologist Professor Hugh Pennington, of Aberdeen Uni, said: “I’m astonished that NHS Greater Glasgow and Clyde did not face criminal charges or other tough sanctions.

"The consequences could be disastrous.”

Health Secretary Jeane Freeman said she’d not seen the report, adding: “I think I should be informed when HSE issues serious compliance notices.”

A health board spokeswoman said they implemented an action plan last December.
