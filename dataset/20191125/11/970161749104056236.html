We're going to move to Mars and it will change life on Earth forever

25 Nov 2019, 11:26 by Jasper Hamill

Humanity took its first giant leap into space less than a century ago – but now we’re seriously considering a move to Mars.

The second space race is already underway, with Nasa planning to land astronauts on the Red Planet in the 2030s and China hoping its taikonauts will follow soon after.

Meanwhile, Elon Musk is plotting his own expedition, meaning the first-ever successful manned mission to another planet could be achieved by a private company, rather than a nation.

And once we’ve touched down on Mars, the real hard work will begin on building a settlement or ‘habitat’ to sustain long-term human populations.

But what many people don’t realise is the profound effect a Martian development will have on people left behind on Earth.

The technology developed to achieve the monumental feat of landing and settling on Mars will also be used to improve life back on our own planet – and there’s a precedent for this because Nasa’s Apollo missions led to the creation of several major technologies which are still in general use today.

To get a sense of how travelling to the Red Planet will change life on Earth, we spoke to Eleanor Watson, curator of a new exhibition at London’s Design Museum called ‘Moving to Mars’ which opens on October 18.

She said: ‘One of the most exciting things about working on this project was realising how much of the technology developed for a mission to Mars will have very relevant Earth applications.

‘The Martian habitats will have to generate zero waste and use low energy, high yield farming systems, for instance.

‘This feels very pressing given our own situation on Earth.’

Watson spoke to many top designers who are working with Nasa and other organisations to develop technology for Mars missions.

One of her favourite creations came from a designer called Anna Talvi, who designed clothes to help astronauts keep healthy whilst travelling through space.

‘In space, due to lack of gravity, your muscles degenerate and you lose bone mass because you’re not getting the exercise you get on Earth by just pushing against gravity,’ Watson added.

‘Her clothes have built-in restraints which force the wearer to use their muscles in an everyday way.

‘They are medical tools which can be worn without making the wearer feel they are being constricted by medical clothing.’
