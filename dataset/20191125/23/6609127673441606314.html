'REAL BOOST'

Dunblane grandad says specially trained dementia dog has given him a new leash of life

25 Nov 2019, 23:08 by Colan Lamont

FOR dementia sufferer Peter Farr, dogs really are man’s best friend after a specially trained pooch gave him a new leash of life.

Peter, 71, was diagnosed with the illness three years ago as his once-encyclopedic memory started to fade.

When the Aussie-born Air Force veteran got the devastating news, he admits it was a hammer blow. Understandably, he found himself getting very frustrated by the debilitating and incurable illness.

But that changed when a very cute two-year-old black labrador named Ruby came running into his life and even shared his passion for ROCK MUSIC.

Peter’s is one of only 11 homes in Scotland to have a specially trained dementia dog.

The grandad-of-two, who is cared for by wife Veryan, says: “The most important thing is that you have to accept you have dementia.

“Once you realise that, you’ll think it’s a bugger but at least you’re not dead!

“Dementia is only a word. Not a sentence. And if I’m having a bad day, Ruby is always there.

“We listen to my rock music together, go out places and she’s been a real boost to me and Veryan. Everybody loves her. I can’t tell you how many people I’ve met because of Ruby.”

Similar to guide dogs for the blind, pooches like Ruby help those with dementia to feel safe and get them out and about.

Unfortunately, most people don’t know that they exist so finding funding is a constant struggle.

However, Alzheimer Scotland, along with MND Scotland, will benefit from funds raised at The Scottish Sun charity shop which opens on Wednesday in Glasgow.

Love Island hunk Anton Danyluk will open our pop-up Sunta Store in Glasgow’s St Enoch Centre at 11am.

The People’s Postcode Lottery has already donated £20,000 to our good causes, while Stagecoach chairman Sir Brian Souter has vowed to match every penny raised over the counter.

As part of Dogs For Good, the Dementia Dog Project takes clever puppies and trains them to help improve the lives of those with the illness.

Ruby knows how to fetch Peter’s medicine bag and can even pull off his clothes and tell him when his lunch is ready.

And Veryan, 65 — who lives with her hubby in Dunblane, Perthshire — reckons the pooch has been the perfect pal for Peter.

She says: “If we’re having a bad day, Ruby is our absolute rock. She’s just a loving, soppy dog who always wants a big hug.

“In the morning I let her out and she comes inside rushing upstairs and wakes Peter up without a murmur from him. If I told him to get up at 7am it wouldn’t work but there’s not a word to Ruby.

“But it’s not just Peter. I often feel she does more for me than him. It’s nice if I’m feeling fragile to be able to take her anywhere.

“I belong to a rambling club in Stirling and they kindly said she is an honorary member. She goes because she’s so well-behaved and everyone loves her.”

Veryan adds: “It’s just amazing how she’s changed our lives. Two years ago, I hadn’t heard of dementia dogs.

“I remember telling Peter what he needed was a guide dog. That was the start of it.

“And now Peter and Ruby are perfect together. They settle down and both listen to his ghastly music on Alexa. But as long as they’re both happy, I’m happy.”

Peter’s big priority is to now raise awareness of dementia dogs so that more people like him can benefit from having their own pooch.

With costs of up to £25,000 for training one dog, it’s vital to spread the word.

Recently, Peter and Ruby have visited local schools to educate youngsters about dementia.

He jokes: “One boy asked me ‘Have you ever forgotten your wife’s name?’ I told him, ‘No! She’d kill me’.

“And many of them have said their gran or grandad has the condition. It’s good to talk about it and they all want to pat Ruby.

“Then they don’t find it a frightening thing and it helps to normalise it. You can’t put a price on that.”
