'Dangerously overworked' paramedics taking more overtime and sick leave

25 Nov 2019, 18:00 by Rachel Clun

Overstretched paramedics are earning in excesss of $100,000 in overtime payments - more than their regular wage - raising serious concerns for their health and safety.

An Auditor-General's report has also revealed that NSW Ambulance staff had the highest rates of overtime, sick leave and excessive annual leave of any NSW Health service in 2018-2019.

"Overtime is paid at premium rates and, if not effectively managed, can result in higher costs and work, health and safety issues, particularly when fatigued employees perform high-risk tasks," the report said.

The amount of overtime being paid to paramedics is increasing: 27 employees were each paid more than $100,000 in overtime last financial year, compared to just eight in 2017-2018.

More broadly, the vast majority (85 per cent) of NSW Ambulance staff received overtime payments, which is a drop from the previous year's 97.8 per cent. However, average overtime payments per employee ballooned to $19,400 in 2018-2019, up from $14,500 the year before.

Paramedics earn from $70,624 up to $86,151 for specialist intensive care paramedics.

As well, 13 per cent of NSW Ambulance staff had an annual leave balance of 30 days or more.

Health Services Union (HSU) secretary Gerard Hayes said the numbers proved the increase in paramedic numbers had not kept up with the growing and ageing population.

“This is a symptom of a paramedic workforce that is extremely stretched,” he said. “Every day, the demand on paramedics increases.

“Paramedics work under extreme stress and it’s no surprise that many are suffering burnout.”

NSW Ambulance was contacted for comment.

Overtime payments grew to $83.1 million in the 2018-2019 financial year, an increase from $74.8 million in 2017-2018.

The main overtime expense at $42.7 million was for ''call out'' overtime, which is planned overtime used to maintain services in parts of regional and rural NSW without 24-hour rosters. Overtime to cover staff absences cost NSW Ambulance $20.6 million, while extended shifts cost $16.7 million.

Paramedic and HSU delegate Tess Oxley said there were not enough crews to cover the work.

"They’re dangerously overworked, and it’s every crew, every day in my region," she said.

Ms Oxley, who works in south-western Sydney, said this meant no breaks in a 12-hour shift and being required to do end-of-shift overtime, which essentially means working close to 14 hours without a break.

"I think the public are very lucky that paramedics are highly skilled and are able to still perform their duties [on shift]," she said.

"However, it's when we get home or we’re driving home that the repercussions are really noticeable that we’ve done such long, tiring shifts, it definitely takes it toll on family and friends."

Ms Oxley said the rate of sick leave was not surprising as there was a higher risk of paramedics being run down if they were excessively fatigued.

"That’s going to be resulting in them being more susceptible to getting illness, and obviously in our industry you can’t come to work when you’re not well," she said.

Ms Oxley said the state needed more paramedics and ambulances, which would allow staff to take much-needed breaks during shifts and make a "massive difference" to their welfare.

"When you get those small amounts of time [to] sit down and clear your head ... you’re able to do your job and able to go home and feel just that little bit less fatigued," she said.
