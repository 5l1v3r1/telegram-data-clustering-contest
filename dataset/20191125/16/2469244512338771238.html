TSB to shut 82 branches - and those in the South West could be affected

It is thought up to 400 jobs could be lost as the bank announces it will close 15 per cent of its branches

25 Nov 2019, 16:33 by Howard Lloyd

TSB has announced that it will shut 82 branches as part of cost-cutting measures.

The Spanish-owned bank, which has 540 outlets, is trying to make £100m of savings by 2022.

The closures will hit around 15 per cent of their branches.

Those in question will be made public on Thursday once staff have been informed. TSB has not disclosed exactly how many job will be lost, but it is believed 300-400 positions will be affected.

The bank has said it will try to find new roles for those staff affected elsewhere in the group, and believes it will cost around £180m to close the branches down.

TSB has 10 branches across Devon and Cornwall, with Devon outlets in Exeter, Exmouth, Torquay, Tavistock, Barnstaple, Okehampton and two in Plymouth.

Cornwall has branches in St Austell and Bude.

The bank's reputation has taken a battering in the last 18 months. Last year saw a massive IT failure which affected 1.9m customers when the bank tried to move date from one IT system to another.

It led to their chief executive, Paul Pester, standing down.

A damning independent report released last week said the bank's board lacked 'common sense' as it prepared to make the switch to the new system, which had not been fully tested.

And the, just days later, an IT failure meant wages and other payments were not paid into some of its customers' accounts due to a 'processing error'.

Despite vowing to leave no customer out of pocket, some complained about being unable to pay their rent and long call wait times when trying to seek help.

TSB boss Debbie Crosbie said: "The plan we're sharing today involves some difficult decisions, but it sets TSB up to succeed in the future.

"Taken together, these changes will help us to serve more customers, better, for the long-term."

She added: "Many of these locations that we are closing are just not fit for purpose for a modern bank and they don't give us the flexibility to adapt to the changing needs of our customers."

Ms Crosbie also did not rule out more branches being shut in the future as it follows in the footsteps of rivals like Lloyds and RBS.

The bank said it expects up to 80 per cent of its sales to be digital by 2022, compared to 46 per cent in September this year.
