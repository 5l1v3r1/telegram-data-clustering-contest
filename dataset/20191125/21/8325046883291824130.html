On season three of Netflix’s “The Crown,” the royals decide to make a documentary about their family – a last-ditch move to rehabilitate their public image, since most people thought them to be stuffy and out of touch. That documentary was real and Queen Elizabeth probably wishes it weren’t.

While “The Crown” takes some liberties in portraying the making of the film, the show accurately portrays the reactions of the royals after it aired, and even replicates some famous scenes from the documentary.

Unfortunately for Queen Elizabeth and her family, the documentary received mixed reviews upon its release – and the Queen was apparently so distraught at the way it portrayed her family that she effectively banned it from being shown after 1972.

Here’s everything you need to know about “Royal Family.”

It was reportedly the idea of the Queen’s press secretary, William Heseltine, as a means of showing the royal family as ‘real people.’

Although Heseltine was the one who came up with the idea, Prince Philip was apparently enthusiastic about the project, and helped orchestrate the entire thing.

The documentary was made in the late 1960s, a time when England, along with the rest of the world, was undergoing radical political and social changes. Many felt that the royal family, with their traditions and formalities, were stuffy, outdated, and hard to relate to -something filmmakers, along with royals, hoped to change with the release of the documentary.

Documentary filmmakers followed the royal family for 18 months, capturing scenes of Prince Philip grilling sausages at Balmoral and the Queen making small talk with President Nixon.

The film began shooting on June 8, 1968, at the annual Trooping of the Colour ceremony (a tradition in which British armies perform exercises and ceremonies for a large audience). In addition to showing Prince Philip and Princess Anne barbecuing at Balmoral, the documentary also featured scenes of the Prince painting, as well as the Queen and Prince Charles making salad dressing (it was “too oily,” according to the Queen).

And one of the more interesting scenes of the documentary saw the Queen make small talk with President Richard Nixon, who visited Buckingham Palace in 1969. “World problems are so complex, aren’t they?” Queen Elizabeth famously remarked to the president.
