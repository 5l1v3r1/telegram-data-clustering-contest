Tory lead over Labour narrows after manifesto launch

25 Nov 2019, 13:09

Support for the Tories has slipped in a new election opinion poll released after the party’s official manifesto launch.

The Survation poll put the prime minister’s party one point down on its previous result, with 41% of respondents saying they would vote Conservative compared with 30% for Labour.

It means the gap between the top two parties has narrowed by three points according to Survation, who polled voters between November 20 and 23.

Survation surveyed 1,010 adults by telephone, asking for their voting intention in the poll conducted for Good Morning Britain.

Labour rose by two points from the company’s first poll one week earlier, with the pro-EU Liberal Democrats up one point at 15% and the Brexit Party unchanged on 5%.

According to the Britain Elects live tracker, which calculates an average based on data from various polling companies, the Tories still have a comfortable 13 point average lead in the polls.

A key policy in the Conservative party manifesto – launched by Boris Johnson in Telford on Sunday – has been slammed as ‘deceit’ by Labour.

The PM has promised to deliver 50,000 more nurses for the NHS but it has been revealed that nearly half of them are already working for the health service.

Mr Johnson’s recruitment pledge was questioned when it emerged 18,500 of the 50,000 promised jobs are existing nurses who the government hopes to persuade to remain in the workforce.

The pledge also included hiring 12,500 foreign nurses who will be recruited from abroad to help plug the existing vacancy gap, which currently stands at more than 43,000.

Labour pointed out only 19,000 posts would actually be filled by brand-new nurse trainees.

Shadow health secretary Jonathan Ashworth said: ‘The Conservatives’ claim on nurses is frankly deceitful – the sums simply don’t add up.

‘First we had Johnson’s fake 40 new hospitals, now we have his fake 50,000 extra nurses.’

Meanwhile Labour have been criticised for promising to spend vast sums of money while only hiking taxes for the top 5% of earners.

The party has been criticised by the director of the Institute for Fiscal Studies over its promise to compensate Waspi women.

The pledge did not feature in the party’s manifesto and would have to be paid for by adding to the UK’s £1.8 trillion debt, shadow chancellor John McDonnell revealed on Sunday.

It takes Labour’s annual spending splurge to more than £97 billion and means their manifesto promises spending £32 for every £1 extra spent by the Tories.

Speaking on BBC Radio 4’s Today Programme, Paul Johnson said the policy’s estimated cost is ‘a very, very large sum of money indeed’.
