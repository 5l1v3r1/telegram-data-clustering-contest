Parents have 'lost faith' in scandal-hit Glasgow hospital claims MSP

Scottish Labour's health spokesperson Monica Lennon's claim follows accusations of a cover up over the deaths of two children linked to contaminated water in 2017.

25 Nov 2019, 04:30 by John Dingwall, https://www.dailyrecord.co.uk/authors/john-dingwall/

Parents have lost faith in safety standards at the Queen Elizabeth University Hospital (QEUH) in Glasgow.

The claim by Scottish Labour’s health spokesperson follows accusations of a cover up over the deaths of two children linked to contaminated water in 2017.

The Daily Record previously reported how three-year old Mason Djemat’s death is being probed by police.

He died three weeks before 10-year old cancer patient Milly Main succumbed to an infection linked to contaminated water.

Yesterday Monica Lennon MSP said: “Over the weekend, parents, staff and experts have continued to raise serious concerns about the safety of the QEUH campus.

“Many parents remain unconvinced that the hospital environment is safe for young cancer patients.”

Anas Sarwar MSP revealed to our sister paper, the Sunday Mail a whistleblower’s claim that 10 infections were identified in the children’s hospital during 2016, a year prior to the unexpected deaths.

They are on top of the 26 incidents revealed in 2017 by the same review, including Milly’s death and 23 cases in 2018 reported by Health Protection Scotland - all in paediatric cancer.

Mr Sarwar said: “These latest revelations from an NHS whistleblower show that problems at this hospital have been going on for years.

“It’s clear that action should have been taken much earlier by senior managers to prevent the tragedy which unfolded.

“Doctors responded to concerns about the circumstances of Milly’s death by starting the formal whistleblowing process in September 2017.

“Dedicated staff repeatedly raised issues about the water supply with senior management, but they weren’t listened to.”

A third child was left fighting for their life after an infection linked to the water contamination crisis.

The child’s mother, who wants to remain anonymous, told the Mail on Sunday: “He was admitted for a simple procedure and was only meant to be in for two days.

“During a line flush he became very unwell., very quickly.

“I believe if he had been at home he would have died.

“He had his line removed and pathology revealed it had five separate bugs in it.

“My son almost died in my arms.

“I don’t want that to happen to another parent.”

The Sunday Post reported that the Health and Safety Executive listed problems with the way staff were trained and equipped to deal with highly contagious diseases, in November 2018.

Jeane Freeman, Health Secretary, is under pressure to resign over the scandal.

She told BBC’s Sunday Politics Scotland programme she not been shown the Health and Safety Executive report into the hospital when it came out in November last year.

Ms Freeman added ministers “wouldn’t normally see” HSE reports, and added that it was about a separate part of the hospital, although she went on to say senior Scottish Government officials were now looking “very carefully” at what information comes to ministers.

“I want to change what is normal procedure so I do know about some of these matters,” she said.

“I think I should be informed when the Health and Safety Executive issues serious compliance notices on any board.”
