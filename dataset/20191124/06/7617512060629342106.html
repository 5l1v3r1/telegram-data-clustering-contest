HeroWork’s 12th project transforms support centre

24 Nov 2019, 06:00 by Roxanne Egan-Elliott

Staff at a grassroots agency in Esquimalt that provides programming and support to sex workers have been serving up to 200 meals a day with an oven that never heated up enough and a broken dishwasher.

After a massive renovation by hundreds of volunteers, Peers Victoria Resources Society has a new kitchen, new floors, new offices, a new classroom and an entirely new space to support clients.

“There’s only one bathroom I think that really didn’t change,” said Rachel Phillips, executive director of Peers. “It was a gut job.”

On Saturday, the agency held an event for the community to visit the revamped space.

The transformation by HeroWork, a charity that completes radical renovations of other charities, will give staff more time and space to support the community.

“Because our space is organized better, there’s room for growth now, so we can add more programs and have the space to deliver them,” Phillips said.

Watching 500 volunteers dedicate thousand of working hours to transform the space showed Phillips that the stigma against sex workers isn’t as strong as it once was.

“This says the community supports sex workers in a very profound kind of way,” she said.

Sarah Smith, who works at Peers as a drop-in coordinator, said she is most excited about a new heated closet. Smith said many clients come in out of the rain, drenched without a change of clothes. The heated closet means they can dry their clothes and shoes before going back outside.

Peers supports nearly 600 current and former sex workers annually, providing a range of services from housing and health care to clothing and meals.

Volunteers started the renovations, valued at more than $625,000, in the first week of October. About 115 companies donated supplies to the renovation, which is HeroWork’s 12th project.

“The transformation of charity buildings helps to transform charities,” said HeroWork founder and CEO Paul Latour. “When you’re serving in a building that doesn’t work, and it’s ugly and debilitating and inefficient, it’s hard for a charity to operate well.”

Peers is recruiting new volunteers to help provide programming to the community.
