An attitude change towards drink-driving is needed to stop number of deaths

The Sunday Mail has launched a Leave the Keys campaign to urge people to not drink and drive.

24 Nov 2019, 04:30 by Mail Opinion, https://www.dailyrecord.co.uk/authors/mail-opinion/

Seemingly impossible behavioural changes can be achieved with a bit of ambition and a lot of public will.

Take the smoking ban.

It would have been regarded as unthinkable 20 years ago that cigarettes could be taken out of Scottish pubs.

People old enough to remember will recall critics describing the 2005 legislation leading to a ban a year later as unworkable.

Now 13 years on, the ban has also been introduced in England, Wales and in countries across the world.

The ban changed public attitudes to smoking. The sight of smokers standing outside the front doors of hospitals is a thing of the past.

Incidences of smoking-related diseases are down and the air in our public buildings has literally cleared.

Laws outlawing drink-driving are already in place but, depressingly, a crime which ought to be a relic of a bygone age remains with us.

Anyone in any doubt about the wreckage caused by drink-driving should read the accounts given by families in today’s Sunday Mail.

Because of one person’s selfish, disgusting decision to drink then get behind the wheel, their lives lie in tatters.

Charles Pease recalled: “I went to bed and was awoken by police at 4am. I knew it wasn’t going to be good news but I assumed they were there about my elderly father.

“Instead, the policewoman said to me, ‘Are you the partner of Theresa Wade and does she drive a white ­Citroen Berlingo van?’ My blood ran cold. I had to sit down.

“She went on to say, ‘I’m sorry to tell you that she was involved in a road traffic accident and as a consequence she died.’

“It’s as if someone had taken a swing at my legs with a baseball bat. It hit my mind, my heart, it hit everything. The world just ended.”

This is not a trivial crime. That’s why we’ve launched our Leave The Keys campaign ahead of the party season.

The fact that there are any incidents at all means there still needs to be a change in attitudes towards the drink-drive culture.

It has never been easier to leave the keys at home. In the age of the Uber and plentiful public transport options excuses are non-existent.

Superintendent Louise Blakelock, deputy head of road policing at Police Scotland, admits warnings to the public are being ignored.

She calls for the friends, partners and families of drink-drivers to report them to the police – confidentially, if necessary.

For many this is something of a taboo. Again it’s a cultural thing – nobody likes a tell-tale.

But it isn’t just justifiable. It’s imperative.

Anyone who turns a blind eye becomes complicit. There should not be a single second’s hesitation in reporting a drink-driver.

The Scottish public can ensure that the abomination of a rise in statistics never happens again.
