BALTIC

Scotland is facing the coldest winter for ten years – with temperatures set to plummet to -13C

24 Nov 2019, 14:27 by Alistair Grant

SCOTLAND is forecast the coldest winter for 10 years - with -13C lows, snow, ice and travel woes, with a weak sun and Arctic chills blamed.

The worst winter since 2009-10 is due as the sun is at the weakest point of its 11-year cycle of strength, said The Weather Company, the world’s biggest commercial forecaster.

The last time the sun's power was as low as now, Scotland saw the bitter 2009-10 winter, Britain's coldest winter since the 1970s, and the 2010 Big Freeze, with the coldest December ever recorded.

The Met Office says weak UV rays cause cold easterly winds rather than mild westerlies.

The forecast comes as Scotland faces rain then a -5C two-week 'polar plunge' starting from Friday.

The Met Office forecast cold polar air arriving from Friday threatening to remain for much of the next two weeks, with snow in the North and threatening the South.

Met Office forecasters say numerous factors point to cold weather this winter.

The worst cold with -13C lows will be between January and March, said The Weather Company, founded in 1982, which was bought by IBM for $2billion in 2016.

An average UK winter temperature 1.4C colder than normal, below 2010-11's 2.43C, would make this Britain's coldest winter since 2009-10's 1.64C, Met Office records show.

Leon Brown, head of meteorological operations at The Weather Company’s Weather Channel arm, said: “This winter is expected to be the UK's coldest since 2009–10.

“Solar activity is at a minimum this winter. Air pressure patterns are expected to allow cold Arctic air to affect Britain.

“Night-time temperatures of -13C are expected this winter. Travel will be affected.”

The Weather Company chief meteorologist Todd Crawford said: “We are quickly approaching the solar minimum.

The epic winters of 2009-10 and 2010-11 occurred during the last solar minimum. “We forecast the cold this winter to be focused in January and February.”

The Met Office forecaster Marco Petagna said: “Many things support cold weather this winter, including large snow cover over Siberia and Scandinavia, hints of weakening polar vortex winds and the solar minimum.”

The Met Office has said: “Low UV output from the sun can contribute to cold winters.

“In years of low UV activity, unusually cold air forms over the tropics and an easterly flow over mid-latitudes burrows its way down to the surface, bringing easterly winds and cold winters to northern Europe.”
