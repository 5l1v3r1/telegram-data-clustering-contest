Mary Beard relies on her husband to do household chores, as she reveals her 100 hour week

24 Nov 2019, 17:32 by Camilla Turner

Professor Mary Beard has said that she relies on her “retired husband” to do household chores, as she reveals her 100 hour working week ahead of a lecturers’ strike.

The renowned Cambridge classicist told how on a typical day she starts work at six o’clock in the morning and works continuously until around 11 o’clock at night with no lunch break. 

She said she has spent the past few weeks calculating how many hours per week she spends working and her current estimate is “over 100”.

Writing on Twitter, she said: “There are grey areas... how do you calculate the travel time to get to an outside lecture? Yep, I outsource cleaning, I confess. My retired husband does the shopping and 'maintenance'. I make the cakes once a year.”  

She added that she “certainly” does not have weekends off, and only “occasionally” goes out for dinner.

 Prof Beard, 64,  was speaking ahead of an eight day walk-out by university employees due to begin on Monday at institutions across the country amid a row over pay and pensions.

Over a million students face disruption to their studies as academics from 60 universities voted in favour of industrial action.  

Members of the University and College Union (UCU) held two separate ballots last month, one over pensions and another over their pay and working conditions.

The union is locked in a battle with Universities UK (UUK), which represents vice-Chancellors, over proposed pension reforms to address a reported deficit and rising benefit costs.

The current dispute is over UCU members being forced to make increased contributions to maintain the same level of benefits.

Employees are now paying in 9.6 per cent of their salary to the scheme rather than the 8 per cent they paid last year.

Prof Beard, who is a fellow at Newnham College, Cambridge University, acknowledged that she is “well remunerated” for her “heavy load” of work.  She confirmed that she will “not be crossing a picket line” during the strike.
