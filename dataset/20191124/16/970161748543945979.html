Skeletal baby in Yemeni hospital lays bare heartbreaking cost of conflict

24 Nov 2019, 16:25

Shocking pictures of a malnourished baby boy at hospital in Yemen highlight the stark claim that a child under five dies every 10 minutes due to extreme hunger in the country.

Doctors are seen treating the infant at Al-Sabeen hospital in the capital of Sana’a on Saturday.

A nearly five-year-long war has created what the UN calls the world’s worst humanitarian crisis, with at least 12 million children caught up in the conflict.

The fighting has killed tens of thousands of civilians and driven millions to the brink of famine, according to the Armed Conflict Location and Event Data Project NGO.

The UN envoy for Yemen, Martin Griffiths, said on Friday that momentum is building to end the war within the first few months of 2020.

Air attacks by the Saudi-led coalition battling Houthi rebels has dropped almost 80 per cent in the last two weeks

‘In recent weeks, there have been entire 48-hour periods without air strikes for the first time since the conflict began,’ Mr Griffiths added.

‘We call this de-escalation, a reduction in the tempo of the war, and perhaps a move towards an overall ceasefire in Yemen.’

Wrecked health infrastructure and chronic poverty could mean child deaths continuing in numbers well beyond any upcoming truce.

The bleak statistic on infant deaths was reported by the public health minister in the Iran-backed Houthi-run administration in an address to the UN given at the hospital, according to al-Masirah TV.

Taha al-Mutawakel added that six newborns also lose their lives every two hours.

‘We are not demanding toys and video game consoles, but we are calling for incubators and other related devices to give children the right to life,’ Mutawakel said in his address.

‘Stop shedding tears for our children who get killed, whilst there is no credibility whatsoever in your international reports and they do not help assuage this tragic situation.’

He went on to claim that 5,000 Yemeni children suffer traumas caused by weapons of mass destruction, some of them internationally banned.

International experts convened by the UN have accused both sides in the conflict of committing war crimes.
