SHOCKING SCENES

Nicola Sturgeon flooded with mail from angry Scots calling for ‘hate-filled’ sectarian marches to be banned in Glasgow

24 Nov 2019, 11:06 by Ewan Mowat

NICOLA Sturgeon was inundated with calls from "embarrassed" Scots to ban the sectarian marches in Glasgow.

Violent scenes were sparked in the city in August and September after Loyalist protesters clashed with those on Republican processions.

Eleven were arrested and a police officer injured by a flare during the ugly scenes.

And now a freedom of information request has revealed members of the public felt "embarrassed" by the marches and subsequent clashes.

According to the Daily Record, the First Minister was flooded with emails and letters from concerned punters.

One punter claimed to have been subjected to vile racist abuse during the processions, calling on the government to clamp down on the marches.

His letter, released under the FOI Act with the his name covered, said: “For the first time in my 43 years of being born, ­educated and living in the west of Scotland I am embarrassed to be a Glaswegian.

“I have been a victim of racially-­motivated hate crime a number of times ­living in ­Glasgow.

“Most recently, I was pushed on to the road and called a 'black b******' when I was walking with my wife on a ­Saturday afternoon.

“I have never felt I was not ­Glaswegian or ­Scottish but I was embarrassed to be both.

“I work for a recruitment agency focused on ­attracting talent to Scotland based on the quality of life.

“After ­witnessing these events, I do not see what the attraction would be for any new immigrant to Scotland.

“I am asking you to question why the Scottish Government has not done more to stop any of the hate-filled marches taking place in Scotland.”

One local emailed Sturgeon, also claiming to be ashamed of the scenes as he showed Canadian friends round the city.

He said: “On Saturday, my wife and I decided to meet friends in Glasgow.

“Little did we know our day would be ruined by bigots on both sides of the divide. Our friends were visiting from ­Canada and could not understand the scenes we witnessed.

“My wife and I were embarrassed listening to the bile and insults hurled. These marches need to be banned to stop the shameful scenes we witnessed.”

Many of the those complaining called for a ban on Orange walks.

One letter said: "What is happening when the powers that be continue to allow anti-­Catholic parades to parade by Catholic churches?

“It seems ­anti-Catholic hatred is getting worse in ­Scotland.”

And another Glaswegian blasted the "simpletons" in the processions, saying: "These ­hateful, bigoted marches are ­disgusting, ­unnecessary and ­embarrassing.

“For these ­simpletons to march and ­celebrate ­something that happened in 1690 is bizarre.”

Others voiced their concerns about the Republican marches in Glasgow.

One letter said: “I was horrified at the ­trouble that came about on Saturday regarding another ­Republican walk.”

Another wrote: “I am angry and ­astonished at the decision to allow another Republican march in Glasgow.

“The fact that they support the ­terrorist group the IRA is totally ­unacceptable and it should never have been allowed to take place.”

One local praised Sturgeon for speaking out against the marches after she blasted the "utterly unacceptable" behaviour in Govan in August.

But they added: “It is not enough to talk about it. These marches cause nothing but hatred and trouble so the time has come for government to step in.”

A spokesperson for the Scottish Government said: “We are clear that while peaceful protest is an important part of our democracy, violent and sectarian disruption is not.

“That is why we support collective action to achieve a zero-tolerance approach to sectarianism and violent and offensive behaviour.

"This protects the right to peaceful public assembly and protest, but also safeguards communities and ensures that scenes such as those witnessed in Glasgow earlier this year are not repeated.

“We have invested more than £14 million in work tackling sectarianism over the last seven years to support over 100 anti-sectarianism projects and fully support Police Scotland taking robust and proportionate action against any perpetrators of such behaviour.

“I have never felt I was not ­Glaswegian or ­Scottish but I was embarrassed to be both [during the marches]."Angry Scot in letter to Nicola Sturgeon

“We welcome Dr Michael Rosie’s independent national review of marches, parades and demonstrations, as well as Glasgow City Council’s additional review.

“It is clear from the correspondence we have received that the general public shares our desire for a Scotland free from sectarianism and violent disorder.”
Violent mayhem erupted after Loyalist protesters met Republican marchers in Glasgow city centre in September.

We revealed later that month that - despite a temporary ban - the council "has no legal power" to stop the processions.

Two schoolboys, aged 14 and 15, were among 11 arrested during the ugly scenes in Glasgow.

Cops blasted the "recklessness and stupidity" of those who launched pyrotechnics which injured an officer along Clyde Street.

Scottish Police Federation vice-chair David Hamilton also warned that someone could even be maimed by a flare if the violence continues.

Six marches were banned the following weekend - with Loyalist groups holding a protest in the city centre.

Hundreds joined the Orange Order and Scottish Protestants Against Discrimination in George Square.

It was revealed that 600 officers were required for just ONE day of marches in Glasgow.

Cops were pulled in from all over the country - at a cost of more than £176,000.

Chief Constable Iain Livingstone said: "We drew officers from across the country away from their key role in our communities.

“In terms of resource levels, is it sustainable? No, it’s not.”
