Box Office: ‘Doctor Sleep’ Fails To Shine With Disappointing $14.1 Million Weekend

10 Nov 2019 by Scott Mendelson

Warner Bros.’ Doctor Sleep fell victim to Blade Runner 2049 syndrome. By that I mean the sequel to The Shining was heavily marketed specifically to fans of Stephen King’s 1979 book and Stanley Kubrick’s 1980 movie, to the point where it didn’t offer that much else to general audiences. While I recognized the marketing strategy (it was kind of hard to miss), I wasn’t that concerned going into the weekend, since a performance akin to Blade Runner 2049 ($89 million domestic and $242 million worldwide) would have been fine on Doctor Sleep’s $45 million budget. Alas, Doctor Sleep earned just $14.1 million this weekend. That was well below the over/under $25 million tracking guestimates and frankly lower than any number of recent non-IP horror flicks in recent years (think Lights Out or Don’t Breathe). It earned a B+ from Cinemascore and a 2.7x weekend multiplier, playing 57% male and 74% over 25-years-old. At best, we’re probably looking at a $40 million domestic finish, but something closer to $30 million wouldn’t surprise me. The curse of Blade Runner 2049 lives again. It’s another reason to fear for Legendary and WB’s Dune.

Perhaps I drank the Kool-Aid a little bit this time, but audiences clearly didn’t care about the property. The Shining was a solid hit in 1980, earning $44 million domestic, its popularity is comparatively confined to horror junkies and film nerds. And, yeah, it’s a slow-burn 143-minute horror flick, so it’s not something that gets introduced during family movie night. For that matter, whatever nostalgia for the property was blunted by being a 152-minute, R-rated horror movie. So, you can’t bring the kids and that babysitter will cost you a little extra.

The film was an adaption of Stephen King’s 2013 novel while acting as essentially a sequel to three versions of The Shining (the book, the first movie, and the ABC mini-series). Much of the media attention was focused on King’s long-standing displeasure with the Kubrick version and how director Mike Flanagan took all three prior versions of The Shining as well as the Doctor Sleep book, in order to make a hybrid adaptation/sequel of sorts. While ambitious and interesting, that info was only valuable to audiences already interested in a sequel to The Shining while being potentially off-putting to those who just wanted a post-Halloween horror flick.

There’s a case to be made that Doctor Sleep could have filled a seasonal gap, since this past Halloween didn’t have a big horror flick. Sure, Joker, Maleficent: Mistress of Evil and Zombieland: Double Tap kinda-sorta filled the bill, but Doctor Sleep could have been positioned as this year’s Saw, Paranormal Activity or what-have-you. To be fair, New Line’s It Chapter Two was a huge hit in September and Warner Bros.’ Joker benefited from the lack of a scary movie in the marketplace. The Ewan McGregor/Kyliegh Curran/Rebecca Ferguson/Cliff Curtis horror flick grossed just $14.4 million over the weekend.

We’re still only talking about a $45 million flick that could play better overseas, but this is one of those scary situations where an IP/brand adaptation performs noticeably worse than a proverbial original in the same respective genre. Think Storks playing better than LEGO Ninjago Movie, with the lesson in this case being that the Stephen King name isn’t enough to drive audiences otherwise uninterested in the movie. I wasn’t high on the movie, but there’s plenty to appreciate and, if you’re a fan, it’s not like WB was thinking about The Shining 3 had this performed better.
