Dad facing death after rare cancer returns terrified son 'won't know who I am'

Tom Cameron, 31, from Poole in Dorset is desperate for a stem cell match to beat a rare form of cancer that is likely to take his life and rob his young son of a father

10 Nov 2019, 20:11 by Milo Boyd, Thomas George, https://www.mirror.co.uk/authors/milo-boyd/

A cancer sufferer terrified his young son won't remember him when he's gone has made a desperate call for a stem-cell donor.

Two months after Tom Cameron and his wife Chloe experienced the happiest day of their lives when baby Otto was born, they received some life-altering news.

Tom visited his GP after a period of coughing to be told he had a tumour in his chest that was a rare form of cancer, stage four primary mediastinal b-cell lymphoma.

In the following 17 months the operating theatre practitioner was twice cleared of the disease only for it come back each time, the Manchester Evening News reported.

Now as he faces a bleak medical outlook, Tom is searching for a stem-cell match and hoping he can have potentially life saving transplant.

If he doesn't, he fears Otto may grow up without a dad.

"We are desperately hoping a donor is found soon because it is my best chance of giving Otto a future," the 31-year-old said.

 

"He is 19 months old now so he is bouncing around, but when I've been in hospital he can't visit. I've not been able to see him for weeks on end.

"If I die in the next six to twelve months, I'm afraid he will never know who I am."

Tom first noticed something was wrong when he couldn't shift a cough and was struggling to talk to patients at his work.

When he noticed a lump in his neck he visited his GP.

The doctor sent the Poole, Dorset man for an X-ray which showed he had a tumour later tests proved was cancerous.

"It's put everything on hold," he said.

"When you have a child, you have plans about what type of parent you are going to be. I've not been able to do any of that."

Tom immediately quit his job and embarked on a course of chemotherapy.

The cancer refused to budge until January, when doctors at King's College Hospital in London tried a pioneering new treatment on him.

Tom was given CAR-T cell therapy, which helps a person's immune system fight cancer.

After ten weeks of treatment in the capital, he was handed a major boost when doctors told him the cancer had disappeared.

 

But his recovery did not last long.

Two months later, he returned for another scan and was told the cancer had returned.

"We were absolutely devastated," Tom said.

"It came completely out of the blue.

"I was feeling the best I had been since my diagnosis. I went into the consultation with no symptoms at all.

"We had just taken Otto up to Manchester for the first time to see family and we had hope of things returning to normal."

Tom met his partner Chloe seven years ago while travelling in New Zealand.

 

They had planned on having another child after Otto was born in March 2018, but those plans - as well as the rest of their lives - were put on hold by the cancer.

As doctors told Tom palliative care looked like his only option, he spent the summer planning his funeral.

In one final roll of the dice, a consultant told him about an immunotherapy drug.

The treatment, unavailable on the NHS, costs £3,500 every three weeks. But with the help of family, friends and even total strangers, Tom managed to raise an impressive £35,000 to cover the costs.

So far, it has proved a success and, in September, doctors told Tom he was cancer-free once more.

However, the drug will only work for so long and Tom's best chance at beating cancer altogether is a stem cell transplant.

So far Tom has not found a stem cell match within the 2% of the UK population that are registered as donors.

He said: "This drug is keeping the cancer at bay for now, but it has a limited lifetime. The cancer is going to come back at some point.

"We never thought we would get to this point but a stem cell transplant is my best hope for a cure.

"It would mean everything to get this treatment and try and return to a normal life.

"There are a lot of people in my situation whose families are going through the same worries, and they are trying to their lives back on track."
