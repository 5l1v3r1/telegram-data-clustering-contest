Teen deaths from London’s ‘knife epidemic’ now at 12-year high

23 Nov 2019, 11:55

The number of teenagers stabbed to death in the capital is now at its highest level for more than a decade.

This year alone some 23 teens have been fatally attacked and grieving relatives have called the statistics ‘seriously alarming.’

The grim toll is one more than the total for 2008 and on the way to surpassing the 26 young lives who were lost in 2007.

This year’s youngest victim was just 14 years old.

Jaden Moodie was the first teenager to be stabbed to death in the capital this year.

The 14-year-old was knocked off a moped and stabbed on January 8 in Leyton.

Mohammed Usman Mirza, 19, became the latest after he was killed in Ilford on Tuesday night.

A total of 25 teenagers have died in 2019, 23 of whom suffered fatal knife wounds.

Jodie Chesney is this year’s only female knife victim after she was attacked in a park in Romford in March.

Her father Peter told the BBC: ‘Over 20 teenagers have been fatally stabbed this year and that is shocking to me.

‘You read about it and you go “oh that sucks” but when it is really close to your heard you wonder why are people doing this?’

Her Uncle Terry called the situation ‘alarming’ and added: ‘It is proof that this knife epidemic is spiralling out of control.’

The Metropolitan Police told Metro.co.uk that they have launched 124 murder inquiries this year.

British Transport Police are dealing with a further three, meaning the total is close to surpassing that of last year.

In 2018 some 135 people were murdered or unlawfully killed and the murder rate overtook that of New York for the first time.

Commissioner Cressida Dick has previously said that detectives were operating in a ‘very challenging’ environment and had been met with a ‘wall of silence’ in some cases.

Campaigners have said knife crime is a symptom of a wider problem within London based around poverty and attraction to gang culture.

Last month the chief executive of charity Barnado’s, Javed Khan, said: ‘It’s totally unacceptable that the knife crime crisis continues to claim so many young lives, with offences at a record high.

‘Knife crime is a symptom of a much wider, complex problem. Too many young people are suffering a “poverty of hope” and facing a future with no qualifications, no job prospects and no role models, making them vulnerable to criminal gangs who force them to deliver drugs and carry knives.’
