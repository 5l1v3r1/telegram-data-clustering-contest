"Gareth Thomas is proof HIV doesn't make you weak – it makes you the opposite"

Gareth Thomas opened up on Loose Women this week in his first TV interview since revealing his HIV status - and now he's doing the most ­important work of his life

23 Nov 2019, 17:19 by Saira Khan, https://www.facebook.com/dailymirror/

Gareth Thomas had a glittering sporting ­career but it’s now that he’s doing the most ­important work of his life.

The former Welsh rugby player is on a ­mission to save lives and change attitudes.

Gareth opened up on Loose Women this week in his first TV interview since he chose to reveal his HIV status in the Sunday Mirror in September.

Sitting next to him, I could only imagine how hard it was for him to come out about his sexuality, especially because he was a rugby star.

He was afraid of losing his people, his town, his game.

But in 2009, he came out as gay and the following year was voted the most ­influential gay person in the UK.

It was during a routine health check-up that Gareth discovered he was HIV positive. And it felt like an express train was hitting him at 300mph.

He wondered how long he had left. He admitted he was so ­uneducated, all he was picturing was the adverts from 20 years ago, with people dying.

He even thought he wouldn’t be able to share a cup with anybody else.

The scariest thing for him was that he’d had no symptoms – he seemed fit and healthy. So he is even more determined that people get tested – because Gareth says the fear of not knowing is far worse.

So many of us still, in 2019, are clueless about HIV. I grew up in the 80s when scaremongering was at its peak.

The perception was that this virus only affected the gay and African communities and it was a death sentence.

It is only through Gareth that I learned how far HIV medication has actually come.

Gareth, who is 45, takes one pill a day now and the virus is ­undetectable in his blood and can’t be passed on.

He says: “I know I have HIV, but HIV doesn’t have me.”

He told me that now he has accepted his condition, he feels more empowered and mentally stronger than at any other time in his life.

And because he has shared his news, he feels the burden has lifted – he doesn’t need to hide and he can now help others.

And getting tested is such an easy process – you can even get self-test-ing kits to do at home.

Gareth started off as a rugby player but he is now an educator – out to save lives.

He’s making HIV less scary and something we should all talk about. He speaks bravely from the heart, he cares and he wants to make a difference.

After everything he has been through, he is standing tall, owning it and being completely selfless.

He is proof that having HIV does not make you weak – it makes you the opposite.
