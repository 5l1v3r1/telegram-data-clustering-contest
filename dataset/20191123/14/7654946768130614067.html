27 awesome books to buy everyone on your holiday gift list

23 Nov 2019, 14:20 by Sara Austin

COFFEE-TABLE BOOKS AND COOKBOOKS

Christmas at Highclere: Recipes and Traditions from the Real Downton Abbey
By Fiona, Countess of Carnarvon (Preface Publishing,) $34.08
Lady Mary meets Martha Stewart in Fiona, the 8th Countess of Carnarvon, a historian and former fashion designer who presides over the English estate where the PBS series and surprise-hit movie were filmed. In this charming coffee-table book for Royal lovers and fans of the show, she takes readers upstairs and downstairs, sharing traditional British holiday recipes and stories of holidays past.

Palm Beach
By Aerin Lauder (Assouline), $85
In this luxurious volume appropriately dressed in Lily Pulitzer pink, socialite Lauder gives a peek behind the manicured hedges at the people, homes, restaurants and shops of the Florida enclave that has long lured New Yorkers (most recently, Donald Trump). Buy it for your preppiest snowbird pal.

Women: The National Geographic Image Collection
By National Geographic, with an introduction by Susan Goldberg (National Geographic Books), $50
This ambitious photo collection aims to capture the past and present lives of women across the globe. Featuring 450 color images from 50 countries and new essays from Oprah Winfrey, Laura Bush, Jane Goodall and other favorite females, it’s an inspiring gift for any woman in your life.

Gray Malin: Italy
by Gray Malin (Harry N. Abrams), $40
Pour a Campari and soak in distinctive helicopter-eye views of Cinque Terre, Lake Como and other glam Italian coastal spots from photographer Gray Malin, who has close to 370K Instagram followers. His book will satisfy anyone suffering from winter wanderlust.

Guitar
by David Schiller (Workman), $35
Each of the 200-plus pages of this coffee-table book (housed in a faux-leather “case”) celebrates a famous instrument — think Willie Nelson’s Trigger, Prince’s Yellow Cloud, and George Harrison’s Rocky. Perfect for the musical obsessive who may or may not have named their own guitar.

A Garden Miscellany: An Illustrated Guide to the Elements of the Garden
By Suzanne Staubach (Timber Press), $27.50
A sweet, alphabetical handbook to all things green, from arbors and arches to water features and yards. The painterly illustrations, quirky factoids and genuinely helpful tips make this an ideal gift for anyone who has a garden . . . or just imagines escaping to one.

The Polaroid Diaries
By Linda McCartney, Ekow Eshun, Reuel Golden (Taschen, $50)
Paul McCartney fans will relish this collection of intimate, unstaged Polaroids taken over two decades by his late wife, Linda, an artist and photographer. A rare peek at the loving family life of a private artist. (For a splurge, get the collector’s edition signed by Paul).

Wine for Normal People (A Guide for Real People Who Like Wine But Not the Snobbery That Goes With It)
by Elizabeth Schneider (Chronicle Books), $24.95
Give this one to an aspiring oenophile who would really rather you stop calling her that, thank you very much. Schneider, host of the Wine for Normal People podcast, is friendly and informative without pandering to newbies, and the well-designed charts and cheat sheets will bring anyone up to speed on all things vino.

Lasagna: A Baked Pasta Cookbook
By Anna Hezel and the editors of Taste (Clarkson Potter), $16.99
Whether you’re cooking for a crowd or a friend in need, lasagna is a bear hug in casserole form. This attractive, accessible cookbook has 50 variations to spread the love.

Rachael Ray 50: Memories and Meals from a Sweet and Savory Life
By Rachael Ray (Ballantine), $32
America’s most effusive chef is facing down her 50th birthday with a smile (natch). Her new bestseller compiles 125 new recipes as well as her drawings, reflective essays, childhood memories and family photos, making it not just a cookbook but an endearing memoir, too.

FICTION

The Institute
By Stephen King (Scribner), $34.99
Twelve-year-old Luke Ellis goes to sleep in his suburban Minneapolis bedroom and wakes up in a windowless room in the mysterious Institute. Can he find his way out with the help of the other “gifted” kids who are imprisoned there? King’s rich thriller should have appeal for adults and mature kids who binged Stranger Things this summer.

The Dutch House
By Anne Patchett (Harper), $30.79
Patchett’s elegant prose likely has fans on your list. Her latest novel introduces siblings Danny and Maeve, who grow up in seeming privilege in a Philadelphia mansion but will have to forge an intense bond to survive what’s ahead. As with Howard’s End or Manderley, the lavish Dutch House becomes a character all its own.

Daisy Jones and the Six
By Taylor Jenkins Reid (Ballantine), $27
This novel has a superfun setup — it’s structured as an oral history of a fictional 1970s rock band that might remind you of Fleetwood Mac. Got a friend who binges on “Behind the Music”? This is as addictive and juicy as reality TV, but with a lot more humor and heart.

The Other Americans
By Laila Lalami (Pantheon, $25.95)
After closing up his restaurant late one night in Los Angeles, a Moroccan immigrant is struck by a car. The mystery of what happened and its aftereffects unfolds via a dozen different narrators, including the victim’s daughter, a police detective and the victim himself. Lalami’s thoughtful page-turner was a finalist for the National Book Award and a perfect pick for highbrow fiction fans.

NON FICTION

One Day: The Extraordinary Story of an Ordinary 24 Hours in America
By Gene Weingarten (Blue Rider Press), $28
Washington Post journalist Weingarten has won two Pulitzer Prizes for his humane and engaging storytelling. Here, he chooses, literally from a hat, a single date — Sunday, December 28, 1986 — to illustrate the wonders, dramas and absurdities of everyday life. Almost anyone on your list will find a story they connect with.

Indistractable: How to Control Your Attention and Choose Your Life
By Nir Eyal (BenBella Books), 26.95
With his 2014 bestseller, “Hooked,” former Stanford lecturer Eyal gave Silicon Valley businesses the framework to develop addictive products. Consider this book his penance, a realistic four-step plan for consumers to push back against the constant interruptions of our devices. Give this to a friend who has resolved to have a healthier relationship with tech in 2020.

The City Game
By Matthew Goodman (Ballantine Books), $29
Only one team in history has won both the NIT and NCAA tournaments: the 1949-50 City College of New York Beavers. Goodman’s twisty-but-true narrative takes you along for the team’s crazy ride. A captivating read for a pal who enjoys college sports and a look at New York’s past.

Maybe You Should Talk to Someone: A Therapist, HER Therapist, and Our Lives Revealed
By Lori Gottlieb (Houghton Mifflin Harcourt), $34.95
Therapist Gottlieb counsels her patients not to avoid negative feelings, but to notice and learn from them. But it’s not until she finds herself on the couch after a breakup that she understands how hard this is. Readers will dog-ear the pages of this book, full of Gottleib’s “loving truth bombs” about what it takes to change your life.

The Optimist’s Telescope: Thinking Ahead in a Reckless Age
By Bina Venkataraman (Riverhead Books), $28
Why is it so hard to save instead of spending, or to choose kale salad over donuts? Venkataraman, a climate-change advisor to the Obama administration, is an expert in the psychology of instant gratification and how individuals and organizations can make better choices for the long run. Perfect for someone whose New Year’s Resolutions include building a more hopeful look to the future.

The Peanuts Papers: Writers and Cartoonists on Charlie Brown, Snoopy & the Gang, and the Meaning of Life
Edited by Andrew Blauner (Library of America, special edition), $24.95
Charles Schulz earned the devotion of children everywhere by taking them seriously. The favor is returned in this anthology, a collection of sharp reflections on the Peanuts gang by A-list authors including Ira Glass, Chuck Klosterman, and Elissa Schappell. A breezy read that will also give Peanuts fans a lot to ponder.

How to Be a Family: The Year I Dragged My Kids Around the World to Find a New Way to Be Together
By Dan Kois (Little, Brown, and Company), $28
Kois, a Slate editor, became so frustrated with modern family life that he did a hard reset and traveled for a year in search of meaningful connection. His lively family makes entertaining company, and Kois is at his funniest when things inevitably do not go as planned. Stressed-out parents of screen-addicted kids will relate.

CELEBRITY

It’s Garry Shandling’s Book
Edited by Judd Apatow (Penguin Random House), $40
When Apatow cleaned out the home of his late friend and mentor, he stumbled on nearly four decades of personal journals, untold jokes, script snippets and hundreds of childhood photos. The resulting compilation is what he calls “the final major Garry Shandling project”—a treat for anyone who misses the influential star.

Over the Top: A Raw Journey to Self-Love
By Jonathan van Ness (HarperOne), $27.99
Van Ness went through some dark days before emerging as a human ray of sunshine on Netflix’s Queer Eye: He overcame childhood bullying, abuse, addiction, loss and an HIV diagnosis. His frank, funny life lessons and encouraging self-care advice will be a balm to a friend going through a tough year.

Dear Girls: Intimate Tales, Untold Secrets & Advice for Living Your Best Life
by Ali Wong (Random House), $27
Wong broke through with hilarious, taboo-busting standup specials filmed while she was hugely pregnant. Now she’s laying down some uncensored life wisdom about growing up, the indignities of dating in NYC and making it in a male-dominated field. Wong’s preschool-age daughters will have to wait a few years to benefit, but you probably know a woman who needs this now.

The Beautiful Ones
By Prince (Spiegel & Grau), $30
Substantial and beautifully designed, this is the memoir Prince was working on before his far-too-soon death at age 57. A unique portrait of a true artist’s evolution that any fan will treasure.

Friends Forever: The One About the Episodes (25th Anniversary Edition)
by Gary Susman, Jeannine Dillon and Bryan Cairns (Harper Design), $35
The ’90s sensation still fascinates. Put this photo-rich episode guide under the tree of a Gen Xer who rocked a Rachel in 1995, a millennial who grew up on the show or a Friends-curious teen who more recently caught it streaming.

FOR THE MARVEL FAN

The Stan Lee Story
by Roy Thomas (Taschen), $200
Want to make a grown man cry? Give him this 624-page, 17-inch-tall ode to the king of the Marvel universe, featuring hundreds of comics, archival photos and a novel-length essay by his Marvel successor, Rob Thomas. It’s a Hulk of a handsome book.
