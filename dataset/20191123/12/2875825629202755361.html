Ciara Phelan column: 'The end of a friendship can be much worse than a romantic break up'

'We talk about a romantic break-up for weeks and comfort one another but we don’t openly discuss or grieve the end of this other relationship'

23 Nov 2019, 12:24 by Ciara Phelan

The chat around Nadine Coyle’s friendship with the Girls Aloud crew is one of the reasons why I’m glued to watching her in the jungle this year.

When the line-up was announced I was excited about the juicy gossip she would spill, especially about her “feud” with Cheryl.

But then I saw how uncomfortable she appeared when campmates questioned her about the rumours which have circulated for years.

And I realised that, in reality, the end of a friendship can be one of the most painful things to go through – and shouldn’t be seen as a form of entertainment for others.

Friends come and go throughout your school years but once you hit your mid-20s, for a lot of people, especially women, that’s when you realise who deserves your loyalty.

And I think the death of a friendship is something that can have a huge impact on your life at that time and it becomes a private ache for so many.

We talk about a romantic break-up for weeks and comfort one another but we don’t openly discuss or grieve the end of this other relationship, which also needs attention in order to cope with.

We have this idea that they should last forever, that the bond with your BFF is unbreakable but as a society we are woefully blinded by the concept of forever.

But as you get older and wiser, you really begin to audit who and what kind of people you want by your side.

Which is something I had to do not too long ago, when I learned to let go of one friend. I always knew deep down she wasn’t great to me despite the time and effort I gave to her, whether it be a listening ear or a shoulder to cry on.

Looking back now, I know we were just mates for the convenience of others.

And since letting her go, it has been a weight off my shoulders.

It was frustrating and mentally draining for me because I never got anything back and felt taken advantage of as I treated her the same as I did all of my other friends.

But it’s the relationships you don’t want to lose but feel you have no other option but to walk away from that can cut the deepest.

The end of some friendships can be worse than a break-up, like losing a limb – especially if it’s sudden.

And the special memories of what you shared are now tainted.

But there comes a stage where you have to draw a line when you feel exhausted from trying to make it work and they don’t reach out to you when you need them the most.

And to stop allowing yourself to constantly be hurt over efforts not being reciprocated.

Yes, life can get in the way for a lot of people but if you value your friendship with someone you will always find the time to check in with them.

People fall out, romance and careers can get in the way, but none of those should be any reason to neglect a true friendship.

It’s only natural that some relationships have an expiry date, but when it gets to a point that you no longer recognise the person you once called a best friend then you shouldn’t feel guilty about walking away.

And know it’s totally normal to grieve the end of a friendship like you would for a romantic relationship.

At the end of the day, those who care for you and appreciate your friendship are the ones who matter the most.

But I’ve learned the lesson that we should all be more aware, gentle and acknowledge the difficulty of losing someone you believed would always be there for you.
