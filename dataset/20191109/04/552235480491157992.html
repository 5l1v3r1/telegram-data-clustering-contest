World War 2 veteran meets son of former POW pal after emotional appeal

Jimmy Johnstone, 98, met up with Sandy Petrie whose late father Bert was a fellow prisoner of war in Nazi-occupied Poland.

09 Nov 2019, 04:30 by Laura Paterson

A World War II veteran who appealed to be reunited with his fellow prisoners of war has met the son of one of his former comrades.

Jimmy Johnstone, 98, launched the appeal in August and was overwhelmed by the worldwide response.

He was looking for survivors of his ordeal. He was captured in France at the battle for Saint-Valery-en-Caux and witnessed men die as the PoWs were taken to camps in Nazi-occupied Poland in cattle trucks after marching hundreds of miles.

He endured five years in the camps, ending with an escape bid on a death march in temperatures of minus 28C, where prisoners who collapsed were shot, as camp commanders retreated into Germany.

The son of one of the four men with whom he tried to escape got in contact after hearing his appeal, and the men had an emotional meeting in Aberdeen, where they both live, organised by Scottish War Blinded.

Sandy Petrie, 73, spoke of his father Bert Petrie, who died 10 years ago aged 86.

Jimmy said: “Meeting Sandy was very emotional as I didn’t think I’d get any response to my appeal.

“We talked about Bert and life in the PoW camps. I told Sandy what a brave man his dad was. He stood up to the German guards and refused to work until they got more food.”

Bert served with the Gordon Highlanders in the 51st Highland Division at St Valery, alongside Jimmy, who served with the Royal Engineers.

 

Sandy said: “When I heard about the appeal, I quickly recognised it was my father being referred to and I was very, very interested to hear the story.

“They were some of the same stories my father had told me. I felt I’d like to meet this chap, and to meet him for my father’s sake too, as I know he would have wanted to meet Jimmy.

“It was very touching to hear him refer to my father. I have the greatest respect for Jimmy and the men and women of his generation, sadly a dwindling group. They saved our civilisation and way of life. It was a privilege to have met him.”

Jimmy thanked those who responded to the appeal and plans to write to as many as he can.

He said: “It is really important that as we approach Remembrance Day, we remember all men who fought in all battles, those that survived and those who died.”
