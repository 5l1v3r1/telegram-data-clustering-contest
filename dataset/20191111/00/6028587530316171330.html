Comparing The College Scorecard And PayScale’s College Salary Report: Four Key Differences

11 Nov 2019 by Michael T. Nietzel

The value of a college education continues to be a hot topic, debated by policy makers and pondered by students and families. These days, a popular measure of that value is to compare the earnings of individuals who’ve earned a college degree to those who haven’t. Another measure - increasingly invoked in college rating schemes such as Forbes or by policy organizations like Brookings and The Equality of Opportunity Project that track college-related social mobility - is to compare the earnings of students who attended one college versus another.

Two widely used alumni earnings databases are the Department of Education’s College Scorecard and PayScale’s College Salary Report. These websites are scoured by students and families evaluating their college options relative to future earnings potential. They’re relied on by researchers studying economic inequalities and socio-economic mobility. And they’re referenced by both college leaders and elected officials to justify various educational initiatives.

How comparable are these two databases? Would a prospective student reach the same conclusion about the payoff from attending Notre Dame if she consulted Scorecard or PayScale? How different would a university’s ROI look to a trustee, depending on which data source he used?

It turns out the methodologies behind the two databases are very different in four major ways so it should come as no surprise that the earnings estimates vary considerably - sometimes dramatically - depending on which source is used.

Scope of Included Institutions

Maintained by the Department of Education, the Scorecard includes all degree-granting, Title IV colleges (where students are eligible to receive federal grants or loans) and some schools closely approximating Title IV institutions.

In earlier years of its Salary Report, PayScale’s sample was criticized for being too small and unrepresentative. PayScale claims that its 2019-20 survey has now grown to a sample of 3.5 million respondents, representing more than 4,000 degree-granting colleges.

Source of the Data

The College Scorecard relies on the Integrated Postsecondary Education Data System (IPEDS) for much of its data. It gathers students’ earnings data from de-identified tax records and then aggregates them at the institutional level.

PayScale is a voluntary system where individuals complete an online survey and self-report their earnings and other data.

Students Included

The Scorecard collects data only on students who receive federal financial aid, a group likely to be less economically secure than students who aren’t eligible for such support. In addition, the Scorecard includes salaries for undergraduates who attended a given college, regardless of whether they graduated, dropped out, or transferred to another institution.

PayScale, on the other hand, includes only students who completed their bachelor’s (or associate’s) degree, and it doesn’t limit its sample to recipients of federal aid.

Time Period Covered

The Scorecard provides earnings estimates for a 6-10 year time period after a student’s attendance at given school. Typically, it is described as being a ten-year figure.

PayScale reports salaries for two time periods: an early-career salary (average post-graduation interval: three years) and a mid-career salary (average interval: 15.5 years).

How Do The Differences Matter?

As an illustration of how such methodological differences matter, consider the median earnings attributed to students from Forbes top 25 colleges.

The median mid-career salary for bachelor’s degree students, according to PayScale was $130,500 at Notre Dame (ranging from $112,200 at the University of Michigan to $158,200 at Harvey Mudd). The median early-career salary was $70,700 at the University of California-Berekely (ranging from $63,400 at Northwestern to $88,800 at Harvey Mudd).

Using the Scorecard, the median earnings was Dartmouth’s $75,500, with a range from Swarthmore’s $56,700 to MIT’s $104,700.

Given the differences in source, scope, sample and timing, differences between College Scorecard and PayScale estimates of college student earnings are to be expected. But the differences - even when restricted to similar time intervals - are often striking. Georgetown University’s Scorecard median is a whopping $27,100 greater than PayScale’s; MIT’s Scorecard median is $18,400 greater than its PayScale number. At Swarthmore, the reverse is true: PayScale’s estimate exceeds the Scorecard’s by $10,800; at Williams the difference is $8,500.

Alumni compensation is just one consideration in choosing where to attend college. But it’s a contributing factor to many decisions. Consumers are therefore advised to consult both sources for estimates of future earnings potential and to remember the limitations of each one.
