Kilmarnock WWII hero recalls brave bid to dismantle unexploded bomb on ship

David Craig and his fellow sailors got the bomb safely out on deck - then put the kettle on for a cuppa

11 Nov 2019, 09:00 by Tara Fitzpatrick

When David Craig was just 18 his quick thinking actions helped save many lives aboard a 1943 wartime vessel.

Now aged 94, the Second World War hero has spoken about the ordeal which could have cost him his life.

David, along with 13 other sailors, received the King’s Commendation for getting rid of an unexploded bomb when it crashed through the deck of his ship in Russia 76 years ago.

Now a great-grandfather of 10, Kilmarnock man David had joined the Dover Hill merchant ship as a radio officer.

The ship, loaded with weapons, military vehicles and explosives, formed part of a 28-vessel convoy which left Scotland for Russia on February 15, 1943.

The crew battled freezing conditions and storms and miraculously survived brutal attacks from the German Luftwaffe on their journey.

The ship delivered its cargo to Murmansk unscathed by the barrage of bombings before anchoring in Kola inlet, where it came under attack again two months later.

 

David recalled being blown across the deck as bombs fell.

“A 1000-pound bomb exploded at the side nearest me,” he said. “I was blown off my feet. I wasn’t conscious of this at the time but it blew me right across the ship and I landed on the steel deck with a wallop.

“Well, I think it knocked sense into me because I came to and I was starting to get up when one of the gunners came running down on to the deck and shouted ‘Are you alright, Sparks?’.

“I looked behind me and there was a nice hole through the steel deck where another 1000-pound bomb had gone right through. It had gone right through the deck below it and into the coal bunkers.”

The captain ordered David to signal to Murmansk and ask for bomb disposal personnel but none were available.

The crew were left to decide how to handle the life-threatening situation alone.

David said: “The captain gave everyone three choices: take a lifeboat to shore for safety, stay aboard and work the ship or volunteer to get the bomb out of the bunker.

“I knew it was a bit of a stupid thing to do so I hesitated for a minute but the officer next to me, who had a wife and two kids, stepped forward so what could I do but also join the squad? Nineteen of us volunteered.

“If the bomb had exploded I would have been blown to bits but I believe that there is always a time to be born and a time to die and I knew my time hadn’t come.”

He said: “We started digging back the coal, trying to find the bomb. We didn’t know we’d have 22 feet to dig before we got to it.

“Unfortunately, the Germans discovered what we were up to and bombed us again, hoping to set off the bomb we were digging for.

“Due to the bomb explosions and the concussion of our own guns, the coal fell back into the space where we were digging and things got difficult at times.”

After two long days and nights the team finally got the bomb up on deck.

The brave squad even precariously inscribed their names on the bomb before the disposal began.

 

He said: “I couldn’t roll the bomb over in case I set it off so I wrote my name using chalk around the detonator after carefully cleaning all the muck off.

“Most of the crew were Scottish or Londoners and the Londoners like a cup of tea before they do anything so, as we had the bomb on deck and had all written our names, we went for a cup of tea in the officers’ mess before we did anything else.”

David was then one of three volunteers who put himself forward to sup-port a Russian air force officer and remove the detonator.

He said: “He started to hammer the side of the detonator. Every time he hit it the hairs on my neck stood up straight against my duffle coat.

“He got it moving and unscrewed it and said the bomb was safe so we dumped it over the side into the Kola inlet, where it probably lies to this day.”

The Dover Hill finally left Kola in May and spent months in the White Sea before arriving in London in December 1943.

David later worked his way up to chief radio officer and sailed all over the world during the war.

When the war ended he came to settle in Kilmarnock and after leaving the Merchant Navy worked in the kiltwear industry.

Originally from Nairn, Moray, the grandfather-of-four now suffers the poor eyesight and is supported by the charity Scottish War Blinded.

But he still remembers his time at sea vividly.

“When I was in Russia I never thought of being courageous,” David said.

“All we wanted to do was to stay alive and stay in one bit.”
