The Dark History Behind The True Meaning Of Black Friday, Unveiled

12 Nov 2019 by Yimin Huang

Black Friday is commonly seen as a joyous occasion, depicted as a day when shoppers would crowd the malls after Thanksgiving, spending so much as to put retailers “in the black” for the year, per Huffpost.
However, the origin of Black Friday was not so positive. The early historical usage of the phrase had nothing to do with Christmas shopping, but instead, it referred to the market crash caused by falling gold prices back in 1869.
However, the modern reference that people are more familiar with dated back to the 1950s or 60s.
Traffic police coined the term in Philadelphia, as they saw it as a day of “traffic jams” and “intense crowding” in retail stores, according to Emmy-winning stylist and author David Zyla. He added that the term was featured in an ad in the 1966 issue of The American Philatelist, as stated in Huffpost.
Black Friday also meant that Christmas shopping season has sprung and the sidewalks and downtown stores would be over-crowded with shoppers from opening to closing time.

The original meaning of the term was also seen in a 1994 Philadelphia Inquirer article, where the late police reporter Joseph P. Barrett mentioned that “Black Friday” was used to “describe” the “terrible traffic conditions.”
The negative connotation of the term is evidenced again in an archived clip published in a 1951 issue of Factory Management and Maintenance. The day was described as a “bubonic plague” that led to a lot of absentees, as they had to rush to “get the production out” and fell sick out of exhaustion.
Over the years, there were even casualties and fatalities on Black Friday, giving it a more somber backstory. It was reported that there were 12 deaths and 117 injuries due to crowd clashes to date.
Now, though businesses have rebranded the term to give it a positive spin. Black Friday now has a reputation as a day full of fun and shopping.
