Braid: Despite attacks, Kenney's ideas aren't separatist or dangerous

12 Nov 2019, 23:26 by Don Braid, Calgary Herald

Premier Jason Kenney and hockey crank Don Cherry went off at nearly the same moment Saturday.

Cherry got more national attention and deserved every negative word. The media eruption showed what’s really important in this country — Coach’s Corner.

Kenney could hardly be ignored, though.

In a long and passionate speech in Red Deer, he laid out a series of prescriptions for improving Alberta’s health in Canada.

This was instantly painted as a path to separatism by the NDP and other critics. Kenney is, in their view, the Don Cherry of politicians — outdated and divisive.

But Kenney is actually being quite moderate. Everything on his long to-do list is permissible within Confederation.

You may like these ideas — Alberta pension plan, provincial police and tax collection, fixing the stabilization program — or you may truly hate them.

But to call them separatist is itself divisive. To say that a province can’t even discuss using its legitimate powers is what’s really un-Canadian.

The late premier Peter Lougheed, the object of modern hagiography from all sides, was once called a nation-wrecker for cutting oil shipments to the rest of Canada.

In 2012, Lougheed was named the best Canadian premier of the past 40 years.

Kenney’s proposals were studied and rejected during the Ralph Klein years. They were also largely scorned when the famous Firewall Letter was published in 2001.

But those ideas weren’t dismissed because they were invalid or improper. Most Albertans, despite their annoyances, just didn’t think they were necessary.

The tensions then were nothing like the mood today. Never have more Albertans felt so abandoned and suppressed by their national government.

There are three general reactions to Alberta’s sense of crisis:

  1. Just hope it all works out, which seems to be the NDP idea, even though in government they favoured several of Kenney’s measures;
  2. Things will never work out, so Alberta should separate. Hence, Wexit;
  3. There’s a better chance of working things out if Alberta pressures Ottawa by using dormant powers. Even if no settlement ensues, Alberta would be better able to protect its interests.

In that wider context, Kenney’s approach is the centrist one.

And yet, the criticism was immediate and unrestrained. Even the idea of a provincial police force was offered as evidence of separatism.

In that case, Ontario’s OPP is a separatist project. So is the proud Constabulary of Newfoundland and Labrador. (Quebec’s Surete is just part of the landscape, no matter what the provincial mood about Canada.)

Among many demands, Kenney asked Ottawa to lift the $60 per capita limit on fiscal stabilization grants.

This is a stunning inequity in a program allegedly designed to help provinces that suffer sudden and severe economic stress.

When Alberta’s revenues fell by $6.9 billion because of the oil crash, the feds paid only $251.4 million — less than the province received in 1986-87, when there was no population cap.

Former NDP finance minister Joe Ceci made Alberta’s case forcefully.

“In order for this program to work for Albertans, we must revisit or remove this cap so that provinces receive adequate support when revenues fall sharply,” he said in a 2018 letter to federal Finance Minister Bill Morneau.

Ottawa refused, of course.

The NDP in office had many of the same concerns as Kenney — equalization, stabilization, pipelines and much else.

But according to Opposition Leader Rachel Notley, Kenney’s demands are now “dangerous.”

Albertans will have many legitimate questions about these proposals.

Is Kenney indeed using them only for political advantage? Will the UCP employ more provincial power to constrain social rights, make even deeper budget cuts?

Most controversial is the thought of a provincial pension system, hived off from CPP.

People would have to be convinced that the benefits would be larger and the contributions smaller. Punishing Ottawa by withdrawing provincial CPP contributions wouldn’t be reason enough.

These are big ideas with many uncertainties around them. Even after study by Kenney’s Fair Deal panel, they may fail to pass a provincial referendum.

But Albertans should not, at this superheated moment, scorn the proposals as un-Canadian and dangerous when they are nothing of the sort. That just encourages separatism.
