Apprentice star tells nurses they should work more if they're using food banks

Lord Alan Sugar's Apprentice adviser Claude Littner sparked a Twitter storm when he told nurses struggling on low pay they should take second jobs

13 Nov 2019, 00:29 by Talia Shadwell, Matthew Dresch, https://www.mirror.co.uk/authors/talia-shadwell/, https://www.mirror.co.uk/authors/matthew-dresch/

An adviser on Lord Alan Sugar's BBC show The Apprentice has sparked fury by suggesting nurses struggling to get by should work more.

Claude Littner has been criticised for his suggestion nurses should work more hours or get a part-time job to make ends meet.

His comments that it would be "less demeaning than visiting a food bank" saw the British-American businessman come under fire today.

Mr Littner, who works as an adviser on the reality show, doubled down on his views on struggling nurses on Twitter despite a flood of criticism.

He wrote: "If a nurse or anyone else need to make ends meet, nothing wrong with extending hours or finding part time work."

 

Many critics were quick to point out that many NHS nurses already work long hours for low pay, and their shift work affected their ability to find second jobs.

But Mr Littner wrote there was: "nothing wrong with extending hours or finding part time work."

His comments come as reports of nurses forced to resort to using Britain's foodbanks.

 

Twitter uses slammed Mr Littner's suggestions, telling him shift-working nurses were "exhausted."

Some warned that forcing nurses to take on extra work would leave them tired and stressed, and ultimately could endanger patients.

However Mr Littner refused to back down, telling the Twittersphere his own niece was a nurse.

The British-American businessman claimed she managed to hold down a side-hustle, tweeting: "My niece is a nurse & has an online business that generates income.. result torrent of abuse!"

In a later post, defending himself from the onslaught of criticism, he said: "No good deed goes unpunished! I responded to a nurse who stated that she could not make ends meet. I suggested that as a stop gap she might find some part time work to make ends meet."

 

But some who responded to Mr Littner's tweets suggested the businessman was out of touch.

Twitter user Claire Yuen wrote: "This response surprises me.. we can't all work for Lord Alan Sugar and have a 6-figure salary!

"I thought you would have expressed the disgust in how little our NHS and emergency service workers actually earn. My opinion on you has changed Claude, what a shame."

Mr Littner retweeted some Twitter users who supported his views, with some saying that nurses had made a choice to follow that career path and shouldn't "whinge" about the outcome.

Mr Littner also wrote that he respected nurses, acknowledging there is a national shortage and that he believed they should be paid properly: "Nursing is a fine profession. No question nurses work long hours, take on responsibility and should be remunerated accordingly."

His comments come as leaders warn Brexit will bring fresh pressures on the NHS, as EU staff who are plugging massive shortages in the UK's hospitals face uncertain futures and fresh immigration costs to move here.

And the number of people applying to study nursing in England has fallen by more than 13,000 since 2016.

The Mirror reported earlier this week that Labour has promised to recruit 24,000 extra nurses if Jeremy Corbyn becomes PM – while the Tories will fall 10,000 short if Boris Johnson stays in power.

Hospital waiting lists are at an all-time high of 4.4 million, and on Tory Health Secretary Matt Hancock’s watch 18-week waits for treatment have soared more than 150,000, from 504,000 to 662,000.
