First Ebola vaccine approved

European regulators have approved the first immunization against the deadly Ebola virus. The drug, made by US company Merck, has already been administered on an emergency basis, including to quell the ongoing outbreak in the Democratic Republic of the Congo. The drug regulator’s approval means that the product can now be stockpiled and, potentially, distributed more widely — in particular in Africa. Seven other Ebola vaccines are in various stages of clinical testing.

Nature | 4 min read

Influential research could be ignored by EPA

The administration of US President Donald Trump is doubling down on its controversial plan to forbid the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) from basing regulations on certain studies that don't make their underlying data publicly available. The restriction would make unusable some foundational public-health research that relies on keeping people’s confidential information private. The New York Times reports that a draft supplement to the proposal would apply the rule more broadly.

“This means the E.P.A. can justify rolling back rules or failing to update rules based on the best information to protect public health and the environment, which means more dirty air and more premature deaths,” says Paul Billings of the American Lung Association. But EPA officials have said they are motivated by a desire for transparency and reproducibility.

The New York Times | 6 min read

More plastic than fish in marine nursery

Larval fish are eating tiny pieces of plastic in the waters off Hawaii. Bits of plastic outnumbered baby fish by seven to one in areas known to be important nurseries for marine life. Researchers dissected hundreds of larval fish and found that almost one in ten had ingested plastic — including species that are food to birds and other fish.

Features & opinion

A drug factory in a box

Engineer Govind Rao’s solution to spiralling health spending is a sleek, stainless-steel briefcase that contains a series of vials, syringes and tubes. Add a source of electricity, some freeze-dried cell parts and a pinch of DNA, and the portable devices allow anyone to start making sophisticated drugs for just a few dollars. And Rao isn't the only one working on this problem: teams at universities and hospitals around the world have been experimenting with producing on-demand pharmaceuticals.

Nature | 11 min read

PhDs: the tortuous truth

The results of Nature’s fifth survey of PhD students tell a story of personal reward and resilience against a backdrop of stress, uncertainty and struggles with depression and anxiety. For the more than 6,300 respondents, the positives generally outweigh the negatives: 75% of them said they were at least somewhat satisfied with their decision to get a doctorate.

Nature | 12 min read

Welcome to Art’s butterfly world

Evolutionary biologist and ecologist Art Shapiro has been observing butterflies at 10 sites in north-central California, pretty much every 2 weeks, for 47 years. In that time, he has single-handedly created the longest-running butterfly monitoring project in North America, and become something of a legend himself.

Los Angeles Times | 9 min read

“[2018] was a terrible—perhaps even catastrophic-- butterfly year at all elevations and no, we don’t know why.” Read Shapiro’s straight-talking yearly reports on Art Shapiro's Butterfly Site.

Podcast: They fight for science

Bambang Hero Saharjo, winner of the 2019 John Maddox prize, is a lead expert witness on illegal peatland fires in Indonesia. “His work serves not only to bring justice in individual cases, but has inspired a vision of what is possible in Indonesia,” says environmental policy researcher Jacob Phelps. Pharmacist and broadcaster Olivier Bernard, who has campaigned against quack medicine in Canada, won early career stage award. They tell the Working Scientist podcast why they stand up for science.

Nature | 19 min listen

The John Maddox prize is a joint initiative between Nature and the charity Sense about Science, which challenges the misrepresentation of science and evidence in public life. It is named in honour of former Nature editor Sir John Maddox.

Correspondence: your letters to Nature

Give CRISPR egg donors a say

The debate raging about ‘CRISPR babies’ and germline gene editing is ignoring the women who donate the eggs for research, write bioethicists Emilia Niemiec and Heidi Carmen Howard. Egg donation comes with health risks and ethical dilemmas, and women must be given the opportunity to make an informed choice and contribute to decisions.

Darwin got others into Nature

Charles Darwin didn’t just contribute to Nature himself. “Roughly half of Darwin’s contributions to Nature were transcripts (with due credit) of reports and findings sent to him,” note biologists Antonio Marques and Klaus Hartfelder.

Research culture must be kinder for all

Wellcome is right to call for more kindness in research, but the drive for excellence is not the main problem, writes geneticist Serena Nik-Zainal. “Some senior scientists might find that conceding a tendency to ‘overvalue’ excellence is easier than admitting to, say, turning a blind eye to poorly conducted investigations, or closing ranks around prominent scientists.”
