Mum-of-three will never see her baby after going blind due to Tory austerity

The mother had been awaiting treatment at a hospital in Southampton but ended up losing her sight due to a series of issues. The woman, who remained anonymous, is believed to have been awarded £3.2m in compensation.

13 Nov 2019, 16:09 by Elaine McCahill, https://www.mirror.co.uk/authors/elaine-mccahill/

A mum-of-three will never see her baby after going blind while waiting for treatment.

Labour ’s shadow health minister today shared the heartbreaking example of the human cost of Tory austerity .

Jon Ashworth said long delays in getting treatment had led to several patients in Southampton going blind.

Mr Ashworth said: “Recently we heard how patients at a hospital in Southampton went blind and suffered severe sight loss because of longer waits for treatment.

"Indeed a pregnant mother was left almost completely blind waiting for care. It was reported ‘she has since given birth and has never seen her daughter’s face.”

The woman, in her 30s, is believed to be the same mum at the centre of a compensation case last month.

She was awarded £3.2million after delays in her treatment for her eye condition led to her going blind.

The woman first went to the optician in June 2016 after noticing her sight worsening.

She was diagnosed with glaucoma and referred to Southampton Eye Hospital. She was prescribed eye-drops and told she would have follow-up appointments.

However, it wasn't until more than 18 months later that she saw a consultant, by which time she was registered blind and the sight loss could not be reversed.

She gave birth to her third child after she went blind.

An internal NHS serous investigation report last year found 15 other patients were left blind or with worsened sight as a result of long delays in identifying their risk factors.

The report showed more than 4,000 patients had not been seen as quickly as needed.

Victoria Hydon of Moore Blatch solicitors acted on the woman's behalf and said: “The impact on our client has been devastating.

“It’s a complete tragedy and there is no reason why this should have happened.

“We are pleased to have secured our client with the compensation she deserves.

“The sizeable compensation reflects the harm that has been done and the support and care she will need for the rest of her life.”

A statement from the University Hospital Southampton NHS Foundation Trust said: “This is a significant national problem and, like many trusts, we are working closely with NHS England and NHS Improvement to address the growing capacity and recruitment issues in ophthalmology.

“We have taken steps to address the backlog in follow-up appointments and continue to introduce measures to make further progress, with all of our patients now risk-assessed to ensure those most in need are seen at the earliest opportunity.”

The statement also added that 88% of trusts have backlogs in diabetes and glaucoma and there are more than 80 consultant vacancies in England.
