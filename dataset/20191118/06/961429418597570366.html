Trust predicts 10pc deficit for at least next four years

18 Nov 2019, 06:14 by Alison Moore
  • QVH predicts £7m deficit for several years but hopes to breakeven in 2023-24 if given support
  • Has not agreed control total for this year so cannot access provider sustainability funding
  • Missed its control total last year and instead ran deficit of £5.2m

One of England’s smallest trusts is facing a £7m annual deficit — roughly 10 per cent of its turnover — for at least the next four years, and is struggling to access sustainability funding.

This month’s board papers for Queen Victoria Hospital Foundation Trust, a specialist trust based in East Grinstead in Sussex, revealed it expects its deficit to be £7.4m for 2019-20 and then to run at around £7m for the next five years, although it added it could breakeven in 2023-24 if it was given support. 

However, the trust does not have an agreed control total with NHS Improvement for this year and is therefore not eligible for provider sustainability funding.

The trust’s chief executive is working with the Sussex sustainability and transformation partnership — Sussex Health and Care Partnership — “to encourage them to recognise the underlying position of the trust and provide realistic support to a sustainable breakeven position in 2023-24”.

Last year, the trust missed its control total of £0.6m surplus and had a deficit of £5.2m — approximately 7.4 per cent of its turnover of around £70.2m. Financial sustainability is rated at a maximum 25 in its risk ratings.

Although the trust did not agree a control total this year, HSJ understands this is because it was still working through its likely deficit at the time it was being set. This problem may be resolved for 2020-21, which would allow the trust to access financial recovery funding. However, the prospect of underlying deficits continuing for so long may raise some eyebrows at NHSI, which is pushing trusts to accept more stretching trajectories.

The trust still has to identify some of the cost improvement programme savings it needs to make this year.

The financial deficit is just one of the issues the QVH faces. It developed as a specialist burns and plastic surgery site after World War II pilots were treated there and now provides specialist services for a wide area, as well as having a minor injuries unit.

But it does not have some 24/7 diagnostic services or other specialties colocated with its services and is increasingly working with the Brighton and Sussex University Hospitals Trust to ensure patients have access to round-the-clock care and expert opinion. In some cases, consultants’ job plans cover both trusts.

This summer, it temporarily stopped taking paediatric burns inpatients and, over the next few years, it expects its adult burns inpatients service to move to Brighton. Outpatient burns services are unaffected.

In 2018, a review found its reported waiting list was a quarter higher than it had been reporting and it had 145 52-week waiters. In September 2019, it still had 25 patients waiting more than 52 weeks, most of whom were in its plastic surgery division, and its 18-week wait performance was below 82 per cent.

The trust declined to comment.
