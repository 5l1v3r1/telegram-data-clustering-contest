Election already defying the traditional patterns

16 Nov 2019, 01:00

Nominations have now closed and we can expect intensified campaigning in what could prove to be the most significant Westminster election of recent times.

The outcome of the December 12 poll could determine the future direction of Brexit with far-reaching economic and political implications for people living on this island.

Boris Johnson goes into this election seeking a clear majority that will allow him to pass the withdrawal deal he agreed with the EU last month and move on to the next stage, trade negotiations.

While his chances have improved since Nigel Farage rowed back from his pledge to stand in every constituency, the presence of Brexit Party candidates in some of the Labour Leave-voting areas could prove a problem as the Tories need to take some of these seats.

However, Mr Farage looks less of a threat generally as his intervention so far has been less assured than in previous campaigns.

Mr Johnson would like the contest to be defined as being one between him and Jeremy Corbyn, who should be in a strong position but leads a deeply divided party.

The allegations of anti-Semitism within sections of Labour are a problem for him as is the lack of a single clear message on Brexit.

There are plenty of Tories attacking Mr Corbyn, indeed that is to be expected in what could be a particularly vicious campaign.

But what is remarkable is the number of Labour candidates who are speaking out publicly against their leader with some former MPs even advocating that people vote for Boris Johnson.

All this suggests we are in for a very unusual election and it could be hard to predict what will happen given that the normal voting patterns are unlikely to apply.

In Northern Ireland the central issue of EU withdrawal has galvanised Remain parties to the extent that we are seeing what are effectively pacts in key constituencies in a bid to maximise anti-Brexit representation.

For the DUP, this issue is plainly one that it prefers not to dwell on too much.

The subject of Brexit is not even referenced in the party's first election broadcast, an incredible omission in the circumstances.

It may be that the DUP, now warning of a threat to the Union, has belatedly realised that the majority in Northern Ireland who voted Remain called it right in 2016.
