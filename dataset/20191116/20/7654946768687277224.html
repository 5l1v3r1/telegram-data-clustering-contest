Astros’ sign-stealing scandal: MLB facing major questions

16 Nov 2019, 20:51 by Ken Davidoff

Imagine if the Patriots dominated the past 18 years of the NFL, tallying equal parts glory and controversy, only they didn’t feature a GOAT quarterback married to a model, a head coach who makes Emperor Palpatine of “Star Wars” fame seem like a softie or a trouble-finding owner who revels in a good public fight.

That’s why Major League Baseball has an Astros problem it must remedy in swift fashion.

Just as the Brandon Taubman scandal and its ugly aftermath dominated the World Series until the Nationals’ remarkable comeback in Houston, you couldn’t stroll more than 10 feet at this past week’s general managers’ meetings without bumping into a non-Astros executive livid with the allegations logged by pitchers Mike Fiers and Danny Farquhar (in The Athletic), and soon after bolstered by video evidence, that the 2017 Astros had utilized an illegal, electronic sign-stealing system at Minute Maid Park.

Now the Yankees and their fans feel their AL Championship Series loss that season — during which they won all three games at Yankee Stadium and lost all four games at Minute Maid — didn’t result from fair play.

We’ll get into the ethics, legalities and more, yet the biggest downside to this mess, from a bottom-line perspective, is its lack of upside.

No evidence exists that the Astros’ rogue persona transcends baseball’s standard audience in a way similar to the Patriots’ graduation into “Saturday Night Live,” bestselling books and enough GIFs to equip a small country. The Pats’ villainy, the questions about their legitimacy thanks to Spygate, Deflategate and the like, draws eyeballs thanks to the larger-than-life stars of the saga in Tom Brady, Bill Belichick and Robert Kraft.

The Astros’ infamy features no such breakout performers, sucking considerable time and energy from their opponents as well as commissioner Rob Manfred’s office without producing any tangible reward.

In honor of the Astros’ four playoff appearances in the past five seasons under their current leadership group, let’s tackle four prominent questions surrounding this storm.

1. What would really hurt the Astros and deter future evildoers?

It starts with a loss of draft picks, the lifeline to any team’s hope at sustainability. Lucky for Manfred, he already spelled this out in the memo he sent to clubs in the wake of the Red Sox’s Apple Watch scandal two-plus years ago, the details of which he publicized on Sept. 15, 2017: “Future violations of this type will be subject to more serious sanctions, including the possible loss of draft picks.”

Lo and behold, just six days into that future, it sure appears that the Astros committed such violations against Farquhar and the White Sox, which we know thanks to the amazing work of Twitter baseball detective @Jomboy_.
