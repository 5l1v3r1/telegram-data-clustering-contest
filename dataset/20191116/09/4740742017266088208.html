The theories behind why Bristol is called Bristol

It hasn't always had the same name

16 Nov 2019, 09:00 by Tristan Cork

Ah Bristol. It’s a city, a county and a state of mind.

It’s given its name to a car, a way of tidying and making secure, a way of categorising poo and a rhyming slang for breasts.

As a place, it’s been replicated across the world, from Peru to Canada. Here in the original Bristol, the word Bristol is said so much it’s easy to forget what it means as a word.

So what does it mean? We know it so well, but where does the word Bristol come from?

Just as Bristol itself is a city of contrasts and diversity, the word itself has several different theories to its origin.

Firstly, it never used to be called Bristol. The very first ‘city’ was up in Leigh Woods, in a beautiful spot with the Avon Gorge on one side, Nightingale Valley on the other, and defended on a third side by a bank and ditch fort.

Back in those Celtic British and Roman days, the place was called Caer Odor, which is said to mean ‘the city of the gap’ - and it’s probable the gap in this case refers to Avon Gorge.

Back in those days, before the Saxons came, the people living around the river spoke an early form of Welsh, but a proposed etymology for Bristol comes from further afield. The Irish had a strong influence in Bristol’s history - it was closely linked through trade, and St Patrick himself was originally from these parts before being taken to Ireland as a slave.

One theory that has been proposed is that Bristol’s name is Irish - from the two words Braos and Tuile, which apparently mean a ‘stream through a chasm’.

But while this theory has bubbled up into a few online lists, it appears to have no basis in reality.

Etymologists and language experts tend to base their theories on tracing the changing ways names were spelled through time - and it is by doing this we can sort their origins.

Back in medieval times, Bristol had a variety of spellings, which often included a ‘C’ or a ‘G’ after the ‘Bri’.

Indeed, the word ‘Brygstow’ or ‘Brigstow’ is in use in the city today, albeit in a kind of renewal rather than survival.

This supports the theory Bristol evolved from the two early English words for Bridge and Stow (meaning meeting place).

So Bristol simply means ‘the meeting place at the bridge’. The spot across the River Avon roughly where Bristol Bridge stands now is likely to have had a bridge for millennia, and the early town - which boasted a huge population of around 10,000 back in the 13th century - would have grown up around that key crossing point.

But we're not quite done - how this became ‘Bristol’ is a matter of some debate.

The story goes that everyone else called it, and wrote it as Brigstow, or Bristow, but because Bristolians have a penchant for adding the letter ‘L’ to the end of vowel-ending words, that morphed over time into Bristol.

However, Brian Iles, who has spent decades researching the unique ‘Terminal L’ in the Bristol dialect, isn’t convinced.

He said there is no definitive evidence of the Terminal L being in use in Bristol before the 19th century, centuries after Bristow became Bristol.

So, we're all clear now, yes? Good.
