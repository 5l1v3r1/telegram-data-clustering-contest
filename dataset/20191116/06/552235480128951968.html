The Corinthian spirit that made Queen’s Park special has gone with their amateur status - Gordon Parks

A former Queen's Park player, Gordon Parks reflects on the momentous end to their amateur status.

16 Nov 2019, 06:00 by Gordon Parks, https://www.dailyrecord.co.uk/authors/gordon-parks/

AS a Queen’s Park alumnus it’s with a heavy heart we hear they’ve cast their Corinthian values to the wind.

Welcome Spiders, to the sordid world of professional football where the vulgarity of it all is about to be laid bare.

In the end it was dressed up as being centred around survival but the truth is something much more basic, it was about the money and the winning.

At an Extraordinary General Meeting on Thursday, my old club voted to bring 152 years of proud amateur status to an end – and for 91 per cent of the members there’s only shame.

What former custodians such as Eddie Hunter made of it all is anyone’s guess but “Off his Heddie” as he was known would probably have had a few expletives aired when the news filtered through.

There was a uniqueness about Queen’s, a true bastion of tradition who showed the game was something more than buying and selling, who can get what out of it in the horse-trading world which has been a race to the bottom in moral standards.

My debut was against Ayr United and after a career of hearing all about this historic Hampden institution, it was a proud moment.

Then Ross Caven shouted seconds before kick-off for me to get my shirt outside my shorts.

It was with an indignant rage that the order came through and a man who played 592 games and went on to become president proceeded to explain that it’s always been this way. To a man, shirts outside the shorts and as for the pants, for 72 minutes before being hauled off, I was.

Caven played 592 games, decades of service, Richie Sinclair the same, Kevin McGoldrick, my best mate since we met as five-year-olds at primary school, gave his career to the Spiders and this was written about them all a few years ago by another past president.

“I can’t overstate my admiration for these guys. All of them had chances to turn professional yet stayed with us.

“Why was this? It is difficult to say. Loyalty? Love for the club? The realisation that the grass may not be greener? Who knows.

“There is no doubt that each of them are proud men with a strong personality; perhaps that is important. Ross is a past president, Richard sits on the club committee.”

 

It’s a brilliant passage. It says everything about what Queen’s were all about.

It was about the service, the camaraderie and about playing for the love of the game.

Nostalgia is one thing and there were some realities which the club have had to confront.

The biggest carrot they offered potential signings was the chance to grace Hampden every second week. That has now gone due to the sale of the national stadium to the SFA but it’s not as though the club is scratching about for the cash to keep them-
selves afloat.

There was a need to protect themselves and their youth structure which had big clubs cherry-picking the best of their talent but there could have been another way.

Money makes the world go round and much has been made of their failure to be land any transfer fees for a host of youngsters who’ve gone on to bigger things. But that again is a bit of a myth.

It’s very rare for any club from Leagues One and Two to develop players who land them huge transfer windfalls.

It almost never happens at all.

What Queen’s can now do is attract boys who’d normally never consider them by throwing a few bob their way.

In my day it was expenses on a Thursday night, some envelopes were bigger than others but that’s a road to go down another day.

They operate in a market where it’s relative pocket money for lower-league players, but they have now heaped a bit of pressure on themselves.

From this moment on they’ll need to find the cash to be the competitive club they crave to be and that’s a situation which puts their long-term security at risk.

There are many shaking their heads and throwing their bowler hats to the floor in disgust as this week’s development.

And with it a warning: Alea iacta est. The die is cast.
