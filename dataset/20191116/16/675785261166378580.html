Heartbreaking photos of Australia's deadly bush fires as whole towns are devastated

Bushfires are ripping through New South Wales and Queensland in Australia- wreaking havoc and causing extensive damage that has been captured in a series of intense photographs

16 Nov 2019, 16:49 by Milo Boyd, Naimah Archibald-Powell, https://www.mirror.co.uk/authors/milo-boyd/, https://www.mirror.co.uk/authors/niamah-archibald-powell/

The devastation that has been wreaked on the Australian bush has been captured in a series of alarming photographs.

Thousands of fire fighters have been battling a ferocious blaze that has gripped New South Wales and Queensland on the east coast for several days.

As of Friday morning four people had lost their lives in the natural disaster, with flames made up of 1,600 individual bushfires stretching out across the states.

Photos taken in NSW show orange and red clouds of heat swallowing up every inch of vegetation, structure and land that is visible in the frame.

In apocalyptic seeming scenes fire tornadoes can be seen spinning and spewing out clouds of smoke, flames and debris.

 

People have been fleeing the fires that have melted road signs and wrecked hundreds of homes.

Animals can be seen lying on the ground confused and seemingly terrified by the raging flames that surround them.

In one photo a baby kangaroo appears alone and lost, its mouth surrounded by soot.

While wildfires and bushfires are a part of Australia’s natural ecosystem, the current blazes are unprecedented in terms of their intensity.

 

In a direct sense the cause of the fires are either the result of a stray, inadvertently caused spark, or an arsonist - with the country's Centre for Research in Bushfire and Arson suggesting around half of Australia's annual 62,000 bushfires are probably deliberately caused.

The reason why they are burning for longer, hotter and more intensely than ever is because of climate change.

Like the UK, Australia is getting hotter and is experiencing more droughts - a combination that creates a tinderbox like environment for bushfires.

Thanks to tree loss, smoke and the perils of heat, approximately 350 koala bears are expected to have perished in the past week.

 

This comes after reports that nearly 5,000 acres of an area which acted as a natural habitat for koalas was destroyed during the fires.

More than $500,000AU has been raised on GoFundMe to help facilitate the rehabilitation of the surviving koalas, but as the fires become more regular, their future is increasingly uncertain.

An estimated 2.5 million acres of farmland and bushland have been destroyed in total.

Among the thousands of firefighters risking their lives to save communities and land is pregnant volunteer Kat Robinson-Williams, who refuses to sit out of firefighting operations despite expecting a baby.

 

Aircraft such as boeings and helicopters are being used to fly across Australia and dispel water or pink retardant material across the affected areas in a bid to help combat the flames.

As the pink substance is sprayed and falls down to ground level, the particles spread and swallow up homes, cars, pavements and entire towns.

More than 300 homes have been destroyed according to the most recent statistics.

One of these homes belongs to Oscar-winning actor Russell Crowe who reassured concerned fans on Twitter that he, his family and his animals were safe from the flames.

Poorer parts of the country have unsurprisingly been harder hit by the fires.

Fairfield was singled out as one of the most deprived areas in Sydney in a report published earlier this year.

The area, which is currently experiencing fires described as “catastrophic”, is in the midst of a debate about fire fighter funding.

As these deadly infernos become more common and continue to wreck livelihoods, it is economically deprived areas like Fairfield which will suffer and burn the most.
