Defence chiefs to buy £75m laser to protect against 'Russian drone swarm attack'

EXCLUSIVE: The state-of-the art equipment will defend civilian airfields, radar dishes and combat jets - and can also stop future chaos that hit Gatwick a year ago

16 Nov 2019, 17:36 by Sean Rayment

Defence chiefs are set to buy a £75million laser capable of protecting the UK from a ­Russian drone swarm attack.

Top brass want the state-of-the-art weapon, which can defend ­military and civilian airfields, radar dishes and combat jets from enemy strikes.

It would guard against the chaos experienced at Gatwick a year ago.

A source said: “The main drone threat is from China and Russia.

“We know they are looking at mass drone attacks in the event of war. A single drone carrying a small explosive charge could take out a radar dish or destroy a jet.

“ISIS has used crude but effective drones in Syria. They are getting smaller and more advanced.”

 

A US computer-controlled laser located, tracked and destroyed drones in minutes during a secret US military exercise last week.

The test, at Fort Sill, Oklahoma, is believed to be the first time a laser has been able to shoot down a large number of “enemy drones”.

The laser – called ATHENA, or Advanced Test High Energy Asset – has been developed by aerospace giant ­Lockheed Martin.

It can be fixed to a building or mounted on a vehicle so it can be moved around.

The device can be fired at rockets, other vehicles, small boats – and even humans.

Britain has already invested more than £130million in its own laser weapon programme.

But it will not be ready for combat operations for at least 10 years, and defence sources admit a decision might be made to buy the US version.

 

Two weeks ago it emerged more than 150 drone attacks had been launched in the UK in three years.

One source said: “Although no decision has yet been taken, it is evident 10 years is too long to wait.

"We need to start looking at what we can buy off the shelf.”

The Government is concerned about the threat of drone swarms against air bases, parked aircraft, missile batteries and radars.

The MoD said: “Laser and radio frequency technologies have the potential to revolutionise the battlefield by offering powerful and cost-effective weapons systems to our forces.”

The system may be tried out first on Navy ships and Army vehicles.
