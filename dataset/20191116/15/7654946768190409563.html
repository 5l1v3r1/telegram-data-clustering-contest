How you should best use a team’s record against the spread

16 Nov 2019, 15:31 by VSiN

Is Ohio State’s gaudy 8-1 record against the point-spread (ATS) an early indicator for a national championship?

You might think that’s a questionable query. But Louisiana-Lafayette is also 8-1 ATS, and nobody’s talking about the Ragin’ Cajuns in those terms. Records against the spread reflect performance versus expectations, not necessarily overall team quality.

But what about obviously very good or great teams that consistently outperform high expectations? Could that impressive combination mean something? Heading into Saturday’s game at Rutgers (3:30 p.m., BTN), the Buckeyes are following in the footsteps of other recent champions.

LSU went 11-2 ATS in advance of its 2003 national championship. Vince Young and Texas matched that 11-2 ATS performance with their 2005 title. Ditto for Tim Tebow and Florida in 2008. Schedule length increased after that. Auburn’s 2010 championship team finished 10-4 ATS. Florida State ended 11-3 ATS in 2013. Ohio State went 10-5 ATS the following season.

Alabama’s 2017 title team finished just 6-8 ATS, the only record below .500 in the past 11 seasons, according to the pull-down standings pages at Covers.com. Going back to 2003, national champions finished their seasons 137-82-1, for a sterling 63 percent mark against the spread.

Point spreads are supposed to be the great equalizer. History has shown that the eventual champions are often underestimated by the composite sum of market influences, even when they’re media-friendly, high-profile programs. Consistently clearing high expectations is a sign of greatness.

Current ATS records for the top 10 teams in this week’s playoff rankings: Ohio State 8-1, Minnesota 6-2-1, Clemson 7-3, LSU 6-3, Utah 6-3, Georgia 5-4, Oregon 5-4, Penn State 5-4, Alabama 4-5, Oklahoma 4-5. In fairness, Minnesota hasn’t been clearing “high” hurdles, and will have to string together upsets to maintain dark-horse status. Ohio State, Clemson, and LSU are obviously thoroughbreds. Utah’s loss at USC suggest the Utes probably aren’t.

Should handicappers use ATS records when picking games? Though this championship indicator is interesting, VSiN still suggests you use them to evaluate “the market” rather than teams.

Don’t think, “Louisiana-Lafayette must be fantastic, it’s covered 89 percent of the time.” Instead, ask what the market’s been missing about Louisiana-Lafayette. Whey are the Ragin’ Cajuns so underrated? Has the line finally caught up, or is there still value?

If the line has caught up, then earlier ATS results are just trivia. Maybe Louisiana-Lafayette (or Ohio State) was underrated, but won’t be moving forward. Successful handicapping is always about reading teams and pricing.

You already know who’s 8-1 ATS this season. Here are other money-makers entering the week:

7-2 ATS: Auburn, Kansas State, Oklahoma State, Tulane. 6-2 ATS: Navy and Minnesota (Gophers actually 6-2-1). 7 -3 ATS: Clemson, Central Michigan, Florida Atlantic, Illinois, Western Kentucky.

6-3 ATS: LSU, Utah, Appalachian State, Ball State, Cincinnati, Colorado State, Indiana, Kentucky, Louisiana Tech, Memphis, Oregon State, Pittsburgh, San Jose State, Utah, Wyoming.

It’s not too early to start handicapping the Final Four. Respecting useful indicators will help you bet smart.
