Keeper of the bodies: Meet the man who greets the dead at Melbourne's morgue

16 Nov 2019, 13:00 by Erin Pearson

Classic ’80s hits ring through the halls of Melbourne's morgue when Peter Bury works the night shift.

Donning navy scrubs and knee-high gumboots, the forensic technical officer greets a steady flow of bodies as they arrive.

He is the keeper of the dead; responsible for a body's first 24 hours inside the coroner’s morgue as pathologists and forensic experts battle the clock to decipher the cause of death for more than 6500 Victorians a year.

Some of the arrivals might be victims of a violent homicide; others are newborns; some have taken their own lives.

About 20 arrive every day, and afternoons and evenings are busiest, Mr Bury says.

“Most cases come in from public areas and houses, and are zipped up inside a bag. Others come in from hospitals and are often wrapped in the sheet they were laying on.

“We’re the first to touch and see them."

If the person had agreed to donate their organs or tissue, then the process is time-sensitive.

“If they’re a donor case, we have 24 hours to take bones and tissues,” he says.

On his weekends off, Mr Bury indulges a passion he’s had for more than 20 years: professional motorsport photography.

He has travelled the country to capture Formula One and V8 Supercar racing and has documented the people who rally through the outback in ageing rigs for the Variety Big Bash.

For more than 25 years, though, the Blackburn father has also taken care of the dead.

“What's normal for me is confronting for most people,” he says.

Mr Bury’s career began with exotic zoo and farm animals as a young university graduate in the early 1990s. He began carrying out autopsies on animals that died suddenly, or when the threat of peculiar disease loomed.

Racehorses in need of blood work and cases of extreme cruelty were among his workload.

But as outsourcing of theanimal work began, he took a job working with human corpses at Victoria's Coronial Services Centre, home to the Victorian Institute of Forensic Medicine, and the coroner’s office.

As a university student, Mr Bury was among RMIT’s first intake of medical laboratory science students and was offered hands-on experience at the Institute of Forensic Medicine – a world-leading provider of forensic pathology and scientific services for the coroner and the justice system.

His first weeks of placement have never left him.

“I hadn't seen a person who had died before, except for a couple of grandparents in a funeral home as a kid," he says. "It was a bit confronting in the first place.

“These days you sort of become used to it; you’re less taken by what you see.”

A walk through Mr Bury’s office is not for the faint-hearted: the air smells wretched.

In one corner of the windowless morgue lies a CT scanner. To the right is the dissection room. Straight ahead sit large freezers that house bodies that will be returned quickly to their families for burial.

Down the end of a long, narrow corridor are the homicide rooms where detectives watch as wounds are measured and intricate details logged.

Upstairs, in the toxicology department, specialist staff are ready to test for 327 different drugs in the first 24 hours.

In Mr Bury’s department at the Southbank centre, 21 fellow forensic technical officers work a 24-hour roster, scanning new arrivals before taking blood and body fluids for testing.

About half of the bodies that arrive require a full autopsy to determine the cause of death. While Mr Bury has worked here for more than 13 years, some things still make him squeamish.

“None of us like dealing with decomposed cases; we all take it in turns with who’s dealing with those,” he says.

“We probably get three or four a week, though we have quite a variety. Some come in as skeletal remains while others are maybe seven days to a couple of weeks old.”

In 2014 the Institute of Forensic Medicine received worldwide recognition for its work on the MH17 Malaysia Airlines disaster after the plane broke up mid-air on the Ukraine-Russia border, killing all on board.

Victorian coronial staff were dispatched to Amsterdam where they helped in the recovery of the 298 dead and brought the bodies of Australian victims home.

Back in Melbourne, institute employees identified the remains of some 30 Australian victims whose bodies were then released to their families for burial within 48 hours.

Staff have also helped piece together some of the state’s most harrowing deaths, including the 2009 Black Saturday bushfires and the Bourke Street attack in January 2017.

Speaking at the recent launch of the institute's behind-the-scenes web series, Afterlife, Attorney-General Jill Hennessy said the centre was vital to gain a better understanding of how and why a person had died and what that might mean for prevention .

“Translating the dead to the living - that’s a pretty spectacular thing,” Ms Hennessy said.

Mr Bury is yet to tell his children exactly what he does for work, but his friends and family are no longer as squeamish as they were.

And tTe irony of his surname isn’t lost on him either; and his dark humour is a welcome addition to mortuary life.
