3D simulation of a 'fire near me' gives residents a wake-up call

16 Nov 2019, 13:00 by Julie Power

Residents on Sydney's upper north shore were shocked this week to see how quickly a bushfire in North Turramurra blazed through a high school and hospital, with high winds carrying embers 12 kilometres away and starting spot fires at random.

If this fire had been real, it would have been too late for many residents. Luckily, it wasn't.

Unlike the real fire that broke out in Lane Cove National Park in South Turramurra on Tuesday, Wednesday's blaze was virtual. To shock residents into developing a bushfire survival plan, the virtual blaze simulated similar but slightly windier conditions, equal to 90km/h, to Tuesday's conditions.

The simulation was part of a workshop run by Ku-ring-gai Council's Climate Wise Communities program, powered by 3D interactive software, developed by the US military, called Simtable.

In the simulation, a 3D map is projected onto a contoured box, filled with fine walnut husks that are shaped to match the topographical lines of a map.

Residents at the workshop planted flags on properties to see what would happen if a bushfire had ripped up the hills.

"That's the scale of event we want you to plan for. This is how fast and unpredictable it can be," Jennifer Scott, leader of the council's sustainability program, said.

To meet demand after Tuesday's fires, the council added extra sessions. The workshop encourages residents to take responsibility for their properties and their own safety in extreme weather events.

The council's Climate Wise website averages 100 users a month. On Tuesday, 1300 new users enrolled.

Before showing how a fire would affect North Turramurra, the workshop started with a recreation of Northern California's deadliest and most destructive fire. Known as the Camp Fire, it killed 86 civilians last year.

"When we bring in people who have done nothing, they are worried," Dr Scott said. "They don't realise it can happen that fast."

When people see the simulation, and get involved, they start to believe it can happen to them. It wasn't just something that happened to others.

The program is run with fire and rescue services, who provide feedback to residents on their bushfire survival plans.

Commenting on the virtual blaze that could have trapped North Turramurra residents, Ku-ring-gai Rural Fire Service Brigade Captain Nic Lyons said this "sort of thing happens on a bad day".

"You can't predict exactly what's going to happen, but this is a really good way of showing how fires behave."

Until Tuesday's fire struck South Turramurra, workshop attendee Sandy Lamb was sceptical that his eucalypt-filled property in Pymble, five kilometres away, was really at risk.

"It was a wake-up call," he said.

Mr Lamb lives across from a reserve with a lot of debris and tall trees. "It would be a simple act of fire hazardry to jump the road and end up in my trees," he said.

After the simulations, Mr Lamb worked with a volunteer from RFS to develop a survival plan. He was advised to download the RFS' Fires Near Me app, and add an alert for fires within 10 kilometres of his home.

"If I had any recommendation to anyone living in Sydney in a bushy region, it would be to get the app," he said.

The simulation was excellent, too. "It really brings it home," he said.

Ku-ring-gai is the only council with the 3D interactive simulation platform. It was funded by a $48,000 grant from Local Government NSW with an additional $20,000 in council funding.

Fires Near Me is available online.
