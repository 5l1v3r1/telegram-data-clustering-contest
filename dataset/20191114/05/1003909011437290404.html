Braid: Worries building again over punishing business tax

14 Nov 2019, 05:00 by Don Braid, Calgary Herald

Calgary’s small business owners, like medieval serfs, once again hope the tax collector leaves them enough beans to survive the winter.

Angst is building fast about another round of devastating tax bills for the city’s most powerless ratepayers. City hall, typically, has dodged the issue until Nov. 18.

It is remarkable, but emblematic of city waffling, that council is now considering overall budget documents that don’t even deal with the business tax problem.

The long-term solution is to raise the proportion of tax, now less than 50 per cent, paid by home and condo owners.

This is the only fair way to fill the mammoth assessment gap created by the hollowing out of the downtown towers.

The average tax hike spread over such a large group — 520,825 residential tax accounts — would be quite minor.

Right now the whole problem is pushed to 13,985 business accounts. This is downright cruel.

But those numbers also explain council’s inaction. On election day, business owners with one vote each are virtually meaningless.

How would a homeowner feel if the annual tax bill, humming along for years at, say, $5,000, was suddenly hiked to $20,000?

And would that homeowner enjoy hearing that the property is suddenly much more valuable, even though they know it’s worth less, and that’s why the taxes went up?

All those techniques and more have been used to put the screws on small business people. They’re paying for the trials of, say, Encana, which is moving its headquarters to Denver.

So, once again, we hear from Kelly Doody, owner of the Social School in Inglewood, who this spring became one leader of a tax revolt against city hall.

The assessed value of her business jumped from $811,000 in 2018 to $2.12 million.

That led to a mammoth monthly tax increase, pushing her yearly total close to $70,000.

Doody appealed and won. The assessed value was lowered to $1.4 million (still a mammoth jump from $811,000).

Her tax bill went down, but only by $15,000. The company fighting the case for her got $7,000 of that.

“So we end up just spending money AND still owing the city more,” she says.

“And we get to do it all over again in a few months. It’s just ludicrous.”

Colleague Chris Varcoe recently cited Owl’s Nest Books, in Britannia Plaza Shopping Centre. The store is trying to shrink its taxes by shrinking the business.

This one’s personal for me. Owners Mike and Susan Hare are friends. Our daughter worked at Owl’s Nest as a teenager, often bringing home more books than wages.

The growing popularity of ebooks and the big chains have made life tough for small independent booksellers. But Mike and Susan, full of ideas and always presenting a beautiful-looking store, fought through it all.

They just never counted on their city tax going from $7,000 to $32,000 in four years.

They’ve decided to close their popular children’s section. In January, a wall will go up, splitting their business space in two.

They signed a new lease with their landlord for less than half the previous square footage.

Susan says they hope their tax will consequently be cut in half. “If it doesn’t shrink, we’d have to close.”

This isn’t a business problem, it’s a tax problem inflicted by the city.

To help them fight through, Susan and Mike have come up with a typically original idea.

They’re asking customers to buy a $100 annual sponsorship, which comes with benefits like advance reading copies, free gift-wrapping, reserved seating at store events and more.

The money would go toward operating costs; that’s how tight the situation is.

Every council member should buy a sponsorship. It might even be good for the conscience.
