PSNI nominate officer for reports of attacks on Irish language signs in Mid Ulster

14 Nov 2019, 01:00 by Connla Young

THE PSNI has nominated an officer to handle reports of vandalism of Irish language road signs in Mid Ulster after a spate of attacks.

Details were revealed after a special meeting between Mid Ulster councillors and police.

Sources have said there were heated exchanges among councillors during the meeting in Cookstown on Tuesday.

Last week The Irish News revealed that from June 2017 to mid-October this year there were 93 “incidences of vandalism” involving dual language signs across the council area.

One sign at Gulladuff, near Maghera in Co Derry, has been targeted 12 times since February.

Council officials recently erected a temporary CCTV camera overlooking the site at Gulladuff Hill.

The cost to repair or replace the 93 road signs was £12,314.

A spokesman for the PSNI last night confirmed that “an officer from a local neighbourhood team has been identified as a single point of contact in relation to reports already received and any further reports of damage to signs”.

Police have said that between January 2017 and November 8 this year they received 39 reports in relation to theft or criminal damage of bilingual signs across the district.

The figures show that 32 of the reports were made this year, with just one received in 2017 and six in 2018.

Mid Ulster District Commander Superintendent Mike Baird said: "Some of these reports involved multiple incidents, which occurred at the one location.

"We are linking each of these reports which, at this time, are being treated as hate crimes.”

Sinn Féin representative Sean McPeake said the council “has adopted `best practice` policies aimed at promoting minority languages in line with the EU Charter for Regional and Minority Languages”.

“Perpetual griping from unionism around why minority language protections are put in place only contributes to the demonising and intolerance towards the language by those with a sectarian agenda,” he said.

DUP councillor Paul McLean claimed the signage “is being used as a territory marking process”.

SDLP council chair Martin Kearney said Irish is "flourishing in Mid Ulster and there’s a culture that goes along with that and that culture must be respected".

UUP councillor Trevor Wilson said: “Obviously I condemn all acts of vandalism no matter who or where they come from and nobody should be afraid of a language.

“But when it’s used as a political weapon then it becomes harder to sell.”
