There's money in scrap as Clearway profits lift to £14.6m

14 Nov 2019, 01:00 by Gary McDonald Business Editor

THE Portadown firm which is Ireland's biggest scrap metal exporter saw its turnover surge to £85.5 million last year - up £10 million on the previous year.

Clearway, which was established in the mid 1960s when John Murphy started in the waste management and scrap recycling business, also saw its bottom line profit jump from just shy of £10m to more than £14.6m, according to its latest set of accounts.

But retained earnings at the end of last December almost halved to £35.5m after the family-owned firm paid out dividends of £41.5m.

The firm - which is still controlled by members of the Murphy family - has depots in Belfast, Dublin, Derry and Sligo.

It also runs the Aughrim Landfill site near Lisburn, which has the capacity to receive up to 250,000 tonnes of waste a year.

The directors said they are pleased with the company's performance and are "cautiously optimistic" for the year ahead.

But they admit they are "acutely aware" of the present instability around Brexit and the volatility in world markets in general.

Clearway processes all grades of ferrous and non-ferrous scrap metals which is then supplies to local and export markets.

It is also a specialist in crushing end-of-life vehicles.

According to its accounts, it had 97 staff on its books at the end of last year, and its wages bill rose to £3.5 million.

The UK metal recycling industry is currently worth more than £5 billion a year, making the market prices for metal extremely competitive.

As scrap is a commodity, prices often fluctuate in the same way as oil or any other natural resource.
