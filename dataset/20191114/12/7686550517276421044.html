Mentally-ill man died hundreds of miles from home and his only family member has no money to attend his cremation

Jason Matthews has been described as a "cheeky chappy" with a big heart

14 Nov 2019, 12:50 by Anna Lewis

A woman faces missing her brother's funeral after he died in a hospital hundreds of miles away.

Jason Matthews, 48, died on October 31, in Birmingham Hospital after suffering complications from a blood clot in the brain.

Tributes paid to Mr Matthews, from Trecynydd, Caerphilly , but who lived in Ebbw Vale, have described a "cheeky chappy" with a "caring heart".

But his sister, and last remaining relative, faces missing his cremation after being unable to afford to make the journey to be by his side.

Tracy Matthews, 49, said: "I haven't got the money for a funeral."

"I have got illnesses so I can't work. It's been absolutely dreadful, I didn't expect to bury my younger brother.

"There's only one year between us, I never thought I would have to be the one to bury him."

Ms Matthews said her brother was transferred from Hereford Hospital to Birmingham after attempting to take his own life.

After his death, a fundraising page was created to help Tracy, who lives in Cornwall, pay £400 for her brother's body to be transported privately back to south Wales.

But now she has been told Mr Matthews may have to be cremated in Birmingham after struggling to meet her target.

She said: "Jason has been gone two weeks and the hospital have said they can't keep him any longer so I will have to have him cremated.

"I don't want him to be cremated in Birmingham but I think it will be out of my hands. I can understand where the hospital is coming from, they can only keep him for so long.

"There are two lots of paperwork which is £164, and then there's the funeral director's costs and petrol on top so I get the cost [of taking him home].

"I asked if the NHS could get him back to Wales so I could arrange for close friends to pick him up until I got there but they couldn't.

"I'm shaking thinking about it. I'm not thinking of anything except him, it's constantly on my mind the thought of him being there."

A fundraising page set up after Mr Matthew's death will now be used to help Ms Matthew travel from Cornwall to Birmingham to collect his ashes.

She hopes to take his ashes back to Wales to allow him to be laid to rest near their parents.

Ms Matthews said: "I want to get him back to Wales.

"He's a Welsh boy, born in Cardiff , and he needs to come back. Our parents and our older brother are buried in Caerphilly."

She added: "I'm on bed rest but I have to do this for him.

"I'm trying to get the travel costs for him. I've got to travel from Cornwall to Birmingham and take him back again. Then I want to make arrangements with close friends and people I know to do something in Caerphilly.

"I would like a memorial, even if it's he just goes next to our parent's grave.

"I don't travel back and for to Wales as it is because of my illness but money stops me as well. When Jason was in Birmingham I had to make the hard decision to say I couldn't go up [after he died]. It was really horrific that I couldn't get up there."

Paying tribute to Mr Matthews, who had suffered from a stroke three years ago, Ms Matthews said: "Jason was well known in Caerphilly. He was very, very funny.

"If he could help someone, he would held them. He had a caring heart.

"For the last few years he was quite disabled - he was in a wheelchair and he lost the use of his left arm.

"He struggled but he was quite independent. He tried to do everything himself but he couldn't take it any more."

In a JustGiving page, Rhiannon Cornwall said: "Jason was 48 and had lived in Ebbw Vale for a good few years but was a Trecenydd/Caerphilly Boy.

"He is fondly remembered by those who knew him as a cheeky chappy who would help anyone if he could. He was a caring man with a wicked sense of humour.

"Please help get him home where he belongs."

She added: "I went to school with Jason. He was a character always getting into scrapes, he had a great sense of humour and he was a sensitive, caring boy too.

"He had a lot of time for people. I had no idea he had the stroke, or was in such a dark place, I wish I had known so I could of been there for him.

"I’ll always remember him fondly."
