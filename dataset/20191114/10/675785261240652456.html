Rags to riches tale of Springbok wing whose try beat England in World Cup final

Makazole Mapimpi breaks Eddie Jones' heart - then gets picked by England boss to play for his team in Barbarians clash

14 Nov 2019, 10:31 by Alex Spink, https://www.facebook.com/alexspinkmirror/

Eddie Jones last night called on the services of the two men who did most to end his World Cup dream.

Tendai Mtawarira, who led the demolition of England’s scrum in the final a fortnight ago, was named by Jones in his Barbarians team to face Fiji.

Alongside the ‘Beast’ for Saturday’s Twickenham showpiece Jones slotted Makazole Mapimpi, scorer of the try which killed England off once and for all.

Jones was in no mood to talk about anything World Cup related. “It’s done and dusted. I can’t do anything about what’s happened,” he said.

 

So South Africa wing Mapimpi stepped into the breach to reflect on what, remarkably, is the first trophy he has ever won.

“I have watched my try I think 20 times,” he said. “I still don’t believe it’s me.”

Mapimipi’s day of joy was a long time coming in a life that has been anything but easy. Never mind the lack of rugby opportunities in his early years, he has buried his mother, his sister and his brother.

 

Mum Eunice was killed in a car accident, sister Zukiswa died from a severe headache, brother Zolani was electrocuted.

If the journey out of poverty made by Siya Kolisi, the first World Cup-winning black captain, is destined for the big screen, Mapimpi’s is no less extraordinary.

Born in a remote part of South Africa’s Eastern Cape the scorer of 14 tries in 14 Test starts did not enjoy private schooling, relying on the kindness of others just for a pair of boots to play in.

 

As recently as five years ago he was turning out in the local Sunday league when a friend told him he had the skills to turn professional.

“No bro,” he replied. “No-one is watching me. We are too far from the system.”

Sitting in an exclusive London hotel overlooking Hyde Park yesterday, Mapimpi, whose name in Xhosa translates as ‘cobra’, stopped just short of pinching himself.

 

“When I was young I would sometimes sit on my couch and pray about things,” he said. “But I didn’t bother with big goals.

“I’m from a rural area, I didn’t make the schools teams. So winning the World Cup is a big thing for me.

“A lot of things happen in South Africa that affect us,” he added. “I’ve seen a lot of things, things I don’t like. But I feel the World Cup has helped bring us together as a country. We have made people happy.”

Not Jones of course.

 

“But he’s still a funny guy, always smiling,” said Mapimpi. “Is it strange that we are on the same side so soon after the World Cup? No, he's a rugby man.

“We play, then afterwards we shake hands. It’s sport. Nothing more.”
