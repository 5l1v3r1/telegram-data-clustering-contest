The week in wildlife – in pictures

15 Nov 2019, 15:10 by Eric Hilaire

The pick of this week’s best flora and fauna photos from around the world, including a tired tiger and sea goldies

A deer covered in bracken undergrowth prepares to clash with a rival during the rutting season in Richmond Park, west London

Photograph: Toby Melville/Reuters

Colourful autumn foliage adorns Mount Nebiyan, in Ondokuz Mayıs district, Samsun, Turkey

Photograph: Mehmet Kumcağız/Anadolu Agency/Getty Images

A frost flower forms in the Full Circle Healing Garden in Forest Park, St Louis. As the stems of white crownsbeard and yellow ironweed freeze in early morning temperatures, leftover moisture expands from the stem, forming delicate patterns. The best time to see frost flower is at sunrise

Photograph: Robert Cohen/AP

A nuthatch on a tree in Minsk, Belarus

Photograph: Natalia Fedosenko/TASS/Getty Images

A squirrel forages for food among the foliage in Phoenix Park, Dublin

Photograph: Brian Lawless/PA

Two silver-backed chevrotains, also known as Vietnamese mouse-deers, in Vietnam. A British wildlife journal this month published the first photos and footage of a silver-backed chevrotain, which was last recorded more than 25 years ago. It is the first mammal on the Global Wildlife Conservation’s list of top 25 most-wanted lost species to be rediscovered

Photograph: Southern Institute of Ecology/Global Wildlife Conservation/Leibniz Institute for Zoo and Wildlife Research/NCNPAFP/Getty Images

Eagles and locusts fly over Karachi, Pakistan

Photograph: Asif Hassan/AFP via Getty Images

A kookaburra perches on a charred tree after a bushfire in Wallabi Point, New South Wales, Australia

Photograph: Courtesy of Adam Stevenson/Reuters

A zookeeper keeps an eye on Bei Bei, a giant panda, which is resting after eating bamboo at the Smithsonian’s National Zoo in Washington. The animal will go to China as part of a breeding agreement that requires all cubs born at the Washington zoo to move there when they are four years old. Bei Bei turned four on 22 August

Photograph: Jim Watson/AFP via Getty Images

Gogh, a male tiger seized on the Polish-Belarusian border, languishes in his temporary enclosure at a zoo in Poznań, Poland. Nine tigers travelling from Italy to Russia were discovered in squalid conditions and brought to Poland after customs intercepted and zoo employees and wildlife activists rescued them

Photograph: Wojtek Radwański/AFP via Getty

Oriental pied hornbills enjoy a banana in Mayong village on the outskirts of Gauhati, India

Photograph: Anupam Nath/AP

A Indian cabbage white butterfly flies near flower buds in Kathmandu, Nepal

Photograph: Navesh Chitrakar/Reuters

Sea goldies shelter under a coral-decorated overhang. The image was captured by a Greenpeace vessel conducting research around Mount Vema, an underwater mountain about 1,000 miles north-west of Cape Town, South Africa

Photograph: Richard Barnden/Greenpeace/AFP/Getty Images

A kudu in the drought-stricken Hwange National Park in Zimbabwe. More than 200 elephants have died, along with other animals, due to lack water and food in the park, which is the size of Belgium and contains 45,000 pachyderms – more than the entire herd in Kenya

Photograph: Zinyange Auntony/AFP/Getty Images

Jump by Csaba Daróczi, the Nature photographer of the year 2019 overall winner and black and white category winner

Photograph: Csaba Daróczi/2019 Nature Photographer of the Year

For the first time, a colony of sea lions on Kangaroo Island in Australia is being treated with topical anti-parasitic medicine and monitored for health and survival. The research, led by Dr Rachael Gray from the School of Veterinary Science at Sydney University, is investigating the effects of hookworm, environmental pollutants and human-associated bacteria on the mortality of sea lions in the first 18 months of their lives

Photograph: Louise M Cooper/Sydney University

Helen Greaves, a PhD student at University College London, holds a frog at a former wetland on farmland near Hindolveston in Dereham, eastern England

Photograph: Emilio Morenatti/AP

A horned beetle in Quezon City, the Philippines

Photograph: Rouelle Umali/Xinhua/Barcroft Media

A jaguar in Yasuni National Park, Orellana. The loss of habitat is the biggest threat to jaguars in Ecuador, particularly along the coast, where more than 70% of the original forest cover has been lost. The vast majority of this destruction has taken place over the past 50 years with the expansion of the logging and agriculture industries, including coffee, cacao, palm oil and bananas, one of the country’s largest agriculture exports

Photograph: Lucas Bustamante/NPL

A coral off Mexico’s Quintana Roo state affected by white syndrome, a disease that is different from coral bleaching and more deadly. The majestic reds, yellows and violets of the reefs along the Mexican Caribbean are slowly turning into a deadly white, which has scientists scrambling to understand and combat the mysterious disease that is spreading south along the Meso-American reef system. This week the British oceanographer Callum Roberts talks to Science Weekly about his journey into exploring marine habitats, and his subsequent work observing the world’s coral reefs

Photograph: Armando Gasse/Adaptur/AFP/Getty Images

Residents of flood-stricken Fishlake village are trying to salvage belongings from their homes and businesses in Doncaster, England, amid further weather warnings after this week’s floodings in parts of Britain

Photograph: Christopher Furlong/Getty Images

Protesters in Rennes, France, hold a poppy emblem of the anti-pesticide movement ‘ Nous voulons des coquelicots’ (‘We want poppies’) in support of a ban on pesticides within 150 metres of homes. A report suggests half of all insects may have been lost since 1970 as a result of the destruction of nature and heavy use of pesticides. The report says 40% of the 1 million known species of insect face extinction

Photograph: Damien Meyer/AFP/Getty Images
