Lives were put at risk by the illegal storage of large amounts of fireworks in West Denton

Yaseen Ashraf, of West Denton, admitted offences including storing explosives without a licence

15 Nov 2019, 17:59 by Rob Kennedy

Lives were put at risk when a large amount of fireworks were illegally stored in what could have left people dead or maimed in a "potential disaster", a court heard.

More than 300 kilos of the explosive-laden goods were stored in an area between a family home and their business in West Denton, Newcastle.

Newcastle Crown Court heard the fireworks were stored next to flammable material liquid petroleum gas cylinders, which were empty but the vapour from which made the situation even more dangerous.

Prosecutors said it was a "flagrant breach of the law" and there was a "foreseeable risk" of serious injury or death to people living upstairs or even walking past.

Now Yaseen Ashraf, of The Burnside, West Denton, has been given a suspended prison sentence after admitted offences including storing explosives without a licence.

Recorder David Gordon told him: "It became clear you were storing a significant amount of fireworks in an area behind some roller shutters and these were of an extremely dangerous nature and in a very large quantity.

"The situation is you had no licence to store fireworks in those premises.

"Even more concerning, the fireworks were being stored in close proximity to flammable material and gas cylinders. Even though they were empty there was still a serious hazard caused because of the vapour that would have remained in them.

"This was a concerning situation indeed. One really worries about what would have happened had they ignited. This was a residential area with a number of dwelling houses in close proximity and they were being stored close to the highway.

"It was perhaps by good luck rather than management that a potential disaster was averted.

"Although, thankfully, no harm was in fact caused, there was very obviously a high risk of death or at least physical harm to those in the vicinity.

"What you did was extremely dangerous, I'm sure you understand that now."

The court heard the case related to two retail and one residential property on The Burnside.

The business at number 18 did previously have a licence to store explosives but that expired in 2016 and was not renewed, while the business at number 30 did have such a licence but only to store 75 kilos of fireworks.

The offences came to light after Ashraf contacted contacted West Denton community fire station about concerns about firework in the area.

During a visit to the shop at number 30 later that date, it wad discovered that there were actually 93 kilos, in breach of the limit.

More seriously, in an area between the other shop and the home, there was a large number of fireworks.

Paul Cross, prosecuting, said: "In the property there were LPG cylinders - they were not full of LPG but would still retain some of the vapour when empty.

"There was a large amount of building waste which was combustible."

Ashraf said there was £15,000 of fireworks there, although invoices suggested they had cost £5,000.

Mr Cross told the court: "There was no licence for these fireworks and there was also a failure to comply with regulations for storing them away from flammable material and also a breach of the separation distance required for the storing of those sort of quantities of fireworks."

In total there were more than 350 kilos of what was described as "explosive mass".

The prosecutor added: "That would require a separate storage building located away from other goods and buildings and public highways.

"When they were moved they weren't put in the same shop for that reason. It's not safe to keep that type of quantity all together.

"If there was a fire, this stuff would make the fire burn extremely aggressively and there would be a risk of some of the fireworks exploding at the same time and the fire would get out of control rather quickly.

"It was a flagrant breach of the law and there was a foreseeable risk. Anyone in the upstairs flat or potentially someone walking past could suffer serious injury or even possibly death."

Ashraf, 32, pleaded guilty to four health and safety breaches - two offences of storing explosives without a licence, failing to maintain the required separation distance between the store and other buildings and failing to take appropriate measures to limit the extent of fire or explosion or its spread.

He was sentenced to eight month imprisonment suspended for 18 months with 150 hours unpaid work an £850 costs. An order was made for the fireworks to be destroyed.

Rachel Hedworth, defending, conceded: "The reality is had there been an explosion and if someone had been there, they would have died, that's the stark reality."

However she pointed out it was Ashraf who had contacted the fire service, there has never been any issues with the family run business previously and he comes from a hard-working, law-abiding family.

Miss Hedworth added: "At the time he didn't appreciate the risk.

"He is completely and utterly remorseful for what has taken place."

She said some of the fireworks were to be sold in the shop but others would have been used for Diwali celebrations and for weddings of friends and family.

The court heard the shop does have an up to date licence for selling fireworks now.
