Verisk Analytics: Entry Opportunity Into This Compounding Grower

15 Nov 2019, 10:10 by Laurentian Research

Summary

  • On the back of its sustainable competitive advantage, through organic growth and acquisitions, Verisk grew revenue at a CAGR of 10.5%.
  • In 2004-2015, EBITDA margin increased from 37.3% to 51.0%, confirming the scalability of the business.
  • And EBITDA margin averages 47.2% after the acquisitions of low-margin WoodMac and financial services subsidiaries.
  • The company increased net income at 13.4% annually, thanks to the healthy EBITDA margin, and EPS at 15.2% per year, due to consistent share buybacks.
  • Wide-moat Verisk epitomizes sustainable compounding growth and is, therefore, the kind of stock an investor can load up on newsbreaks of idiosyncratic disasters.

Growth strategy

The key components of Verisk's growth strategy include the following:

  • Increase Solution Penetration with Customers. The company expects to expand the application of its solutions in customers’ internal processes. Building on its deep knowledge of, and embedded position in, various industries, it expects to sell more solutions to existing customers tailored to individual market segments. By increasing the breadth and relevance of its offerings, it can strengthen its relationships with customers and increase its value to their decision-making in critical ways. It has opportunities to expand solution penetration to the insurance, energy, and financial services customers.
  • Develop New, Proprietary Data Sets and Predictive Analytics. The company works with its customers to understand their evolving needs. It plans to create new solutions by enriching its mix of proprietary databases, analytic solutions and effective decision support across the markets it serves, to further leverage its analytic methods, technology platforms, and intellectual capital.
  • Leverage Its Intellectual Capital to Expand into Adjacent Markets and New Customer Sectors. Verisk is built on more than four decades of intellectual property in risk management. The company can continue to expand the use of its intellectual capital profitably and apply its analytical methods in new markets where significant opportunities for long-term growth exist, as shown by the effective expansion into non-insurance financial services. It also continues to pursue growth through targeted international expansion.
  • Pursue Strategic Acquisitions that Complement Its Leadership Positions. The company continues to expand its data and analytics capabilities across industries. In addition to organic growth, it has acquired and will continue to acquire assets and businesses that strengthen its value proposition to customers. The company has developed an internal capability to source, evaluate, and integrate acquisitions that have created value for shareholders. The company's history of acquisition is discussed in a previous article.

The data analytics space serving the P/C insurance industry was and continues to be highly-fragmented in the ex-Verisk part and, therefore, has been a prolific supplier of bolt-on acquisition targets for Verisk. With acquisitions, Verisk strengthens its core databases and enriches its suite of solutions, which are then being cross-sold tapping into its unique access to the customer base (Fig. 1).

Over the years, Verisk also made inroads into industry verticals while sticking to its operational model, i.e., recurring revenue, scalable solutions, and low capital intensity. They expanded into mortgage, health, natural resources, and financial. Please note, it exited from the mortgage business in 2014 and the health segment in 2016 (see here).

  • In the Energy and Specialized Markets segment, Verisk faces a number of competitors, including IHS Markit (natural resources), Rystad Energy (upstream), RS Energy (upstream), Global Data PLC (upstream), PIRA Energy Group (oil and gas markets), and CRU Group (metals) and Bloomberg New Energy Finance (power and renewables). In this highly differentiated market, there are numerous targets for potential acquisition.
  • Within the Financial Services segment, the competitive landscape is also differentiated. Key competitors for Verisk's major brands are EverCompliant, WebShield, Trustwave, LegitScript, Lexus Nexis, American Infosource, and Phin Solutions.
  • In 2018, energy and specialized markets contribute 13.7% of the adjusted EBITDA, while financial services 5.4%. These adjacent verticals, however, do not generate the kind of EBITDA margin as high as 54% achieved by its P/C insurance segment. The EBITDA margin for the energy and specialized markets segment was around 30%, while that for financial services approximately 34% (see here).

Verisk targets a CAGR of 7-8% in organic revenue growth, which is translated into 8-10% organic EBITDA growth thanks to its operational leverage, which is, in turn, converted into double-digit growth in EPS on the back of its capital efficiency and share buyback (Fig. 2).

Revenue growth

Year-over-year revenue growth is a key performance metric for measuring Verisk's ability to generate increased revenue through (1) increased sales to existing and new customers, and sales of new or expanded solutions to existing customers, and (2) strategic acquisitions of new businesses.

From 2007 to 2018, with acquisitions and divestments inclusive, Verisk was able to more than quadruple revenue, or grow revenue at a CAGR of 10.5% (Fig. 3). During the same period of time, the company managed to keep an organic revenue growth rate of 6.8% (Fig. 4).

  • That organic growth accounts for 2/3 of the total growth clearly suggests Verisk did not create a record of growth just by buying up businesses. It also implies that the management was able to successfully integrate the acquired businesses into its operating model for continued organic growth.

Profitability

The metric of EBITDA margin measures segment performance and scalability of Verisk. The EBITDA margin can only be maintained at an elevated level if Verisk is able to increase revenues while simultaneously keeping expenses under control.

From 2004 to 2015, Verisk was able to increase the EBITDA margin from 37.3% to 51.0%, confirming that the company (then mostly P/C insurance business) enjoys an effect of economies of scale.

The uptrend in the EBITDA margin broke due to the acquisition of Wood MacKenzie, an Aberdeen, U.K.-based oil consultancy, in 2015 and the $139.7 million one-time profit resultant from the sale of Verisk-Health in 2016. Verisk also accelerated acquisition in the financial services sector during that period of time. Both the financial services segment and WoodMac division have an EBITDA margin which is respectable but significantly lower than Verisk's flagship P/C insurance business; WoodMac also introduces commodity cyclicity into the financial performance of Verisk. Consequently, the EBITDA margin has softened in 2017 to date. Nonetheless, on a consolidated basis, the EBITDA margin was still maintained as high as 47.2% in 2018 and seems to begin to improve since the 2Q2019 (Fig. 5).

Fig. 5. The operating, net, and EBITDA margins of Verisk. The 2016 margins are distorted by gains due to the sale of Verisk-Health while the 3Q2019 margins are adjusted for the one-time charge of $125 million resultant from a jury decision. Source: Laurentian Research based on company released annual reports.

Thanks to its high EBITDA margin, Verisk was able to grow EBITDA at a CAGR of 11.8% and net income at 13.4% from 2007 to 2018 (Fig. 3). During this time, Verisk increased EPS at a CAGR of 15.2% (Fig. 6).

  • Stock repurchase contributed to the EPS expansion. The company reduced the diluted shares outstanding by 1.6% per year, adjusted for the change in the number of shares in the IPO.
  • It is worth noting that even the global financial crisis of 2007-2008 failed to put a dent on the company's revenue growth nor EBITDA margin (Fig. 3; Fig. 6).

Fig. 6. The net EPS and free cash flow per share of Verisk. Please note, in each of 2009 and 2016, there are major one-time effects from discontinued businesses. Source: Laurentian Research based on company released annual reports.

Impact of jury decision

Verisk suffered a major setback thanks to a jury verdict in the U.S. District Court for the District of New Jersey on September 25, 2019. The verdict states that Verisk and its Xactware Solutions subsidiary infringed the proprietary intellectual property of Eagle View Technologies, a provider of aerial imagery and data analytics. The jury awarded Eagle View $125 million in damages. The Court has also issued a temporary restraining order stopping, among other things, Verisk's sale of Property InSight, Roof InSight, Geomni Roof, and Geomni Property, as well as its sale or use of Aerial Sketch version 2, after September 25, 2019, and until October 8, 2019, when the Court will hold a hearing on a permanent injunction (see here).

Despite the scary size of the financial penalties assessed, I believe the jury decision may only have a limited impact on Verisk's overall business. It only affects a relatively small portion of Verisk's insurance offerings, i.e., Xactware and Geomni, with ISO, the main workhorse, entirely unaffected (Fig. 7). In addition, Geomni has been running at a loss (see here); given the poor financial performance of Geomni has not been able to drag down corporate-level profitability in the past, it follows that the division plays a relatively insignificant role in the sprawling business of Verisk. In the worst case, even if the company were to write off Geomni entirely, it would be unimaginable the impairment can be anywhere near the $3.51 billion market cap reduction caused by the 13% sell-off triggered by the jury decision.

Fig. 6. The insurance business segment of Verisk. Source.

Therefore, I believe the courtroom setback, as well as the $125 million penalties, leaves the durable competitive advantage commanded by the company largely unscratched and will not have a substantial negative impact on the long-term growth prospect of the company.

Investor takeaways

Chain of conversion. I especially like the fact that Verisk was able to transform 6.8% of organic growth (or 10.5% of total growth) in revenue into 13.4% of yearly increase in net income, banking on the scalability of its business (Fig. 2), and to further convert that 13.4% annual growth in net income into a 15.2% CAGR in EPS through a share buyback. Such an ability to convert fast revenue increase, via faster net income growth, into yet faster EPS expansion, I believe, highlights the strength of Verisk's business model and management's track record of prudent capital allocation and shareholder friendliness.

Compounding growth. As I have shown above, Verisk has been able to deliver consistent, rapid, and profitable growth. Even general market recessions, as severe as the 2008 global financial crisis, did not stop its pace of expansion. Going forward, I believe its unique business model, in combination with the industry settings that it operates in, will lead to unimpeded growth into the foreseeable future. Such consistent growth, year after year, is an excellent recipe for compounding growth of wealth.

Entry opportunity. The bane of seeking investment opportunities in such high-quality businesses as Verisk is that the stock seems to be expensive the vast majority of the time. Rare entry opportunities only appear when a general market recession occurs or an idiosyncratic disaster strikes.

Since the newsbreak of the jury decision, the share price of Verisk has declined by some 13% (Fig. 7). Because the impact of the courtroom setback is limited to a small portion of one business segment and will likely be short-lasting as discussed above, the sell-off is probably an over-reaction. Therefore, the sell-off may provide a rare entry opportunity for investors who believe in Verisk's durability of competitive advantage and prospect of long-term growth.
