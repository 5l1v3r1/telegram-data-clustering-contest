Weekly Unemployment Claims: Up 14K

15 Nov 2019, 07:27 by Doug Short

Summary

  • The latest seasonally adjusted 225K new claims were up 14K from the previous week's 211K.
  • This was worse than the Investing.com forecast of 215K.
  • The 4-week moving average was 217,000, an increase of 1,750 from the previous week's unrevised average of 215,250.

By Jill Mislinski

Here is the opening statement from the Department of Labor:

In the week ending November 9, the advance figure for seasonally adjusted initial claims was 225,000, an increase of 14,000 from the previous week's unrevised level of 211,000. The 4-week moving average was 217,000, an increase of 1,750 from the previous week's unrevised average of 215,250. [See full report]

Thursday morning's seasonally adjusted 225K new claims, up 14K from the previous week's 211K, was worse than the Investing.com forecast of 215K.

Here is a close look at the data over the decade (with a callout for the past year), which gives a clearer sense of the overall trend in relation to the last recession.

As we can see, there's a good bit of volatility in this indicator, which is why the 4-week moving average (the highlighted number) is a more useful number than the weekly data. Here is the complete data series.

The headline Unemployment Insurance data is seasonally adjusted. What does the non-seasonally adjusted data look like? See the chart below, which clearly shows the extreme volatility of the non-adjusted data (the red dots). The 4-week MA gives an indication of the recurring pattern of seasonal change (note, for example, those regular January spikes).

Because of the extreme volatility of the non-adjusted weekly data, we can add a 52-week moving average to give a better sense of the secular trends. The chart below also has a linear regression through the data. We can see that this metric continues to fall below the long-term trend stretching back to 1968.
