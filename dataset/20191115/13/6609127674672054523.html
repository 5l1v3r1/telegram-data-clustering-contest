Dunfermline mum blessed with ‘miracle babies’ after refusing hysterectomy for endometriosis

15 Nov 2019, 13:26 by Katy Pagan

UNDERGO a hysterectomy and never have children or endure years of excrutiating pain with your "insides wrapped in barbed wire".

These were the two devastating options offered to a 21-year-old who hadn't even thought about starting a family yet.

Lucy-Anne Patterson, from Dunfermline, was forced to choose after being diagnosed with endometriosis, a chronic and debilitating gynaecological condition that can lead to infertility.

Now 35, the endo warrior can proudly say she is a "full-time mummy" to two adorable little girls.

Recalling the decision she faced in her 20s, Lucy-Anne is relieved she opted not to have the major surgery to remove her womb.

According to NHS guidelines, it is usually only performed on women who have found other treatments unsuccessful and who have decided to not have any more children.

Lucy-Anne, engaged to Partick Thistle star Joe Cardle, said: "I have lived with endometriosis for years and only got diagnosed when I was 21.

"I started having pain in my teens, and everyone told me it was normal.

"It continued to get worse over the years until finally I was diagnosed with endo."

Before diagnosis, Lucy-Anne was told she had ulcerative colitis, then IBS - and one doctor even suggested the pain was all in her head.

She added: "I was missing school and work a lot and during my periods I couldn't get out of bed.

"I looked six months pregnant with bloating, was constantly sick and tired.

"At 21 I was told I had to have a hysterectomy - it was the only cure.

"Back then, nobody specialised in endo."

Thankfully, a family friend who specialised in endometriosis in the US swooped in to help after hearing about the hysterectomy plans.

Lucy-Anne, who is originally from Aberdeen, was flown out to Houston where a dedicated team used laser excision to rid her of her extensive endo damage.

To everyone’s surprise, she found out she was seven weeks pregnant when she went back to the States for her check up.

But she sadly she miscarried four days later.

Fertility problems are not uncommon with endometriosis sufferers because of damage to the fallopian tubes or ovaries.

Surgery to remove visible patches of endometriosis tissue can sometimes help, but there is still no guarantee you will get pregnant.

Women with moderate to severe endometriosis also tend to have a lower chance of getting pregnant with IVF than usual.

Brave Lucy-Anne told the Scottish Sun Online she went on to have a further three miscarriages before being blessed with her two miracle babies.

She said: "I had another laparoscopy back in 2013 just to check what was going on.

"I was then diagnosed with lupus and put on Fragmin, for blood clots, and aspirin daily.

"After that I fell pregnant straight away and this time managed to carry my little princess Josie-Anne with constant care throughout my pregnancy. She is five now."

Lucy-Anne then had her second daughter, Lyla Jo, in June 2017.

But she has started to feel those familiar endo pains since giving birth.

She said: "A recent scan showed my endometriosis was back and my ovaries are kissing.

"I am waiting on a specialist but more surgery will be required.

"I have been given an injection to suppress my ovaries for the time being and it’s taken the extreme pain away."

Determined not to let endo control her life, she added: "Having lived with it for so many years and with expert treatment and knowledge I really feel I can keep this under control with certain medicines and most importantly diet."

And she has urged the public to be more aware of invisible illnesses.

She said: "People find it hard to believe anything is wrong with you as they don’t see why you're in so much pain.

"No one ever takes my fiance seriously when he says how much pain I was and am in as they say 'she looks great'.

"I looked normal on the outside but on the inside felt like I was wrapped in a barbed wire fence and being torn."

What are the symptoms of endometriosis?

There are many different symptoms of endometriosis and they can vary in intensity. Some women with endometriosis will have no symptoms at all.

The most common symptoms are:

  • Painful periods
  • Painful sex
  • Infertility
  • Painful bowel movements
  • Fatigue

Other symptoms include:

  • Lethargy
  • Nausea
  • Extreme tiredness
  • Depression
  • Frequent infections such as thrush
  • Feeling faint/fainting during periods

Lucy-Anne said: "It’s such a huge relief that this disease is being specialised, spoken about and taken seriously in the UK now and girls don’t need to suffer like I did.

"Speak up if you’re hurting, don’t hide away in pain. I was so lucky to have the care I did."
