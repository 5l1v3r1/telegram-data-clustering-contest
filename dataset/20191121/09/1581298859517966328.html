Esperance Shalom House supporters begin working towards $150K fundraising goal

21 Nov 2019, 09:30 by Sarah Makse

The founder of Shalom House visited Esperance on November 20, to discuss the future of the town's proposed rehabilitation centre.

Shire staff met with Peter Lyndon-James at the potential building site to discuss the project's next move.

Community members who had volunteered their time, skills and resources to bring the program to life were also present.

Despite a property being offered to the group rent free for a year, plans were put on hold in September, after it was revealed about $200,000 was needed for mandatory reclassification and modification.

In order for the property to be used as a rehabilitation facility, it was required to be brought from a class 1 residential property to a class 3.

Mr Lyndon-James said he'd like to see the house open by May, but it depended on the community raising the initial funds to begin works.

However, he said the November meeting was a positive step forward, with draftspeople helping to determine the scope of works.

"We have essential support from the Esperance Shire staff both on the planning side and on behalf of the chief executive officer and councillors, who have all been exceptional," Mr Lyndon-James said.

"They've given us some guidance about what needs to happen before we take possession of the property.

"There's no place as good as Esperance, it is a really beautiful town.

"I am blown away by the amount of people that want to help.

"It's up to the community of Esperance to raise the money and it is my job to send down all the trained staff, plus ten residents to get it started.

"What I say to every community, is before we are able to run a 100 per cent self-funded rehab, we need a house rent free for a year and $150,000 to start it off."

Mr Lyndon-James said some local farmers had already pledged a percentage of their crop sales to help get the project off the ground.

Esperance local Jo-Anne McLaren said she would rally to support and fundraise by selling mangoes.

"I'm a recovered addict and my heart is for people to find freedom from addiction," she said.

"It is incredible the amount of people who know somebody who has done Shalom, who is in Shalom or who needs Shalom.

"I'm so happy to be on the front line and to be able to share my passion."

Mr Lyndon-James welcomed any community fundraising initiatives or tradespeople to offer their skills.
