Shunsuke Nakamura and the iconic Celtic moment that sparked the most ferocious Fergie hairdryer in history

Thirteen years ago to the day, the Japanese star scored one of his iconic goals - here's how it all happened.

21 Nov 2019, 10:49 by Liam Bryce, https://www.dailyrecord.co.uk/authors/liam-bryce/

There's an angle from which you're left wondering just how it happened.

The camera low to the ground, the ball skewing off high to the left, looking destined for Row Z.

"Why are those faces in the Manchester United so contorted with shock and fear? It's not going anywhere."

But then it did.

By some power that only Shunsuke Nakamura and a few select others can harness, it swerves and dips into the top corner, leaving all 6ft5 of Edwin van der Sar flailing hopelessly at the Glasgow night air.

Gordon Strachan , perhaps thinking it's too far from goal for even the Japanese, leans casually against the dugout.

As it crashed into the back of the net, there's an almost awkward hop, skip and a jump from the Celtic manager, he and 60,000 others not quite sure what to do with themselves.

United, who would go on to win the Champions League the following season, were floored. Celtic, meanwhile, were in the knockout stage.

It mattered not that Louis Saha was gifted a chance to level from the spot with nine minutes remaining, he and his team-mates looked punch drunk, overwhelmed by the earthquake that followed the most stunning of goals.

Watching on from the other end of the pitch, Gary Neville predicted Saha would fail and, watching it back 13 years to the day, was there ever a man who looked so certain to miss?

Saha admitted this week he'd been angered by Neville's lack of faith, which had made its way into the next day's papers.

Sorry Louis, but the man had a point.

Minutes before the spot-kick the Frenchman bore down on goal, before inexplicably appearing to believe he'd been flagged offside, surrendering an opportunity to level.

By the time he stepped up to take the penalty, Saha's head had quite clearly gone.

Artur Boruc's save and the final whistle that followed gave away to the one of the most sustained roars ever witnessed at Celtic Park.

It was the sort of titanic night, defeating a truly elite side, that has become a rarity in years since, the financial gap between the top tier and the rest stretching far into the horizon.

As much as its significance hasn't dulled with age, there was a real sense at the time Celtic had achieved something special.

This was a United side still helmed by Sir Alex Ferguson and would go on, in the next three years, to become perhaps the last truly great team he ever constructed.

Ryan Giggs, Paul Scholes, Wayne Rooney, Rio Ferdinand and a certain Cristiano Ronaldo who was only just beginning to harness the extent of his talents.

All were left stunned by a Celtic side who had fought their way to an earth-shattering result.

Neville dragged Saha away by his collar after the shell-shocked striker planted himself on the Parkhead turf, but the worst was yet to come.

Years later Rooney recalled the mother of all Ferguson hairdryers being dished out in the bowels of Celtic Park.

No prizes for guessing who bore the brunt.

"In the dressing room, the manager lets loose.

"It’s the worst hairdryer I’ve seen. He’s in Louis’ face, shouting and screaming. But Louis isn’t the only one getting an earful.

“The manager knows I’ve been negotiating a new deal with the club and he saves some for me.

 

“Players wanting more money from the club and new deals… you don’t deserve anything after that performance!

“He doesn’t look my way but I know where he’s aiming the comments. I don’t like it, but he’s right.

“After that performance, I don’t deserve an improved contract and there’s nothing worse than getting the hairdryer from Sir Alex.

“When it happens, the manager stands in the middle of the room and loses it at me.”

Forty-five minutes earlier, in the home dressing room next door, Strachan had told his players they'd either be remembered for a first-half during which United should have sewn the game up, or the forty-five to come.

It's fair to say they responded.
