Woman's face sliced open after getting skin cancer despite avoiding the sun

21 Nov 2019, 11:22

Warning: Graphic images

A mum who spent her life avoiding the sun was shocked when a tiny scab on her nose turned out to be deadly skin cancer.

Katy Flynn, 31, ignored the pin prick-sized speck on the side of her nose for a year and only mentioned it to her GP as an aside during another appointment.

Even then the mum-of-two was told it was ‘nothing to worry about’ and a dermatologist went on to prescribe a gel to clear abnormal cells.

After many more months of failed treatment, Katy was eventually diagnosed with basal cell carcinoma.

She has now been left with a four-inch scar after medics cut away the tumour and pulled down the skin from her forehead to cover the wound.

Katy, who is engaged to boyfriend Chris, 33, and is mum to daughters Miley, nine, and Abbie, five, said she feels lucky to be alive.

The bank worker, from Harpurhey, Manchester, said: ‘I just woke up with it one day, it was just a tiny scab the size of a pen dot so I assumed one of the girls had caught me with their nails.

‘I was in shock when doctors told me it was skin cancer.

‘I’ve always been a shade-seeker, I avoid the sun at all costs.

‘When I go on holiday, I’m head to toe in factor 50 all the time.

‘My friends laugh at me because they’ll all be laid out in the sun and I’ll be sat in the shade with my book.

‘The only time I ever tried going on a sunbed was for a minute as a teenager and I hated it so I came straight off.

‘I never thought this would happen to me but it has made me realise that anyone can get skin cancer – whether you’re a sun seeker or not.’

Katy first spotted the scab in December 2016 and turned to creams and moisturisers to get rid of it, thinking it was eczema or a bacterial infection.

The following December she mentioned it to her GP and in January 2018 she was referred to dermatologists.

By this point, it was about the size of a 5p piece and Katy was diagnosed with solar keratosis, pre-cancerous sun damage which if left untreated could turn into skin cancer.

She was prescribed a topical gel to kill any pre-cancerous cells and sent on her way.

The scab would fall off and regrow even bigger than before but Katy thought that was because she kept knocking it when washing her face.

Eventually in December 2018, dermatologists took biopsies from her nose and she was diagnosed with cancer in February this year.

Medics performed Mohs surgery at Salford Royal Hospital where thin layers of skin are progressively removed and examined until only cancer-free tissue remains.

After six hours of surgery, Katy was referred to The Christie Hospital in Manchester for a facial reconstruction.

Doctors took skin from her forehead and pulled it down over her nose to graft over where the cancerous cells had been removed.

However, part of the process involved leaving her with an open flap of skin for a month so that the skin would remain alive and start to regrow.

Katy added: ‘As soon as I knew it wasn’t life threatening and there was something I could do about it I felt relieved.

‘I’m not ecstatic with the way it looks but there’s no point being down about it.

‘When I had the forehead flap, I didn’t really leave the house apart from to pick the kids up from school.

‘I couldn’t face all the questions, I didn’t want to have to answer them.’

At home Katy said she never used the word cancer to her daughters as they had recently lost their grandfather to the disease.

But she showed them images of what the skin flap would look like so they were prepared.

She is about to return to work and admits to being nervous about dealing with customers who might stare at her scar.

Katy continued: ‘I do wonder if the doctors had taken biopsies in the first place rather than trying to diagnose me from pictures whether it would have gone this way.

‘But it’s happened now and I’m just lucky that it wasn’t life threatening and I’m still here for my children and husband-to-be.’
