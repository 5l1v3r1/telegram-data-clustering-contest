'NO EXCUSE'

Health Secretary admits girl, 10, may have died because of contaminated water at Glasgow’s superhospital

21 Nov 2019, 08:43 by Blair Meikle

THE Health Secretary has admitted contaminated water at Glasgow's superhospital may have been behind the death of a 10-year-old girl.

Jeane Freeman has slammed NHS Greater Glasgow and Clyde over their handling of the passing of cancer patient Milly Main at Queen Elizabeth University Hospital.

This came after she was forced to make an apology at Holyrood, where she also brought up concerns raised by other patients.

She branded the health board's handling of the situation 'appalling' and blasted them for criticising the whistleblower who exposed the scandal.

The source claimed Milly died after contracting an infection from dirty water at the hospital - one of 25 who had picked up the bug.

A probe was launched but never made public by NHSGCC.

Milly's mum Kimberly Darroch said she felt "let down and lied to" and "100 per cent convinced" that he daughter was killed by contaminated water.

Freeman told the Daily Record: "There is no excuse for Milly’s parents not to have been given the full information at the time when their child died.

“I don’t believe people are wilfully intending to cause more pain and upset to others, but their actions, or their failure to act, clearly in this instance, absolutely do.”

The health board previously admitted Milly contracted a bug, but said there was no proof it came from the hospital.

But Freeman added: "That’s not really helpful to Milly’s mum, and it is reasonable that, knowing what she knows now, that she would suspect that it came from the water in the hospital.”

Asked whether she suspected Milly’s infection had come from the QEUH, Freeman said: “I don’t know, but given what we know about levels of water borne infections in and around the period that I know of, which is 2018….then I think it is a reasonable position to take that it could have come from the hospital.”

She also urged them to make the findings of the investigation public, adding: “First of all, why did we not know? Secondly, what does it say? Give us it.

"Let’s see that information in full. And thirdly, what did you do when you got that information?”

On the criticism of the whistleblower, she said: "I thought that was appalling. That’s why in the statement I made today in Parliament I said whistleblowers should not be publicly or privately criticised.

"I think the board have moved away from that position in recent days, but nonetheless that doesn’t mean that what they initially said in their comments is the least bit acceptable, because it is not.”

NHS Greater Glasgow and Clyde has been contacted for comment.
