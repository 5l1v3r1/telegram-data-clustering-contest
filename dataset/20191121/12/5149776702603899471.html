Business owner criticises university decision to scrap arts degree that helped her set up Nottingham business

"The contribution to the economy from the arts is so much bigger than people think."

21 Nov 2019, 12:53 by Gurjeet Nanrah

A business owner has criticised a Nottingham university's decision to scrap its decorative arts course, which gave her the skills to set up her own creative business in the city.

Chloe Rose, 25, graduated with a decorative arts degree at Nottingham Trent University (NTU) in 2016 and has gone on to set up her own ceramic-making business and work as an art psychotherapist with sexually abused children.

The ceramicist from Gedling said the course allowed students to develop skills and techniques with materials such as wood, clay, glass and textiles.

Her time on the course helped her see how art could be used therapeutically and developed her clay-making skills.

But scrapping the course has left her "sad" and worried about future creative jobs.

NTU has said the decision was made due to falling numbers in those applying to study decorative arts.

Chloe said: "I think quite a lot will be missed out through this course being scrapped because the same skills aren't learnt in other more contemporary art courses. I feel really sad for the next generation of makers who will no longer have the enriching opportunities we did.

"For the past year I have been working within childhood sexual abuse. I have often used clay in sessions which is a skill I learnt on the course – a skill others now may not be able to learn.

"Using clay within art therapy is a powerful tool which has been found to connect thoughts to feelings. Many young people have found clay a useful tool to manage and process their thoughts and feelings."

Chloe, who had around 46 course mates studying with her, said that through the course the university encouraged students to engage with volunteer work in the city.

She added: "I ran a project for bereaved children with complex needs for the three years I was on the course through a charity. I know other decorative arts students have run similar projects or volunteered in other ways this was a way that many students contributed so it’s a real loss.

"The contribution to the economy from the arts is so much bigger than people might imagine. Even for example walking into a shop like IKEA, it's courses like decorative arts that lead into those roles."

Another former decorative arts student, Becky Dick, who lives in Gloucestershire, added: "I ended up doing a taxidermy placement whilst I was studying in my second year. Even as something as niche as taxidermy I was able to still get the guidance and encouragement I needed."

The 27-year-old, who now works as a taxidermist, added: "Without this course I couldn't have done any of that and I’m truly saddened and devastated the course couldn't be saved.

"By the end of the course in 2015 I began trading as Taxtiles - a merge of the two words textile & taxidermy."

Annie McGraw, 22, graduated from the course at NTU last year and works as a barista and receptionist in Nottingham.

She said: "I'm so gutted. Decorative arts was such a humble and one-off course and there aren’t many like it left in the UK.

"The impact of it being scrapped will be very negative for any future artists who have a variety of skills and want to explore and find themselves."

A spokesperson for NTU said: “Demand for places on decorative arts degrees is falling and we have experienced a reduction in applications at NTU.

“Applications for our undergraduate degree in decorative arts will now cease. All students currently on BA Decorative Arts at NTU will be able to complete their degree as normal.

“No job losses are planned as existing staff are expected to provide the enhanced making and crafts provision in other subject areas, including fine art and product and furniture design."
