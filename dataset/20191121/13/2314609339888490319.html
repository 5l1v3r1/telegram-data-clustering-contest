Australian Oscar entry shines at Brisbane's Asia Pacific Screen Awards

21 Nov 2019, 13:00 by Toby Crockford

The film chosen to be Australia's submission for next year's Oscars has taken out one of the top gongs at the prestigious 13th Asia Pacific Screen Awards in Brisbane.

This year's awards, which are the region's highest accolade in film and encompass 70 countries, featured 37 nominated films from 22 countries.

Queensland actress Deborah Mailman co-hosted Thursday night's ceremony for the first time, at the Brisbane Convention and Exhibition Centre.

Buoyancy, directed by Australian Rodd Rathjen, won the APSA for Best Youth Feature Film amid a series of premieres across the world.

Mr Rathjen said it was his first feature film and focused on shining a light on human trafficking and modern slavery in Cambodia.

"I read a report about what was going on a few years ago and I was just shocked and felt immense sadness for these men and boys whose voices had been forgotten and not heard," he said.

"The film is about a young boy who's looking for independence and a new life outside Cambodia.

"He runs away from home, is trafficked, tricked into crossing the Thailand border, enslaved on a trawler and has to work out how to escape."

Mr Rathjen, who is from regional Victoria, said the film was set to premiere in France next week and had already screened in Australia, India, the United States and Turkey.

He was in Los Angeles last week for Oscars pre-screenings.

The film came to fruition after several years of hard work, including rigorous research.

"There were lots of challenges including the language barrier, logistically filming on water, and only having 27 days to shoot it," Mr Rathjen said.

"I don’t want to complain too much because the people in this film have lived through some dehumanising experiences and the challenges of making this film don’t compare.

"But it was critical we researched to the extent we did and made it as authentic and real as possible."

The APSAs - dubbed the "Oscars for the rest of the world" - will have a permanent home in Brisbane if lord mayor Adrian Schrinner has his way.

Cr Schrinner committed to locking in the major international awards for the River City long-term.

"Previously it [awards ceremony] was held in other parts of the state, but permanently it will be here [in Brisbane] going forward," Cr Schrinner said on Thursday.

"We want Brisbane to continue to have a growing and thriving film industry, but to have these international awards right here in Brisbane is something incredibly important for our city.

"Major events such as the Asia Pacific Screen Awards contribute $150 million into Brisbane’s economy and create thousands of local jobs for Queenslanders."
