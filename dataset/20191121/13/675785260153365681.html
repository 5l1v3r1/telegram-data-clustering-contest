8 'radical' Labour manifesto policies that are totally normal in other countries

Everyone's talking about how the Labour Party's general election manifesto is the most radical in years - but a lot of the policies in it are the norm elsewhere in the world

21 Nov 2019, 13:37 by Mikey Smith, https://www.mirror.co.uk/authors/mikey-smith/

Labour's general election manifesto has been described as the most radical political pitch in years.

Running to 107 pages, it includes pledges to renationalise the railways, take water services into public ownership and scrap tuition fees.

The party promise to extend free childcare to one and two year olds.

And it promises to return corporation tax to 26%.

This may be seen as radical in Britain, but some of Jeremy Corbyn 's policies already look familiar if you live abroad.

Here's eight 'radical' proposals in the Labour manifesto that look pretty ordinary overseas.

1. Nationalising the railways

Labour would repeal the Tories' 1993 Railways Act and bring the railways back under state ownership.

This would happen over a decade or so one network at a time, when each private franchise expires.

In Germany state-owned national operator Deutsche Bahn ran 94% of services on time and earned back nearly €2bn in 2015.

In France, government-owned SNCF Group recorded a net profit of €567m in 2016.

And in Canada, Via Rail is a 'Crown Corporation' which runs the rails on behalf of the government. It received $267m of state funding to plug losses in 2016, but passenger revenues grew almost 9.5%.

Admittedly EU law is a stumbling block to new nationalisation, because it pushes "greater competition" on rail networks.

But that would cease to be a stumbling block if Brexit happened.

 

2. Nationalising water

Labour promise to bring the water network into public ownership.

That's just normal in most of the world - it's normal in many parts of the United Kingdom.

Publicly owned Scottish Water provides plumbing north of the border.

And while it's not totally nationalised, much of Wales' water is provided by Welsh Water - a not-for-profit company, which serves 3 million people.

In fact, England is one of the only countries in the world to have fully privatised its water and sewerage system.

 

3. Restrictions on gambling advertising in sports

The Labour manifesto promises to "curb gambling advertising in sports."

It doesn't go into more detail than that - but even in countries where sports betting is legal it's far from unusual for restrictions to be placed on advertising.

Many states in the US outlaw gambling on sports events altogether. Australia has banned gambling ads during sporting events - including on streaming services.

And Italy last year announced plans to ban gambling firms from sponsoring sports teams and stadiums, a move that has already been taken by Argentina and India.

4. Free university tuition

"We will abolish university tuition fees," the manifesto boasts. "No one should be put off educating themselves for lack of money or fear of debt."

Fees were introduced under Tony Blair and hiked to £3,000 a year in 2006 and £9,000 a year in 2012.

The Tories say it's right that people pay their way because fees subsidise the middle class, and the number of people going to university has soared.

But in Germany tuition's free, despite a flirtation with fees in the last decade and a recent move to charge foreigners.

There's been much controversy in Germany and talk of this being unsustainable, but Germany actually spent a smaller percentage of its GDP on higher education than the UK in 2012.

And it's not alone - neither Finland nor Norway nor Slovenia charge tuition fees for their own citizens.

5. Higher corporation tax

Britain's corporation tax rate is 19% and set to fall lower after the Tories cut it from 26%.

Labour is pledging to reverse this - which Boris Johnson falsely claimed would see Britain have the "highest rate in Europe".

But even 26% would be the lowest rate among G7 countries, according to the OECD.

Taking into account local government taxes, Italy 's rate starts at 27% and can go as high as 34.5%, Germany's works out between 30 and 33%, Japan 30.86 % - its lowest rate in decades, and France between 31 and 33%.

Labour's Jonathan Ashworth told the BBC: "We've had huge cuts in corporation tax under the Conservatives and normal people are not feeling the benefit of that.

"So we are going to ask corporations to pay a higher rate of corporation tax - something well in line with what other nations across the European Union are asking them to pay.

"This is not radical by the standards of our competitors and neighbours."

Boris Johnson had promised to cut the rate to 17% - but abandoned this pledge this week, despite having claimed just months ago that "every time corporation tax has been cut in this country it has produced more revenue."

The IFS rubbished this claim.

 

6. A rent cap

The manifesto promises to "stop runaway rents by capping them with inflation".

This does happen in other countries - but the best-known examples are at a local level.

Dublin and Cork in Ireland capped rises at 4% last year, with the Housing Minister saying: "We're putting a bridle on the horse that has almost been out of control."

Most parts of San Francisco in the United States have a rent rise cap of 2.2% this year.

German capital Berlin banned landlords from raising rents by more than 10% above the local average in 2015.

 

7. Free childcare for one-year-olds

The manifesto vows to extend 30 hours of free childcare a week to two-year-olds and "move towards" it for some one-year-olds.

Tory policy currently offers it to three- and four-year-olds.

This would be a massive policy for parents, but it's not unheard of.

Finland gives a universal right to childcare for all children between about nine months old and school age. This can include state-provided services.

The Tory government has outraged green campaigners by refusing to ban fracking. Labour says it'd end it.

8. A ban on fracking

Opponents fear fracking - in which liquid is pumped deep underground at high pressure to fracture rock and release gas - can cause problems including water contamination, earthquakes and noise and traffic pollution.

And it's already been banned in France, Bulgaria and Germany.

That's not to mention, erm, Scotland, where the SNP -run government announced an indefinite ban on fracking in 2015.
