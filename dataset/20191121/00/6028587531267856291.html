Billionaire Bloomberg Takes Another Step To Run For President⁠—But Still Unclear If He Actually Will

21 Nov 2019 by Lisette Voytko

Topline: Billionaire and former New York City mayor Michael Bloomberg filed paperwork to run in the 2020 presidential race on the Democratic ticket⁠—but it’s still unclear if he’s actually running as a late entry crowded field of 17 candidates. 

  • Bloomberg, 77, has previously said he was considering a 2020 run but had not officially made a decision.
  • He has already filed to appear on primary ballots in Texas, Alabama and Arkansas.
  • If Bloomberg ran, he would likely present himself as a centrist candidate in contrast to progressives Elizabeth Warren and Bernie Sanders.
  • In March he said he would not enter the race, citing “the difficulty of winning the Democratic nomination in such a crowded field.”
  • Bloomberg has also committed $15 million to $20 million for a voter registration drive in five battleground states and $100 million for an online advertising campaign to attack President Trump in four swing states. 
  • A spokesperson for Bloomberg told MSNBC that the federal filing is “not an announcement and not indicative of a decision.” 

Big number: $100 million. That’s how much of his own fortune Bloomberg might spend on a presidential run, according to one of his political advisors in 2018.

Key background: Bloomberg cofounded financial information and media company Bloomberg LP in 1981. He put in the seed funding for the company and now owns 88% of the business, which has estimated revenues of $10 billion. Forbes estimates Bloomberg’s net worth at $52 billion. He has also reportedly been in contact with allies to rethink the 2020 race. If he ends up running, he will likely focus on Super Tuesday states and forgo early primaries in Iowa, South Carolina and New Hampshire. 

Tangent: Bloomberg was not the only one considering a last-ditch presidential run as moderate Democrats in the party worry about their chances in a general election if a more liberal candidate gets the nomination. After initially saying he wouldn’t run, former Massachusetts Governor Deval Patrick entered the race on November 14 and is likely to seek out the same centrist voters as Bloomberg.
