Ground-breaking study of children may stop burns victims being scarred for life

21 Nov 2019, 00:01

Burns victims could soon no longer be scarred for life, thanks to a ground-breaking new study starting in the UK.

The Scar Free Foundation has announced plans to launch the world’s biggest study of children with burns, in partnership with University Hospitals Bristol NHS Foundation Trust.

Over at least the next five years, researchers will collect blood and small tissue samples from victims across Britain.

They will then analyse the genetic information to try and identify factors and causes which lead patients to either scar badly or not at all.

This will then create a bank of data, which the Scar Free Foundation hopes can be used to create a scar free world within a generation.

For children like Elizabeth Soffe, five, healing her scars could mean being going swimming without any pain or not being stared at in the street.

The youngster suffered extensive burns to her body at just six months old, after an air conditioning unit caught fire in her family’s home in Qatar.

She has scarring to her legs, face and arms, and lost her hair, a lot of her fingers, part of her nose and one of her ears in the blaze.

Parts of her body that weren’t burned were used to provide skin grafts and she has restricted movement due to scarring on her elbows, wrists and other joints.

Answering questions with her mum Sinead, Elizabeth revealed that people often stare at her or say ‘mean things’ to her, including calling her ‘ugly’.

She said: ‘My scars stretch and hurt and they are itchy, I can’t hold onto the bars in gymnastics because I don’t have very many fingers and it hurts my arms.

‘People say mean things because I look different to them, but I know I’m beautiful.’

Elizabeth has to have frequent operations and skin grafts, and her scars itch constantly which can keep her awake at night.

She also has to have cream massaged into her skin several times a day and daily physiotherapy to help her movement.

Her dad Liam said: ‘Elizabeth just wants to do the things that other five-year-olds enjoy – playing with friends, swimming and having fun.’

Simon Weston, 58, was badly burned at the age of 20 during the Falklands War, when the RFA Sir Galahad was destroyed in the Bluff Cove Air Attack in 1982.

He was the closest person to the bomb to survive and suffered severe burns to 46% of his body.

The veteran is now known for his motivational speaking and charity work, and was awarded a CBE in 2016.

He said: ‘As people with scarring, our contribution is as valuable as all the clinicians and the researchers. We’ve been the tapestry for them to work with.

‘There’s a heck of a lot of work to do – we need to have something in place to minimise scarring and eventually eradicate it. To end up with a scar free world, wouldn’t it be wonderful?’

It is expected that the new study will officially launch early next year.

Brendan Eley, Chief Executive of the Scar Free Foundation, said: ‘The NHS provides excellent burn care within the boundaries of what is currently possible.

‘However, we want to highlight that burn care – particularly the prevention of scarring from burns and scalds – has not moved on in the way that other areas of medicine have, which is why we need more medical research in this area.

‘Burns patients tell us that they are most concerned about longer term scarring and the impact that it has on their movement and quality of life.

‘We want to help people living with scarring achieve their full potential, as well as moving forward towards a scar free future.

‘A cohort study would be groundbreaking – enabling us to map the DNA of an enormous range of children with burns, so we can isolate those factors which influence the severity of scarring and improve patients’ lives.’
