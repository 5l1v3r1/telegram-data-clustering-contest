'My sons died side by side on ecstasy' Heartbroken dad wants to legalise the drug that killed his two boys in one night

Aberystwyth University student Torin, 19, was found dead alongside his brother Jacques, 20

17 Nov 2019, 18:33 by Beth Abbit, Anna Lewis

The father of two brothers who died "side by side" after taking ecstasy bought on the dark web has called for all drugs to be legalised.

Aberystwyth University student Torin, 19, was found dead alongside his brother Jacques, 20, in Manchester in the heart-breaking turn of events weeks before Christmas.

The boys, who had always been close, had arranged to meet in Manchester to watch a football match as they knew they faced spending Christmas apart.

Astrophysics student Torin had just started his second year at the University of Aberystwyth.

Meanwhile, talented guitarist Jacques had moved to London to live with Ray’s mum and had started a new job at a hotel.

So the boys arranged to meet in Manchester to watch United play Hull City at Old Trafford on November 29, 2014.

That day, the siblings checked into the Grapes pub, in Stoneclough, Bolton, where they started drinking, the Mancester Evening News reports.

CCTV captured them buying two bottles of WKD and a packet of chewing gum which they took back to their hotel, but never consumed.

Torin was the last to be seen when he came down from their room at around 9.30pm to ask for the pub's Wi-Fi password.

Speaking to the Manchester Evening News, father Ray Lakeman said: "I know they would have done what they normally did - just watch a video together.

"That was the last anyone saw of them. That was Saturday night at 9.30pm. They were not found until 2.30pm on Monday."

The brothers’ bodies were found in the room two days later - after two missing persons reports had been filed.

During an inquest into their deaths in 2015 a coroner recorded their cause of death as fatal MDMA toxicity.

Bolton Coroners’ Court heard that Jacques' had almost six times the ‘recreational’ dose of MDMA in his body, while Torin had an even higher amount.

Pathologist Dr Patrick Ward told the inquest that a lethal limit of ecstasy is an average of 1.7mg.

The dealer who supplied the drug to the brothers is currently serving a 16-year sentence in prison.

Ray says: “The obvious thing everyone expects me to say is ‘they should be banned’. If I thought banning drugs works I would support it, of course.”

“When I went to the inquest in Bolton one of the things that resonated with me was that they said the boys had exceeded what everyone understands to be a recreational dose.

“It struck me that the authorities know there is a recreational dose that won’t kill you.

“Of course wherever you buy it from you can’t get a recreational dose because it’s not a regulated market.”

He adds: “Drugs are banned but it didn’t stop my boys getting hold of it and wanting to take it.

“I don’t like it and I’m not condoning it but people need to realise their kids are doing it too. You would be surprised how many kids are doing it.

“Banning it doesn’t stop it. If it did I would be 100 per cent behind banning it, but it won’t stop people wanting to take it.

“Statistically, my boys were unlucky.”

He adds: “There is this entrenched argument that drugs are bad. I think most of these deaths are preventable.”

At the end of the month, Ray will travel to Manchester to retrace his sons' final footsteps on the fifth anniversary of their death.

He will also be speaking at the Manchester: Take Drugs Seriously conference, organised by campaigning group Anyone’s Child .

Ray will be calling for legal regulation of drugs along with politicians and former police officers who will discuss a new approach to illegal drugs.

“I think we need to understand why people are using drugs and I mean drugs generally,” he says.

“The number of drugs deaths is rising, the strength is increasing and more people are using them. There needs to be a seachange in our thinking, otherwise these deaths will continue.

“We can’t just enforce the law and arrest our way out of this.”

Originally from Old Trafford himself, Ray had taken the boys to watch United play when they were children.

He will return to United’s ground as part of his pilgrimage this month.

“My boys were very very close,” he says. “They did everything together. They even played in a band together.

“I know they were just trying to have a good time that night.
