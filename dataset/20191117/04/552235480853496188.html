Forced marriages and honour killings on the rise in Scotland’s ethnic communities

Police and women’s support agencies have seen a surge in the number of cases in the past four years.

17 Nov 2019, 04:30 by Norman Silvester, https://www.dailyrecord.co.uk/authors/norman-silvester/

Forced marriages and so-called honour-based violence are on the rise in Scotland’s ethnic communities, new statistics reveal.

Police and women’s support agencies have seen a surge in the number of cases in the last four years.

Official figures show that the number of forced marriages recorded – including girls as young as 14 – jumped from 40 in 2015 to 53 last year.

Honour-based violence against women rose from 104 cases four years ago to 131 incidents in 2018.

Girijamba Polubothu, of Shakti Women’s Aid in ­Edinburgh, explained that the charity is regularly contacted by girls who fear they will be forced into marriage when they turn 16.

 

She said: “The figures do not ­surprise me. The problem is under-reported. In the last year, we helped 23 women  who were victims of forced ­marriage.

Some were under 16.

“We’re helping nine people aged between 15 and 19 and several girls aged 14.

“Victims of forced marriage often have disabilities and mental health problems.”

In 2017, Shakti helped 533 women who were victims of domestic abuse including honour-based violence.

Victims are mainly female and come from Asia, the Middle East, Far East, and Africa although some are also born in the United Kingdom.

Girijamba said: “The grooming for forced marriage can begin in childhood.

Girls are normally taken abroad to marry, when 16 or 17.

 

“They’re then presented with a man, or a few men and asked to select who they want to marry. It looks like there’s a choice but there isn’t.

Often victims are meeting their husbands for the first time.”

Shakti helps women affected by forced marriage and honour-based violence abuse in Edinburgh, Dundee, the Forth Valley and Fife.

Police and social services are automatically told of cases ­involving under-age girls.

Dr Marsha Scott, chief ­executive of Scottish Women Aid said: “The figures we have on this issue are the tip of the iceberg.

“Many honour-based abuse victims are vulnerable.

“They’re often poor, English is not their first language and they can’t access benefits or housing.

 

“It’s difficult to for them to find help. This is happening all over Scotland.”

Scottish Women’s Aid is ­carrying out lectures across the country to police officers, ­teachers, social workers and healthcare experts to tell them about the growing problem.

Detective Chief Inspector Debra ­Forrester said: “If you or someone you know is in a forced marriage, or living in fear of being forced to marry, then the law is there to protect you.

“Honour-based abuse is a violation of human rights and has no justification.

“We take all reports seriously.”
