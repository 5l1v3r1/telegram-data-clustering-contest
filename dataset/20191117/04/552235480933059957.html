US brain expert backs SFA heading ban to protect footballers from dementia

Dr Chris Nowinksi, a former WWE wrestler, says the links between repeated concussion and developing the illness are clear.

17 Nov 2019, 04:30 by Gordon Blackstock

A US brain expert has called for new laws that will protect young footballers from developing dementia in later life.

Dr Chris Nowinksi, a former WWE wrestler, said the links between repeated concussion and developing the illness were clear.

The neuroscientist said he was satisfied the Scottish FA will ban children heading the ball in an effort to protect them.

But Dr Nowinski, a graduate of Harvard University who set up campaign group the Concussion Legacy Foundation, has urged authorities to also move towards legislative protection for footballers.

The 41-year-old, whose wrestling career ended in 2003 when he was kicked on the chin, causing post-concussion syndrome, said: “I’m thrilled the Scottish FA is acting.

“I was hurt in 2003 and we started the foundation in 2007 to campaign on concussion in sports and the consequences it has.

“In America, we’ve been having this national conversation since 2007.

“There’s been a reluctance in football in Europe to do the same that’s been driven by three factors.

“We’re talking about the most popular sport in the world.

“There’s a lot of money in the game and you also have a global organisation running it that has credibility issues, FIFA. That all contributes to not acting.

“In the US, we had hearings in Congress over the matter in 2009 and there’s been regional legislation introduced to protect players from the effects of concussion.

 

“If it takes laws to get protection for footballers from concussion, then make it a law.”

 Dr Nowinski has recruited former NFL players to leave their brains to medical research when they die to find out more about what happened to them during their careers.

He added that US laws require young athletes to be trained in the signs of concussion.

Players are banned from returning to the field while still suffering from the effects of the temporary condition.

Dr Nowinski said the way football deals with concussed players was far behind the US.

The current protocol sees games stopped for three minutes to allow assessment of suspected concussion by medical staff.

He said: “The current standards to assess players in three minutes is inappropriate.

 

“It’s not possible to carry out an assessment in that time. In America, it’s accepted that it takes between eight and 10 minutes.

“I’d like to see the current  practice trebled in football.”

Last month, we told how former Watford striker George Reilly, 62, had been diagnosed with dementia when he was just 57.

He said he regularly played games with concussion and had received 250 stitches to his head in knocks over his career, which he believes had contributed to developing dementia.

The Scottish Government said: “We are the first country in the world to produce national
guidance on dealing with concussion in sport and we are taking a range of other actions to help make sport safe.

“We also welcome the commitment of the Scottish Youth Football Association and the
Scottish Football Association to provide advice and take action on this issue.

“We would support any measures taken, particularly for primary school-age children.”
