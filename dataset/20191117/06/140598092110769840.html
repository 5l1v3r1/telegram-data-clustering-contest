Kathryn Flett reviews Henry & Joe's, Berkshire: 'An unpretentious life-enhancing, cockle-warming gaff' 

17 Nov 2019, 06:00 by Kathryn Flett

After a three-hour drive to Newbury, Berkshire, on a November Saturday afternoon, I exited the car in an al fresco NCP corral at the mercy of frankly apocalyptic rain, coupled with a wind so biting its own teeth were chattering. I was soaked through to my smalls in seconds.

This was a recipe for the drowning-of-pre-drowned sorrows, PDQ. Lacking a sprauncy-looking boutique hotel with deep-pile focaccia, millefeuille bed linen and cocktails in glasses the size of Premier League trophies, under extreme weather-siege we prepared to compromise on Henry & Joe’s – which, I prayed, had robust central heating and a decent cellar. Have sorrows, will drown.

H&J’s is two years old this very month, and quite the toast of Newbury, at least if TripAdvisor is your guide. Henry is in the kitchen and Joe is front of house, and praise be I’d had the foresight to book online – because it is a tiny joint.

Ducking through the door a young man who I assumed was Joe greeted me with “Kathryn! Wow! Gosh, you are wet! Let’s get you out of that coat and seated next to a lovely, warm radiator smartish” – or similarly kindly and mollycoddling words to that effect. Enveloped in Joe’s hospitality duvet, I naturally spent at least three quarters of the next two hours falsely assuming that he knew “who I was” – or at least “why I was here”.

But no, he didn’t. Eventually, as the small room filled to the brim and our host moved from table to table dispensing good cheer – often squatting on his haunches, nose level with the cutlery, asking earnestly what “you guys” need – I realised Joe was to H&J’s front-of-housery what Mr Tumble is to CBeebies: one without the other is inconceivable, not least because a restaurant called “Henry &” is a bit silly.

As ever, it’s all about context. Inside a West End venue of chilly faux-swankery, I would not only be horrified if the maître d’ hunkered down next to me to discuss the state of his sourdough, I would probably leave.

Here in the Thames Valley, on an End-of-Days Saturday, however, it very nearly made me cry with joy. I felt seen, I felt heard, I was this far from going the full Sally-Field-at-the-Academy-Awards. Nonetheless, masking my feelings with haute-critical froideur, I said “thanks” with a tight little smile as we took our table adjacent – blessings! – to a radiator. There was clearly at least one reason why this modest space, with pale grey walls, wood tables and open kitchen – is awash with five-star reviews on TripAdvisor, namely Joe. Henry now had everything to play for.

At Saturday lunch the menu is set: two courses for £25, three for £30, bread and butter £1.50 per person and sides £4 a pop. It’s a bold choice ordering a chilled first course when you are wet: however I went for swede remoulade, confit egg yolk, soy gel and the local Spenwood cheese; my partner for smoked haddock kedgeree tart with kaffir lime leaf.

I became immediately fond of the piquant remoulade topped by gelatinous yolk. This centre staging of a vegetable about which nobody ever says “words cannot express how much I love swede” is why I’d ordered it.

Meanwhile, my partner’s tart ranneth over, struggling to contain the smoked haddock. It didn’t matter though because the taste was there; a cosy dish. Our bread was ace, too.

Relaxing, I requested a large glass of a sunny Chilean chardonnay because I wasn’t driving home, and ordered all the sides – triple-cooked chips with Marmite hollandaise, purple broccoli with peanut and malt crumb and “parsley braised sand carrot” (which sounds like a song from Trumpton) to go with my pork cutlet, braised cheek, salt-baked pineapple, crushed pumpkin and anchovy, and his cod, celeriac, caramelised kohlrabi, black pudding and walnut ketchup.

I’ve barely eaten pork (and have given up bacon) for months because it feels like one of the most ethically out-of-step-with-our-times meats around – however, this was a lovely smidgen, and the accompanying collapsible flavourful braised cheek was gorgeous, succulent meat and a robust nom-nom reduction.

All the veg were on form and the flavour pile-up was entirely satisfying. The man opposite was pleased by his double whammy of white fish dishes, topped and tailed with another tart for dessert – this time a crème brûlée with a banana and passionfruit sorbet.

Meanwhile, I returned to the creamy Spenwood ewes’ milk cheese with crackers and chutney.

As we mopped up our crumbs, dry by now and teetering on the brink of “happy”, at the table next to ours a party of four regulars was attempting to book another lunch on a weekday in December. “Sorry,” said Joe, “we’ve no tables for four free in December.”

Poor Newbury. Turns out the reason Henry and Joe’s is a proper small-scale success story is down to both Joe and Henry, whose unpretentious life-enhancing local properly cockle-warmed a couple of wet and miserable strangers.

Don’t go out of your way, reader; however, if you’re ever in Newbury do pop in and give them both a hug.
