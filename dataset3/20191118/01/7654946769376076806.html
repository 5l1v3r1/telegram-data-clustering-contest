Congestion fee panel can meet in secret, MTA brass claims

18 Nov 2019, 01:54 by Carl Campanile

Despite calls for transparency in its formation of Manhattan’s congestion-pricing plan, the MTA claims the powerful committee that will propose the tolls is not covered under the state’s Open Meetings Law — and can operate in secret.

The state-controlled transit authority has yet to name the members of the Traffic Mobility Review Board, which will study tolling levels and make recommendations.

“The TMRB is tasked only with making recommendations and it doesn’t perform a governmental action subject to the open meetings law,” claimed MTA spokeswoman Abbey Collins.

She promised that “once constituted the TMRB will determine a public engagement process, which we believe is critically important.”

Officials hope the tolling program will pay for $15 billion of the $51.5 billion the MTA says it needs to improve subways, buses and commuter railroads.

Drivers will be charged a yet-to-be-determined toll to enter the new congestion zone, which will cover Manhattan south of 60th Street.

A coalition of 20 transit watchdog groups on Friday demanded the MTA swiftly appoint the toll board, and argued it should be required to follow state transparency laws.

Those groups immediately took issue with the MTA’s legal position Sunday.

“These recommendations will affect millions of people. Sunshine is the best way to have these conversations,” said Lisa Daglian of the Permanent Advisory Committee to the MTA.

Good government groups said there is a precedent for their push, pointing out that the state’s Committee on Open Government ruled another MTA panel — the Sustainability Advisory Workgroup — was subject to the Open Meetings Law.

“The same standard would seem to apply in this case, particularly around votes to approve any recommendations,” said Reinvent Albany’s Rachael Fauss.

She said the coalition will request a formal opinion from the state Committee on Open Government if the MTA refuses to be budge.

State lawmakers legally prohibited the MTA’s congestion pricing toll board from releasing its pricing recommendations until after the 2020 election to avoid a possible backlash from outer-borough and suburban drivers at the ballot box.

The Post broke the story in April exposing the provision, which bars the panel from producing its report until 12 days after the Nov. 3, 2020, election.

Additionally, the six appointed TMRB members will have the power to establish hardship exemptions from the toll and allow for bridge and tunnel tolls to be rebated.

One representative will be drawn from the Long Island Railroad and MetroNorth territories and a second will be appointed by the mayor.

The rest of the members will be appointed by the MTA’s Triborough Bridge and Tunnel Authority, which oversees the East River bridges and tunnels.
