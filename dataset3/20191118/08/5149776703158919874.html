Father-of-two determined to work after brain tumour left him struggling to see

"If I close my right eye the left eye is just light and shade."

18 Nov 2019, 08:41 by Jamie Barlow

Father-of-two Martin Hales has said he is determined to carry on working after being diagnosed with a serious brain tumour.

Mr Hales, 59, of Vernon Drive, Nuthall, said 'there’s no point sitting around feeling sorry for yourself'.

Mr Hales had worked as a furniture maker but has now been left unable to drive.

Keen to continue working, he is now employed by a charity called Headway, a brain injury association.

After noticing problems with his sight and "feeling really low", medics told him the devastating news he had a benign tumour in 2011.

"It was a big shock," he said. "You just did not expect them to come out and say it's a brain tumour.

"As soon as you hear the news 'brain tumour' you think the world's ending. It was operable.

"They explained to me with it being benign it does not spread but it could still grow."

He described the tumour as "like a boiled egg behind my left eye and under the brain".

Parts of the tumour, which was about an inch-and-a-half in diametre, have been removed.

Mr Hales also underwent radiotherapy at Nottingham City Hospital. But his vision has been affected and he suffers from fatigue.

"If I close my right eye the left eye is just light and shade," he said.

"I can't make anything out. There's no actual picture."

“During my time in recovery, I found that there wasn’t really much help about, other than what charities were able to offer,” he said.

“If it weren’t for charities like Headway, people would really struggle. I know I would have.”

He became involved with Headway after enrolling on a return to work scheme.

Mr Hales now works at the charity's shop in Nottingham Road, Eastwood.

"I find it so worthwhile to work for a charity. I’m around people who understand what I’ve been through and some of the challenges I might face.

“I love coming to work. It helps me to take my mind off things and focus on the positive," he said.

"Life after a brain injury can be tough sometimes but there’s no point sitting around feeling sorry for yourself, you’ve got to get out there and do something you love.

“The damage caused to my left eye as a result of the tumour means that I’m always bumping into things in the shop, but everyone’s learnt to laugh about it now.

"A couple of times I’ve even walked into a customer but it’s always taken on the chin.

“I just have to explain my situation and people are usually very understanding, especially the staff at Headway.”

Mr Hales was asked to open a new Headway shop on Thursday (November 14) in High Street, Beeston.

His wife Teresa, 54, a hairdresser, said: "Headway has given him the stability of having something to get up and go for everyday.

"It's very important to Martin, otherwise he will feel worthless."

Jo Plant, Headway’s director of fundraising, said: “We would like to thank our incredible team of staff and volunteers who worked tirelessly to get the shop ready for its big opening.

“Martin has achieved so much since his brain injury and we’re honoured that he opened the shop for us.

“All the money raised in the shop will help our charity to support people across the UK coming to terms with the devastating consequences of brain injury.

“Like all the Headway shops, the Beeston store will rely entirely on stock donations and the selfless efforts of our volunteers."

She added: "We’re always looking to welcome new volunteers to the team and we have lots of opportunities to get involved - whether it's greeting customers with a friendly smile, sorting through stock or manning the till.”
