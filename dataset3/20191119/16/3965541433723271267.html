Tree boom: Why Bellevue is planting thousands of large trees

19 Nov 2019, 16:01 by Dyer Oxley | KOMO News

BELLEVUE -- If you're worried about the addition of thousands of giant, tall trees slated for Bellevue, don't forget that the city has 2,000 acres of undeveloped public forests for them to spread around.

The city of Bellevue started a considerable tree planting effort this week. By the end of winter, it aims to have planted 1,000 giant sequoia trees and 5,000 native conifers.

"Although native to areas in Northern California, sequoias have thrived throughout the Pacific Northwest region for decades," said Christina Faine with the city of Bellevue's Parks and Community Services.

Volunteers with Cedar Crest Academy helped start the effort by planting 300 giant sequoia trees throughout Bellevue's Wilburton Park on Nov. 16 -- though they are currently only 2-feet-tall. Giant sequoias can grow to be well over 200 feet tall, and more than 20 feet in diameter. But it takes time to get to that size -- they can live up to 3,000 years.

Officials didn't just choose the sequoias for their impressive statistics. The city has experienced a shrinking tree canopy for decades, and has a goal of increasing coverage. Bellevue currently have about 1.4 million trees. While its tree canopy has held over the past decade, the city has experienced a decline in trees since the 1980s.

A 2017 assessment concluded that the city has about 37% tree coverage. That's about 3% short of its goal. In recent years, environmental challenges have arisen, making that goal more difficult to reach.

"Over the past five years, the Pacific Northwest has experienced some of the driest summers on record," Faine noted. "This has led to a greater degree of drought stress for native conifer trees, making them more vulnerable to fungal diseases, insects and other pathogens. As a result, we have seen a higher rate of decline and mortality in native conifers. Sequoia trees are more tolerant to drought stress and resist many of the pathogens that impact native conifers."

Moving forward, city officials intend to plant approximately 7,000 new conifer trees within parks and other open spaces each year.

"Planting and introducing longer-lived conifer species into these areas is an important step in ensuring the long term health of these public forests for future generations."

The small sequoias were donated with the help of local resident Philip Steilstra, a retired Boeing employee, and the nonprofit PropagationNation. They're worth about $8,000.

The remaining 700 sequoia trees will be planted over the winter. The 5,000 native conifers also expected over the season will include western hemlock, western red cedar, and Douglas fir.
