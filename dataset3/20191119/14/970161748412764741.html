Gareth Thomas on taking the ‘power’ over his HIV diagnosis: ‘I’m okay with it’

19 Nov 2019, 14:03

Gareth Thomas has opened up about being diagnosed with HIV, explaining he has taken the ‘power’ over his condition.

The former rugby player told the world of his diagnosis back in September, sharing a video on his Twitter account explaining a tabloid newspaper had found out about his status and revealed the news to his parents.

And, during an appearance on Loose Women, the 45-year-old explained that he is hoping to educate everyone else in a bid to end the stigma.

‘When I was told that my parents knew I had HIV, and I hadn’t had the opportunity to tell them… I love my parents more than anything and I didn’t want to worry them, I didn’t want them to think I was going to die like I thought,’ he told the panel.

‘I had to try and calm them all down, I had to try and get them to understand what living with HIV was. And at that point, I learnt to live with it but I hadn’t learnt to accept it myself.

‘It was a struggle for me to expect someone else to accept it when I was struggling to accept it myself.

‘I had to take control, and by taking control, I had to take the power. To have power, you have to have knowledge,’ he continued. ‘I did my own research. I spoke to other people who were living with HIV, I spoke to my doctors, I listened to my doctors.

‘I realised that, through having all this knowledge, I’m okay with it.’

The athlete revealed that he is living with HIV in a video on social media, shared back in September.

In the clip, he explained: ‘Now you have that information, that makes me extremely vulnerable but it does not make me weak.

‘Even though I have been forced to tell you this, I choose to fight to educate and break the stigma around this subject.’

Now that Gareth has come to terms with the news, he is doing whatever he can to educate everyone else on the truth about his condition.

‘Now what I have to do is educate everybody else, so everybody else will be okay with it. Not for me, this is bigger than me – HIV, the stigma, education, it’s bigger than me,’ he explained on the ITV show.

‘I wanted to create environments where people wouldn’t be stigmatised, and by doing that, you have to educate people.

‘It’s really easy to educate people, but it’s really hard to re-educate people. To try and get people to unthink these thoughts and plant new ones is really, really difficult.

‘It’s impacted my life in a way that has made me so powerful. I am stripped bare. I have nothing to hide which is actually quite a free feeling, it’s really nice. It’s really, really nice.’
