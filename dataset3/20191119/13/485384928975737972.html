Cricket star Meg Lanning has come from the only girl on the team to Australian cricket captain

19 Nov 2019, 13:17 by Clint Thomas

Meg Lanning has emerged as a household name in world cricket as captain of both Australia and the Perth Scorchers.

But perhaps even more significantly she has become what she never had as a kid growing up — a visible cricket role model for young girls.

"We really didn't get to see them [female cricketers] on our TVs or in the news," she told the ABC.

"So, Ricky Ponting was the person I looked up to.

"I just loved watching him bat and captain and he seemed to be really competitive, which is similar to me as well.

"I really didn't realise how much women's cricket there was until I got involved a little bit more."

The challenge of finding a team

But even finding a way to get involved in the game when at primary school proved difficult for Lanning.

She found a female senior club side, but as a 12-year-old Lanning was too young for that.

A talented student and cricketer at Carey Grammar in Melbourne, Lanning also had the hurdle of there being no girls' team at the co-educational private school.

But once she reached Year 7, the solution was for Lanning to play in the Associated Private Schools (APS) competition for the Carey Grammar boys' team.

She was the first girl to do so and it got people's attention.

"We also had schools like Melbourne Grammar, Xavier College and Scotch College who were all boys' schools. so that was a little bit different," Lanning recalled.

"There were a few articles and talk about it at the time, but I wasn't too sure what all the fuss was about honestly."

She soon proved to be more than capable of playing at that level.

"Initially I guess they were a little bit surprised," she said.

"I started doing it from Year 7 so once you start doing it a few times, [it showed] I could compete with them as well, which probably helped.

"I played all the way through until the end of Year 12, so once I got to the back end of it was just sort of normal I guess.

"It was actually a really good pathway for me to go through."

From school player to international star

Fast forward a few years and Lanning is now a star dominating world cricket.

In 2014, at the age of 21, Lanning became the youngest captain of Australia when she replaced an injured Jodie Fields in that year's Ashes series.

Soon after, she was named Wisden's inaugural Leading Female Cricketer in the World.

The top-order batter has scored 13 one-day centuries for Australia at an average of almost 53.

Last year, Lanning captained Australia to a fourth ICC Women's World T20 title.

Lanning said her and the Australian team's success was helping normalise the female game with the public.

"That is changing over time I think," she said.

"We can still keep thinking about how we go about that sort of stuff, but from where I started until now it has been a massive shift and we are certainly in a pretty good space, which is great."

Lanning's Australian team is currently on a world record 18-game winning streak in one-day internationals.

There will be opportunity for more silverware early next year when Australia hosts the women's T20 World Cup.

Organisers hope the tournament's final at the Melbourne Cricket Ground — which will be held on International Women's Day in March — will set a new world record for attendance at a women's sporting event.
