Jeremy Corbyn and Boris Johnson slug it out over Scottish independence in crunch TV debate

IndyRef2 was the centre of clashes between the two men who could be Prime Minister in three weeks’ time.

19 Nov 2019, 23:10 by Torcuil Crichton, https://www.dailyrecord.co.uk/authors/torcuil-crichton/

Jeremy Corbyn and Boris Johnson slugged it out over independence in the crunch TV debate that could decide the future of the UK.

IndyRef2 was the centre of clashes between the two men who could be Prime Minister in three weeks’ time.

The Tory PM once again accused the Labour leader of being prepared to enter a “coalition of chaos” with Nicola Sturgeon to get to No10 with SNP backing in exchange for a second independence vote next year.

Johnson said: “Jeremy Corbyn and the Labour Party, be in no doubt about it, in order to secure the keys to No10, are going to do a deal, or probably have done a deal, with Nicola Sturgeon and the SNP to form a Corbyn-Sturgeon coalition. And the price of that deal would be a second referendum on the Union.”

But Corbyn snapped back at the Tory leader: “Nonsense”, adding: “Coalition of chaos? We’ve had that for the last nine years.”

 

He added: “There’s not going to be a coalition between Labour and anybody else. There are no deals that have been done and there will be no deals that are done.”

Pressed to rule out IndyRef2 before the end of the first year of a Labour government, Corbyn said: “I’ve said there would be no deal with the SNP, there would be no support for a Scottish referendum in the early years of the next Labour government because I want to invest in Scotland and give it the £70billion it needs in investment.”

He said it was “their choice” if the SNP leadership “chooses to put the Tory government back in office”.

Johnson shot back: “I listened very carefully to Mr Corbyn. I didn’t hear him say he was going to rule out a referendum on Scotland. Did you?”

With both men scoring glancing blows on Scotland in the opening clashes of the hour-long tv debate, Johnson was emphatic that the Union was more important than Brexit, even though his Brexit deal creates different trading rules for Northern Ireland.

He said: “The Union is the most important thing. It’s a fantastic thing.”

On the Union, Corbyn said he “hoped” the UK would stay together, leading to rivals piling in to criticise him afterwards. He added: “Our country is obviously very important and we have to bring this business to a close, and that’s why we’re proposing a trade deal with Europe or staying in, one or the other, as a way of bringing this issue to a close.”

 

On Brexit, Johnson tried to exploit Corbyn’s stance on whether he would back or vote against a Brexit deal he‘d negotiate with the EU.

But Corbyn dismissed Johnson’s pledge to “get Brexit done” by the end of January as “nonsense”.

Johnson said: “As soon as we can get that deal through Parliament, we can get on with people’s priorities.”

But Corbyn hit back: “The idea Johnson’s deal can be dealt with and finished by the end of January is such nonsense.

“What he is proposing is a trade deal which will take at least seven years to negotiate while at the same time saying he will negotiate a special trade deal with the EU. The two things are incompatible.”

The audience displayed contempt for both leaders. They sniggered at Corbyn’s stance on Brexit but laughed at Johnson when it came to questions on trust and integrity.

Corbyn was at home and winning applause when he spoke about the NHS and austerity, driving home his attack with a personal story about a friend who died of cancer this week.

 

There was an awkward moment when host Julie Etchingham asked the PM and Corbyn to “make a gesture” towards each other and shake hands before the ad break.

Corbyn later responded well to questions on anti-Semitism in the Labour Party, saying: “Anti-Semitism is an absolute evil and scourge within our society. I have taken action in my party where anyone who has committed any anti-Semitic acts or made any anti-Semitic statements, they are either suspended or expelled from the party.”

Corbyn got the tone right for the audience on the monarchy, saying: “It needs improvement.” And on the question of what he’d give Johnson for Christmas, he said he’d buy Johnson a copy of A Christmas Carol so the PM could see “what Scrooge was really like”.

But Corbyn needed a break-out moment and it did not come.
