Great North Air Ambulance Service wants to launch 24/7 service - but needs another £310k a year

The region's air ambulance service says 'four nights is not enough' and that the money will enable them to reach hundreds more severely ill and injured people every year

19 Nov 2019, 06:00 by Katie Dickinson

The Great North Air Ambulance Service has launched a bid to provide a 24/7 service in the North East , but says it needs another £310,000 a year.

The region's air ambulance service is asking supporters to help it raise the additional funds needed each year to bring the project to fruition.

Clinicians from GNAAS say the money will enable them to reach hundreds more severely ill and injured people every year.

The charity currently provides air ambulance services across the region, mainly in hours of daylight.

In 2015, in partnership with North East Ambulance Service, GNAAS also started providing a rapid response car service, staffed with a doctor and paramedic on Saturday and Sunday nights. This was expanded last year to include Thursday and Sunday nights.

Now, the charity is asking the public to help raise the additional funding required to activate its team every hour of every day if needed and has launched the Critical Care 24/7 Appeal to help achieve its aim.

Jamie Walsh, GNAAS paramedic, said: “Four nights is not enough. We want to be there for even more people.

“We want to be able to help 24 hours a day, seven days a week and for you to know that the time of day, or day of the week does not determine the level of care you receive in a life-threatening situation.”

The GNAAS rapid response cars come equipped with all the same kit as is found on the charity’s aircraft. This includes blood and plasma supplies which often make a critical difference to patients on scene.

Before 2015, such treatments were not available outside of the hospital at times when the aircraft was offline.

The service responded to 524 call-outs in the last financial year in its present four-day form. These call-outs included 140 assaults, 98 road traffic incidents, and 95 cardiac arrest.

Mr Walsh said the charity could attend hundreds more incidents if it could afford to work the additional three nights a week.

The charity’s Critical Care 24/7 appeal has won the support of former patient James Kavanagh, who benefitted from the charity’s rapid response car service on Christmas Day, 2015.

Mr Kavanagh, 22, of Newcastle , sustained multiple injuries in a head-on collision while travelling home after visiting family. His mother Elizabeth was also injured in the incident.

He said: “We’ll be forever thankful. Life is so precious and you just have to treasure it and those closest to you.

“We are supporting the campaign because it means more people like us can be rescued, and lives saved.”
