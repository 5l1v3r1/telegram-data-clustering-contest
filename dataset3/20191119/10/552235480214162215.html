Coatbridge conman jailed after posing as tax official and raking in £30k

Ryan Boyle will spend the next 18 months behind bars.

19 Nov 2019, 10:44 by Airdrie and Coatbridge Advertiser, https://www.dailyrecord.co.uk/authors/airdrie-and-coatbridge-advertiser/

A callous Coatbridge conman raked in more than £30,000 after using other people’s personal details to make fraudulent benefit claims.

Ryan Boyle will spend the next 18 months behind bars for posing as a tax official and calling dozens of unwitting people to get their National Insurance numbers and other information.

He persuaded Department of Work and Pension (DWP) staff that the applications he made were genuine and proceeded to pick up regular cash sums at pay points throughout Lanarkshire.

Boyle, 33, of Argyll Gardens, admitted forming a fraudulent scheme to defraud the DWP and North Lanarkshire Council between March 2015 and June the following year.

This is the second time brazen Boyle fraudulently got his hands on other people’s cash after he withdrew a three-figure sum – and tried to take out a further £1000 – from someone else’s bank account in his home town’s Halifax Bank of Scotland a decade ago; he was then ordered to repay £280 to the bank and fined £250.

Relating to Boyle’s latest offence, Paula Russell, prosecuting, said the scam came to light when a man who had stopped claiming benefits because he had become self-employed was challenged by tax officials about money being applied for in his name.

An investigation began and it emerged that Boyle had managed to get personal details from 55 people. He had failed in another 12 cases.

The scam netted opportunistic Boyle, who was sentenced at Hamilton Sheriff Court last week, a whopping £30,174.71.

Ms Russell said those involved could face “disastrous” consequences as a result of future benefits being refused because of their apparent involvement in fraudulent claims.

The fiscal described how Boyle “hijacked” other people’s personal information.

She explained: “People received phone calls from someone purporting to be from the Department of Work and Pensions.

“This person cleared security with them and then took their details.

“A number of similar claims were then made to the DWP; requests were made to have addresses and telephone numbers changed, and a common telephone number was found in relation to the new claims.

“The method of payment was changed to ‘simple payment’.

“This is used when a claimant doesn’t have a bank account or is unable to obtain one.

“A code was sent to Boyle, enabling him to withdraw funds at pay points which are found in hundreds of shops nationwide; he used outlets mainly in Lanarkshire.

“A number of supposedly different callers to the DWP appeared to have the same voice and Boyle was suspected as the department had his voice on file.

DWP officials spoke to staff at various outlets and a number of them queried the fact that the same man had been coming in using different identities.

“This man was Boyle, who had a distinctive tattoo between his thumb and forefinger. He was seen on CCTV footage.”

Defence agent Rowan Myles said that Boyle carried out the fraud to fund his alcohol and gambling addictions.

The addictions, he explained, were brought on by the disclosure of “devastating” family news.

Mr Myles said that his client suffers from depression and pointed out that he has not been in trouble since the fraud came to light more than three years ago.

He added that Boyle is employed and that he has started paying back the DWP.

But Sheriff David Bicket said only a prison sentence was appropriate, telling Boyle: “This scheme persisted for more than a year and involved a number of vulnerable people.

“It was carried out in a thorough and determined manner and it involved a significant amount of money.

“I appreciate there have been changes [in your life] but the offence is so serious that it can be dealt with only by a custodial sentence.”

Sheriff Bicket reduced the jail term from two years to 18 months to reflect the fact that, by not going to trial, Boyle had saved the court “considerable” time.
