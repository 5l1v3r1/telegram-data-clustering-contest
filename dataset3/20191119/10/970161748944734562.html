Prince Charles and Camilla touch noses with Maori group in New Zealand

19 Nov 2019, 10:27

Prince Charles and Camilla kicked off another day in New Zealand with a full Maori welcome on the grounds of Auckland’s Government House.

The Prince of Wales and Duchess of Cornwall performed the hongi with elders Kaumatua and Kuia, a greeting which involves two people pressing their noses and foreheads together.

They then watched as a Maori warrior lay down a dart in a challenge, known as a wero, carried out to ensure visitors come in peace.

Members of the New Zealand Defence Force’s Maori cultural group then performed a karanga, a call of welcome, followed by a haka – and also greeted the royals with the hongi.

Charles took the Royal Salute and inspected the Guard, before going on to hold private audiences with New Zealand’s Prime Minister Jacinda Ardern and opposition leader Simon Bridges.

Meanwhile, Camilla enjoyed high tea and listened to speeches from a paralympian, model and two schoolgirls at a Women of the World (WOW) event.

The festival was launched by Jude Kelly in 2010 and aims to celebrate female achievement and examine what obstacles women and girls face in achieving their potential.

Among the speakers was Alexia Hilbertidou, 20, who founded GirlBoss New Zealand as a teenager to close the gender gap in science, maths, technology and leadership.

She said: ‘When I was 16, I was the only girl in my digital technology class, and later the only girl in my advanced physics class and it was a very isolating experience.

‘I started to wonder “Why are so many of my female friends who are so intelligent and so capable not going into these fields and not feeling like they belong?”‘

She pointed out that there were more people called John than female chief executives of New Zealand’s biggest companies.

Camilla, president of WOW, also spoke at the event and thanked the ‘wonderful ladies’ for being brave enough to share their experiences.

She continued: ‘Wherever on the planet the festival takes place, it addresses particular issues affecting women’s lives there.

‘It gives women a chance to face the obstacles that block their paths and talk about the different solutions to the challenges in their life.

‘Above all, it celebrates and supports the achievements of multiple women and girls of every culture and community.’

The duchess also paid tribute a visit to staff at domestic violence charity Shine, which stands for Safer Homes in New Zealand Everyday.

During the meeting, she told them: ‘You’re all doing a brilliant job. Just listening to the survivors is so important. Hopefully today we can up the ante and raise a bit of awareness.’

Charles and Camilla are visiting New Zealand from November 17 to 23, in their first joint visit since 2015.

The Prince of Wales recently completed a two-day tour of India without the duchess.

The couple will next move on to Waitangi, a significant region in the history of New Zealand where a treaty was signed on the relationship between the Maori and Crown.
