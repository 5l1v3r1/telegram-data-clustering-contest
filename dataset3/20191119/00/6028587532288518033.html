Georgia On Their Mind

19 Nov 2019 by Bill Whalen

As the Democratic National Committee invariably obsess over locations when choosing presidential debate locales (curiously, and unlike Republicans, they never seem to settle on presidential libraries), let’s spend a moment on Atlanta’s Tyler Perry Studios, the host for Wednesday night’s 10-candidate fun-fest.The television show The Oval, which runs Wednesday evenings on the BET television network, is filmed on this studio’s grounds. The candidates won’t have to go far to find a replica of the White House’s West Wing.

If they came back in a couple of weeks, they’d encounter this year’s Miss Universe competition – yes, the same beauty pageant once owned by President Trump.

As for the pageantry on display Wednesday night in Atlanta, which may not be as beautiful as it is tedious (mercifully, only two hours this time), a few thoughts:

Welcome To The (Im)Peach State. We seem to go through this folly with every debate – the choice of location pertaining to a potentially influential state in 2020 (Florida, Michigan, Texas, Ohio, now Georgia); yet the moderators, coming from the nation’s capital, giving the proceedings a decidedly “swampy” feel.

Will Wednesday night be any different?

Here’s a simple way to decide: use your smartphone’s stopwatch to time how many minutes are devoted to Trump impeachments vs. more Georgia-centric topics like health care (currently a bone of contention with Georgia state lawmakers).

My guess: impeachment trumps Peach State politics. As it likely will again at December’s debate in Southern California.

Who’s Gone With The Wind? The most famous novel and movie pertaining to Atlanta (sorry, Baby Driver fans) begs this question: how many of the 10 Democrats can exit the stage on Wednesday night and optimistically offer, “Tomorrow is another day”?

This is especially true of California Sen. Kamala Harris, who in the past few days has had to endure calls for her campaign manager’s resignation – and even the candidate herself withdrawing from the race before she goes deep into debt (I contributed to this week’s media grave-shoveling with this Washington Post column suggesting that Harris’ problem is the same as her showcased Oakland – “no there there.”)

Other Democrats on this debate stage seem to be near-certain casualties before or soon after Iowa – New Jersey Sen. Cory Booker, Hawaii Rep. Tulsi Gabbard and Minnesota Sen. Amy Klobuchar come to mind.

But it’s Harris who bears watching because (a) she was the breakout star of the first debate back in June; (c) this debate’s African-American overtones and the proximity to South Carolina, once a key to Harris’ fortunes; and (c) if she soon exits the race, it’s yet another cautionary tale of peaking too early.

Georgia – And South Carolina – On His Mind? The flip side to the question of why Harris struggles to remain viable: South Bend Mayor Pete Buttigieg’s surge built almost entirely upon lilywhite support – and the question of what’s in store for his candidacy if he can’t catch on with minority voters beyond Iowa and New Hampshire.

How much of a minority problem does Buttigieg have at present? Here’s a Quinnipiac Poll showing the South Bend mayor doing worse than “spiritual-transformation” lodestar Marianne Williamson (“They’re going to miss me. Hashtag Boring Without Her”) among black voters in South Carolina, the scene of an influential February primary.

The good news for Mayor Pete ( if you can call it that): the same poll showed 60% of black voters didn’t have an opinion as to his candidacy. 

Maybe that’s why Buttigieg spent part of his debate walk-up giving a speech at historically-black Morehouse College.

Does “Woke” Joe Wake Up? Former Joe Biden will have a prominent friend at this debate: Atlanta Mayor Keisha Lance Bottoms, who’s campaigned in Iowa on Biden’s behalf.

As for how Biden fits in with this Georgia crowd: Driving Miss Daisy comes to mind – the tale of an elderly Atlanta matriarch coming to terms with a world changing around her.

Just how “woke” Biden intends to be in this debate – playing up his support among black voters, extolling the virtues of the New South in transitional Atlanta, referring to “my friend, Barack” – is much-watch television. Then again, how much of presence will he be given the recent surges by Buttigieg and Warren?

Speaking of whom . . .  

The Scarlet W. In the aforementioned Ohio debate, Elizabeth Warren played the part of Scarlet O’Hara – a heroine under siege not from marauding Yankees but the likes of Biden and Beto O’Rourke (remember him?) poking holes in her various policy stances.

If Warren wants to regain the momentum she had going into last month’s debate, it starts with the senator stepping up her game (by the way, not being stuck in a month-long Senate impeachment, as the Iowa vote approaches, would also help).

On the Ohio stage, she wasn’t suitably forceful when explaining how she’ll pay for her ambitious healthcare scheme (“costs will come down” doesn’t cut it). If Warren’s going to stick with the line that working-class taxes won’t rise, she needs to do a better job of presenting the math.

To the old southern adage that when you die, regardless of your final destination, you have to go through Atlanta first, this debate seems apt: depending on one’s political preference, prepare for two hours of Heaven or Hell.
