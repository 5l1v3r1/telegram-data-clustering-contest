Transit workers’ union demands more cops in NYC subways

19 Nov 2019, 01:50 by David Meyer, Jacob Henry and Bruce Golding

The transit workers’ union demanded Monday that the NYPD step up crime-fighting in the subways, after The Post revealed a massive spike in offenses that include random assaults on riders.

Transport Workers Union Local 100 President Tony Utano accused cops of focusing on fare-beaters while MTA employees face “assaults, threats and other forms of disgusting abuse all the time.”

“The MTA and the city so far have utterly failed to adequately protect transit workers. They have to a much better job deploying officers,” Utano said.

“We want to see uniformed officers on the trains and platforms and buses, not just standing near turnstiles.”

The Post exclusively reported that crime in the subway system rose 10.9 percent between Jan. 1 and Sept. 30 of this year, compared to the same period in 2018.

The increase was driven by a staggering 23 percent surge in complaints about misdemeanor crimes including low-level assault, harassment and groping.

Meanwhile, the NYPD has touted a 1.4 percent decline, based solely on the number of serious felonies such as murder, robbery and rape.

Con Ed worker Thomas Manning, who said he’s been riding the subways for 25 years, complained that “crime is all over the place.”

“You’d have to be blind not to see it,” the 73-year-old Long Island resident said outside the Fulton Center subway hub in lower Manhattan.

“It’s way out of hand and I’m sick and tired of it . . . This is bringing the city down.”

Process server Claude Brown of Brooklyn said that while “I see crime, I haven’t been affected.”

“My wife is giving me horror stories about people pulling down their pants,” he said. “Do I feel safe? No. Am I on guard? Yes.”

Paola Nunez, a 33-year-old cleaner from The Bronx, said she’s scared of the “fighting and gangs” she sees underground.

Outside Brooklyn’s Church Avenue station in Flatbush, Shaquille Renwick, 25, griped that “it’s as if the NYPD can’t multi-task.”

Renwick feared the situation was likely even worse than statistics show because “so much goes on where I’m sure people didn’t report it.”

The MTA, which Gov. Cuomo has said will flood the subways with 500 new cops, said that “ensuring public safety is an integral part of the transportation mission.”

The NYPD referred to a Sunday statement by Transit Chief Edward Delatorre, who said, noting an uptick in sex crimes, “We take these misdemeanor reports extremely seriously.”

“Major felony crime in transit is down across the city as we have drastically reduced overall arrests while increasing our arrests for violent crime,” he added.
