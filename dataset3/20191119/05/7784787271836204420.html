Dylan Stephens starred for South Australia. It’s why his national carnival self-assessment seems so harsh

19 Nov 2019, 05:23 by Matt Balmer

If there was an award for the harshest self-critic in this year’s draft class, Dylan Stephens would be a hot favourite to win it.

Every year, AFL recruiters put a large emphasis on the Under 18 championships — an annual event where the best draft prospects go head-to-head on the national stage — to determine where players sit on the ‘draft board’.

On paper, Stephens — a gun South Australian prospect — produced a promising 2019 national carnival, averaging 23.5 disposals and earning selection in the All-Australian team — all while playing through groin soreness.

But for Stephens, he fell short of his own lofty standards, saying he had a few “poor games”.

“I did have a little bit of a groin issue (in the middle of the year) but it wasn’t going to affect me too much,” Stephens told foxfooty.com.au.

“I wasn’t training throughout the championships because I was trying to rest my groins. Coming down from League level maybe it took a bit of adapting to do. I wanted to be on the inside and I was out on a wing and a lot of the time I wasn’t involved with the play as you can be with a wingman.

“I think that happens in footy. Prior to that, I hadn’t really played any poor games so I was probably due and I got them all in one section and it was pretty frustrating.”

Still, Stephens was in no panic, particularly after he witnessed a fellow South Australian struggle through the 2018 champs then star in his debut AFL season.

Connor Rozee had a subpar championships campaign in 2018, averaging 13.3 disposals while playing in multiple positions. Months later, he took the AFL by storm by booting 29 goals in 2019 for Port Adelaide.

“I was aware of (Connor struggling) and coming to the finals series and knowing the finals series he had last year (was very good), I knew how much (good) footy was ahead of me. (I) just put it behind me and I was ready to attack the end of the year,” Stephens said.

Stephens bounced back from his carnival with impressive outings for Norwood in the SANFL League, playing 13 games of senior football in, including a best performance of 26 disposals and eight tackles against North Adelaide.

The 18-year-old then took part in the reserves grand final and ended his year on a high.

“One person I actually spoke to was Ned McHenry just after the champs and he said from the championships period to the end of the year to the draft it is a bit of a drag,” he said.

“So just enjoy the games you get with your club before it could all change and he said to make the most of it.

“To get the opportunity to play 13 games or whatever it was and a Grand Final in Reserves, I was pretty happy with that. I had a patch during the championships where I wasn’t playing my best footy.

“I was pretty proud after the championships when my confidence was down to string a good month of footy together straight after that, I’m really happy with that.”

Stephens might be considered a South Australian to some, but deep down his roots are in his hometown back in Victoria.

“I was playing footy in Red Cliffs, my hometown back in Victoria, I was lucky enough to have a pretty good year and won the league medal in Under 13s,” he explained.

“I got a phone call probably about three weeks before school was about to start and got offered to go to St Peter’s (College) in Adelaide. I didn’t even know what boarding school was at that time but it took a bit of thinking. It was Year 9 and I went straight over there.

“It was very daunting and I remember going on a tour in the summer at the end of the year before. It was a tour of the whole school and I did not say a word the whole day. I was with my parents and I was so overwhelmed with the whole thing.”

Moving away from friends and family doesn’t worry Stephens, who already has eyes on a potential home if he moves to his third state.

The smooth moving midfielder has been linked with Carlton (Pick 9), Hawthorn (Pick 11) and Port Adelaide (Pick 12) — but is none the wiser as to who may call his name out on draft night.

“If it is Sydney for example, I’ve got Will Hayward (who) I went to St Peter’s with,” he said.

“I spoke to him on the phone and he said ‘I’ve got a spare room in my apartment with Ollie Florent’.

“West Coast said you can come to a host family and (chose) whether that is on the beach or suburban. They really look after you.”
