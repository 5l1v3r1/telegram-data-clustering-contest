Niall Daly admits that Padraig Pearse's will need to get everything right to upset Corofin in Connacht final

The perennial Galway champions are bidding for a fourth successive AIB Connacht club title

20 Nov 2019, 06:00 by Pat Nolan

Niall Daly accepts that a number of stars will have to align if Padraig Pearse’s are to shock Dublin-like Corofin on Sunday.

The perennial Galway champions are bidding for a fourth successive AIB Connacht club title, which would bring them one step closer to becoming the first club to win three All-Irelands in-a-row.

Having just emerged from Roscommon for the first time ever, Daly accepts that Pearse’s are in “bonus territory” but insisted: “If we can produce a performance like we produced against Roscommon Gaels [in the county final] for the first 40 minutes and they’re a small bit off the levels they can hit, we’re going to be in with a good chance.”

He continued: “Everyone looks at Corofin and says, ‘Jesus, what football they play’. They should be compared, I don’t know if they are, to the Dublin team at the moment, like.

“Yeah, of course you watch them and you admire them but on Sunday we won’t be trying to do that anyway. Hopefully they’ll be admiring us.”

The Roscommon star said that they were “nearly seen to be the Mayo” of the local club scene after losing a number of county finals over the years, though having finally scaled the summit, it outstripped the Connacht title he won with the county team this year.

“It’s been brilliant to get over the line. The Connacht championship, we took it game by game. The first game against Tourlestrane was a tricky game; going down there, we weren’t sure what way it was going to go. We were only hopeful that it would go well.

“Thankfully it did. Then we had the game in London … that was as much a club event as anything. We had a huge crowd over from the club – people who were never seen in the club ground at times!

That Daly, 28, is joined on the team by his brothers Conor, 26, Ronan, 24, and Lorcan, 21, makes it even more unique for their family.

“Our back garden would have been fairly intense at times when we were younger, the four of us playing against each other. It definitely does make it special.

“On the day you’re just fighting your own corner. Mam and Dad in the stand would have been very nervous; they were probably more nervous than us. But it’s special for them.

“Once you throw on the jersey, you don’t really be worrying about that. You've 14 other lads beside you, and another lot on the bench.”
