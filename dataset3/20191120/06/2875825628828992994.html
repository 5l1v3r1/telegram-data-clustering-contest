Noise pollution is ruining mating routines and threatening survival of up to 100 species, Irish researchers find

"Just like humans trapped next to the speakers in a mega-loud club, wildlife can’t chat up potential dates because of the noise"

20 Nov 2019, 06:00 by Jerome Reilly

The modern world is like living in a blaring disco for our birds and bees, experts say.

And just like humans trapped next to the speakers in a mega-loud club, wildlife can’t chat up potential dates because of the noise.

Noise pollution is now threatening the very survival of up to 100 species, researchers at Queen’s University in Belfast have found.

And our high decibel world is changing the behaviour of a wide range of species.

“Noise must be considered as a global pollutant and we need to develop strategies to protect animals,” said lead researcher Dr Hansjoerg Kunc from the School of Biological Sciences.

The study analysed the effects of noise in more than 100 species including amphibians, arthropods, (which includes insects) as well as birds, fish, mammals, molluscs and reptiles.

All are finding life on 21st century Earth simply too loud.

It’s a threat to their very survival - including their ability to reproduce.

Many species including birds and mammals communicate with potential mates using acoustic signals.

But their best chat up lines are being drowned out by environmental noise.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                             

As well as the important task of choosing a mate, wildlife can’t warn family members of threats such as predators.

While noise pollution prevents some creatures escaping predators, it’s the opposite story for nature’s hunters.

Bats and owls rely on the sounds of potential prey to track them down.

If they can’t hear, they can’t eat.

In our oceans, fish larvae find their home based on the sound emitted by reefs but increased noise at sea mainly as a result of ships makes it more difficult for young fish to find a good home.

The researchers found that noise pollution also has a huge impact on migration.

Many birds will avoid noise polluted areas during migration, which in turn, affects where they will establish their long term homes to raise their young.

“The study found clear evidence that noise pollution affects all of the seven groups of species,” Dr Kunc added.
