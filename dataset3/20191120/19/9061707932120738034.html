Derby dad with cancer says worst thing is being too weak to play with his kids

The family are hosting a fantastic festival weekend

20 Nov 2019, 19:50 by Noah Martin

A young Derby dad who was diagnosed with bowel cancer says the hardest thing about the illness is being too weak to play with his kids.

In August, Adam Cattermole, 36, discovered he had a rare, aggressive form of bowel cancer after going to A&E with stomach pains.

Doctors found a tumour in his bowel but were reluctant to estimate how long he had left to live because statistics are based on older patients.

Since then, Mr Cattermole's family have been fundraising to pay for a live-saving treatment called Avastin, which isn't available on the NHS.

Mr Cattermole also applied to be a part of an Avastin medical trial but was sadly unable to participate due to having a weak liver condition.

That was a couple of weeks ago and the dad-of-three, from Littleover, has now started on his third cycle of chemotherapy.

He said: "Physically, I'm feeling a lot better than I usually do after chemo.

"My liver function has improved so they have upped the dosage of one of my drugs to 100% which is good news.

"Most of the time it's the fatigue that gets me but I'm seeing a physiotherapist now so that should reduce."

Mentally, however, Mr Cattermole said he wasn't feeling as good and has been suffering with depression and anxiety.

The young man said that everything he has had to deal with was "getting on top" of him.

Mr Cattermole added it's "heartbreaking" when he can't play games with his children - Harry, six, Jacob, four, and 18-month-old Rosie.

"It's quite hard with three kids because when friends and family come round, they are making them laugh and messing about with them," he said.

"It's something that I can't physically do because I don't have the strength and I don't want to damage myself."

But, he said the kids understand that they need to be careful with him, especially after a visit to the hospital.

Mr Cattermole said: "We have told the kids as much as we know, that I have cancer and that I'm on drugs, but I don't know any prognosis."

Mr Cattermole said he doesn't want to know how long he has left to live because it would cause too much stress.

"I don't want to have that extra thing in my head, every time the time comes around to be expecting the end," he said.

To raise money for Mr Cattermole's treatment, there will be a festival at Bustler's Market on November 22 and November 23.

Mole Knows Fest is a two-day event filled with live music, craft and food stalls, a children's rave and a charity auction.

Starting at 5pm on Friday the programme will continue until late on Saturday night.

Mr Cattermole said Bustler's have kindly offered the venue for free and said he "couldn't thank Derby enough" for the support he has received.

But most of all, he said it was his wife Emma that he couldn't do without.

"She's just always there when I need her, even if it's just making a cup of tea.

"If there was a prize for best carer, Emma would be up there," he said.
