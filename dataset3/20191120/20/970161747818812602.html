Boris Johnson lets slip manifesto pledge to cut National Insurance

20 Nov 2019, 20:34

Boris Johnson has pledged to change rules around National Insurance so workers don’t have to pay the tax until they earn £12,500 a year.

The Prime Minister said his party would raise the current threshold of £8,632 to ensure ‘low tax for working people’ if it wins the general election.

He let the policy slip after being pressed by an employee at a Teesside fabrication yard about whether he would help ‘people like us’, not just the rich.

However, it quickly emerged that the Tories are only pledging to raise the threshold to £9,500 next year, with no timetable for the additional £3,000.

The PM initially claimed bringing in the £9,500 threshold sooner would ‘help with the cost of living’ and ‘put £500 in the pocket of everybody’.

However, a later press release claimed the saving would be around £100 per person with the aim of working towards savings of £465.

Both figures have been disputed by the Institute of Fiscal Studies (IFS), who said raising the threshold to £9,500  would save workers about £85 per year and cost the government £2bn.

Xiaowei Xu, a research economist with the IFS, said without knowing the proposed timescale of the increase ‘we do not know how big a policy this is’.

The economic think tank said raising the threshold to £12,500 would cost £11bn

Labour say the tax cut would harm public services and only save workers £1.64 a week.

Labour’s Shadow Chancellor John McDonnell said: ‘Even after 10 years of cruel cuts and despite creaking public services, the Tories still think the answer to the challenges of our time is a tax cut of £1.64 a week, with those on Universal Credit getting about 60p.

‘Meanwhile independent experts have said this will cost up to £11bn, so everyone who relies on public services and social security will be wondering whether they will be paying the price.’

The Prime Minister appeared caught off guard by the question from 35-year-old Claire Cartlidge, a fuel quality manager at the yard.

She asked whether he was promising low taxes for all or just people like himself.

He told her he meant ‘low tax for working people’.

He said: ‘If you look at what we are doing and what I said in the last few days, we are going to be cutting national insurance up to £12,000. And we are going to make sure we cut business rates for small businesses, we are cutting tax for working people, by the way.’

He later had to correct the figures in an interview with the BBC, revising it to a £9,500 threshold next year, with an eventual goal of £12,500 over may years.

BBC political correspondent Nick Eardley said it sounded like Mr Johnson had accidentally revealed his big election tax pledge when visiting workers at the North East engineering plant.
