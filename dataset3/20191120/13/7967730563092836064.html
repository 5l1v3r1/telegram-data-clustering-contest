Trump has a lot to learn about how currencies work

20 Nov 2019, 13:10 by Stephen Bartholomeusz

Donald Trump had a "good and cordial" meeting with Federal Reserve Board chair Jerome Powell on Monday at which, he tweeted later, he had protested that US rates were too high, that they should be lower than those of competitor countries and that a strong dollar was hurting manufacturers and growth.

While the tone of the meeting and his tweets were considerably tamer than his previous commentary on Powell and the Fed (an "enemy of the state", "boneheads," "pathetic" etc) it shows that Trump still believes the Fed can and should manipulate the value of the dollar relative to its trading partners.

His belief that the value of the currency of an economy that, while slowing, is still growing more strongly than that of Europe or Japan, should be "lower than all others" – is as baffling as his rationale. It’s because "we are the US".

The dollar has strengthened against those of its major trading partners this year largely because it is the US dollar.

Beyond the relative performance of the US economy against other major economies – although it’s a reflection of that performance – there are sizeable differentials between the yields available on US bonds and the minuscule-to-negative yields available in Europe or Japan. That’s despite three Fed rate cuts.
