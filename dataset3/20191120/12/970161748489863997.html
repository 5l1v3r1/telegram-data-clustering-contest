Can you still register to vote in the general election if you're homeless?

20 Nov 2019, 12:03

Everyone who wants to vote in the general election must be registered by 26 November – which is Tuesday next week.

If you’re homeless, don’t have a permanent or fixed address or are currently sofa surfing, you can still register to vote for the 12 December poll.

Here’s how…

To vote in the general election you must be registered and you need proof of your identity to register.

This can be your National Insurance number, birth certificate or passport.

You can still register to vote even if you do not have a fixed address.

This may be because:

  • you are homeless or have no fixed address
  • you are a person who has been remanded in custody, but you have not yet been convicted of any offence
  • you are a patient in a mental health hospital

If you are staying at an address for an extended length of time then you can be considered as residing there and can register for that address. This could be a hospital, hostel, prison facility or similar place.

If you haven’t got a fixed address

If you are sleeping in a hostel or on the street, you can still vote as long as you’ve registered.

To register, you need to fill in a form called a ‘Declaration of local connection’.

You can get this form from your electoral registration office, or download it from GOV.UK.

There are two forms – one for people in England and Wales, and one for people in Scotland.

You may be able to get help filling it in from local homelessness support services.

You can return this to your electoral registration office by hand, by post or by email. You can find their address at yourvotematters.co.uk

What address should you use?

To register, you need to give an address and it can be one where you would be living if it were not for your current circumstances.

It could also be an address where you are staying temporarily or have lived at in the past.

If you are homeless, you can give details of where you spend a substantial part of your time, including:

  • a friend’s house
  • a hostel
  • a day centre

Crisis says you could also give an address that is closest to, for example, a park bench, bus shelter or doorway.

How to vote on the day

If you are eligible and registered to vote you can vote:

  • in person at a polling station
  • by post
  • by proxy - getting someone else to vote for you

You can apply for a postal or proxy vote online or by contacting your electoral registration office.

Who is allowed to vote in UK elections?

Anyone aged 18 or over AND

  • Registered to vote in the UK
  • A British citizen or Irish citizen resident in the UK OR
  • A Commonwealth citizen of Cyprus or Malta OR
  • A Commonwealth citizen who has leave to remain in the UK or who does not require leave to remain in the UK

Who can’t vote?

Convicted prisoners are not allowed to vote in an election, but those on remand and civil prisoners can vote if they are registered
