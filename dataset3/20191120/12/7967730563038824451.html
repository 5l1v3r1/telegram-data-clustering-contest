CBD Melbourne: Liberal loyals line up for key positions

20 Nov 2019, 12:59 by Samantha Hutchinson and Kylar Loussikian

Tony Nutt’s lengthy and almost no-holds-barred post-mortem of the Victorian Liberal division’s botched 2018 state election effort is predictably dominating chatter in the parliamentary party.

But for Liberal members outside the Spring Street bubble, it’s AGM season, as local branches shore up their office-bearers for the year ahead and a contentious state council scheduled for this weekend.

Some branches including newly installed MP Katie Allen’s Higgins branch have their pick of Collins Street for key positions.

After all, candidates up for election on Tuesday in Higgins included JB Hi-Fi chief executive Richard Murray, former state director and Carlton United Breweries executive Julian Sheezel, Enterprise Connect boss Natalie Stirling and Myer media boss Martin Barr.

Meanwhile, other branches attract a more diverse crowd, including Burwood, the seat former state MP Graham Watt lost to Labor’s most colourful new arrival Will Fowles.

The branch in Melbourne’s eastern suburbs had its AGM on Tuesday night. Watt, who also spent some time this year working for Chisholm MP Gladys Liu, made it into the vice-president’s position.

But the result of the treasurer’s ballot raised some eyebrows, with prominent LSD campaigner Greg Kasarik winning the post.

Kasarik, made headlines in 2017 when he arrived at Melbourne’s Magistrates Court as a defendant in a hearing, and volunteered on the stand that he was breaking the law because he was carrying an illegal drug – LSD – in his pockets.

He was escorted outside by three cops, who arrested and then charged him again. He wasn’t trying to break the law, he said, but described his admission as part of a strategy to try to get the drug LSD regulated by the state government so he and other users could trip safely.

“The reality is some bastard has got be bunny and I’m happy to make the sacrifice,” he told Nine at the time.

Call it a novel – if not noble – cause. But good luck trying to get a Free Acid discussion item onto the conservative party’s state council agenda for this weekend. We’ll keep you posted.

A SHORT SEA CHANGE

Former Nine Entertainment boss David Gyngell barely managed five minutes in the witness box on Wednesday, having been hauled to Melbourne from his Byron Bay hideout to appear before the corporate watchdog's court pursuit of ex-Tennis Australia director Harold Mitchell.

Did he talk about broadcasting rights at a 2012 meeting, ASIC's counsel Michael Pearce, SC, queried. Can you write down your mobile number, Mitchell's barrister Nick De Young wanted to know.

Then, err, that was it. "I come from Byron Bay for that?" Gyngell asked as he left the stand.

Pearce said: "It’s the first time I’ve seen a witness disappointed to leave the witness box."

Gyngell is one of several high-profile former media executives to be drawn into the legal action against Mitchell and his fellow former Tennis Australia director Stephen Healy, with the regulator claiming they had breached their duties to help Seven West Media secure broadcast rights.

Among those to be called are Seven executive Bruce McWilliam, former Ten chief Hamish McLennan (now the chairman of REA Group) and one-time Nine managing director Jeffrey Browne.

But the trip south wasn't a total waste of time for Gyngell. We spotted him breaking bread with Browne at Spring Street's Di Stasio Citta the night before his court date.

FRIENDS FROM WAY BACK

It has been a nightmare week for Victorian politicians of both major parties this week as Ferrari-driving developer John Woodman and the City of Casey’s colourful former mayor Sam Aziz go under the microscope of the state’s anti-corruption watchdog.

While Aziz hot-footed it out of the country to Egypt after he first met Independent Broad-based Anti-corruption Commission investigators, his links to other politicians, local councillors and power brokers will come under scrutiny at the commission.

It's a wide cast of characters given Aziz worked alongside the AFP and ASIO inside the Attorney-General’s office before spending 14 years working as a director in Customs.

Victoria Police Chief Commissioner Graham Ashton is also a former colleague.

As it turns out, Aziz also had a stint working with Ashton, who was an Australian Federal Police officer working out of Indonesia and Melbourne in the late 1990s, before becoming national manager of counter-terrorism in 2003.

And in 2017 City of Casey residents recall Ashton visiting the region to attend a neighbourhood watch meeting alongside Aziz, who introduced the police chief as a “friend” and spoke at length about how the pair had worked closely on a number of joint operations.

This week a spokeswoman for Ashton accepted the pair had worked together.

Whatever the project was, it was short-lived, apparently.

“Chief Commissioner Graham Ashton knew Mr Aziz in a professional capacity during a brief period when he was working at AFP,” a spokeswoman said on behalf of Mr Ashton, but she refused to say anything else, including the extent of the working relationship.

“It would be inappropriate for him to be commenting on any claims made against Mr Aziz.”
