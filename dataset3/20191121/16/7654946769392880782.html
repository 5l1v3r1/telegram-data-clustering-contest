Coldplay postpones new album tour over carbon footprint concerns

21 Nov 2019, 16:03 by Hannah Sparks

Fans know Coldplay for their song “Yellow,” but the band prefers to be associated with “green.”

The multi-platinum rock band has announced that it’s postponing shows to promote their new album “Everyday Life,” which drops Friday, until they find a way to make them “actively beneficial” for the environment.

“Our next tour will be the best possible version of a tour like that environmentally,” said frontman Chris Martin, telling BBC News that the band was pausing a tour tied to the album while they develop a carbon neutral production.

“We would be disappointed if it’s not carbon neutral,” Martin said. “We’ve done a lot of big tours at this point. How do we turn it around so it’s not so much taking as giving?”

Coldplay marks the latest in a trend of entertainers pledging to be more eco-conscious.

Grammy nominee Billie Eilish recently told Jimmy Fallon in September that she was working to make her world tour “as green as possible.”

She plans to ban plastic straws, encourage refillable water bottles and recycling at the venue. Her shows will also feature the educational “Billie Eilish Eco-Village,” where fans can learn more about climate change and eco-activism — and get a chance to win free tickets to coveted shows.

The band 1975 recently released a single featuring climate activist Greta Thunberg as a call to action to pop audiences. The group also promised to make their shows more carbon efficient, and also plans to plant one tree for every ticket sold during their 2020 UK tour. Declaring band tour tees “unsustainable,” the group will also rehash unsold merch from past tours and print new graphics right on top of the old — with varying avant garde affects.

The weight of responsibility is heavy on some entertainers, whose careers leave a disproportionately sized carbon footprint.

Thom Yorke recently admitted to BBC radio listeners that’s he’s a climate “hypocrite.”

“The thing I’ve always struggled somewhat with is that if I’m campaigning on climate change, I’m someone who has to fly for my work, so, boom, I’m a hypocrite,” said the Radiohead frontman and Friends of the Earth activist.

He continued, “I totally agree I’m a hypocrite, but I’m trying to do something about it, and yes, you’re right, I’m a hypocrite.”

However, Yorke added that “the real stuff has to happen in Parliament and the UN, and has to happen now, we’re out of time.”
