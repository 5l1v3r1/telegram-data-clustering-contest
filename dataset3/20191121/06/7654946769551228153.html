Democratic debates: Snoozefest has top 4 running in place as other 6 feebly jab

21 Nov 2019, 06:24 by Marisa Schultz and Aaron Feis

ATLANTA — Ten Democratic presidential candidates went through the motions Wednesday night in a fifth-round debate long on reheated anti-Trump takes, canned zingers and Biden blunders, but short on guidance for undecided voters.

The four presumed top contenders — former Vice President Joe Biden, Massachusetts Sen. Elizabeth Warren, Vermont Sen. Bernie Sanders and South Bend, Ind., Mayor Pete Buttigieg — seemed content to tread water while some among the six likely also-rans feebly tried to vault an ever-widening polling gap.

In two of the few charged exchanges on a slow-as-a-drawl evening in Atlanta, Minnesota Sen. Amy Klobuchar and Hawaii Rep. Tulsi Gabbard took shots at Buttigieg, who was propelled into the evening by surging poll showings in the key battleground states of Iowa and New Hampshire.

“I have actually done this work,” said Klobuchar of navigation through Beltway gridlock, after referring to Buttigieg as a “local official” at the helm of a city that barely cracks 100,000 residents.

“There’s more than 100 years of Washington experience on this stage, and where are we right now as a country?” challenged Buttigieg. “Washington experience is not the only experience that matters.”

Gabbard tagged in to question fellow military veteran Buttigieg’s judgment for suggesting that US troops help fight the scourge of drug-cartel violence along the Mexican border.

Buttigieg bristled at what seemed to be Gabbard’s implication.

“Do you seriously think anybody on this stage is proposing invading Mexico?” he asked, before turning from ­defense to offense.

“Let’s also talk about judgment,” Buttigieg said to Gabbard. “One of the foreign leaders you mentioned meeting was [Syrian dictator] Bashar al-Assad.”

The response drew “oohs” from an audience at Tyler Perry Studios that had largely sat on its hands through the debate’s early going.

California Sen. Kamala Harris previously launched another apparent broadside at Gabbard over the controversial 2017 Syria summit, but was rapped in return by Gabbard for “continuing to traffic in lies and smears and innuendos.”

Biden — a once prohibitive frontrunner whose campaign has stagnated in recent months, primarily at the expense of Warren and Buttigieg — also drew flak.

The most memorable shot came from New Jersey Sen. Cory Booker, who dinged birthday boy Biden, who turned 77 Wednesday, for recently calling marijuana a “gateway drug” and opposing its legalization.

“I thought you might have been high when you said it!” quipped a grinning Booker, in another rare crowd-pleasing line.

But Biden’s biggest opponent on Wednesday night, as in previous debates, was himself.

In one particularly cringeworthy moment, the gaffe-prone former veep invoked an unfortunate choice of words while attempting to articulate the importance of combating domestic violence.

“We have to just change the culture . . . and keep punching at it and punching at it and punching at it,” he said, while ­repeatedly pumping his clenched fist.
