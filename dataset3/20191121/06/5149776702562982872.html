Amputee who lost leg after horror crash has transformed himself through bodybuilding

"I could have come away in a wheelchair to be fair, or dead."

21 Nov 2019, 06:00 by Jamie Barlow

A Nottinghamshire man who lost a leg following a horror crash more than a decade ago has transformed himself competing as a bodybuilder.

Craig Earley, 40, was hit by a van while riding a motorbike in Queen's Road, Beeston, in 2003.

It happened near the junction with Dovecote Lane and Mr Earley counts himself lucky to be alive.

He found himself entangled under the front passenger wheels of the van, breaking his right leg and left arm.

His left leg was shattered and later had to be amputated from below the knee.

Mr Earley, who runs alternative gift shop Happy Daze, in Wollaton Road, Beeston, with his wife, went through a dark period after the crash.

"I got quite depressed, I suppose," said Mr Earley, of Trent Road, Beeston Rylands.

"I didn't look at life in a nice way. I bloated up to maybe 15-and-a-half stone. All the medication they gave me. I didn't really see a reason to live."

He got his life back on track when his wife, Alex, 42, fell pregnant with their first son, Elliot, now 13.

"Something happened. I got up, I pushed myself forward, I went to the Prince's Trust, we opened our business, I started doing the gym and I think my son was the catalyst for me trying to find new avenues to make myself feel better," said Mr Earley.

He has been going to the gym for several years and has been working for the past few months with personal trainer John Clarke, who launched PF Aesthetics in Willoughby Street, Lenton, earlier this year with a colleague.

With the help of Mr Clarke, Mr Earley is now in prime shape - describing himself as a "fine wine".

"I got better as I got older," he said.

He finished fourth last weekend in the qualifying stage of a competition called the UK BFF Sugar's Classic, competing in a category for people weighing less than 70kg in Leicester. "I've been crying for the past two days," said Mr Earley, who weighed in at 10st 8lbs.

"I wanted to have done better but I realise now I did amazing because the standard of people that were there.

"There's no way I could have beaten them because they've been training for 28 weeks. I knew about this competition for six weeks."

The top three have gone through to the finals in October at Nottingham's Theatre Royal where Mr Earley has been invited to pose as a guest.

Pleased with his body transformation, he said: "The pictures, they speak for themselves. It's there in black and white. It's amazing."

Mr Earley's wife, Alex, said her husband's depression had 'lifted' since he'd been going to the gym.

She said: "He feels more confident in his body image and in the way he feels in himself because he's managed to do this transformation."

She described the journey as 'unbelievable', saying: "I took our youngest on Sunday (November 17).

"All the time he (Mr Earley) was on stage he (son Isaac, eight) cried. It's such an achievement.

"As soon as he got on stage he was like 'mummy, I can't stop crying with pride'."

Personal trainer Mr Clarke, 40, of Carlton, said he knew Mr Earley was "good enough for a competition" after seeing him in a vest, saying he had a "phenomenal physique".

"It's unbelievable what he's done," said Mr Clarke. "He has not quite realised what he's achieved.

"He went against the country's best and he actually beat some of them."

He added: "He brought the house down. Everyone was going crazy.

"It could not have gone any better. It was just phenomenal."

Describing the crash, Mr Earley said: "We were at a T-junction at a crossover, I pulled up, I wanted to go straight across the road.

"There's traffic coming from the left and the right, the car that wanted to come from the right he slowed down, he wanted to turn - in the direction I was going to go - across the road.

"As soon as the traffic from the left was clear he flashed me so I went across.

"Then a van undercut him and hit me in the middle of the road basically.

"I went through his windscreen, I came back out, went under his wheels and that's where the damage was done then to my legs."

He added: "I couldn't actually feel the accident. When I was under the van, loads of people stopped.

"They tried to pick the van up to get my legs out. You could just see my legs were just hanging like pieces of spaghetti.

"I remember it all but couldn't feel a thing.

"Looking back at it, it could have been so, so much worse. The van absolutely hammered me. I came away with a missing leg.

"I could have come away in a wheelchair to be fair, or dead."
