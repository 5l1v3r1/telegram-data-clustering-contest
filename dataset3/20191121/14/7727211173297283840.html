Killer of Helen McCourt free to walk streets despite never telling family where her body is

Murderer Ian Simms, who was a pub landlord, has been granted parole

21 Nov 2019, 14:05 by Steve Graves

A pub landlord who killed a young woman after she got off a bus near her home is free to walk the streets - despite never revealing where her body is.

Ian Simms abducted and murdered insurance clerk Helen McCourt, 22, in 1988.

He has never told police where he disposed of her body despite her family repeatedly appealing for him to do so.

Simms, the landlord of a pub then called the George and Dragon in Billinge, was convicted of Helen's murder in 1989 and was given a life sentence with a minimum tariff of 16 years.

Helen's mum Marie led a long campaign for the introduction of a 'Helen's Law' in her daughter's name, which would mean killers would have to disclose the whereabouts of their victim's remains before being considered for parole.

But the Mirror reports Simms, who has never revealed where Helen's remains are, has been granted parole after a hearing earlier this month.

A spokeswoman for the Parole Board said:  “The Parole Board must make its decisions solely focused on whether a prisoner would represent a significant risk to the public after release.

"The panel will have carefully looked at a whole range of evidence, including details of the original evidence and any evidence of behaviour change.

“This is done with great care and public safety is the number one priority.”

One of the first UK cases to be solved using DNA, Simms was convicted after he was found with blood on his clothes which matched that of Helen’s parents, and her opal and sapphire earring was found in his car.

His bloodstained clothes were recovered from banks of a river, and a knotted strangling flex discovered nearby had teeth marks in it, matching those of the landlord’s dog and hairs identical to Helen’s caught in it.

In the last three months, Marie has started a fresh search for her daughter, with new suggestions she could be in Rixton clay pits, in nearby Warrington, 15 miles from Helen's home in Billinge, in a 200m zone, where Simms held a fishing license in another man’s name.

What is a life sentence?

People often complain that, in terms of UK sentencing laws, life doesn't mean life.

But if that is the case what does it means to get a life sentence?

A life sentence means a convicted criminal will remain on licence permanently and can only apply for parole after serving a minimum term set by the judge.

But they will only ever set foot in the community if a parole board deems they no longer pose a risk to the public.

As an example the starting minimum term for someone who has committed a murder with a knife is 25 years, although this can be adjusted up and down by circumstances.

Many people who are set a minimum term will still not be released, because they are still considered dangerous. Moors murderer Myra Hindley was one such prisoner who died in jail.

The average jail term served by a prisoner on a life sentence in England and Wales is 15 years according to the Sentencing Council.

The minimum term sets life sentences aside from standard determinate sentences in which prisoners are expected to serve around one half of their sentence in prison and the rest on licence, or two thirds and having to go before a parole board if they are considered dangerous and given an extended sentence.  

There are situations where life literally does mean life. They are called whole life tarrifs and are very rarely handed out by judges, usually to notorious criminals whose crimes have shocked the nation.

Criminals serving whole life tariffs include Thomas Mair, who murdered the Labour MP Jo Cox, and serial killers such as the 'Yorkshire Ripper' Peter Sutcliffe and Rosemary West. There are currently more than 50 prisoners serving whole life sentences in the UK.

A recent recipient of a whole tariff is Mark Felllows, an underworld assassin known as the Iceman who murdered gangland figures Paul Massey in Salford and John Kinsella who was shot dead while walking his dog in Rainhill.

Marie previously told the ECHO: "After Helen's death, my life has changed drastically, I didn't go out, I avoided people and only went outside on a Sunday when I was searching for her.

"I don't think Simms will ever tell me where she is.

"It's like a power thing for killers like him, he's just a chip off the old block."

Time Line

Helen McCourt murder: how a tragedy unfolded

  1. February 9, 1988 - Helen says she'll be home for tea

    Helen phones her mum to say she'll be home for tea by 5.30pm, and has a date planned that night with her boyfriend.

    It is the last time Marie McCourt will ever speak to her daughter.

  2. February 9, 1988 - Helen vanishes near local pub

    Insurance clerk Helen, 22, takes a bus home from work in Liverpool.

    She vanishes on her walk home, which takes her past the George and Dragon pub in Billinge.

    Minutes later, a man getting off another bus hears a loud scream from the pub, but it is quickly cut short.

  3. February, 1988 - the search begins

    Around 2,000 people meet at a local church to comb fields, ditches and woods around Billinge, looking for Helen.

    But her body is not found.

  4. March, 1988 - Helen's clothes are found and linked to pub landlord

    Helen's handbag, coat, mittens and other clothes are found about 20 miles away, in a bin liner taken from a roll in the George and Dragon pub.

    A cable is also found, with tooth marks matched to the dogs of pub landlord, Ian Simms.

  5. March, 1989 - Pub landlord Ian Simms goes on trial

    Ian Simms denies murder at Liverpool Crown Court.

    He says he has been framed, claiming someone broke into his pub while he was there, murdered Helen, dressed in his clothes and stole his car to take away the body.

  6. March, 1989 - Ian Simms convicted of murder with DNA evidence

    The pub landlord shakes his head as the jury find him guilty, in one of the first murder convictions using DNA evidence.

    Traces of blood and an opal and sapphire earring of Helen's had been found in the boot of his Passat car.

    He is ordered to serve at least 16 years in prison.

  7. January, 1990 - Marie McCourt resumes the search for her daughter

    Simms maintains his innocence, and will not say where Helen's body is.

    Marie continues the search for her daughter's body every weekend, helped by her son and relatives.

  8. 1995 - Marie starts supporting other victims

    Marie helps other victims by joining Support After Murder and Manslaughter, as a counsellor and campaigner.

  9. 1999 - Simms challenges DNA evidence

    Ian Simms disputes the findings of the DNA technology, but the evidence suggests the chances the blood found is not Helen's are nine million to one.

  10. 2007 - Simms' parole bid rejected

    Simms appeals for parole, but it is rejected and he cannot apply for another two years.

  11. July, 2008 - Marble bench made as tribute to Helen

    A marble bench is placed in the grounds of St Mary's Church, Billinge, to mark her 43rd birthday.

  12. April, 2009 - Marie relieved after parole denied again

    Marie McCourt attends her daughter's killer's parole hearing, and is relieved to hear it has been blocked.

  13. October, 2013 - parole hearings delayed

    Marie attends Simms' parole hearing at Garth Prison, near Preston, in September, but finds Simms has not attended in October as planned.

    The hearing is delayed.

  14. October, 2013 - Torment after body found is not Helen's

    Hopes grow of finding Helen's body as Merseyside Police exhume a grave at St Aidan's Church, Billinge, after a tip-off.

    But Marie calls it 'heartbreaking' after no trace of her remains is found.

  15. June, 2014 - Simms' children urged to pressure him over body

    Marie urges Simms' children to 'give their dad an ultimatum' when visiting him to reveal the whereabouts of Helen's body.

    Simms had refused to speak to detectives who went to Garth Prison to quiz him again over the body in March.

  16. July, 2015 - Marie's pain on Helen's '50th birthday'

    Marie says Simms has 'killed Helen and almost killed me' on what would have been her daughter's 50th birthday.

  17. December, 2015 - Campaign for Helen's Law

    Marie starts a petition with the mums of two other murder victims for a new 'Helen's Law'.

    The three want killers kept behind bars for good until they reveal where their victims' bodies are.

    The Ministry of Justice says Simms' failure to reveal the body's whereabouts will be 'taken into account' at an upcoming parole hearing.

  18. Simms to move to open prison

    St Helens MP Conor McGinn "surprised and disappointed" by Ministry of Justice decision to move Simms to open conditions.
