Israel PM Benjamin Netanyahu indicted on corruption charges

21 Nov 2019, 17:01 by Yaron Steinbuch

Israel’s attorney general announced Thursday that Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu will be charged in a series of corruption cases, threatening the longtime leader’s grip on power in a country mired in political chaos after two inconclusive elections.

In his long-awaited announcement, Attorney General Avichai Mandelblit said Netanyahu will face charges of bribery, breach of trust and fraud — becoming the first sitting Israeli prime minister to be charged with a crime.

According to the indictment, the embattled leader wrongfully accepted $264,000 worth of gifts — including cigars and champagne — from tycoons, offered to trade favors with a newspaper publisher and used his influence to help a wealthy telecom magnate in exchange for favorable coverage on a popular news site.

Netanyahu could face up to 10 years in prison if convicted of bribery and a maximum three-year term for fraud and breach of trust.

A defiant Netanyahu — who has been prime minister since 2009 and served a previous term between 1996 and 1999 — has strenuously denied any wrongdoing in the three cases and called the allegations a media-orchestrated witch hunt aimed at kicking him from office.

In the co-called “Case 4000,” the most serious of the three, he faces a charge of bribery for allegedly influencing regulatory decisions that netted hundreds of millions of dollars for a telecom giant in exchange for positive coverage on its subsidiary news site Walla.

The allegations involve his “bribe-based relationship” with Shaul Elovitch, the Bezeq company’s controlling shareholder. Mandelblit also intends to charge Elovitch with bribery, according to the Times of Israel.

In “Case 1000,” where Netanyahu is accused of accepting gifts and benefits worth about $300,000 from business executives — including Israeli-born Hollywood producer Arnon Milchan — in return for political favors, he faces charges of fraud and breach of trust. Milchan will not be charged.

And in “Case 2000,” Netanyahu faces charges of fraud and breach of trust for allegedly agreeing with newspaper Yedioth Ahronoth’s publisher Arnon Mozes to promote a bill to restrict the circulation of the daily’s main competitor, Israel Hayom, in exchange for favorable coverage. Mozes will be charged with bribery.

Netanyahu is under no legal obligation to resign, though he will likely face calls to step down. If he is ultimately cleared, it would give him a major boost as the Jewish state braces for a possible third election in under a year.

It could take months before formal charges are filed, since Netanyahu is expected to ask the Knesset for parliamentary immunity, according to the Times of Israel.

Only once a new coalition is formed — or after the next round of elections slated for March — can a Knesset committee take up the question.

Even if Netanyahu’s immunity request is rejected, it could take until May or June for a formal decision to be issued, while a formal indictment could still be more than seven months away, the news outlet reported.

Thursday’s decision comes at a tumultuous time for the country.

After an inconclusive election in September, both Netanyahu and his chief rival, former military chief of staff Benny Gantz, have failed to form a majority coalition in parliament.

The country now enters an unprecedented 21-day period in which any member of the Knesset, Israel’s parliament, can try to rally a 61-member majority to become prime minister. If that fails, new elections would be triggered for early 2020.

President Reuven Rivlin formally informed the Knesset of the deadlock on Thursday, a day after Gantz’s window for forming a government expired, calling it a “miserable political situation” and pleading with lawmakers to find a compromise.

Netanyahu is desperate to remain in office, where he would be best positioned to fight criminal charges — but he could come under pressure to step aside, even from within his ruling Likud party, whose leaders have so far remained solidly behind him.

A recent poll by the Israel Democracy Institute, a nonpartisan think tank, found that 65 percent of Israelis thought Netanyahu should resign as head of the Likud party if indicted, with 24 percent opposed.

In the first sign of rebellion, Netanyahu’s top Likud rival on Thursday called for a leadership primary should the country head to new elections.

Gideon Saar said he supports the establishment of a unity government with Gantz’s Blue and White Party to avert another election.

“If we go to new elections, it will not be reasonable to think that the prime minister will be successful in forming a government after the third elections,” Saar said. “I think I will be able to form a government, and I think I will be able to unite the country and the nation.”

Netanyahu’s predecessor, Ehud Olmert, who resigned before he was indicted on corruption charges a decade ago, later served 16 months behind bars for fraud and bribery.
