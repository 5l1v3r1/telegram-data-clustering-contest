Braid: Minister says CBE blindsided her on layoffs; might reverse them

21 Nov 2019, 00:55 by Don Braid, Calgary Herald

She won’t quite say it — yet — but Education Minister Adriana LaGrange might force the CBE to cancel layoffs of 300 temporary teachers.

That’s one of the UCP government’s options: a legal directive to the school board, ordering a halt.

LaGrange acknowledged this Wednesday in an interview, while adding, “I am considering all options.

“For now, we are ordering an audit of finances and a review of CBE governance.”

Whatever happens, it’s clear the province won’t accept this status quo.

The UCP simply does not agree that a $32-million cut to a $1.2 billion CBE budget requires the layoff of so many teachers, or any at all.

They’re also determined not to take the blame.

On Tuesday, the board suddenly announced the most drastic and alarming response possible to the funding cut.

It will terrify parents and the roaming contract teachers most affected. The layoffs will surely drive up class sizes and add stress to staff in many schools.

Furious, LaGrange says she was completely blindsided by the CBE.

“On Monday, we had a meeting in Edmonton with the chairs, superintendents and CFOs of the four metro boards from Calgary and Edmonton,” she said.

“Not once did anybody from the CBE mention layoffs — not once.”

Within 24 hours of the meeting, the layoff announcement came.

Those layoffs had actually been approved at a board meeting the week before.

Two other big boards, Edmonton Catholic and Edmonton public, are cutting costs but have not announced layoffs.

Calgary Catholic specifically said Wednesday no teachers will be let go.

LaGrange also noted that the province offered assistance from provincial officials to deal with the cuts.

Several boards responded, she said, “but the CBE refused.”

The UCP concludes that the Calgary board defied LaGrange’s directive “to minimize impact on front-line staff and teachers.”

It’s hard to disagree when the CBE’s first move only affects the front line.

CBE superintendent Christopher Usih said when the layoffs were announced that the CBE will make “several other decisions that cover our budget gap.”

You have to wonder why the board wouldn’t do that first, rather than going after teachers.

If the CBE officials wanted to make the government as angry as possible, they’ve handled it exactly right.

There is a history. In 2013, for instance, the board debated a pay freeze for teachers while voting $1 million in bonuses for senior executives.

“I can’t believe anyone would be that stupid,” then-education minister Jeff Johnson said when he heard the news.

Johnson, like LaGrange, was appalled. But the Progressive Conservative government of that day was far less dangerous to cross than this one.

The CBE is no more popular with its own employees than it has been with the public or the provincial government.

In a survey of more than 9,000 employees, the percentage of positive answers in 10 leadership categories never got above 32 per cent.

On four questions about trust in superintendents and directors, approval was as low as 24 per cent.

LaGrange regularly brings up the disastrous high-rent deal for the CBE headquarters on 8th Street S.W.

And she cites an accounting error that made $9.1 million in office spending look like classroom cost.

The CBE is in a tighter spot than other boards because its reserves are very low.

The provincial government doesn’t care. LaGrange believes it’s the CBE’s own fault, and there’s plenty of padding to cut a long way from the classroom.

Layoffs are going on all over the public sector, of course. The UCP sprung them without notice, after saying education spending would not fall.

Nobody likes this. The government will face anger for months or even years.

But the UCP has always said the front line should be protected as much as possible.

The CBE, in its usual inexplicable, stumbling way, appears to openly defy that wish.

Now, there’s a possibility those teachers will not be laid off. The UCP could see this as a perfect case to make the point.
