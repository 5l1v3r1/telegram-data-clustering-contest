Chris Powell recalls racist abuse which stopped mum and dad from watching him play

Chris Powell is now part of Gareth Southgate's England set-up and trying to help the next generation of black footballers, such as Raheem Sterling and Marcus Rashford

15 Nov 2019, 17:00 by John Cross, https://www.facebook.com/johncrossmirror/

Chris Powell can still remember a time when his mum and dad would be reluctant to go and watch him as a player.

These days, Powell is trying to be a trailblazer as a coach in Gareth Southgate’s England set-up and those appalling days of widespread racism within football are supposed to be a distant memory.

But former England full back Powell, 50, is still trying to lead by example for a future generation of managers and coaches with his role with the FA while stadiums should be safer now than 30 years ago.

Powell, recalling days of playing during the late 1980s and 90s, said: “If things are going to improve for future generations I want to be part of that. I always felt like I wanted to be in football, I still want to be in it, and actually I want to make it better for others, I really do.

“I remember playing for Southend when Dave Webb was manager and we played up north, I won't say where, and there were three black players and for 90 minutes we got it, as such.

“I remember afterwards the three of us were quite shaken - we had got on with the game - but I remember looking at his face, he looked at us and said: 'whatever happens I am with you.'

“That was so powerful and I will never forget it because we couldn't go anywhere with it but he was our manager and he was there to support us.

“This was at a time when you couldn't really do much about it, nothing was being done as much as these days.

“It was a different time, I accept that, people's views in football and society were very, very different. I know we still have issues now but football was very, very different back then.

“My dad wouldn’t go. No chance. He wouldn’t go. He wouldn’t go from a safety aspect, I don’t know. My mum would come when I was a manager, she came more when I was managing than when I played. But you get supported now and it is a bit safer in the main.”

Powell is a hugely likeable man, always calm and well respected both as player and coach. Having started at Crystal Palace , he had spells at Charlton, West Ham and Leicester and, most memorable, got an England call-up from nowhere after Sven Goran Eriksson took charge.

That was perhaps a nod to a steady, reliable player who was respected by his team mates but did not always get the recognition he deserved.

Having managed Charlton, Huddersfield and Southend, perhaps there is some symmetry now with his role as BAME coach in Southgate’s set-up with the FA idea of appointing a black coach for every England team through the age groups.

Powell has immediately blended in within Southgate’s team but also hopes that he can make a difference and pave the way for other black coaches to make their way in the game. It is glaringly obvious that not enough black players become managers.

Powell said: “I suppose there will be an argument that it could have happened years ago but I look at it in a different way and let’s look to the present and the future.

“We can’t deal with much what happened in the past, all we can do maybe do is try to be positive as we can, show people its’ achievable.

“In many ways, I feel that what I’m doing and for many others - the next generation and the generation after that - we have to have a starting point, we have to begin somewhere, we can’t right the wrongs of the past. We can’t do that. What we can do is make things right now.

“It’s very tough, I understand that, there are people who don’t want to go into the managerial world because it can be tough.

“Sometimes you get one opportunity and that’s it. You have to deal with setbacks, you have to deal with them as a player, you certainly have to deal with them as a manager.

“Actually, it doesn’t matter about the colour of your skin, I think the managerial world is if you don’t get the results, you lose your job.

“But I feel some people, and there’s always been coaches of colour, but sometimes they just want to stay at the academy level because they feel they can build a career there, they can stay there, they can stay in the game.

“As a manager, the old saying is that you’re six games away. If you don’t win for six games, you’re under pressure. Some people don’t want that.”
