Children given bikes as part of a generous charity and business collaboration

Recyke y'Bike, in Newcastle, is providing bikes for children from disadvantaged backgrounds, with the help of donations from North East businesses

15 Nov 2019, 17:43 by Nicole Goodwin

A generous charity is providing bikes for children in the North East , who may otherwise miss out on the opportunity to have their own.

David Gray Solicitors, in Newcastle, and ARP Design, in Gateshead , are among the businesses in the region who have donated money and held fundraisers, to enable the charity to repair and deliver the bikes to deserving children.

Through the scheme, Recyke y'Bike, in Byker, has brought happiness to children from low income families or disadvantaged backgrounds.

In one giveaway, the charity were able to replace a bike that had been awarded for good attendance, and later stolen from outside of the child's home.

Mesk Ali Mohamed Idie, aged 12, and Yahya Ali Mohamed Idie, aged seven, are some of the latest children to receive bikes from the charity.

The young family fled from Somalia to Egypt, before moving to the UK in July. The family are now settling in to their new home in Leam Lane, Gateshead.

Mother, Amina Ahmed, said: “This is the first time the children have ever had a bike. It makes me so happy to see the children with their bikes.”

It is hoped that the bikes will help the children become integrated into their new community, and allow them to make friends with other children in the area.

Mesk, who has aspirations of becoming a doctor or a teacher, said: “I feel so happy to have received this bike today and when I learn to ride, I want to help my brother to ride his bike.

“I’m looking forward to riding my bike to school as many of my friends at school also ride bikes.”

Joanne Murray, refugee resettlement worker at the Gateshead Housing Company, helped to deliver the bikes to the children she has been working with since their arrival to the country.

She said: “When the families arrive, they don’t really have toys or possessions as they haven’t been able to bring much with them.”

After watching the Syrian refugee crisis on the TV, Joanne decided to work with refugees, as she was moved by the terrible conditions people are facing in other countries.

The 38-year-old, adds: “Having a bike means they can go outside and play, and not be stuck indoors so much.

“It will give them a chance to explore the community and keep active , so it makes me really happy to see the children and mum happy.”

Sara Newson, general manager at Recyke y'Bike, said: “We're passionate about getting more people on to bikes. We love being able to give them to children who don't have the chance to have a bike, as we believe it's an important rite of passage.

“It provides freedom, independence and fun, and it also helps to establish cycling as a habit, which we hope stays with them into adulthood.

“It's wonderful to be able to help people in such a practical way, and the ability to put a smile on a child's face makes our day.”
