Venice's St. Mark's Square re-opens after worst flooding in decades

16 Nov 2019, 18:28

Venice’s famous St. Mark’s Square has been re-opened to residents and tourists after it was closed due to a series of high tidal waters that devastated the city centre.

On Saturday, the public returned to the square to slosh in the remaining shallow water and stroll around, following almost a week of the worst flooding the city has seen in decades.

Despite sunny skies, the city remained on edge due to possibly more wind-propelled high tidal waters during the weekend, as the water began to rise again in the square.

At noon on Saturday the square was inundated with more than 8 inches of water.

Museums filled up again with tourists and the city’s gondolas were back in business but they were expected to shut down on Sunday due to further threats.

The weather forecast for Sunday is even worse and the high water mark is expected to reach 5.2ft above sea level.

Late on Tuesday, water levels in the historic Italian city reached 6ft 1in above sea level, resulting in its worst floods since 1966.

Luigi Brugnaro, the city’s mayor, estimated damages from the flooding would reach at least €1 billion (£856,192,063) but the final figures are yet to be calculated.

‘Venice is once again being watched by the world and it needs to show that it can succeed and pick itself back up,’ the mayor told Gazzettino and Messaggero newspapers.

The mayor added that programmes were being set up to help compensate residents, with families expecting to receive up to €5,000 (£4,317) and businesses up to €20,000 (£17,123).

Among those recovering from Tuesday’s devastating high waters was Sabrina Laggia and her husband Alfredo, who own a stone jewelry workshop near St. Mark’s Square.

She was dreading forecasts for more high water on Sunday, adding: ‘We have been here 30 years and we have never seen anything like this’.

Alfredo said they used to feel safe if the forecast said anything up to 4.6ft but they were shocked when it surged way above that on Tuesday night.

They spent the night moving their creations to higher shelves in their store but when the levels rose so high there was no way of saving them.

On Thursday, the government declared a state of emergency, approving €20 million (£17,123,841) to help Venice repair the most urgent damage.

Built on a series of tiny islets amid a system of canals, Venice is particularly vulnerable to a combination of rising sea levels due to climate change coupled with the city’s well-documented sinking into the mud.

The sea level in Venice is 4 inches higher than it was 50 years ago, according to the city’s tide office.

This week’s devastation has left Italians exasperated at the long delay in building the city’s Moses flood defence project.

Moses consists of a series of movable barriers in the lagoon that can be raised when high winds and high tides combine to threaten to send high tides rushing across the city.

Completion of the multibillion-euro project, under construction since 2003, has been delayed by corruption scandals, cost overruns and opposition from environmentalists worried about its effects on Venice’s delicate lagoon ecosystem.

‘They need to finish the Moses tomorrow,’ said Sabrina Laggia. ‘Not next year.’
