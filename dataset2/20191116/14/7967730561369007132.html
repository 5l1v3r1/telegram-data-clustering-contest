Queensland advertising blitz to lure schoolies into apprenticeships

16 Nov 2019, 14:00 by Tony Moore

School leavers and businesses are being asked to take on something big – an apprenticeship or an apprentice – as the Queensland government begins a four-month, statewide apprenticeship advertising blitz.

From Sunday, the 38,000 Year 12 students who left school on Friday will be targeted to consider apprenticeships in 139 different areas.

The multimillion-dollar advertising blitz is the latest stage of the Labor government's $32 million policy announced in August to pay businesses to train new apprentices.

The aggressive move comes as Queensland’s unemployment rate stubbornly remains at 6.4 per cent, well above the 5.2 per cent national rate.

While Brisbane’s unemployment rate for the July quarter decreased to 5 per cent, in regional Queensland it was in double figures in some areas.

The government wants 60,000 new apprentices under 21 years of age in 139 traineeship areas across four years.

Since August, businesses have already taken on more than 2200 apprentices.

Premier Annastacia Palaszczuk said the timing of the advertising campaign – named "Take your future on" – was deliberate.

"Right now, school leavers and their parents are thinking about the future," Ms Palaszczuk said. "The message of ‘Take your future on’ goes straight to this vital decision-making time.

"And the government is ready to give young people a real good go with the quality training they need to secure employment."

The campaign, which begins on Sunday and runs until March 2020, will feature heavily on Facebook, Instagram and online in areas where career advice is sought by young people.

It will also feature on television, outdoor billboards, and in shopping centre displays.

The program covers apprenticeships in electrical work, plumbing, engineering, health care, hospitality, early childhood education, baking, commercial cookery and hairdressing, among other areas.

If a business signs up through an Apprenticeship Network Provider, the Queensland Government will cover the cost of an apprentice’s training.

It could save up to $3000 over the life of the apprenticeship for businesses, which currently fund the courses themselves.

Ms Palaszczuk asked young school leavers to strongly consider their apprenticeship options.

"Whenever I meet and talk with school-leavers, they often tell me about the fears they have concerning their job prospects and futures," she said.

"Apprentices and trainees have always been an integral part of our state’s economy, and over the next four years, my government is committed to assist another 60,000 young people into a trade.

"The statewide campaign is a call to action for employers and young people so they can hire an apprentice or start training in an apprenticeship knowing the training will be delivered for free."

In October, as the latest figures were released, Queensland’s Chamber of Commerce and Industry encouraged the government to take steps to reduce unemployment.

"The latest job print for Queensland is unacceptable, from both a business and economic stand point," spokesman Dan Petrie said.

"This is a terrible read on the state’s job numbers where policy makers have to act to ensure every project that can be brought forward is done so without delay."
