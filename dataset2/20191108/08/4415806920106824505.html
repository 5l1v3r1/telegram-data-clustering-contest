Laws around 'honest belief' and consent need to be changed: Law Reform Commission

08 Nov 2019, 08:09 by By Digital Desk staff

Current laws that allow a man accused of rape be acquitted if he honestly believes the woman consented to sex need to be reformed, The Law Reform Commission has said.

The Commission says the defence of "honest belief" needs to be removed.

The Commission is recommending that the defence of honest belief in a case where the accused believes a woman has consented to sex, even when she did not, should be replaced with a provision that such belief must be "reasonable".

A detailed examination of the law was carried out on the request of the attorney general following a 2017 supreme court ruling that focused on what the defendant believed in relation to consent, rather than whether it was a reasonable belief.

The commission is recommending that where an accused argues that he had a reasonable belief that the woman consented to sex, the commission recommends the jury in the trial should consider a "specific list of circumstances" relating to the personal capacity of the accused, and what steps the accused took to ascertain whether the woman consented to sex.
