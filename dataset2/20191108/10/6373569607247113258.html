Bolton Wanderers boss Keith Hill on Plymouth Argyle FA Cup clash, Ryan Lowe and Remi Matthews

The former Pilgrims' defender has spoken to Plymouth Live ahead of the first round tie

08 Nov 2019, 10:41 by Chris Errington

The last time that Plymouth Argyle played Bolton Wanderers was as long ago as January 1993.

Peter Shilton’s Pilgrims lost 3-1 away to Bolton at their former ground, Burnden Park.

Lining up for Argyle that afternoon was defender Keith Hill, who made 151 appearances for the club between 1992 and 1996.

Now all that time later Hill is the manager of Bolton and his first FA Cup tie in charge of them is against Ryan Lowe’s Pilgrims tomorrow.

Hill was appointed as Bolton boss at the end of August shortly after the club was rescued out of administration by Football Ventures.

Wanderers started their season with a 12-point deduction and while they remain bottom of League One they have won their last three games.

So there is a mood of optimism around the University of Bolton Stadium as they prepare to take on Argyle.

Hill spared some time to talk to Plymouth Live about the FA Cup clash, Lowe, life at Bolton and Wanderers’ former Pilgrims’ goalkeeper Remi Matthews.

How much are you looking forward to having a FA Cup tie against one of your former clubs, Plymouth Argyle?

First and foremost, It’s great that it’s at home. I still can’t understand why the early rounds are not regionalised, from an expense point of view and a travelling distance point of view.

So I’m really pleased that we are at the University of Bolton Stadium, but against Plymouth it’s a difficult tie.

They are a good side, a big club, unfortunately relegated last season, but I have got no doubt that they will get promoted again under Ryan Lowe and the team that he is trying to assemble, both on and off the field.

I know the chairman (Simon Hallett) is doing a great job there. I know in the summer that he bought existing shares and got rid of the debt, which is superb for the infrastructure and the future of the football club.

So I think there is a bright times ahead for Plymouth, 100 per cent, and it’s going to be a difficult tie for us.

Ryan Lowe has talked about playing against your sides in the past, and David Flitcroft (Hill’s No 2 at Bolton) was his manager at Bury. What are your thoughts on Ryan?

He deserves his opportunity and you have got to take that opportunity. I was a similar age when I went into management – 37, I think I was.

It’s important that you get the belief of the players on the training pitch and the way that you want to do things.

Obviously, as a result of the way you want to do things, matchday results are really important in galvanising that belief amongst a group of players.

He seems to be a very good man-manager, astute in the business he has done over the summer, and I believe that Plymouth will be successful this season.

It always takes time when you are putting a team together, not just a team on the field of play but his support staff – the team behind the team.

It’s important that they integrate well and I think you have got to understand that.

Listen, he has had a great start to his management career. He seems as though he studies quite well, and I’m sure he has studied us.

We have crossed paths as managers and players as well, so I’m looking forward to doing the opportunity of doing it again on Saturday.

You certainly took on a stiff challenge when you were appointed by Bolton. How have your first few months been? They are a big club who are more than capable of playing at a lot higher level.

It’s a massive club. We are trying to create a big club mentality. There has been a bit of a malaise for the last two seasons.

They have been in and out of certain sanctions that have been imposed by the EFL and they have been under an embargo but it’s clean now.

“It’s a massive football club and an unbelievable opportunity for myself and David, and a new set of players that we are trying to galvanise, and we are trying to balance off with the players that we inherited.

And I must say the players we have inherited have been absolutely magnificent.

There has been no hangover from the administration or last season. We are just looking forward now – we are looking at the future.

We are trying to build and re-establish this great football club to get as high up the football pyramid as we possibly can.

This club has got Premier League ambitions and Premier League infrastructure, and we have got a sound business plan.

Three wins in a row, it’s a good run of form for you. How hopeful are you of staying in League One this season and escaping relegation despite the points deduction?

We are optimistic. We are challenging the players, we are challenging ourselves as staff every day and every game that we play.

More importantly, what we are doing on the training pitch is to establish that rhythm so we can challenge every League One opponent we are playing against.

The three games that we have won on the bounce have been a product of the previous work that we have done.

The scoreboard – the end result – that’s the emotional side of the game, but the work we are doing on the training pitch has been evident in all of the games we have played.

We are optimistic about giving ourselves a fighting chance. We have still got 31 games left so there is a lot of points to play for.

At this moment in time there is nobody that should be writing us off, and we certainly aren’t writing ourselves off.

The league will be your bread and butter, of course it will. How important is a FA Cup run to you this season?

Again, we are treating it as just another game. We feel as though running into the second half of the season that we want as many games as we can.

I have got no fear of playing Saturday-Tuesday, Saturday-Tuesday so we get some sort of continuity and consistency to our week.

If we get the players that we have injured back fit, and on the training pitch, we will be able to cope with two games a week.

A FA Cup run is a product of being successful in the work that you are doing during the week, so bring it on.

If we can stay in the Leasing.com Trophy or the FA Cup for as long as we can it’s success, and it’s what I want at this football club.

I want the players to embrace those successes so we do want a cup run, but we are not arrogant in any way, shape or form.

We have got full respect to what is a big task on Saturday playing against Plymouth.

The last time that Argyle played against Bolton was at Burnden Park in January 1993 and you were in the team that day. Bolton won 3-1. Andy Morrison scored an own goal. Do you remember that game?

Yes, I do. I remember travelling up on the day. The consequence of that second half of the season was that the next season we were in a play-off situation.

We had a really good squad of players that were brought together and in the next season we were beaten by Burnley in the play-offs.

We got used to each other, we built relationships on and off the pitch that season we got beaten by Bolton, but the next season was a really successful one.

If I have any regrets it’s that season because we were a team that was good enough to get promoted and we missed out narrowly.

We ended up going up to Hartlepool on the last day of the season and I think we beat them 8-1.

Then we went into the play-offs and got beaten by a Burnley side that finished 12 points behind us.

I still believe that was a travesty because that team was more than good enough to have got promoted.

Unfortunately, the next season after the play-offs unravelled in relegation, which sometimes happens, but we missed a real opportunity there not getting promoted.

Playing Bolton at Burnden Park, it was a difficult task and I think Bolton got promoted that season (as runners-up to Stoke City).

We played against a good side and it was warranted that we got beat 3-1.

But the product of those games in the second half of that season held us in good stead for the next season, where I thought we were superb.

Apparently, Bolton had won 2-0 at Liverpool in a FA Cup replay the midweek before you played them so you caught them at a bad time.

They were a very good side. I think we had beaten them earlier on in the season at Home Park (a 2-1 win) but when we played them in January they were hitting their straps.

They were beating everybody and they had a successful campaign. I think that was under Bruce Rioch, and they had (Andy) Walker and John McGinley playing up front.

One last question. Remi Matthews had an excellent loan spell with Argyle when they nearly got into the League One play-offs in 2017/18. How he is doing for Bolton?

Brilliant, superb. He was a little bit damaged when we took over. He had been conceding quite a lot of goals.

I had to reassure him that none of them goals were really down to him individually. It was just the amount of ammunition that was getting fired at him.

He’s an excellent goalkeeper. He’s leading by example on the training pitch.

His performances have been superb. He knows which type of game we want to play and I believe he’s at least a Championship, if not a Premier League, goalkeeper in the making.

He’s still very young as a goalkeeper but every day is an important one to him to progress.

He has been absolutely superb. He has made some unbelievable matchwinning saves for us and we are really pleased to have him.

I knew he was a good goalkeeper having seen him perform so well for Plymouth Argyle in that season.
