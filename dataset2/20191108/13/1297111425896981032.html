Johnson: One-of-a-kind ’Vette pickup

"Coleton Nickol of Swift Current has a pickup that really stands out. He doesn’t have to worry about his pickup looking like all the others."

08 Nov 2019, 13:00 by Dale Edward Johnson

Pickups are everywhere — especially in Saskatchewan. This is indeed F-150, Ram and Silverado country.

But Coleton Nickol of Swift Current has a pickup that really stands out. He doesn’t have to worry about his pickup looking like all the others. His rig is one of a kind.

And it’s not just that it’s older than average. It’s a 1982 model. Sure, there are a few ’82 pickups still on the road. That’s not what sets his apart from the crowd.

His pickup has a diesel engine — like plenty of other trucks. Nothing special there.

But its diminutive size makes people stare.

His diesel pickup is the smallest one on the parking lot. At about 4188 mm, it’s 1165 mm (almost four feet) shorter than a new Chevrolet Silverado Crew Cab.

And what really sets Nickol’s pickup apart is the model name.

It’s a Chevrolet Chevette.

The Chevette was Chevrolet’s entry-level model from 1976 to 1987. It was the smallest and cheapest Chevy — and for a time it was the best-selling small car in North America. In all, more than 2.8 million Chevettes were sold.

The Chevrolet Chevette was the North American version of a small car that had been developed by GM in Germany, and sold in other countries under various names: the Opel Kadett C in Germany, the Isuzu Bellett Gemini in Japan, the Vauxhall Chevette in England and the Holden Gemini in Australia. Later, there were Pontiac versions called the Acadian and the T1000.

Nickol created his Chevette pickup from a four-door hatchback Chevette.

He saw an ad on Kijiji in the fall of 2017 for a 1982 Chevette diesel. It wasn’t running, and had gone 152,000 kms.

“After negotiations the seller and I agreed on $425. I drove to Saskatoon with my dad’s truck, some friends, and a trailer. The car had supposedly received ‘professional’ body work and was rust-free,” Nickol recalls.

It turned out that when Nickol met the seller, the car wasn’t there. The seller provided him with directions to pick it up.

“In retrospect, a bit sketchy. Good thing I picked it up in the dark, because not only was it rusted, the paint was also stripped. I loaded it up and home we came,” he remembers.

“We pulled it in to the shop to change the starter, and after a full inspection, my dad and I realized that mechanically there was nothing major wrong with the car.”

But it was a different story with the body, which had extensive rust, especially on the posts supporting the roof at the back of the car.

His father was helping with the project. His father had put more than 850,000 kms on his Duramax, a V-8 6.6 litre diesel engine used on GM trucks.

“It appeared the Chevette had been rolled or jumped on. My dad suggested that we pull out the cutter and turn it into the world’s smallest Duramax pickup. He was kidding — but I wasn’t.”

In the early 1980s, Chevettes popularized the idea of diesels in passenger cars, in order to get better fuel economy.

GM dropped a 51-horsepower 1.8 litre diesel from Isuzu (a Japanese automaker affiliated with GM) in the Chevette to create the diesel version. It was available only with a five-speed standard transmission, and air conditioning was not available, as both automatics and air conditioning take power from engines — and this engine has no surplus power.

“With a zero-to-sixty time of 21.2 seconds and a top speed of barely 80 mph, it always feels as if you’ve left the hand brake on,” Car and Driver magazine said in July 1981.

But the goal of outstanding fuel economy was accomplished, with the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) rating the Chevette diesel at 5.9 litres per 100 kms in the city and 4.3 l/100 kms on the highway.

Car and Driver also had praise for the Chevette diesel, saying, “It starts almost instantly from cold, runs smoothly, and is no noisier than a gas-engined small car on the highway.”

Motor Trend magazine tested a diesel Chevette along with two other diesels, a VW Rabbit and an Isuzu I-Mark, in August 1981, and said the $7,578 Chevette, “needs more interior room and a more modern dashboard/instrument panel layout…but even at that, we think it’s the winner in this three-vehicle comparison. The price is right, the mileage is the best of the three, the ride and handling with the optional equipment are superior.”

Nickol drives his Chevette pickup year-round, and the minus-30-degree weather in the winter doesn’t cause any problems.

He recently drove it to Castlegar, B.C., and says he “had a great time driving as slow as semi-trucks up the long, steep grades.”

He says the best part of owning a Chevette diesel pickup is the reaction it gets from other drivers.

“For the most part it’s just thumbs-up and rubbernecks.”
