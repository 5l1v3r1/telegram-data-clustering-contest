Sainsbury's Christmas advert 2019 sees Dickensian fairy tale come to life

The atmospheric and heartwarming Christmas ad tells the tale of the origins of St Nick (or Santa Claus) and is set in Victorian England

12 Nov 2019 by Ruki Sayid, https://www.mirror.co.uk/authors/ruki-sayid/

A heartwarming Christmas ad from supermarket chain Sainsbury’s is set to fill the nation with a much needed feelgood factor.

The two and a half minute “epic” brings together the store’s 150 years as a grocer and one of the oldest tales of them all - the origins of St Nick or Santa Claus.

Set in Victorian England, the atmospheric mini-film centres on the first Sainsbury’s grocery store that was opened in Drury Lane, London in 1869 by James and Mary Ann Sainsbury.

And it stars dirty faced chimney sweep Nicholas, played by nine-year-old London schoolboy Chris Dunkley, who is framed by his cruel boss - a Fagin style character.

The workmaster steals a hatful of clementines from the store’s outdoor fruit stand and when orphan Nick picks up a fallen one from the ground to put it back, an over zealous police officer spots him and wrongfully has him carted off in a prison wagon.

The Dickensian setting sees the hateful mob calling for Nick to be banished for stealing but Mrs Sainsbury has spotted the real culprit - his scheming master who is tucking into the fruit and declaring “some people are just rotten to the core”.

As the boy is sent into snowy exile with no coat or blanket to shield him from the blizzard, Mrs Sainsbury - played by actress Bethan Cullinane - comes to his rescue.

Nick pleads his innocence and she reassures him that she knows he wasn’t the thief and in a Christmas gesture of goodwill hands him a bag of clementines.

She tells him: “If you can’t do something special for someone at Christmas, then when can you?”

Unsure how to pay her back for her kindness, Nick has an idea - and in the dead of night heads to the children’s home where all his chimney sweep pals are asleep and pops a clementine into each of their empty Christmas stockings.

And removing the naughty list from the wall, he crosses out all the kids names including his own and uses it to wrap a lump of coal for his callous boss adding the words “Naughty List YOU” - to the fury of the workmaster.

As the excited kids race around with their gifts of the now traditional stocking filler fruit, the camera pans to Nick standing gazing at the town’s skyline from afar.

Wrapped in the red blanket Mrs Sainsbury gave him, he unfolds his hat, puts it on and with a backward glance to the camera walks towards grazing reindeer, looking every inch like a young Santa Claus.

The blockbuster ad ends with the narrator’s words: “And that is a totally true story” as Mrs Sainsbury winks at the camera.

Filmed just outside Bucharest, Romania over five days, the atmospheric soundtrack was specially composed by Chris White of rock band The Zombies.

The “Helping make Christmas, Christmas since 1869” ad will air during the Emmerdale break tonight (Tues) with the full version on YouTube from this morning.

Laura Boothby, head of broadcast marketing at Sainsbury’s admitted the ad uses “artistic license” to re-imagine how a Christmas fairytale might have played out in 1869.

She added: “This is the only year we can talk about being 150 years old and tell of the Christmas traditions we have today that came from Victorian England.

“We wanted to tell a heartwarming story full of joy. There is an expectation on us every Christmas and we don’t want to disappoint.”

And she revealed: “The more times you see the ad, the more little details you will spot.”

10 things to look out for in the ad:

  1. A poster for “plug boy” from last year’s ad on a lamp post.
  2. The jail master says “ho, ho, ho”.
  3. Nicholas falls asleep under a Christmas tree when he’s banished.
  4. The red blanket Mary Ann Sainsbury gives him becomes his red coat and snow on the collar looks like the white trim.
  5. Nick’s hat turns from brown to red at the end.
  6. One of the reindeer in the closing scenes has a red nose.
  7. The horse and cart that rescues Nick is marked “zero emissions”.
  8. The city scape at the end is a nod to the modern London skyline such as outlines of The Shard and London Eye.
  9. The Banishment Gate takes him to the North Pole.
  10. Nicholas is a chimney sweep meaning he can get up and down people’s chimneys easily like You Know Who.
