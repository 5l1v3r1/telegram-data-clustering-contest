When we talk about the gut, what we’re really talking about is the community of symbiotic bacteria and other microorganisms that live in the human digestive tract. Over the last few decades, scientists have discovered that this ecosystem plays a huge role in our overall health, affecting almost every system in the body, including the digestive system, immune system, and cardiovascular system. Some studies have even linked gut health to a number of specific diseases and conditions, such as diabetes, obesity, irritable bowel syndrome, and colon cancer. However, because everyone’s gut microbiome is different, there is no single course of action doctors and scientists can prescribe to improve gut health. That’s where microbiome tests come in.

A microbiome test is kind of like a DNA test. Instead of mapping out your genes and identifying markers for certain diseases and conditions, a microbiome test maps out the microorganisms that live in your gut and identifies how they affect your health.

Trying to improve gut health without taking a microbiome test is like playing darts while blindfolded. You have a general idea of where the dart board is, so you’ll probably hit the board a couple of times. If you’re really lucky, you might even get a bullseye. However, you’d do a lot better with your eyes open.

The same can be said for building a healthy gut. Everybody knows there are certains foods that are supposed to improve gut health. But you won’t know for sure what’s best for your gut until you take the blindfold off and take a microbiome test.
