'I was a pop star and WAG but after a stabbing I now spend time on the streets'

Chinyere McKenzie, the ex-wife of former Premiership footballer Dickson Etuhu, has moved back to Moss Side, Manchester and is taking on knife and gun crime in the neighbourhood

12 Nov 2019, 07:30 by Matthew Dresch, Adam Maidment, https://www.mirror.co.uk/authors/matthew-dresch/

A former pop star has returned to the troubled neighbourhood of her childhood after watching a boy get stabbed to death.

Chinyere McKenzie has devoted herself to tackling knife and gun crime in Moss Side, Manchester, since she moved back to the area.

The 39-year-old went back to her roots her marriage with Premiership and ex-Manchester City footballer Dickson Etuhu broke down.

The ex-'WAG' originally left Moss Side in 1999 to pursue a career in music and dance in London, the Manchester Evening News reports.

In 2004, she was selected to be a member of pop band VS with Marvin Humes, who would later go on to be in JLS.

 

The band, managed by Simon Webbe from boyband Blue, secured three top 40 tracks including a number seven hit with ‘Love You Like Mad’ before splitting up in 2005. 

In that time, she appeared on the Top of the Pops stage and performed in front of tens of thousands at the Manchester Arena, before she met Mr Etuhu.

The couple soon married and had two boys, now aged ten and eight, but divorced after five years together.

“I came back (to Moss Side) in 2018 because I needed support from my close family and friends,” Chinyere told the Manchester Evening News.

 

“As I arrived back, I saw a young boy in the street who was stabbed and killed.

"I was shocked, and it instantly made me see that things had gone too far and things had to change.”

With two young boys and a new perspective on life, having moved from what Chinyere calls ‘Poshville’ back to her family neighbourhood, she soon decided that something needed to be done to help those affected by knife and gun crime.

“When I was younger growing up, Moss Side went through lots of turbulence and violence,” Chinyere said.

“I realised that some parts of the community have still not been able to get over that era.

“Coming back and looking at where I was brought up with a fresh perspective, I saw that people really needed to come together as a community and talk to one another if any change was going to happen.”

It was then that she decided to take on a real battle and try and help prevent and combat crime in Moss Side.

Chinyere launched Muma Knows Best, a weekly show on community radio station Legacy 90.1 FM where listeners could ring in and provide each other with advice and support.

"I feel that I’ve been in a great position being a pop star and being at a place where I was financially able and now I feel I am at a point where I am able to do what I want to do," Chinyere says.

“What I want to do is help bridge that gap that I see between the rich and poor and between opportunity and perception."

Not only has the radio show helped her listeners, Chinyere says it has had a massive impact on her own self-discovery too.

"I was left defeated after the divorce, I lost a sense of myself.

"As a woman, when you've given everything to everyone, you can lose a sense of who you are.

"I've learnt so much from my listeners, and what's beautiful is that I'm on a journey of self-discovery too.

"Being able to reconnect with myself is important and I'm doing that on this journey with everyone else."

Chinyere is currently seeking fundraising to help evolve the brand into a social enterprise, giving people affected by trauma the tools to get themselves back off the ground, such as financial management, cognitive behavioural therapy and community workshops.

The former pop star’s journey is also being documented in a fly-on-the-wall series on YouTube as she fights to spread the message of Muma Knows Best.

The ten-part series has already received a positive response from viewers.

Chinyere says the series is something she felt compelled to do, especially if she wants others to open up about their own experiences.

“If I open up, I hope it will have a knock-on effect on other people and encourage them to do the same.

“Some people have real issues that are difficult to share but what I've discovered with the radio show and hopefully the documentary series is that they are willing to talk about it with me and the community we have built up.

“It solidifies to me that what we’re doing is right and we need more support because there’s people out there that need the help and aren’t potentially getting it.

"It’s not about fearing our differences, it’s about celebrating our differences and coming together to enrich those differences."

Asked about the elusive Muma, which the whole business is named around, Chinyere says it's more about a community than an individual.

"Muma is not me, I am certainly not saying I know it all," she laughs.

"I'm the person going on the journey trying to find the answers to the questions

"Muma is the voices of everyone else that comes up with the answers and goes through those experiences."
