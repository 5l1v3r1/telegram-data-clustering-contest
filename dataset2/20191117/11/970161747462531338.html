Men queue down the street for free prostate cancer test

17 Nov 2019, 11:07

Pictures show thousands of men queuing for a free prostate cancer test in what organisers said was the ‘biggest such event in the world’.

More than 2,000 men were tested for the disease, which is the most common killer of male cancer patients in the UK.

Volunteers from the Reading Lions Club helped organise the event at the Rutherford Cancer Centre in Thames Valley, west London, on Saturday.

Men were offered free blood tests, known as the PSA test, which normally cost around £140.

This checks for raised levels of a protein in the blood, which can indicate cancerous cells.

Prostate cancer is the most common form of cancer and 11,000 men die from the disease in the UK every year.

Alan McMahon, 71, from Berkshire, said going to last year’s free testing event saved his life.

He explained: ‘I had my blood test and when I received the Lions result letter, I was shocked to see that my PSA reading was abnormally high.

‘I immediately contacted my GP and she referred me to a specialist urologist.’

He added: ‘After a month of radiotherapy I was placed on a three-monthly hormone injection programme and my PSA reading is now virtually zero.

‘It is not an exaggeration to say that the Lions have saved my life as the cancer was caught just in time before it spread elsewhere.

‘I cannot stress enough how important it is that all men over 50 be tested and therefore take advantage of this event at the Rutherford centre and book a test.’

Chris Howes, 48, also found out he had prostate cancer after last year’s event, and is now cancer-free after successful treatment.

Mr Howes, also from Berkshire, said: ‘I was extremely fortunate to come to this event and have a test which flagged up a potential problem.

‘I was completely symptom-less, were it not for this test, my cancer would have been left undiagnosed.’

Dr Christopher Blick, consultant urologist at the Rutherford Cancer Centre, said: ‘It is very important that men are aware of the risks of prostate cancer and when it is likely to occur.

‘While in most cases it will occur in later life, it is still possible for men in their 50’s or even late 40’s to get prostate cancer.’
