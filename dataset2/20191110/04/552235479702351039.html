Family of woman murdered 25 years ago in Irvine in new plea to help catch killer

Shona Stevens was battered to death as she was coming back from the shops in 1994 and her mum and daughter are still hopeful of justice.

10 Nov 2019, 04:30 by Norman Silvester, https://www.dailyrecord.co.uk/authors/norman-silvester/

The family of a woman ­murdered 25 years ago say they have not given up hope of police catching her killer.

Shona Stevens was found on a footpath near her home on November 10, 1994, after being battered in a frenzied attack.

The 31-year-old died three days later in hospital from head injuries.

Police have launched a new appeal on the anniversary of her death.

Shona’s mum Mhairi Smith, 85, and daughter Candice Stevens, 32, still believe someone will be arrested for her murder.

Mhairi said: “It’s been 25 years since Shona was taken from us and we’re still as hopeful as ever that those responsible for her murder can be brought to justice.

“I can’t emphasise enough how ­important even the smallest piece of information could be in being able to give me and my family closure.

“I want to know who was responsible for this attack or why they did it.”

Shona was attacked shortly after 1pm around 200 yards from her home in Alder Green in Irvine’s Bourtreehill as she came back from a supermarket shopping trip.

Candice added: “I was only seven at the time of mum’s murder but that does not make it any easier to deal with.

“I spent a large part of my ­childhood growing up ­without my mum and I would ask­ ­anyone who knows ­anything about the incident to please come forward.”

Detective Superintendent Paul Livingstone said: “I firmly believe that ­someone has seen who was responsible for this. In 25 years, people’s allegiances change. They should have no fears about coming forward after all this time.”

Vital evidence from the crime scene was feared to have been blown away by the downdraught of the helicopter ­airlifting Shona to hospital in Glasgow.

Shona’s death is part of a cold case review. Officers hope advancements in science could lead to a breakthrough.

 

Livingstone added: “The investigation has never been closed.

“It doesn’t just stop because we’ve not brought anyone to justice.

“The support of the local ­community here is vital. We’ll never know what evidence was there or what was lost.

“The priority today, as it was then, would be the ­preservation of life.

“Murder inquiries do not just rely on forensic ­evidence. For such a young person with a young child to be ­murdered in broad ­daylight is ­unacceptable.”
