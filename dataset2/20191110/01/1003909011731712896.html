Combat veterans find camaraderie and healing in Alberta's outdoors

10 Nov 2019, 01:01 by Bill Kaufmann

The Canadian convoy had just left the safety of Camp Julien near Kabul when Pte. Matthew Ritchie heard the detonation.

“We knew it wasn’t a typical explosion or vehicle backfire,” said Ritchie, whose squad quickly mobilized to offer help.

“We were on the scene less than three minutes after the explosion.”

Ritchie recalls yelling at a soldier to escape the burning wreckage of the stricken vehicle.

“But he was dead,” said the veteran, who was a member of the Royal Canadian Regiment tasked with providing security and stability in the area in the early days of Canada’s deployment in Afghanistan.

The Jan. 27, 2004, suicide bombing cost Ritchie his friend Cpl. Jamie Murphy of Conception Harbour, N.L., while two other Canadian soldiers and several civilians were injured.

Being witness to such carnage was traumatic enough, but it was the plight of civilians trapped by the war’s cruelty that left the deepest impression, he said.

“What hit me was hardest was watching a child starve to death, said Ritchie, 36, of Hinton.

Returning home from his 2003-04 tour of duty proved a psychological challenge he initially tried to deny.

“I had trouble acclimatizing. I didn’t like to dwell on any of it and it took several years for me to understand I needed counselling,” said Ritchie.

Re-integrating didn’t get any easier when images of apparel recalling his Afghan service that he’d posted online drew the wrath of those hostile to the military, he said.

Those social media attacks were noticed by Penhold residents Ray McKay and Ray McKinnon, who stood up for the Afghan vet and invited him out to a parcel of wooded land in central Alberta to enjoy outdoor activities such as hunting, fishing and camping.

“It meant a lot for someone to reach out to me, that we’re not forgotten that someone would say ‘we love you, come out and have some fun,’ ” said Ritchie.

Since then, word has spread about the free outdoors excursions and host McKay figures more than 30 military veterans have taken up the offer.

“They don’t get the recognition,” said cement finisher McKay, who adds he’s dug deep into his own pockets to fund the activities while also collecting donations.

“Nobody in my family was in the wars but I just see what the government does to them, nobody really helps them.”

The demand among the veteran community for that outdoor bonding reached a point where McKay’s outfit, dubbed Hoggin’ Alberta, needed more space.

That led to a landowner last spring offering 32 hectares of property covered in spruce, poplar and tamarack near Caroline, about 175 kilometres northwest of Calgary, at a discount rental rate for the ex-warriors to use as they wish.

“It’s nice to see this has just gotten bigger and bigger,” said McKay, 53.

“I give them the keys, they can come and go as they please.”

Ex-paratrooper Will Shaw spent part of last summer clearing bush for a campsite on the Caroline land after being put in touch with McKay.

He’d reached a low point, darkened by physical and psychological injuries related to his military service.

“It was quiet out there, something I needed,” said Albertan Shaw, 31.

“There’s enough space that it’s pretty free-wheeling out there for veterans.”

He also credits McKay for “levelling me out” psychologically.

“Because of him, I’m not off the tracks,” said Shaw.

Knowing McKay’s a wild boar rancher, Shaw said he recently requested a sample of bacon harvested from that herd of Russian razorbacks.

“I said ‘can I try your bacon’ and he gave me a whole pig,” he said.

That’s been the theme of Hoggin’ Alberta for several years.

“I received a bow but gave it away to somebody who needed it more than I did,” said Ritchie.

“But it’s not all about the hunting, it’s the people you spend time with and I came out of it with a very good friend.”

McKay and McKinnon’s efforts have also funneled money to veterans’ food banks and the Red Deer Ronald McDonald House which helps families of hospitalized kids.

While he says funding all of those activities is always a challenge, McKay said he and Hoggin’ Alberta won’t give up making life more livable for those who’ve served.

“Why stop? I have a tonne of veteran friends now,” he said.
