Recession Via Inverted Yield Curve? Gone, So Focus On Stocks

10 Nov 2019 by John S. Tobey

As soon as the Federal Reserve announced it was through with lowering short-term rates, Wall Street sold its bets on more rate reductions. “Poof” went the inverted yield curve, thus proving that “recession” had nothing to do with it. The graph below shows the effects of Wall Street’s speculations: First, pushing yields up in anticipation of Fed increases, then pushing them down when expectations turned to decreases.

Now what?

With the return of a positive-slope yield curve, perhaps a form of “normality” has returned.

However, the changes happened too fast for a full adjustment. While Wall Street is efficient at making instant trades, not so for investors (both institutional and individual). The latter need time to digest 180-turnabouts.

A few weeks ago, the popular beliefs were recession ahead, too-low inflation, falling consumer confidence, weakening corporate results and tariff-trade wars. Adding up all that negativity produced expectations of further Fed rate cuts and high risk from holding stocks.

Reality strikes

Instead, earnings reports are good, stocks are rising, consumer confidence is retracing its drop and the Federal Reserve says not to expect any further cuts because conditions look fine.

Therefore, stocks are acceptable again, but they have risen into new high territory. As a result, investors now wonder if they should jump in or wait. Likely, they are taking the latter approach, either by decision or by indecision.

And that brings up bonds. Look at that 10-year US Treasury bond yield of below 2%. That means it is at best breaking even on a “real” (inflation-adjusted) basis. Based on the bond’s yield over the past ten years of Fed-controlled low rates, that level is too low. Taking on the risk of a ten-year commitment in order to earn a real 0% (ignoring any tax effect) when economic and business conditions are fine just isn’t right. Therefore, there appears to be risk without an appropriate return from holding bonds at this time.

But selling bonds means needing to put the proceeds somewhere else

Short-term investments now earn above 0%, so the income reduction is not as onerous as before, plus the risk of capital losses is reduced.

As to stocks – while the market is in all-time high territory, it has only just broken out of an almost two-year “pause.” Importantly for income investors, there are high quality stocks that offer good dividend yields. (Of the 30 Dow Jones Industrial stocks, 21 have yields over 2%.)

And that brings up the possibility of a widespread investor action that was popularly anticipated in 2013: the “Great Rotation.” When bond prices stopped rising and the economy was doing well, the reasoning went, investors would move out of their low-yielding bonds into stocks offering good return potential. The theory petered out back then (as do most popular beliefs), so now would be the perfect time to occur – when nobody is talking about it.

The bottom line

The no-longer-inverted yield curve and the other recent developments are significant and potentially rewarding – if investors take action.

It can seem like a spin in the barrel, but that is investing. From own bonds and avoid stocks to own stocks and avoid bonds. Really? Yes!
