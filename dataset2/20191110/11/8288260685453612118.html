Flood alerts issued for Tamworth, Solihull and Studley after torrential rain fills rivers

Environment Agency officials are monitoring water levels and say there has already been some flooding on low-lying land and roads including caravan parks in Warwickshire

10 Nov 2019, 11:14 by David Bentley, https://www.facebook.com/davidbentleybm/

Environment Agency officials have today issued flood alerts for parts of the Midlands.

They have warned of flooding in Tamworth, Solihull and Studley after torrential downpours.

The Government agency says flooding is possible on the Bourne Brook at Tamworth where river levels have risen "as a result of persistent heavy rainfall overnight."

Flooding of roads and farmland is possible from Sunday morning, it says, and is already affecting low-lying land and roads adjacent to the Bourne Brook.

No further rainfall is forecast today but river levels are expected to remain high into Monday.

 

Staff are closely monitoring the river levels and forecasts and have urged people to avoid roads, footpaths and bridges near local watercourses.

Another alert is in place for the River Blythe in Solihull borough, where there has also been heavy rain.

 

Flooding is already affecting low-lying land and roads adjacent to the river between Cheswick Green and Blyth End.

But no further rainfall is forecast today. River levels will remain high into Monday.

Incident response staff are actively checking river levels and the weather forecast and are urging people to avoid roads, footpaths and bridges near the river.

A further alert is in force for the River Arrow and River Alne in Warwickshire, where water levels are high after heavy rain.

The agency says flooding is possible and is already affecting low-lying land and roads adjacent to the River Arrow between Studley and Salford Priors, and between Bird in Hand and Alcester on the River Alne including farmland and caravan parks.

River levels are expected to remain high into Monday and, once again, the agency is urging people to plan driving routes to avoid low-lying roads near rivers, which may be flooded, and avoid using low-lying footpaths and bridges near local watercourses.
