BREAKING General election: Humiliated Nigel Farage pulls Brexit Party out of 317 seats

It is a humiliation for the Brexit Party leader after he repeatedly issued an empty threat to stand 600 candidates in the general election 2019

11 Nov 2019, 12:15 by Ben Glaze, Dan Bloom, https://www.facebook.com/MirrorPolitics, https://www.facebook.com/mirrordanbloom

Nigel Farage today pulled the Brexit Party out of all 317 Tory seats in a general election humiliation after Boris Johnson refused his repeated pleas for a formal pact.

Mr Farage announced: "The Brexit Party will not contest the 317 seats the Conservative won at the last election".

He claimed it would pose a new threat to Labour as the party focuses all its effort on Labour-held seats.

But it is also a humiliation after Farage's allies ramped up pressure on him to drop Brexit Party candidates and form a 'Leave alliance' with the Tories - only for none to happen.

Allies of Farage and of Boris Johnson were in secretive last-minute talks over the weekend to try to strike a deal ahead of Thursday's 4pm cut-off for candidate nominations.

But Farage had been standing by a threat to stand 600 Brexit Party candidates if a deal fell through - leading to candidates quitting and fury from long time ally Arron Banks.

The insurance tycoon claimed it could "split the vote so badly it could open the door of 10 Downing Street to Jeremy Corbyn ". He added: "It is time for Nigel and his supporters to be realistic."

It was a humiliation for Farage, who originally demanded Boris Johnson "renounce" his Withdrawal Agreement to save facing the Brexit Party in 600 seats across the country.

Days later he watered down the threat after it prompted a massive backlash and some Brexit Party candidates quit.

Instead he demanded to the Political Declaration, to rule out an extension of the transition period beyond 2020, or a future trade deal that has "regulatory alignment" with the EU.

Today Mr Farage attempted to claim his demands had been met.

He pointed to Boris Johnson's aim for a "super Canada plus" trade deal with no alignment, and his verbal commitment not to extend the transition period beyond 2020.

But he admitted it had been a "difficult decision to make”.

He added: "I’ve genuinely tried for months to put about the idea that putting country before party at a moment like this is the right thing to do but that effectively has come to nought."
