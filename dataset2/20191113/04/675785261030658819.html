Mum finds daughter, 21, dying after she went to bed complaining of headache

Touching tributes have been paid to Jessica Cain, from Gateshead, after her shocking death in October from meningococcal meningitis and septicaemia

13 Nov 2019, 04:58 by Sonia Sharma, https://www.facebook.com/sharminator78/

A mum has shared the devastating moment she found her daughter dying hours after she went to bed complaining of a headache.

Tributes have been paid to Jessica Cain, from Gateshead, after her tragic death from meningococcal meningitis and septicaemia last month.

The 21-year-old's mum Shelia Rowell has told how her daughter complained of feeling groggy and took two paracetamol before going to sleep.

However, the 49-year-old told Chronicle Live when she went to speak to her the following morning she found her unresponsive.

 

She added: "She said she had a bit of a headache and felt a bit groggy. She took a couple of paracetamol and water and went to bed.

"The next morning, I went to speak to her but I couldn't wake her up."

She phoned for an ambulance and attempted CPR as she received instructions over the phone. Paramedics then arrived, however they could not revive Jessica.

"Everyone is still in shock," said Sheila. "It hit everybody hard. It happened so quickly and was so sudden."

Jessica, who was born and brought up in Gateshead, was aiming to pursue a career in art therapy and began studying at the University of Derby.

  

She spent her last week travelling to Manchester, Derby, Birmingham and Sheffield with her friends before returning to the North East.

Her mum said: "She was stunning and was always happy and smiley. She is smiling in every picture I have of her."

Other touching tributes said the 21-year-old lit up the room with her smile and was adored by her family and friends.

She was also described as "a gorgeous girl both inside and out" and a "true Angel of the North".

Sheila now wants to raise awareness of potentially fatal illnesses, such as meningococcal meningitis and septicaemia, and is urging people to get help as early as possible if they spot any signs of them.

According to the charity Meningitis Now, early symptoms of meningitis and septicaemia can include fever, headache, vomiting, muscle pain and fever with cold hands and feet.

Patients can get a lot worse very quickly and people are urged to "trust your instincts" and get medical help immediately.
