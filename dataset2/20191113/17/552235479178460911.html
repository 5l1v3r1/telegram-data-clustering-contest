Father removes child from Rutherglen school over safety concerns

The Reformer revealed last week how parents said their children were being "failed" by the school they described as chaotic

13 Nov 2019, 17:01 by Edel Kenealy

Following on from our front page story last week over parental concerns with the standard of education offered at Rutherglen High, a disillusioned dad has told the Reformer he removed his son from the school.

Chris Harrison decided to take third -year pupil Michael, who has autism and is nonverbal, out of school after a number of alleged incidents.

He said: “My son is still intelligent. He sits at home, reads and looks things up and researches them.

“Then they have him sitting in school watching a Finding Nemo DVD or Mr Tumble.

“It’s not right.”

Chris says this has had a devastating impact on his son, with the once confident learner now lacking any enthusiasm.

He continued: “My son has always been the sort of pupil who liked going to school.

“He was keen to go. Over the past year his whole demeanour has changed – all his enthusiasm is gone, and that’s because of the school.”

One of Chris’ main concerns centred around Michael’s relationship with food.

Pupils at Rutherglen High are expected to have a co-ordinated support plan (CSP) in place to help them deal with some of their issues.

Sensory needs are an issue for Michael, affecting what he eats, processes and the environment that he can be in.

Chris says that the school’s support plan was intended to improve his diet, only for that to never happen.

“Improving his exposure to different foods and textures was set into Michael’s CSP as he lives on toast and yoghurt and has had dieticians involved to change this.

“His diet is the main risk to his wellbeing, so the school were to offer a range of the usual greens, chips, chicken, pasta etc to further support his progress in scents, taste and so on.

“I spoke with both teachers and support staff two months ago, and they asked me about his diet.

“They revealed he hadn’t been receiving any hot food, never mind asked if he’d like some.

“It has been pretty much the opposite of exposing him to new smells or tastes.”

In a hard-hitting e-mail to the school, Chris also detailed other issues including pupils “spending hours unattended”, which he says places the youngsters safety at risk.

South Lanarkshire Council is investigating a number of complaints from concerned parents.
