County board payout to Kegworth survivor who was refused match entry

13 Nov 2019, 01:00 by Connla Young

The GAA in Co Antrim has agreed to pay compensation to a man disabled in the Kegworth air disaster after he was refused entry to last month’s senior football final.

Toome man Stephen McCoy uses a wheelchair after being seriously injured in the 1989 air accident, when aged 16.

The undisclosed figure will now be divided between a homeless charity, helping to pay for someone to travel to Lourdes and Cargin GAA club in Co Antrim.

A former boxing champion, Mr McCoy’s injuries were so bad he did not regain consciousness for 18 months and spent three years in hospital.

There was controversy after it emerged that the 47-year-old was denied access to the Antrim senior football final between Cargin and Lamh Dhearg at Corrigan Park in west Belfast last month.

Mr McCoy had attended the Antrim showpiece with his brother when the pair were denied entry to the ground.

Antrim County Board chairman Ciarán McCavana later apologised and said that “new volunteers were manning the gate and were told to adhere to Belfast city council rule of no car access after 2pm”.

The Toome man was present at Corrigan Park when the two sides met in a drawn game the previous week.

There was an outpouring of sympathy for Mr McCoy, whose family is well known and respected in Co Antrim, after details of the incident emerged.

His solicitor Joe Rice, of John J Rice and Co, wrote to Mr McCavana threatening legal action last week.

It has now emerged that Antrim County Board has agreed to pay compensation to Mr McCoy and last night reiterated its apology.

Mr McCavana said: “We take this opportunity to again apologise to Stephen and are pleased to make a compensatory gesture to charities of Stephen’s choice.

“We are pleased that this matter has resolved to Stephen’s satisfaction.”

Mr McCoy’s sister Yvonne, who cares for her brother, said her family is still upset by the incident.

“We are still very cross about the way Stephen was treated,” she said.

“No person, never mind a disabled person, should be treated like that again.

“That’s why we are doing this, that no disabled person will go through that again.”

Ms McCoy added that “it was never about the money” and said the cash will be given to good causes.

“It is going to be split between a homeless charity, Cargin club and Down and Conor Diocese to take someone who can’t afford to go to Lourdes,” she said.

“Cargin and Lourdes are the two loves of his life,” she added.

Mr Rice said: “I welcome the change of heart on behalf of Antrim County Board and the deal that follows from it.

“This was never about money from Stephen’s point of view and now the county board has realised that no disabled person should be put in that position again.”
