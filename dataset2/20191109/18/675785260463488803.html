Bookies pay £100,000 to gambler they 'failed to protect' during betting spree

Lawyers for the gambler argued the bookies had breached the Gambling Commission's social responsibility code

09 Nov 2019, 18:15 by James Pedesta

A bookmakers accused of failing to protect a 'vulnerable' gambler during a betting spree has agreed to pay him more than £100,000.

The high street bookies settled out of court with the 32 -year-old man who lost more than £134,000 playing roulette and blackjack online over an eight year period.

His lawyers argued the bookies had breached the Gambling Commission's social responsibility code by allowing the regular customer to continue betting, despite him showing 'very obvious signs of problem gambling'.

The customer, who like the bookmakers cannot be named under the terms of the agreement except to say he was based in the south of England, ended up in a lot of debt by borrowing money from family and friends.

His lawyers were able to recoup 89 per cent of the punter's losses, securing a six figure payout, before the case went to the High Court.

 

Henrietta Dunkley, of the Ellis Jones solicitors who acted in the case, said the gambler made numerous smaller deposits which added up to thousands of pounds each day.

She said: "Our client had been gambling for eight years with a number of different providers but we were able to secure a settlement with this one as they had acted particularly badly.

"It should have been apparent quite early on there was an issue as he was making lots of little deposits adding up to thousands of pounds each day.

"He had shown very obvious signs of problem gambling for a considerable amount of time and was spending far beyond his means, losing well in excess of £100,000.

"But instead of referring him to the Responsible Gambling team they ignored the red flags and let him carry on.

"We were able to persuasively argue that there had been clear failings by the betting operator.

"The firm had failed to protect him as a vulnerable customer and therefore breached a number of obligations set out within the Gambling Commission's Social Responsibility Code."

Paul Kanolik, a partner in the firm's betting disputes team, argued the legal outcome underlined how customers should be given more protection by betting firms.

He said: "This fantastic result is reflective of a much wider recognition by the Gambling Commission and other organisations that more protection is needed for gambling customers, particularly those that display obvious signs of problem gambling."
